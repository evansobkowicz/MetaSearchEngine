<p><i><b>Jurassic Park</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Media_franchise" title="Media franchise">media franchise</a> consisting of novels, films, comics, and video games centering on a disastrous attempt to create a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_park" title="Theme park">theme park</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">cloned</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>. It began in 1990 when <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a> bought the rights to the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(novel)" title="Jurassic Park (novel)">novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a> before it was even published.</p>
<p>The book was successful, as was the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">1993 film adaptation</a>, which led to three sequels, although the third and fourth films were not based on novels, as the first two were. The software developers <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Software" title="Ocean Software">Ocean Software</a>, <a href="/wiki/BlueSky_Software" title="BlueSky Software">BlueSky Software</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_of_America" title="Sega of America">Sega of America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Telltale_Games" title="Telltale Games">Telltale Games</a> have had the rights to developing video games since the 1993 film, and numerous games have been produced.</p>
<p>The <i>Jurassic Park</i> Ultimate Trilogy was released on <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> on October 25, 2011 in North America. The first film was re-released in 3D on April 5, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> On June 21, 1996, <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios_Hollywood" title="Universal Studios Hollywood">Universal Studios Hollywood</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal%27s_Islands_of_Adventure" title="Universal's Islands of Adventure">Universal's Islands of Adventure</a> opened <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park:_The_Ride_(Universal_Studios_Hollywood)" title="Jurassic Park: The Ride (Universal Studios Hollywood)">Jurassic Park: The Ride</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_River_Adventure" title="Jurassic Park River Adventure">Jurassic Park River Adventure</a></i>. The rides are heavily themed on the main trilogy. The rides are similar to <a href="/wiki/Thorpe_Park" title="Thorpe Park">Thorpe Park</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tidal_Wave_(Thorpe_Park)" title="Tidal Wave (Thorpe Park)">Tidal Wave</a></i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parc_Asterix" title="Parc Asterix">Parc Asterix</a>'s <i>Le Grand Splash</i> which translated in English is <i>The Big Splash</i>.</p>
<p>The fourth film <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_World" title="Jurassic World">Jurassic World</a></i> was initially scheduled to be released in the summer of 2005, but has since been pushed back to a June 12, 2015 release. In March 2013, <a href="/wiki/Colin_Trevorrow" title="Colin Trevorrow">Colin Trevorrow</a> was announced as the director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Frank Marshall and Patrick Crowley are producing the film with a script written by Trevorrow and <a href="/wiki/Derek_Connolly" title="Derek Connolly">Derek Connolly</a>. Trevorrow said sequels to <i>Jurassic World</i> have been discussed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a> originally conceived a screenplay around a <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaur</a> being cloned from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_DNA" title="Ancient DNA">fossil DNA</a>. After wrestling with this idea for a while, he came up with the idea of <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(novel)" title="Jurassic Park (novel)">Jurassic Park</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> learned of the novel in October 1989 while he and Crichton were discussing a screenplay that would become the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/ER_(TV_series)" title="ER (TV series)">ER</a></i>. Before the book was published, Crichton put up a non-negotiable fee for $1.5 million as well as a substantial percentage of the gross. <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tim_Burton" title="Tim Burton">Tim Burton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Donner" title="Richard Donner">Richard Donner</a>, and <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Dante" title="Joe Dante">Joe Dante</a> also bid for the rights,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Production_notes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Production_notes-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Universal further paid Crichton $500,000 to adapt his own novel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> but in May 1990, Universal eventually decided on Spielberg making the adaptation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Production_notes_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Production_notes-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Universal desperately needed money to keep their company alive, and partially succeeded with <i>Jurassic Park</i>, as it became a critical<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and commercial<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> success.</p>
<p>After <i>Jurassic Park</i> was released to home video, Crichton was pressured from many sources for a sequel novel. Crichton declined all offers until Spielberg himself told him that he would be keen to direct a movie adaptation of the sequel, if one were written. Crichton began work almost immediately. After the novel was published in 1995, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park">The Lost World: Jurassic Park</a></i> began production in September 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Before the production of the second film, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Johnston" title="Joe Johnston">Joe Johnston</a> approached <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> about directing the project. While Spielberg wanted to direct the first sequel, he agreed that if there was ever a third film, Johnston could direct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Making_of_Jurassic_Park_III_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Making_of_Jurassic_Park_III-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Production began on August 30, 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BFI_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Novels">Novels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Novels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(novel)" title="Jurassic Park (novel)">Jurassic Park (novel)</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(Crichton_novel)" title="The Lost World (Crichton novel)">The Lost World (Crichton novel)</a></div>
<p><i>Jurassic Park</i> stemmed from the idea of a screenplay about cloning a <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaur</a> from fossilized DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mc_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mc-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Crichton worked on the idea for several years; he decided his first draft would have a theme park for the setting and a young boy as the main character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mc_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-mc-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Response was extremely negative, so Crichton rewrote the story to make it from an adult's point of view, this story was much more well received.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mc_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-mc-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A sequel novel began after readers and Steven Spielberg himself pressured Crichton for a sequel novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prod_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-prod-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Crichton confirmed that his novel had elements taken from Sir Arthur Conan Doyle's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(Arthur_Conan_Doyle)" title="The Lost World (Arthur Conan Doyle)">novel of the same name</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The book was also an outstanding success, both with professional and amateur critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prod_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-prod-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> A film adaptation was released in 1997.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="InGen">InGen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: InGen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fictional company <b>InGen</b> (<b>In</b>ternational <b>Gen</b>etic Technologies, Inc.) is based in <a href="/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California" title="Palo Alto, California">Palo Alto</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and has one location in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-source-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Nevertheless, most of InGen's research took place on the fictional islands of Isla Sorna and Isla Nublar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-source-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> While official records indicated InGen was just one of any number of small 1980s genetic engineering start-ups, the events of the novel and film revealed to a select group that InGen had discovered a method of cloning <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> and other animals (including a <a href="/wiki/Quagga" title="Quagga">quagga</a>) using blood extracted from <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitoes</a> trapped in amber during various periods in time, ranging from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesozoic_era" title="Mesozoic era">Mesozoic era</a> to the 1800s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-source_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-source-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Beacham's Encyclopedia of Popular Fiction</i> describe InGen as comparable to another "sleazy organization".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Other sources reference the company's receiving the baby T-Rex as an allusion to other exploitative entrepreneurs depicted in <i><a href="/wiki/King_Kong_(1933_film)" title="King Kong (1933 film)">King Kong</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Ken Gelder describes InGen as "resolutely secretive, just like the firm in <a href="/wiki/John_Grisham" title="John Grisham">Grisham</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Firm_(novel)" title="The Firm (novel)">novel</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Films">Films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurassic_Park_.281993.29"><i>Jurassic Park</i> (1993)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Jurassic Park (1993)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park (film)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jurassic_Park_3D.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="466" data-file-width="295" height="348" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Jurassic_Park_3D.jpg/220px-Jurassic_Park_3D.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c7/Jurassic_Park_3D.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c7/Jurassic_Park_3D.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Before Crichton's book was even published, studios such as Warner Bros., Columbia TriStar, 20th Century Fox, and Universal had already begun bidding to acquire the picture rights. Spielberg, with the backing of Universal Studios, acquired the rights to the novel before its publication in 1990, and Crichton himself was hired by Universal Studios for an additional US$500,000 to adapt the novel into a proper screenplay. <a href="/wiki/David_Koepp" title="David Koepp">David Koepp</a> wrote the final draft, which left out much of the novel's exposition and violence, and made numerous changes to the characters.</p>
<p>When an incident results in the death of an employee, Jurassic Park owner John Hammond (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Attenborough" title="Richard Attenborough">Richard Attenborough</a>) brings in three specialists to sign off on the park to calm investors. The specialists, paleontologist Alan Grant (<a href="/wiki/Sam_Neill" title="Sam Neill">Sam Neill</a>), paleobotanist Ellie Sattler (<a href="/wiki/Laura_Dern" title="Laura Dern">Laura Dern</a>), and chaos theorist Ian Malcolm (<a href="/wiki/Jeff_Goldblum" title="Jeff Goldblum">Jeff Goldblum</a>), are surprised to see the island park's main attraction are living, breathing dinosaurs, created with a mixture of fossilized DNA and genetic cross-breeding/cloning. However, when lead programmer Dennis Nedry (<a href="/wiki/Wayne_Knight" title="Wayne Knight">Wayne Knight</a>) shuts down the park's power to sneak out with samples of the dinosaur embryos to sell to a corporate rival, the dinosaurs break free, and the survivors are forced to find a way to turn the power back on and make it out alive. The film also stars <a href="/wiki/Bob_Peck" title="Bob Peck">Bob Peck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Ferrero" title="Martin Ferrero">Martin Ferrero</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ariana_Richards" title="Ariana Richards">Ariana Richards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Mazzello" title="Joseph Mazzello">Joseph Mazzello</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_L._Jackson" title="Samuel L. Jackson">Samuel L. Jackson</a>.</p>
<p><i>Jurassic Park</i> is regarded as a landmark in the use of computer-generated imagery, and received positive reviews from critics, who praised the effects, though reactions to other elements of the picture, such as character development, were mixed. During its release, the film grossed more than $914 million worldwide, becoming the most successful film released up to that time (surpassing <i><a href="/wiki/E.T._the_Extra-Terrestrial" title="E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial">E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial</a></i> and surpassed 4 years later by <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">Titanic</a></i>), and it is currently the <a href="/wiki/List_of_highest-grossing_films" title="List of highest-grossing films">15th highest grossing feature film</a> (taking inflation into account, it is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_highest-grossing_films_in_Canada_and_the_United_States" title="List of highest-grossing films in Canada and the United States">20th-highest-grossing film</a> in North America). It is the most financially successful film for <a href="/wiki/NBCUniversal" title="NBCUniversal">NBCUniversal</a> and Steven Spielberg.</p>
<p><i>Jurassic Park</i> was converted into 3D and it was re-released on April 5, 2013 for its 20th Anniversary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park_.281997.29"><i>The Lost World: Jurassic Park</i> (1997)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Lost World: Jurassic Park (1997)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park">The Lost World: Jurassic Park</a></div>
<p>After the success of the first 1993 film, fans and critics alike pressured Crichton for a sequel novel. Having never done one before, Crichton originally declined, but when <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> finally started pressuring Crichton, a sequel novel was announced. As soon as the novel was published, a film was in pre-production, with a target release date of mid-1997. The film was a commercial success, breaking many box-office records when released. The film had mixed reviews, similar to its predecessor in terms of characterization. Much like the first film, The Lost World made a number of changes to the plot and characters from the book, replacing the corporate rivals with an internal power struggle and changing the roles/characterizations of several protagonists.</p>
<p>When a vacationing family stumbles upon the dinosaurs of Isla Sorna, a secondary island where the animals were bred in mass and allowed to grow before being transported to the park, Ian Malcolm (<a href="/wiki/Jeff_Goldblum" title="Jeff Goldblum">Jeff Goldblum</a>) is called in by John Hammond (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Attenborough" title="Richard Attenborough">Richard Attenborough</a>) to lead a team to document the island to turn it into a preserve, where the animals can roam free without interference from the outside world. Malcolm agrees to go when he discovers his girlfriend, paleontologist Sarah Harding (<a href="/wiki/Julianne_Moore" title="Julianne Moore">Julianne Moore</a>) is already on the island, while at the same time Hammond's nephew Peter Ludlow (<a href="/wiki/Arliss_Howard" title="Arliss Howard">Arliss Howard</a>) has taken over his uncle's company and leads a team of hunters to capture the creatures and bring them back to a theme park in San Diego. The two groups clash and are ultimately forced to work together to evade the predatory creatures and survive the second island. The film also stars <a href="/wiki/Pete_Postlethwaite" title="Pete Postlethwaite">Pete Postlethwaite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Schiff" title="Richard Schiff">Richard Schiff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vince_Vaughn" title="Vince Vaughn">Vince Vaughn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanessa_Lee_Chester" title="Vanessa Lee Chester">Vanessa Lee Chester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Stormare" title="Peter Stormare">Peter Stormare</a>, and a young <a href="/wiki/Camilla_Belle" title="Camilla Belle">Camilla Belle</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurassic_Park_III_.282001.29"><i>Jurassic Park III</i> (2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Jurassic Park III (2001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_III" title="Jurassic Park III">Jurassic Park III</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joe_Johnston" title="Joe Johnston">Joe Johnston</a> had been interested in directing the sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> and approached his friend <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> about the project. While Spielberg wanted to direct the first sequel, he agreed that if there was ever a third film, Johnston could direct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Making_of_Jurassic_Park_III_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Making_of_Jurassic_Park_III-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Spielberg, nevertheless, stayed involved in this film by becoming its executive producer. Production began on August 30, 2000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BFI_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> with filming in California, <a href="/wiki/Oahu" title="Oahu">Oahu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Molokai" title="Molokai">Molokai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> It is the first Jurassic Park film not to be based on a novel. The film was a success and had mixed reviews from critics. Most were split on whether the third installment was better or worse than its predecessor. The film once again suffered reviews mentioning little to no characterization.</p>
<p>When their son goes missing while parasailing at Isla Sorna, the Kirbys (<a href="/wiki/William_H._Macy" title="William H. Macy">William H. Macy</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A9a_Leoni" title="Téa Leoni">Téa Leoni</a>) hire Alan Grant (<a href="/wiki/Sam_Neill" title="Sam Neill">Sam Neill</a>) under false pretenses to help them navigate the island. Believing it to be nothing more than sight-seeing, and that he will act as a dinosaur guide from the safety of their plane, he's startled to find them landing on the ground, where they are stalked by a super-predator, the <a href="/wiki/Spinosaurus" title="Spinosaurus">Spinosaurus</a>, which destroys their plane. As they search for the Kirbys' son, the situation grows dire as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velociraptors" title="Velociraptors">Velociraptors</a> (more intelligent than ever) hunt their group, and they must find a way off the island. The film also stars <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Nivola" title="Alessandro Nivola">Alessandro Nivola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jeter" title="Michael Jeter">Michael Jeter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Morgan_(actor)" title="Trevor Morgan (actor)">Trevor Morgan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Harelik" title="Mark Harelik">Mark Harelik</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Laura_Dern" title="Laura Dern">Laura Dern</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurassic_World_.282015.29"><i>Jurassic World</i> (2015)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Jurassic World (2015)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_World" title="Jurassic World">Jurassic World</a></div>
<p>Steven Spielberg devised a story idea for a fourth film in 2001, during production of <i>Jurassic Park III</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2002, <a href="/wiki/William_Monahan" title="William Monahan">William Monahan</a> was hired to write the script,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> with the film's release scheduled for 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Monahan finished the first draft of the script in 2003,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> with the film's plot revolving around dinosaurs escaping to the mainland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Sam Neill and Richard Attenborough were set to reprise their characters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Keira_Knightley" title="Keira Knightley">Keira Knightley</a> was in talks for two separate roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2004, <a href="/wiki/John_Sayles" title="John Sayles">John Sayles</a> wrote two drafts of the script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Sayles' first draft involved a team of deinonychus being trained for use in rescue missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> His second draft involved genetically modified dinosaur-human mercenaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Both drafts were scrapped. In 2006, a new script was being worked on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Laura Dern was contacted to reprise her role, with the film expected for release in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The film was further delayed by the <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_Writers_Guild_of_America_strike" title="2007–08 Writers Guild of America strike">2007–08 Writers Guild of America strike</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mark_Protosevich" title="Mark Protosevich">Mark Protosevich</a> wrote two <a href="/wiki/Film_treatment" title="Film treatment">film treatments</a> in 2011, which were rejected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rick_Jaffa_and_Amanda_Silver" title="Rick Jaffa and Amanda Silver">Rick Jaffa and Amanda Silver</a> were hired in 2012 to write the script,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> but they were replaced by Derek Connolly. In 2013, <a href="/wiki/Colin_Trevorrow" title="Colin Trevorrow">Colin Trevorrow</a> was announced as a director and co-writer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-director1_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-director1-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-director2_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-director2-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> with the film scheduled for release on June 12, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> The film will be shot in 3D.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While no official plot has been released, the film is said to feature a new park, Jurassic World, built on the remains of the original park on Isla Nublar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jurassic_Park_4_titled_.27Jurassic_World.27_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jurassic_Park_4_titled_.27Jurassic_World.27-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> The film sees the park run by Simon Masrani (<a href="/wiki/Irrfan_Khan" title="Irrfan Khan">Irrfan Khan</a>) and Masrani Corp, and features the return of Dr. Henry Wu (<a href="/wiki/BD_Wong" title="BD Wong">BD Wong</a>) from the first film, who harbors a grudge against his former employer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E2.80.98Jurassic_World.E2.80.99_Director_Offers_Filming_Details_and_Confirms_Returning_Character_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.98Jurassic_World.E2.80.99_Director_Offers_Filming_Details_and_Confirms_Returning_Character-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chris_Pratt" title="Chris Pratt">Chris Pratt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bryce_Dallas_Howard" title="Bryce Dallas Howard">Bryce Dallas Howard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jake_Johnson" title="Jake Johnson">Jake Johnson</a> star, while <a href="/wiki/Vincent_D%27Onofrio" title="Vincent D'Onofrio">Vincent D'Onofrio</a> will portray the main antagonist, Vic Hoskins. The cast also includes <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Lapkus" title="Lauren Lapkus">Lauren Lapkus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ty_Simpkins" title="Ty Simpkins">Ty Simpkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Robinson_(American_actor)" title="Nick Robinson (American actor)">Nick Robinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omar_Sy" title="Omar Sy">Omar Sy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Judy_Greer" title="Judy Greer">Judy Greer</a>.</p>

<p>On the possibility of potential sequels, Trevorrow said: "We wanted to create something that would be a little bit less arbitrary and episodic, and something that could potentially arc into a series that would feel like a complete story."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_books">Comic books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Comic books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topps_Comics">Topps Comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Topps Comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From June 1993 to August 1997 the now-defunct <a href="/wiki/Topps_Comics" title="Topps Comics">Topps Comics</a> published comic adaptions of Jurassic Park and The Lost World: Jurassic Park, as well as several tie-in series.</p>
<ul>
<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></b></i> #0–4 (June – September 1993). Adaptation of the film, adapted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_Simonson" title="Walter Simonson">Walter Simonson</a> and pencilled by <a href="/wiki/Gil_Kane" title="Gil Kane">Gil Kane</a>. Each issue had two covers – a main cover by Gil Kane, with the variant by <a href="/wiki/Dave_Cockrum" title="Dave Cockrum">Dave Cockrum</a>. Issue #0 features two prequel stories to the film, and was only available with the trade paperback of the film adaption.</li>
<li><i><b>Jurassic Park: Raptor</b></i> #1–2 (November – December 1993). First part of the "Raptor" trilogy. Written by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Englehart" title="Steve Englehart">Steve Englehart</a> and pencilled by Armando Gil and Dell Barras.</li>
<li><i><b>Jurassic Park: Raptors Attack</b></i> #1–4 (March – June 1994). Second part of the "Raptor" trilogy Written by Steve Englehart, pencilled by Armando Gil (#1) and Chaz Truog, with covers by Michael Golden.</li>
<li><i><b>Jurassic Park: Raptors Hijack</b></i> #1–4 (July – October 1994). Third part of the "Raptor" trilogy. Written by Steve Englehart, pencilled by Neil Vokes, with covers by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Golden_(comics)" title="Michael Golden (comics)">Michael Golden</a>.</li>
<li><i><b>Jurassic Park: Annual</b></i> #1 (May 1995). Featuring two stories, one being a sequel and one being a prequel. Written by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Almond" title="Bob Almond">Bob Almond</a> Michael Golden and Renée Witterstaetter, pencilled by Claude St. Aubin and Ed Murr, with a cover by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Golden_(comics)" title="Michael Golden (comics)">Michael Golden</a>.</li>
<li><i><b>Return to Jurassic Park</b></i> #1–9 (April 1995 – February 1996). Ongoing series. The first four issues were written by Steve Englehart and pencilled by <a href="/wiki/Joe_Staton" title="Joe Staton">Joe Staton</a>. The next four issues were written by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Bierbaum" title="Tom Bierbaum">Tom Bierbaum</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Bierbaum" title="Mary Bierbaum">Mary Bierbaum</a>, being drawn by Armando Gil. The first 8 issues had covers by Michael Golden. The ninth and final issue was a jam book written by <a href="/wiki/Keith_Giffen" title="Keith Giffen">Keith Giffen</a> and Dwight Jon Zimmerman, featuring artwork by such acclaimed artists as <a href="/wiki/Jason_Pearson" title="Jason Pearson">Jason Pearson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Hughes" title="Adam Hughes">Adam Hughes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gulacy" title="Paul Gulacy">Paul Gulacy</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Byrne_(comics)" title="John Byrne (comics)">John Byrne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Maguire_(artist)" title="Kevin Maguire (artist)">Kevin Maguire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Zeck" title="Mike Zeck">Mike Zeck</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_P%C3%A9rez" title="George Pérez">George Pérez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Chadwick" title="Paul Chadwick">Paul Chadwick</a>, with a cover by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Bolton_(comics)" title="John Bolton (comics)">John Bolton</a>.</li>
<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park">The Lost World: Jurassic Park</a></b></i> #1–4 (May – August 1997). Adaptation of the second film. Adapted by <a href="/wiki/Don_McGregor" title="Don McGregor">Don McGregor</a> and pencilled by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Butler" title="Jeff Butler">Jeff Butler</a> (#1–2) and Claude St. Aubin (#3–4). Each issue of the series featured two covers – one by Walter Simonson and a photo cover.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IDW_Comics">IDW Comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: IDW Comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in June 2010, <a href="/wiki/IDW_Publishing" title="IDW Publishing">IDW Publishing</a> began publishing Jurassic Park comics. They also acquired the rights to reprint the issues published by Topps in the 1990s, which they began to do in trade paperback format starting in November 2010. <a href="/wiki/Mark_Schultz_(comics)" title="Mark Schultz (comics)">Mark Schultz</a> will be drawing a future cover.</p>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Jurassic Park: Redemption</b></i> #1–5 (June 2010 – October 2010). Five-issue series written by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Schreck" title="Bob Schreck">Bob Schreck</a> with art by Nate van Dyke. Each issue has a main cover penciled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Yeates" title="Tom Yeates">Tom Yeates</a>, with variant covers by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Miller_(comics)" title="Frank Miller (comics)">Frank Miller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Adams_(comics)" title="Arthur Adams (comics)">Arthur Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Pope" title="Paul Pope">Paul Pope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Wrightson" title="Bernie Wrightson">Bernie Wrightson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Stout" title="Bill Stout">Bill Stout</a>, respectively.</li>
<li><i><b>Jurassic Park: The Devils in the Desert</b></i> #1–4 (January 2011 – April 2011) Four-issue series written and illustrated by <a href="/wiki/John_Byrne_(comics)" title="John Byrne (comics)">John Byrne</a>.</li>
<li><i><b>Jurassic Park: Dangerous Games</b></i> #1–5 (September 2011 – January 2012) Five-issue series written by <a href="/wiki/Greg_Bear" title="Greg Bear">Greg Bear</a> and Erik Bear, with art by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jorge_Jimenez" title="Jorge Jimenez">Jorge Jimenez</a> and a variant cover by <a href="/wiki/Geof_Darrow" title="Geof Darrow">Geof Darrow</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_video_games" title="Jurassic Park video games">Jurassic Park video games</a></div>
<p>When the first film was released in 1993, two different video game publishers were given the rights to publish games based on it, Sega and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Software" title="Ocean Software">Ocean Software</a>. They both produced several different games based on the movie for several different game systems, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NES" title="NES">NES</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Sega Genesis</a>. In 1994, Ocean Software produced a sequel to the first game in the series for the Game Boy and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SNES" title="SNES">SNES</a> systems. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Interactive" title="Universal Interactive">Universal Interactive</a> also produced an interactive game for the ill-fated <a href="/wiki/3DO_Interactive_Multiplayer" title="3DO Interactive Multiplayer">3DO</a> system.</p>
<p>For the second film in the franchise, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DreamWorks_Interactive" title="DreamWorks Interactive">DreamWorks Interactive</a> released 5 games for the most popular systems at the time. The third film had the biggest marketing push, spawning seven video games for <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">PC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a>. A number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightgun" title="Lightgun">lightgun</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcade_games" title="Arcade games">arcade games</a> were also released for all three films.</p>
<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park:_Operation_Genesis" title="Jurassic Park: Operation Genesis">Jurassic Park: Operation Genesis</a> was released for the Xbox, PlayStation 2, and PC.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurassic_Park:_The_Game"><i>Jurassic Park: The Game</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Jurassic Park: The Game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A new episodic video game based on the <i>Jurassic Park</i> franchise, bridging the story of the first two movies and entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park:_The_Game" title="Jurassic Park: The Game">Jurassic Park: The Game</a></i>, was developed by <a href="/wiki/Telltale_Games" title="Telltale Games">Telltale Games</a> in a deal with Universal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> It was released on November 15, 2011 to a generally good reception. The game was acknowledged canon with Jurassic Park continuity by writers of the original <i>Jurassic Park</i>. The game takes place during and after the events of the original film, and features <i>Troodon</i> and a <i>Mosasaur</i>, amongst other creatures. The game is available on PC, Mac and iPads.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurassic_Park:_Builder"><i>Jurassic Park: Builder</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Jurassic Park: Builder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A freemium game that is based on the Jurassic Park franchise, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_Builder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jurassic Park Builder (page does not exist)">Jurassic Park Builder</a></i>, was released July 23, 2012 as an app for Android and iOS devices, developed by <a href="/wiki/Ludia" title="Ludia">Ludia</a> games. Taking place in a possible future following the events of the first three films and preceding the events of the fourth, the player is tasked with rebuilding Jurassic Park on Isla Nublar and populating it with various species of dinosaurs, both carnivorous, herbivorous and insectivorous. John Hammond, Alan Grant and other film characters appear as they did in the films to deliver missions essential to progressing the game. All the dinosaurs seen in the films can be created and added to the park, as well as many new, previously unseen species. It is theorized that the building of the park is supposed to be a DIY construction of <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_World" title="Jurassic World">Jurassic World</a>.</p>
<p>Further expansions to the game have introduced both an Aquatic Park, featuring Aquatic creatures such as Crustaceavores and Piscivores, and a Glacier park, featuring Ice Age era mammals. A further update to the game added Battle Mode, which was inspired by the <i>T-rex</i> vs. <i>Spinosaurus</i> battle from <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_III" title="Jurassic Park III">Jurassic Park III</a></i>. Battle Mode allows players to make certain dinosaurs fight each other, and fight other friends' dinosaurs.</p>
<p>The game has met with generally favourable reviews, with most praising the attention to detail and faithfulness to the source material, while others approved of the interesting new direction presented in the game, referring to the expanded range of species as well as the two new parks.</p>
<p>The game is available to download for free on the Apple AppStore and the Google PlayStore, and is also available to play on <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>.</p>
