<p><b>Andrew Stanton</b> (born December 3, 1965) is an American film director, screenwriter, producer, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voice_actor" title="Voice actor">voice actor</a> based at <a href="/wiki/Pixar" title="Pixar">Pixar Animation Studios</a>. His film work includes writing and directing Pixar's <i><a href="/wiki/A_Bug%27s_Life" title="A Bug's Life">A Bug's Life</a></i> (1998) (as co-director), <i><a href="/wiki/Finding_Nemo" title="Finding Nemo">Finding Nemo</a></i> (2003), and <i><a href="/wiki/WALL-E" title="WALL-E">WALL-E</a></i> (2008), and his first live-action film, <i><a href="/wiki/John_Carter_(film)" title="John Carter (film)">John Carter</a></i> (2012). He also co-wrote all three <a href="/wiki/Toy_Story_(franchise)" title="Toy Story (franchise)"><i>Toy Story</i> films</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Monsters,_Inc." title="Monsters, Inc.">Monsters, Inc.</a></i> (2001)</p>
<p><i>Finding Nemo</i> and <i>WALL-E</i> earned him two <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Animated_Feature" title="Academy Award for Best Animated Feature">Academy Awards for Best Animated Feature</a>. He was also nominated for three <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Screenplay" title="Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay">Academy Awards for Best Original Screenplay</a>, for <i>Finding Nemo</i>, <i>WALL-E</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Toy_Story" title="Toy Story">Toy Story</a></i> (1995), and for an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Adapted_Screenplay" title="Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay">Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Toy_Story_3" title="Toy Story 3">Toy Story 3</a></i> (2010).</p>
<p>He is currently in charge of directing the sequel to <i>Finding Nemo</i>, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Finding_Dory" title="Finding Dory">Finding Dory</a></i>, planned for 2016.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Stanton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an interview with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Magazine" title="World Magazine">World Magazine's</a></i> Megan Basham, Stanton explained his singular vision for <i><a href="/wiki/WALL-E" title="WALL-E">WALL-E</a></i>: "What really interested me was the idea of the most human thing in the universe being a machine because it has more interest in finding out what the point of living is than actual people. The greatest commandment <a href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a> gives us is to love, but that's not always our priority. So I came up with this premise that could demonstrate what I was trying to say—that irrational love defeats the world's programming. You've got these two robots that are trying to go above their basest directives, literally their programming, to experience love."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to his direction and writing work for Pixar, he has also done some voice work, most notably as Evil Emperor Zurg in <i><a href="/wiki/Toy_Story_2" title="Toy Story 2">Toy Story 2</a></i> (1999) and Crush the laidback turtle in <i><a href="/wiki/Finding_Nemo" title="Finding Nemo">Finding Nemo</a></i>.</p>
<p>Stanton made his live-action directing debut with Disney's <i><a href="/wiki/John_Carter_(film)" title="John Carter (film)">John Carter</a></i>. The film was based on Edgar Rice Burroughs' novel, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Princess_of_Mars" title="A Princess of Mars">A Princess of Mars</a></i>. It was released in March 2012 and received mixed reviews from critics. The big-budget science fiction film did not meet Disney's domestic box-office expectations; they stated that they would lose $200 million on the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
