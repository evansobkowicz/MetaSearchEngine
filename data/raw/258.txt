<p>In <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.theguardian.com/film/her">Her</a>, a film arguably about the future of relationships, Joaquin Phoenix portrays a lonely, alienated and heartbroken man who reverts to technology to fulfil his romantic and sexual needs. The technology in question is an artificial intelligence (AI) algorithm (the voiceover by Scarlett Johansson) whose only weakness is the inability to avoid falling in love with her client. Though technically unfeasible by todays AI standards, the broad premise of the movie is more realistic than most people may think. Indeed, in the past 10 years our lives have been transformed by technology and love is no exception. With <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/valentines-day">Valentines Day</a> around the corner, theres no better time to examine some of the recent developments in this area.</p>
<p>Taobao, Chinas version of Amazon, offers virtual girlfriends and boyfriends for around <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/blogs-news-from-elsewhere-28982481">$2 (1.20) per day</a>. These are real humans, but they only relate with their paying customers via the phone  calls or text  in order to perform fairly unromantic tasks such as wake up calls, good night calls, and (perhaps the most useful service) sympathetically listen to clients complaints. If this is all you expect from a relationship, it at least comes at a cheap price.</p>
<p>Similar services already exist in India, where <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.timeslive.co.za/lifestyle/2011/02/01/indian-website-offers-bachelors-a-virtual-wife">biwihotohaisi.com</a> helps bachelors practice for married life with a virtual wife, and Japan, where <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.businessinsider.com/afp-virtual-boyfriends-cat-ears-optional-at-tokyo-game-show-2014-9?IR=T">romance simulation games</a> are popular with men and women, even when they feature animated avatars rather than human partners.</p>
<h2>Invisible Boyfriend</h2>
<p>A new US app, <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://uk.businessinsider.com/invisible-boyfriend-lets-you-fake-a-relationship-2015-1">Invisible Boyfriend</a>, allows users to customise the looks, personality and interests of an imaginary male partner and pay $25 (16) for his virtual company. Particularly appealing is the apps ability to recreate a real-looking remote boyfriend via hyper-realistic texts, pictures and interactions. This is the closest to having a real boyfriend who is just physically absent, hence the name of the app.</p>
<p>Another site, <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.98rock.com/pages/virtual-girlfriend/virtual-girlfriend.html">98rock.com</a>, which comes up as top hit if you google virtual girlfriend, attempts to offer an interactive soft-porn experience shot from a point of view. Each day the site features a different babe of the day a real physical woman in her underwear sitting on a red sofa  and the option to type a command. It is the digital equivalent of a sex-by-window-display formula popularised in Amsterdams red light district. Now, I only tried this on one occasion, so I dont know if other virtual girlfriends on this site are more cooperative, but the one I got only responded to the jump command, ignoring my request for her to smile, talk, or sing, among others.</p>
<p>Virtual communities, such as <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0747563213004081">Second Life</a>, recreate all aspects of the relationship cycle, including pregnancy and birth. In fact, there are now <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.second-life-adventures.com/second-life-sex/">multiple guides</a> on how to have sex in a virtual world, which perhaps suggests that physical skills are not necessarily transferable to our avatars. One salient difference is that most avatars are not equipped with genitals. As an expert notes: The first thing you need to do, get yourself a penis/vagina. Heres a piece of advice: Second Life is a visual medium. Hence it does have a certain importance that you choose a penis that actually looks realistic and is in-line with the colour of your skin. Other suggestions include upgrading looks  because with the prefab [Second Life] avatar you will find it very difficult to get laid  and making an effort to role-play a seductive or erotic situation. Indeed, directness and bluntness is as discouraged as in the analogue world.</p>
<h2>Tinder</h2>
<p>Many of you will find these developments absurd or pathological, but lets not forget that the contemporary notion of relationships as romantic has not always represented the norm  in fact, love as we define it today is a fairly recent idea. In the times of the cavemen and primitive tribes, women were simply captured. In medieval times, marriages were engineered for pragmatic rather than emotional reasons, as is still the case today in much of the developing world. More recent rituals, such as random drunken encounters at a bar or club, or online dating, are not always driven by love either and hardly qualify as romantic. The impressive success of <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.theguardian.com/media-network/media-network-blog/2014/jan/17/tinder-dating-psychology-technosexual">Tinder</a> is arguably based on its ability to simultaneously cater to our primordial instincts and our postmodern obsession with efficiency.</p>
<p>Still, all forms of cybersex or, if you prefer, cyberlove, are still viewed negatively by most people and the media. In fact, it is quite common to focus on the detrimental aspects of technologically-mediated relationships, as <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.theguardian.com/technology/2008/nov/14/second-life-virtual-worlds-divorce">this piece for the Guardian demonstrates</a>. It is also easy to be shocked by the latest manifestations of love-related technologies, even though they are typically just minor variations of previous tools, activities or rituals to which we have clearly habituated already. For instance, phone sex was widely regarded as normal by the time chat forum sex emerged, sms-sex was widely accepted by the time sexting apps like <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.theguardian.com/media-network/media-network-blog/2014/sep/04/snapchat-message-valuation-technology-bubble">Snapchat came along</a>, and online dating seems rather conventional now that mobile dating and hook-up apps have taken over.</p>
<p>Online pornography is still taboo yet porn is estimated to be a <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.news24.com/Technology/News/Porn-spikes-on-smart-mobile-devices-20130925">$97bn</a> industry and there are over <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://internet-filter-review.toptenreviews.com/internet-pornography-statistics.html">4m porn sites</a> in the world. Importantly, most new technologies  not just in the realm of sex and love  transition rather quickly from being merely a niche trend used by early adopters to becoming mainstream. It is estimated that <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="https://lra.le.ac.uk/handle/2381/14033">over 30% of Britons</a> are looking for some form of love or sex online. As the futurist <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Gibson">William Gibson</a>, who coined the term cyberspace, noted: The future is already here  its just not evenly distributed.</p>
<p>Finally, it is likely that virtual relationships fulfil the same psychological needs fulfilled by real-world or physical relationships, though the boundaries between these categories are increasingly hard to define. A recent <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22544305">scientific study</a> on Second Life users  conducted in the actual virtual community  found that most residents saw cybersex as a form of self-therapy, a source of instant pleasures, liberation from social norms, a tool for self-expression, and exploration and novelty, and that it enabled them to experience close emotional bonds and intense, meaningful, and erotic feelings. The study also revealed that virtual relationships surpass analogue ones by a three-to-one ratio, not least because they are more likely to enable quick, superficial and ephemeral interactions. This makes Valentines Day a more expensive affair online than offline (for a list of Second Life Valentines presents, see <a class=" u-underline" data-component="in-body-link" data-link-name="in body link" href="http://community.secondlife.com/t5/Featured-News/Put-Your-Valentine-s-Gift-from-Linden-Lab-to-Good-Use/ba-p/1889629">here</a>).</p>
