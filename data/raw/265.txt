<p><b>Edward John Smith</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reserve_Decoration" title="Reserve Decoration">RD</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Reserve" title="Royal Naval Reserve">RNR</a> (27 January 1850 – 15 April 1912) was an English naval reserve officer. Raised in a working environment, he left school early to join the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Reserve" title="Royal Naval Reserve">Royal Naval Reserve</a>. After earning his master, he entered the service of the <a href="/wiki/White_Star_Line" title="White Star Line">White Star Line</a>, a prestigious British company. He quickly rose through the ranks, and graduated in 1887 his first command aboard the <a href="/wiki/SS_Celtic_(1872)" title="SS Celtic (1872)">SS <i>Celtic</i></a>. He served as commanding officer of numerous <a href="/wiki/White_Star_Line" title="White Star Line">White Star Line</a> vessels,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-igi_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-igi-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS_Majestic_(1890)" title="SS Majestic (1890)"><i>Majestic</i></a> '​ (which he commanded for nine years) and attracted a strong and loyal following amongst passengers.</p>
<p>In 1904, Smith became the commodore of the <a href="/wiki/White_Star_Line" title="White Star Line">White Star Line</a>, and was responsible for controlling its flagships. He successfully commanded the<a href="/wiki/RMS_Baltic_(1903)" title="RMS Baltic (1903)"><i>Baltic</i></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_Adriatic_(1907)" title="RMS Adriatic (1907)"><i>Adriatic</i></a> and the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Olympic" title="RMS Olympic"><i>Olympic</i></a>. Until 1911, his career was disturbed by two transport missions troops during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Boer War</a>.</p>
<p>In 1912, He was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_Captain" title="Sea Captain">captain</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Maiden_voyage" title="Maiden voyage">maiden voyage</a> of the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a>, which <a href="/wiki/Sinking_of_the_RMS_Titanic" title="Sinking of the RMS Titanic">struck an iceberg and sank</a> on 15 April 1912; Smith and over 1,500 others perished in the sinking.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edward John Smith was born on 27 January 1850 on Well Street, <a href="/wiki/Hanley,_Staffordshire" title="Hanley, Staffordshire">Hanley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a>, England<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC17447183_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC17447183-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> to Edward Smith, a potter, and Catherine Hancock, born Marsh, who married on 2 August 1841 in Shelton, Staffordshire. His parents later owned a shop.</p>
<p>Smith attended the <a href="/wiki/Etruria,_Staffordshire" title="Etruria, Staffordshire">Etruria</a> British School until the age of 13, when he left and operated a steam hammer at the Etruria Forge. In 1867, aged 17 he went to <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> in the footsteps of his half-brother Joseph Hancock, a captain on a sailing ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC17181461_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC17181461-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> He began his apprenticeship on <i>Senator Weber</i>, owned by A Gibson &amp; Co. of Liverpool.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_children">Marriage and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 13 January 1887, Smith married Sarah Eleanor Pennington at <a href="/wiki/St_Oswald%27s_Church,_Winwick" title="St Oswald's Church, Winwick">St Oswald's Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winwick,_Cheshire" title="Winwick, Cheshire">Winwick</a>, Cheshire. Their daughter, Helen Melville Smith, was born in <a href="/wiki/Waterloo,_Merseyside" title="Waterloo, Merseyside">Waterloo</a>, Liverpool on 2 April 1898. The family lived in an imposing red brick, twin-gabled house, named "Woodhead", on Winn Road, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highfield,_Hampshire" title="Highfield, Hampshire">Highfield, Southampton</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_commands">Early commands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early commands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Edward Smith joined the <a href="/wiki/White_Star_Line" title="White Star Line">White Star Line</a> in March 1880 as the Fourth Officer of <a href="/wiki/SS_Celtic_(1872)" title="SS Celtic (1872)">SS <i>Celtic</i></a>. He served aboard the company's liners to Australia and to New York City, where he quickly rose in status. In 1887, he received his first White Star command, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS_Republic_(1872)" title="SS Republic (1872)"><i>Republic</i></a>. In 1888, Smith earned his Extra Master's Certificate and joined the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Reserve" title="Royal Naval Reserve">Royal Naval Reserve</a>, receiving a commission as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_(Royal_Navy)" title="Lieutenant (Royal Navy)">Lieutenant</a>, which entitled him to add the letters "RNR" after his name. This meant that in a time of war he could be called upon to serve in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. Later, after he had been promoted a <a href="/wiki/Commander_(Royal_Navy)" title="Commander (Royal Navy)">Commander</a> in the Royal Naval Reserve, Smith's ship had the distinction of being able to fly the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ensign" title="Blue Ensign">Blue Ensign</a> of the RNR; British merchant vessels generally flew the <a href="/wiki/Red_Ensign" title="Red Ensign">Red Ensign</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bigger_commands">Bigger commands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bigger commands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Smith was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS_Majestic_(1890)" title="SS Majestic (1890)"><i>Majestic</i></a> '​s captain for nine years commencing in 1895. When the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Boer War</a> started in 1899, <i>Majestic</i> was called upon to transport troops to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a>. Smith made two trips to South Africa, both without incident, and in 1903, for his service, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_VII_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Edward VII of the United Kingdom">King Edward VII</a> awarded him the <a href="/wiki/Transport_Medal" title="Transport Medal">Transport Medal</a>, showing the "South Africa" clasp. Smith was regarded as a "safe captain". As he rose in seniority, he gained a following amongst passengers with some only sailing the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> on a ship he captained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Smith even became known as the "Millionaires' Captain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> From 1904 on, Smith commanded the White Star Line's newest ships on their maiden voyages. In 1904, he was given command of what was then the largest ship in the world, the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Baltic_(1903)" title="RMS Baltic (1903)"><i>Baltic</i></a>. Her maiden voyage from Liverpool to New York, sailing 29 June 1904, went without incident. After three years with <i>Baltic</i>, Smith was given his second new "big ship," the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_Adriatic_(1907)" title="RMS Adriatic (1907)"><i>Adriatic</i></a>. Once again, the maiden voyage went without incident. During his command of <i>Adriatic</i>, Smith received the long service <a href="/wiki/Decoration_for_Officers_of_the_Royal_Naval_Reserve" title="Decoration for Officers of the Royal Naval Reserve">Decoration for Officers of the Royal Naval Reserve</a> (RD) along with a promotion to Commander.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Olympic_class_command"><i>Olympic</i> class command</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Olympic class command">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As one of the world's most experienced sea captains, Smith was called upon to take first command of the lead ship in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_class_ocean_liner" title="Olympic class ocean liner">new class</a> of ocean liners, the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Olympic" title="RMS Olympic"><i>Olympic</i></a> – again, the largest vessel in the world at that time. The maiden voyage from Southampton to New York was successfully concluded on 21 June 1911, but as the ship was docking in New York harbour, a small incident took place. Docking at Pier 59 under the command of Captain Smith with the assistance of a harbour pilot, <i>Olympic</i> was being assisted by twelve tugs when one got caught in the backwash of <i>Olympic</i>, spun around, collided with the bigger ship, and for a moment was trapped under <i>Olympic</i> '​s stern, finally managing to work free and limp to the docks.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hawke_incident">The <i>Hawke</i> incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Hawke incident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 20 September 1911, <i>Olympic</i> '​s first major mishap occurred during a collision with a British warship, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Hawke_(1891)" title="HMS Hawke (1891)">HMS <i>Hawke</i></a>, in which the warship lost her <a href="/wiki/Prow" title="Prow">prow</a>. Although the collision left two of <i>Olympic</i> '​s <a href="/wiki/Ship_floodability" title="Ship floodability">compartments</a> filled and one of her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_shaft" title="Propeller shaft">propeller shafts</a> twisted, she was able to limp back to <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>. At the resultant inquiry, the Royal Navy blamed <i>Olympic</i>, alleging that her massive size generated a suction that pulled <i>Hawke</i> into her side. Captain Smith had been on the bridge during the events.</p>
<p>The <i>Hawke</i> incident was a financial disaster for White Star, and the out-of-service time for the big liner made matters worse. <i>Olympic</i> returned to <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> and, to speed up the repairs, <a href="/wiki/Harland_and_Wolff" title="Harland and Wolff">Harland and Wolff</a> was forced to delay <i>Titanic</i> '​s completion, in order to use one of her propeller shafts and other parts for <i>Olympic</i>. Back at sea in February 1912, <i>Olympic</i> lost a <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propeller</a> blade and once again returned to her builder for emergency repairs. To get her back to service immediately, Harland and Wolff again had to pull resources from <i>Titanic</i>, delaying her <a href="/wiki/Maiden_voyage" title="Maiden voyage">maiden voyage</a> from 20 March to 10 April.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="RMS_Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: RMS Titanic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sinking_of_the_RMS_Titanic" title="Sinking of the RMS Titanic">Sinking of the RMS Titanic</a></div>
<p>Despite the past trouble, Smith was again appointed to be in command of the newest ship in the Olympic class when the RMS <i>Titanic</i> left Southampton for her maiden voyage. Although some sources state that he had decided to retire after completing <i>Titanic</i> '​s maiden voyage, an article in the Halifax <i>Morning Chronicle</i> on 9 April 1912 stated that Smith would remain in charge of <i>Titanic</i> "until the Company (White Star Line) completed a larger and finer steamer."</p>
<p>On 10 April 1912, Smith, wearing a bowler hat and a long overcoat, took a taxi from his home to Southampton docks. He came aboard <i>Titanic</i> at 7 am to prepare for the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a> muster at 8:00 am. He immediately went to his cabin to get the sailing report from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Officer" title="Chief Officer">Chief Officer</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Tingle_Wilde" title="Henry Tingle Wilde">Henry Wilde</a>. After departure at noon, the huge amount of water displaced by <i>Titanic</i> as she passed caused the laid-up <a href="/wiki/SS_City_of_New_York" title="SS City of New York"><i>New York</i></a> to break from her moorings and swing towards <i>Titanic.</i> Quick action from Smith helped to avert a premature end to the maiden voyage.</p>

<p>The first four days of the voyage passed without incident, but shortly after 11:40 pm on 14 April Smith was informed by First Officer <a href="/wiki/William_McMaster_Murdoch" title="William McMaster Murdoch">William Murdoch</a> that the ship had just collided with an iceberg. It was soon apparent that the ship was seriously damaged; designer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Andrews_(shipbuilder)" title="Thomas Andrews (shipbuilder)">Thomas Andrews</a> reported that all of the first five of the ship's watertight compartments had been breached and that <i>Titanic</i> would sink in under two hours. During the evacuation, Captain Smith, aware that there were not enough lifeboats for all of the passengers and crew, did all in his power to prevent panic and did his best to assist in the evacuation; Major <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Godfrey_Peuchen" title="Arthur Godfrey Peuchen">Arthur Godfrey Peuchen</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Yacht_Club" title="Royal Canadian Yacht Club">Royal Canadian Yacht Club</a> said "He was doing everything in his power to get women in these boats, and to see that they were lowered properly. I thought he was doing his duty in regard to the lowering of the boats".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titanicinquiry.org_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-titanicinquiry.org-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Williams_Daniel" title="Robert Williams Daniel">Robert Williams Daniel</a>, a first class passenger also said:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Captain Smith was the biggest hero I ever saw. He stood on the bridge and shouted through a megaphone, trying to make himself heard.':<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></i></dd>
</dl>

<p>Just minutes before the ship started its final plunge, Smith was still busy releasing <i>Titanic'</i>s crew from their duties; he went to the Marconi operators room and released Junior Marconi Officer <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bride" title="Harold Bride">Harold Bride</a> and senior wireless operator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_George_Phillips_(wireless_officer)" title="John George Phillips (wireless officer)">John "Jack" Phillips</a> from their duties. He then carried out a final tour of the deck, telling crew members: "Now it's every man for himself."<span class="citation book">Butler, Daniel Allen (1998). <i>Unsinkable: The Full Story of RMS</i> Titanic. Mechanicsburg, PA: Stackpole Books. p. 130. <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Book_Number" title="International Standard Book Number">ISBN</a> <a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/978-0-8117-1814-1" title="Special:BookSources/978-0-8117-1814-1">978-0-8117-1814-1</a>.</span><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AEdward+Smith+%28sea+captain%29&amp;rft.au=Butler%2C+Daniel+Allen&amp;rft.aufirst=Daniel+Allen&amp;rft.aulast=Butler&amp;rft.btitle=Unsinkable%3A+The+Full+Story+of+RMS+Titanic&amp;rft.date=1998&amp;rft.genre=book&amp;rft.isbn=978-0-8117-1814-1&amp;rft.pages=130&amp;rft.place=Mechanicsburg%2C+PA&amp;rft.pub=Stackpole+Books&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook"><span style="display:none;"> </span></span> At 2:10 a.m., Steward Edward Brown saw the captain approach with a megaphone in his hand. He heard him say "Well boys, do your best for the women and children, and look out for yourselves.” He saw the Captain walk onto the bridge alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titanicinquiry.org_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-titanicinquiry.org-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> This was the last reliable sighting of Smith. A few minutes later Trimmer Samuel Hemming found the bridge apparently empty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Five minutes later, the ship disappeared beneath the ocean. Smith perished that night along with around 1,500 others, and his body was never recovered.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_goes_down_with_the_ship" title="Captain goes down with the ship">Captain goes down with the ship</a></div>
<p>There are conflicting accounts of Smith's death. Some survivors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> said they saw Smith enter the ship's <a href="/wiki/Pilothouse" title="Pilothouse">wheelhouse</a> on the bridge, and die there when it was engulfed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Herald" title="New York Herald">New York Herald</a></i> in its April 19, 1912 edition quoted <a href="/wiki/Robert_Williams_Daniel" title="Robert Williams Daniel">Robert Williams Daniel</a>, who jumped from the stern immediately before the ship sank, in its April 19, 1912 edition as have claimed to have witnessed Captain Smith drown in the ship's wheelhouse. <i>"I saw Captain Smith on the bridge. My eyes seemingly clung to him. The deck from which I had leapt was immersed. The water had risen slowly, and was now to the floor of the bridge. Then it was to Captain Smith's waist. I saw him no more. He died a hero."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Captain Smith himself made statements hinting that he would go down with his ship if he was ever confronted with a disaster; A friend of Smith's, Dr. Williams, asked Captain Smith what would happen if the Adriatic struck a concealed reef of ice and was badly damaged. "Some of us would go to the bottom with the ship." was Smith's reply. A boyhood friend, William Jones said: "Ted Smith passed away just as he would have loved to do. To stand on the bridge of his vessel and go down with her was characteristic of all his actions when we were boys together."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seaofglass_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-seaofglass-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Because of these factors as well as the accounts of Smith going inside the wheelhouse; this has remained the iconic image which has remained of Smith and has been perpetuated by film portrayals.</p>
<p>Initially, rumors that Smith was the officer who committed suicide by shooting himself in the last minutes of the sinking, an incident that was reported by several survivors, were reported by the <i>Washington Times</i> and the French paper <i>L'Excelsior</i> based off the survivor accounts of Ms. Gretchen Longley and Mrs. Washington Dodge;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> a boy who was one of the last to leave the ship also told Dr. J.F. Kemp, a passenger on the Carpathia, that "Captain Smith put a pistol to his head and then fell down."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Surviving crewmen, however, vigorously denied this rumor. Also, Smith's appearance, with a full white beard, would have made him stand out, whereas not one of the witnesses described the officer concerned as having a beard. There is also no evidence of Smith ever having possession of a revolver or ever having fired a gun.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>When working to free Collapsible B, Junior Marconi Officer <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bride" title="Harold Bride">Harold Bride</a> said he saw Captain Smith dive from the bridge into the sea just as Collapsible B was levered off the roof of the officers' quarters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> a story which was corroborated by first class passenger Mrs Eleanor Widener, who was in Lifeboat No.4 (the closest to the sinking ship) at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Also second class passenger William John Mellors, who survived aboard collapsible B, stated that Smith jumped from the bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shots_in_the_dark_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shots_in_the_dark-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> It has been affirmed that the witnesses could here be mistaking Captain Smith for Second Officer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lightoller" title="Charles Lightoller">Charles Lightoller</a>, who was known to have done exactly this at this time, first swimming towards the crow's nest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.co.uk_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.co.uk-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Several accounts say that Smith may have been seen in the water near the overturned Collapsible B during or after the sinking. Colonel Archibald Gracie reported that an unknown swimmer came near the capsized and overcrowded lifeboat, and that one of the men on board told him "Hold on to what you have, old boy. One more of you aboard would sink us all,"; in a powerful voice, the swimmer replied "All right boys. Good luck and God bless you.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Gracie did not see this man, nor was able to identify him, but some other survivors later claimed to have recognised this man as Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Another man (or possibly the same) never asked to come aboard the boat, but instead cheered its occupants saying “Good boys! Good lads!” with “the voice of authority”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANTR_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ANTR-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> One of the Collapsible B survivors, fireman Walter Hurst, tried to reach him with an oar, but the rapidly rising swell carried the man away before he could reach him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANTR_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-ANTR-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Hurst said he was certain this man was Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANTR_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-ANTR-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Some of these accounts also describe Smith carrying a child to the boat. Harry Senior, one of Titanic's stokers, and second class passenger Charles Eugene Williams, who both survived aboard Collapsible B, stated that Smith <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shots_in_the_dark_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shots_in_the_dark-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> swam with a child in his arms to Collapsible B, which Smith presented to a steward, after which he apparently swam back to the rapidly-foundering ship. Williams' account differs slightly, claiming that, after Smith handed the child over to the steward, he asked what had become of First Officer Murdoch. Upon hearing news of Murdoch's demise, Smith "pushed himself away from the lifeboat, threw his lifebelt from him and slowly sank from our sight. He did not come to the surface again." These accounts are almost certainly apocryphal, according to historians featured in the A&amp;E Documentary <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanic:_Death_of_a_Dream" title="Titanic: Death of a Dream">Titanic: Death of a Dream</a></i>. Lightoller who survived on Collapsible B never reported seeing Smith in the water or receiving a child from him. There is also no way in which survivors on Collapsible B would have been able to verify the identity of the individual concerned under such dimly lit and chaotic circumstances. It is more likely based upon wishful thinking that the person they saw was indeed the Captain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Captain Smith's fate will probably remain uncertain.</p>
<p>For many years, there were also conflicting accounts of Smith's last words. Newspaper reports said that as the final plunge began, Smith advised those on board to "<i>Be British boys, be British!"</i> Although this is engraved on his memorial and portrayed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanic_(1996_TV_miniseries)" title="Titanic (1996 TV miniseries)">1996 TV miniseries</a>, it was just a myth popularised by the British press at the time; Smith was an experienced transatlantic captain and a cosmopolitan, sophisticated man. Had he been prone to this type of jingoistic statement, he certainly wouldn't have been so popular with the prominent Americans and Canadians who preferred to travel on ships he captained and to dine with him while on board. If he said these words to anyone, it would have been to the crew, but not one of the surviving crew members claimed he said this. Since Steward Brown's account of Smith giving orders before walking onto the bridge was the last reliable sighting, this would make Smith's last words simply "Well boys, do your best for the women and children, and look out for yourselves.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.co.uk_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.co.uk-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>A statue, sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Scott" title="Kathleen Scott">Kathleen Scott</a>, wife of Antarctic explorer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Falcon_Scott" title="Robert Falcon Scott">Robert Falcon Scott</a>, was unveiled in July 1914 at the western end of the Museum Gardens in <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Park" title="Beacon Park">Beacon Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lichfield" title="Lichfield">Lichfield</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The pedestal is made from Cornish granite and the figure is bronze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noz_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-noz-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Lichfield was chosen as the location for the monument because Smith was a Staffordshire man and Lichfield was the centre of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Lichfield" title="Diocese of Lichfield">diocese</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-LM-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The statue originally cost £740 (£60,000 with inflation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup>) raised through local and national contributions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-LM-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>In 2010, as part of the 'Parks for People' programme, the statue was restored and the green <a href="/wiki/Patina" title="Patina">patina</a> removed from its surface at a cost of £16,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-LM-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2011 an unsuccessful campaign was started to get the statue moved back to Captain Smith's home town of Hanley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmslsa2_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmslsa2-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Smith had already been commemorated in Hanley's Town Hall with a plaque reading:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This tablet is dedicated to the memory of Commander  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] Edward John Smith RD, RNR. Born in Hanley, 27th Jany 1850, died at sea, 15th April 1912. Be British.</p>
<p>Whilst in command of the White Star SS <i>Titanic</i> that great ship struck an iceberg in the Atlantic Ocean during the night and speedily sank with nearly all who were on board. Captain Smith having done all man could do for the safety of passengers and crew remained at his post on the sinking ship until the end. His last message to the crew was "Be British."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The plaque was removed in 1961, given to a local school and then returned to the Town Hall but remounted in the interior of the building in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Titanic_Brewery" title="Titanic Brewery">Titanic Brewery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burslem" title="Burslem">Burslem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoke-on-Trent" title="Stoke-on-Trent">Stoke-on-Trent</a> is in honor of him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Smith’s half-sister Thyrza died in 1921 and his widow, Sarah Eleanor Smith, was hit and killed by a taxi in London in 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper2011_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper2011-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Their daughter, Helen Melville, married and gave birth to twins, Simon and Priscilla. Simon, a pilot in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a>, was killed in World War II. Priscilla died from polio three years later; neither of them had children. Helen died in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC17447183_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC17447183-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
