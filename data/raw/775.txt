<p><b>Maurice Ernest Gibb</b>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ɔr/ 'or' in 'born'">ɔr</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></a></span><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span></span> (22 December 1949 – 12 January 2003) was a musician, singer, songwriter, and record producer who achieved international fame as guitarist,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-released_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-released-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> bassist and keyboardist of the <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a>. Although his brothers <a href="/wiki/Barry_Gibb" title="Barry Gibb">Barry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robin_Gibb" title="Robin Gibb">Robin Gibb</a> were the band's primary lead vocalists, most of the groups albums included at least one or two Maurice Gibb compositions, including "<a href="/wiki/Lay_It_on_Me_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="Lay It on Me (Bee Gees song)">Lay It on Me</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Country_Woman" title="Country Woman">Country Woman</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/On_Time_(song)" title="On Time (song)">On Time</a>". The Bee Gees were one of the most successful rock-pop groups ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoF_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HoF-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Gibb's role in the group focused on <a href="/wiki/Melody" title="Melody">melody</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arrangement" title="Arrangement">arrangements</a>, providing <a href="/wiki/Backing_vocalist" title="Backing vocalist">backing vocal</a> harmony and playing a variety of instruments.</p>
<p>Born on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, he started his music career in 1955 in Manchester, England, joining the skiffle-rock and roll group <a href="/wiki/The_Rattlesnakes_(1955_band)" title="The Rattlesnakes (1955 band)">the Rattlesnakes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-around_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-around-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> that later evolved into the Bee Gees in 1958 when they moved to Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> They returned to England, where they achieved worldwide fame. In 2002, the Bee Gees were appointed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Most_Excellent_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire">CBEs</a> for their "contribution to music", and following his death in 2003, Maurice's son collected his award at <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Palace" title="Buckingham Palace">Buckingham Palace</a> in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBE_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBE-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Gibb's earliest musical influences included <a href="/wiki/The_Everly_Brothers" title="The Everly Brothers">The Everly Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Richard" title="Cliff Richard">Cliff Richard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Anka" title="Paul Anka">Paul Anka</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mills_Brothers" title="The Mills Brothers">The Mills Brothers</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> were significant later influences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-influences_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-influences-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1964 he began his career as an instrumentalist, playing guitar on "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claustrophobia_(song)" title="Claustrophobia (song)">Claustrophobia</a>". After the band's break-up in 1969, Gibb released his first solo single "<a href="/wiki/Railroad_(song)" title="Railroad (song)">Railroad</a>" but his first solo album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Loner_(album)" title="The Loner (album)">The Loner</a></i> has never been released.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1949.E2.80.9357:_Early_years">1949–57: Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 1949–57: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gibb was born in <a href="/wiki/Douglas,_Isle_of_Man" title="Douglas, Isle of Man">Douglas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> on 22 December 1949, the son of <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Gibb" title="Hugh Gibb">Hugh</a> and Barbara Gibb. He was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraternal_twin" title="Fraternal twin">fraternal twin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Robin_Gibb" title="Robin Gibb">Robin Gibb</a> and was the younger of the two by 35 minutes. At that time, he had one sister, Lesley, and one brother, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Gibb" title="Barry Gibb">Barry</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_bands">Early bands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early bands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 1955, the Gibbs moved back to <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, England. Around 1955, Gibb and his brothers were heard harmonising by their parents. Also in 1955, he started his music career when he joined the skiffle/rock and roll group <a href="/wiki/The_Rattlesnakes_(1955_band)" title="The Rattlesnakes (1955 band)">the Rattlesnakes</a> with his brothers and two friends, Paul Frost and Kenny Horrocks, who were their neighbours. The group's first major appearance was on 28 December 1957 when they performed at a local Gaumont cinema where children were invited to sing between films. They had planned to sing along to a 78 rpm record which Lesley had just been given as a Christmas present, but on the way Gibb and his brother Robin dropped and broke it, so they sang live. The audience were pleased by their singing, which may have been the song "<a href="/wiki/Wake_Up_Little_Susie" title="Wake Up Little Susie">Wake Up Little Susie</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Everly_Brothers" title="The Everly Brothers">the Everly Brothers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-around_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-around-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 March 1958, the youngest Gibb child, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Gibb" title="Andy Gibb">Andy Gibb</a>, was born. By May 1958, the Gibbs moved to Northern Grove as Horrocks and Frost left the band. The band name was changed to Wee Johnny Hayes and the Blue Cats. Later, in August, the Gibbs emigrated to Australia, settling in Redcliffe near <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1958.E2.80.932003:_Bee_Gees">1958–2003: Bee Gees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1958–2003: Bee Gees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:171px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maurice_Gibb_1968.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="169" height="382" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/30/Maurice_Gibb_1968.jpg" width="169"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maurice_Gibb_1968.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gibb performing in Dutch television <i>Twien</i> in 1968.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After numerous group name changes, finally the name evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a>. By January 1963 the Gibbs moved to <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>. The group's first single was "<a href="/wiki/The_Battle_of_the_Blue_and_the_Grey" title="The Battle of the Blue and the Grey">The Battle of the Blue and the Grey</a>" but it failed to chart. Around 1963, the Gibb brothers worked with Judy Stone, Johnny Devlin and Jimmy Hannan. By 1964, Gibb made his first appearance as an instrumentalist on the beat-influenced "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claustrophobia_(song)" title="Claustrophobia (song)">Claustrophobia</a>". Also in 1964, the Gibb brothers worked with Johnny Devlin and <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Gordon" title="Trevor Gordon">Trevor Gordon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Around 1965, the Gibb brothers worked with Trevor Gordon, Michelle Rae and Noeleen Batley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1966 <a href="/wiki/Barry,_Robin_%26_Maurice_Gibb" title="Barry, Robin &amp; Maurice Gibb">the three Gibb brothers</a> wrote their first song, "The Storm". Also in 1966, Gibb began his career as the lead guitarist and bassist for the band; around the same time, he recorded his first solo composition, "<a href="/wiki/All_by_Myself_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="All by Myself (Bee Gees song)">All by Myself</a>", on which he also played guitar. Around 1966, the Gibb brothers worked with Bip Addison, Sandy Summers, Anne Shelton, <a href="/wiki/Vince_Melouney" title="Vince Melouney">Vince Maloney</a>, April Bryon, MPD Ltd, Ray Brown and the Whispers, <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Burns_(singer)" title="Ronnie Burns (singer)">Ronnie Burns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lori_Balmer" title="Lori Balmer">Lori Balmer</a>, Marty Rhone, Vyt, Python Lee Jackson, Dennis Knight, Barrington Davis, Jenene and Jon Blanchfield. Their second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Spicks_and_Specks_(album)" title="Spicks and Specks (album)">Spicks and Specks</a></i> (1966), included "<a href="/wiki/Where_Are_You_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="Where Are You (Bee Gees song)">Where Are You</a>", Gibb's first solo writing credit to be released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-released_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-released-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9; padding:8px;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Maurice is closer to my attitudes and ideas. He has the same kind of humour as I have. We have other common interests like playing chess. He's the kind of guy who will come over and give you a hand washing the car".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-around_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-around-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;">—<a href="/wiki/Colin_Petersen" title="Colin Petersen">Colin Petersen</a>, drummer for the Bee Gees on telling his band mates' personalities</div>
</div>
<p>In March 1967 the Gibb brothers worked with <a href="/wiki/Billy_J._Kramer" title="Billy J. Kramer">Billy J. Kramer</a> recording "<a href="/wiki/Town_of_Tuxley_Toymaker,_Part_1" title="Town of Tuxley Toymaker, Part 1">Town of Tuxley Toymaker, Part 1</a>". Around the same time, <a href="/wiki/Colin_Petersen" title="Colin Petersen">Colin Petersen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vince_Melouney" title="Vince Melouney">Vince Melouney</a> joined the Bee Gees. In the middle of 1967 <i><a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees%27_1st" title="Bee Gees' 1st">Bee Gees' 1st</a></i> was released. The album was praised by the critics who compared it to <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper%27s_Lonely_Hearts_Club_Band" title="Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band">Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band</a></i> which was released weeks earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>'s Bruce Eder wrote that Gibb's <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">Mellotron</a> on "<a href="/wiki/Every_Christian_Lion_Hearted_Man_Will_Show_You" title="Every Christian Lion Hearted Man Will Show You">Every Christian Lion Hearted Man Will Show You</a>" 'was close in the spirit of <a href="/wiki/The_Moody_Blues" title="The Moody Blues">The Moody Blues</a> and was opened by a Gregorian chant.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The band's first number 1 single in the UK, "<a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="Massachusetts (Bee Gees song)">Massachusetts</a>" was released in September 1967. In early 1968, the <i><a href="/wiki/Horizontal_(album)" title="Horizontal (album)">Horizontal</a></i> was released. In August 1968 <a href="/wiki/The_Marbles_(duo)" title="The Marbles (duo)">the Marbles</a> released their first and only successful hit "<a href="/wiki/Only_One_Woman" title="Only One Woman">Only One Woman</a>" a song which co-written by Gibb. In the middle of 1968, the band's third international LP <i><a href="/wiki/Idea_(album)" title="Idea (album)">Idea</a></i> included the song "<a href="/wiki/Kitty_Can" title="Kitty Can">Kitty Can</a>", which features Gibb's high harmony vocal, with Barry Gibb singing low harmony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In early 1969, they released <i><a href="/wiki/Odessa_(Bee_Gees_album)" title="Odessa (Bee Gees album)">Odessa</a></i>, which features Gibb's solo vocals on "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suddenly_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="Suddenly (Bee Gees song)">Suddenly</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/I_Laugh_in_Your_Face" title="I Laugh in Your Face">I Laugh in Your Face</a>". On 19 March 1969, the same day that Robin Gibb announced his plans as a solo artist, Gibb and his bandmates recorded and later released "<a href="/wiki/Tomorrow_Tomorrow_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="Tomorrow Tomorrow (Bee Gees song)">Tomorrow Tomorrow</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-album_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-album-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Maurice and Barry alone comprised the Bee Gees on the 1969 album <i>Cucumber Castle</i> during Robin's absence as a soloist. They sang the hit "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Forget_to_Remember" title="Don't Forget to Remember">Don't Forget to Remember</a>" (which reached number two on the UK chart while Robin's first solo single, "Saved By The Bell", also reached number two) and follow up singles; "IOIO" and "Tomorrow, Tomorrow" with Barry taking lead and Maurice providing harmony vocals, but the duo version of the group enjoyed dwindling success. On 1 December 1969, Maurice and brother Barry announced that the Bee Gees had broken up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Maurice later began taking a few lead vocals such as on "You Know it's For You" on "To Whom it May Concern" (1972), and later "Wildflower" (on "Living Eyes" 1981). In September 1986, the Bee Gees began writing and recording songs for their upcoming album <i><a href="/wiki/E.S.P._(Bee_Gees_album)" title="E.S.P. (Bee Gees album)">E.S.P.</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brennan_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brennan-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> on which Maurice took lead vocal on the song "Overnight".</p>
<p>The Bee Gees released their 23rd album, <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_Where_I_Came_In" title="This Is Where I Came In">This Is Where I Came In</a></i>, which included his compositions, "Walking on Air" and "Man in the Middle".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_projects">Outside projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Outside projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_career">Solo career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solo career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9; padding:8px;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>I suppose it was a good thing to get it out of my system, but at the same time, I never thought we would never sing together again. I started off intending to make go of it, but I soon found something was missing. I'd write songs and want desperately to play them to my brothers, but because of all the squabbles, I didn't feel I could. Then our record company unintentionally seemed to be trying to sabotage our solo careers. Distribution problems hit my solo single, ['Railroad'] Barry's first single, ['<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Kiss_Your_Memory" title="I'll Kiss Your Memory">I'll Kiss Your Memory</a>'] and Robin's second single ['<a href="/wiki/One_Million_Years" title="One Million Years">One Million Years</a>']</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;">—Gibb, on telling his first experience as a solo artist<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-around_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-around-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>In April 1970 Gibb released his first solo single, "<a href="/wiki/Railroad_(song)" title="Railroad (song)">Railroad</a>" backed with "I've Come Back". His debut solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Loner_(album)" title="The Loner (album)">The Loner</a></i>, has not yet been released. All of the songs on the album feature guitar work by <a href="/wiki/Stone_the_Crows" title="Stone the Crows">Stone the Crows</a>' <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Harvey" title="Leslie Harvey">Leslie Harvey</a>. In May 1970 the album <i>Sing a Rude Song</i> was released in the UK with Gibb singing lead vocals on the three songs on the album. By 1976, New Blood Records issued <i>Bee Gees Information</i> which was credited to Gibb himself and features four songs from <i>The Loner</i>, but was only a limited edition for fan club, although the EP has no title but the picture sleeve of that has the club name ("Bee Gees Information") in large letters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1981, he recorded some instrumental tracks for his unreleased instrumental album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strings_and_Things" title="Strings and Things">Strings and Things</a></i>, including "Image of Samantha" seems refer to his daughter Samantha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1982, Gibb appeared at the San Remo Festival and performed "Wildflower", a Bee Gees' song on which he provided lead vocals from <i><a href="/wiki/Living_Eyes_(Bee_Gees_album)" title="Living Eyes (Bee Gees album)">Living Eyes</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1983, Gibb re-recorded "On Time" at the same time as "<a href="/wiki/Hold_Her_in_Your_Hand" title="Hold Her in Your Hand">Hold Her in Your Hand</a>". By February 1984, he collaborated with arranger <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Haskell" title="Jimmie Haskell">Jimmie Haskell</a> for the <a href="/wiki/A_Breed_Apart_(soundtrack)" title="A Breed Apart (soundtrack)">film soundtrack</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Breed_Apart" title="A Breed Apart">A Breed Apart</a></i>, recorded at Gold Star Studios in Los Angeles. By March, he recorded "Miami, A Musical Score", this song was later used for a promotional film in Miami. His second single "Hold Her in Your Hand" was only issued in UK, Australia and South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-both_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-both-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Gibb composed and recorded the instrumental "The Supernaturals" in July 1985, it was later dubbed on the film of the same name, he also appeared on the film doing a cameo appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-track_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-track-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_bands">Other bands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other bands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A supergroup in 1969 called <a href="/wiki/The_Fut" title="The Fut">The Fut</a> was formed at a Tin Tin session and they recorded "<a href="/wiki/Have_You_Heard_the_Word" title="Have You Heard the Word">Have You Heard the Word</a>" and released later as a single, the band consisting of Gibb on vocals/bass/guitar, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Kipner" title="Steve Kipner">Steve Kipner</a> and Steve Groves on vocals with Billy Lawrie also providing background vocals. "Have You Heard the Word" features Gibb impersonating <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a> by singing the song, and the Beatles fans thought that it was the lost Beatles song which was not released. In 1985, <a href="/wiki/Yoko_Ono" title="Yoko Ono">Yoko Ono</a> attempted to register the song as Lennon's composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1970, he formed a <a href="/wiki/Supergroup_(music)" title="Supergroup (music)">supergroup</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Bloomfields" title="The Bloomfields">The Bloomfields</a> with Billy Lawrie and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heads,_Hands_%26_Feet" title="Heads, Hands &amp; Feet">Heads, Hands &amp; Feet</a> member <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Harris_(musician)" title="Johnny Harris (musician)">Johnny Harris</a>, and they recorded a short version of "<a href="/wiki/The_Loner_(Maurice_Gibb_song)" title="The Loner (Maurice Gibb song)">The Loner</a>", which was released as a single in 1972 and intended for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Bloomfield_(film)" title="Bloomfield (film)">Bloomfield</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Session_musician_and_producer">Session musician and producer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Session musician and producer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1966, Gibb became a session musician in Australia for other artists including Bip Addison, Anne Shelton, April Bryon, Barrington Davis and singer Jon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-released_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-released-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Around 1969, Gibb worked with his next-door neighbor, <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">Beatle</a> <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr" title="Ringo Starr">Ringo Starr</a>, and the result was the track "Modulating Maurice" but it was not released. Gibb played piano on the track "<a href="/wiki/Saved_by_the_Bell_(song)" title="Saved by the Bell (song)">Saved by the Bell</a>" as well as bass on "<a href="/wiki/Mother_and_Jack" title="Mother and Jack">Mother and Jack</a>" both songs performed by Robin Gibb for his first solo album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-album_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-album-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Around 1970, Gibb played piano on <a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Isn%27t_It_a_Pity" title="Isn't It a Pity">Isn't It a Pity</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a>' <a href="/wiki/Phil_Collins" title="Phil Collins">Phil Collins</a> remembers him being present when he played on that song. In 1971, he produced <a href="/wiki/Lulu_(singer)" title="Lulu (singer)">Lulu</a>'s 1971 song "Everybody Clap", which features Gibb on guitar, Leslie Harvey on guitar, former <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Mann" title="Manfred Mann">Manfred Mann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cream_(band)" title="Cream (band)">Cream</a> member <a href="/wiki/Jack_Bruce" title="Jack Bruce">Jack Bruce</a> on bass, and <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a>'s <a href="/wiki/John_Bonham" title="John Bonham">John Bonham</a> on drums. Also in that year, he played bass on Billy Lawrie's 1973 song "Freedom". <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wings_(band)" title="Wings (band)">Wings</a>' <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_McCulloch" title="Jimmy McCulloch">Jimmy McCulloch</a> played guitar on that song, and he suggests that it was recorded at around the same time as "Everybody Clap", with Stone the Crows' <a href="/wiki/Maggie_Bell" title="Maggie Bell">Maggie Bell</a> providing backing vocals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> On 17 January 1972, Gibb produced, with Billy Lawrie, "Baby Come On Home" and "(Have You Seen My) Angelina", released as a single also in that year. In April 1972, Gibb produced <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Stevens_(musician)" title="Jimmy Stevens (musician)">Jimmy Stevens</a>' album <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Freak_Me_Out" title="Don't Freak Me Out">Don't Freak Me Out</a></i>, called <i>Paid My Dues</i> in US. Also in 1972, Gibb produced <i>Drift Away</i>, an album released by Bob Saker and Mike Berry; it features a cover version of "<a href="/wiki/On_Time_(song)" title="On Time (song)">On Time</a>", Gibb's composition from 1971, and was originally used as the B-side of "<a href="/wiki/My_World_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="My World (Bee Gees song)">My World</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1973, Gibb produced and play bass on Jimmy Stevens' songs "Maggie May", "Fiddler's Tramp", "Tennessee", "Sinner", "Yoko", "She Put Love In My Soul", with Bee Gees guitarist <a href="/wiki/Alan_Kendall" title="Alan Kendall">Alan Kendall</a> and Jimmy McCulloch on guitar, Pete Willsher on acoustic guitar, <a href="/wiki/Zoot_Money" title="Zoot Money">Zoot Money</a> on keyboards, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Jones_(singer)" title="Paul Jones (singer)">Paul Jones</a> on harmonica and others. In August 1973, Gibb participated on <a href="/wiki/Andy_Gibb" title="Andy Gibb">Andy Gibb</a>'s first recording session performing "Windows of My World" and "My Father's a Rebel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1978, Gibb produced with Steve Klein <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osmonds" title="Osmonds">Osmonds</a>' album <i>Steppin' Out</i>, a cover version of "<a href="/wiki/Rest_Your_Love_on_Me" title="Rest Your Love on Me">Rest Your Love on Me</a>", which was originally recorded by the Bee Gees on <i><a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_World" title="Children of the World">Children of the World</a></i> sessions in 1976, but was used in 1978 as the B-side of "<a href="/wiki/Too_Much_Heaven" title="Too Much Heaven">Too Much Heaven</a>". Also in 1978, Gibb composed "The Love That Was Lost", with <a href="/wiki/Blue_Weaver" title="Blue Weaver">Blue Weaver</a> on keyboard and arranged by Mike Lewis, who also arranged for the disco group <a href="/wiki/KC_and_the_Sunshine_Band" title="KC and the Sunshine Band">KC and the Sunshine Band</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1980, Gibb co-wrote with <a href="/wiki/Tim_Rice" title="Tim Rice">Tim Rice</a> "Last Affair of the Heart", which was recorded by <a href="/wiki/Elaine_Paige" title="Elaine Paige">Elaine Paige</a> in AIR Studios in London along with "Secrets" and "Carried Away".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1982, Gibb took part in Barry's recording of the song "Eyes That See in the Dark", a song that was originally intended for <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Rogers" title="Kenny Rogers">Kenny Rogers</a>' album <a href="/wiki/Eyes_That_See_in_the_Dark" title="Eyes That See in the Dark">album of the same name</a>. In August 1982, Gibb wrote and recorded "Spirit of the Snow", which was originally intended for the film <i><a href="/wiki/A_Christmas_Carol_(1984_film)" title="A Christmas Carol (1984 film)">A Christmas Carol</a></i>. Gibb worked with Robin on the album <i><a href="/wiki/How_Old_Are_You%3F_(album)" title="How Old Are You? (album)">How Old Are You?</a></i>, which was released in 1983. In 1984, Gibb also worked with Robin on his fourth solo album <i><a href="/wiki/Secret_Agent_(Robin_Gibb_album)" title="Secret Agent (Robin Gibb album)">Secret Agent</a></i>. Gibb co-wrote "<a href="/wiki/Shine,_Shine" title="Shine, Shine">Shine, Shine</a>" and "The Hunter", and both songs were released on Barry's second solo album <i><a href="/wiki/Now_Voyager" title="Now Voyager">Now Voyager</a></i> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-both_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-both-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1985, Gibb produced Robin's fifth LP <i><a href="/wiki/Walls_Have_Eyes" title="Walls Have Eyes">Walls Have Eyes</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Tom_Dowd" title="Tom Dowd">Tom Dowd</a>. In 1985, Gibb sang background vocals on <a href="/wiki/Larry_Gatlin" title="Larry Gatlin">Larry Gatlin</a>'s songs "Indian Summer", which was released on Gatlin's album <i>Smile</i> with Gatlin and <a href="/wiki/Roy_Orbison" title="Roy Orbison">Roy Orbison</a> on lead vocals; another track "Didn't We Call It (Falling in Love)" was not released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-track_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-track-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1986, Gibb produced <a href="/wiki/Carola_H%C3%A4ggkvist" title="Carola Häggkvist">Carola</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Runaway_(Carola_album)" title="Runaway (Carola album)">Runaway</a></i>. Also in 1986, Gibb participated on The Bunbury songs "Up the Revolution", "Fight (No Matter How Long)", "Bunbury Afternoon" and "Seasons".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musicianship">Musicianship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Musicianship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DJ <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gambaccini" title="Paul Gambaccini">Paul Gambaccini</a> said: "Maurice was the talented multi-instrumentalist, I mean here is a guy who played <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_instrument" title="Keyboard instrument">keyboards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bass_(guitar)" title="Bass (guitar)">bass</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percussion" title="Percussion">percussion</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bass_guitar">Bass guitar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bass guitar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1967 to 1979, he played bass guitar both studio and live. On live performances of the group, an additional musician is taking the bass part when Gibb switched to piano. He was less influential in the Bee Gees' <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a> sound of 1975 to 1979, when he primarily played bass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocals">Vocals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vocals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He was the lead vocalist on some Bee Gees tracks including "<a href="/wiki/On_Time_(song)" title="On Time (song)">On Time</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Lay_It_on_Me_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="Lay It on Me (Bee Gees song)">Lay It on Me</a>". His first composition is "<a href="/wiki/All_by_Myself_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="All by Myself (Bee Gees song)">All by Myself</a>" recorded in 1966 but not released until 1970 on <i><a href="/wiki/Inception/Nostalgia" title="Inception/Nostalgia">Inception/Nostalgia</a></i>. On the Bee Gees' performance of "<a href="/wiki/Nights_on_Broadway" title="Nights on Broadway">Nights on Broadway</a>", Gibb sang falsetto on it, rather than Barry who was originally sang falsetto on the studio recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> He also showcases his singing career on his unreleased LP <i>The Loner</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Keyboards">Keyboards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Keyboards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gibb played <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">Mellotron</a> on several Bee Gees songs, most notably "<a href="/wiki/Every_Christian_Lion_Hearted_Man_Will_Show_You" title="Every Christian Lion Hearted Man Will Show You">Every Christian Lion Hearted Man Will Show You</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Kilburn_Towers" title="Kilburn Towers">Kilburn Towers</a>" The <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> on songs like "<a href="/wiki/Words_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="Words (Bee Gees song)">Words</a>", "<a href="/wiki/First_of_May_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="First of May (Bee Gees song)">First of May</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Lonely_Days" title="Lonely Days">Lonely Days</a>" is Gibb's. He played the <a href="/wiki/Moog_synthesizer" title="Moog synthesizer">Moog synthesizer</a> on the song "Sweet Song of Summer" (1972). Since 1987, he played <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_instrument" title="Keyboard instrument">keyboards</a> on most of the songs in the group's performances.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustic_guitar">Acoustic guitar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Acoustic guitar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gibb debuted his career as an instrumentalist in 1964 on "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claustrophobia_(song)" title="Claustrophobia (song)">Claustrophobia</a>". Also occasionally lead guitar (including the acoustic guitar given to him by <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a>, which Gibb used on "<a href="/wiki/This_Is_Where_I_Came_In_(song)" title="This Is Where I Came In (song)">This Is Where I Came In</a>", 2001).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Album_credits_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Album_credits-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> In the reunited Bee Gees from 1987 onward, Gibb was the group's resident expert on all technical phases of recording and coordinated musicians and engineers to create much of the group's sound.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Songwriting_and_composing">Songwriting and composing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Songwriting and composing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a songwriter, Gibb contributed primarily to melody, with his brothers writing the lyrics that they would sing on the finished song (for the most part). It is difficult to identify his contributions because the songs were so shaped to the singer, but his brothers' continued writing collaboration with him on solo projects shows how much they relied on him. On average, Gibb sang lead on one or two songs per album and he was sometimes known as "the quiet one" for his less obvious contributions to the group, but privately he was a good teller of stories, who immensely enjoyed talking with fans. His reputation as a mild-mannered, stabilizing influence with two very ambitious brothers continued through his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:Bilyeu_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:Bilyeu-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> His comic personality shone on stage during Bee Gees concerts when the brothers would perform an acoustic medley. Many times during the song "<a href="/wiki/Holiday_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="Holiday (Bee Gees song)">Holiday</a>", Maurice would mock Robin while he was singing or pretend he was bored (Maurice did not sing on the original record).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gibb said he had "battled the booze" since the 1970s. <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a> introduced him to his favourite drink, whisky and coke:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Transcript_of_CNN_Larry_King_Weekend_interview_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Transcript_of_CNN_Larry_King_Weekend_interview-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> "If he had given me <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a>, I would have drunk the cyanide, I was so in awe of the man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drink.2Fref_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-drink.2Fref-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> With his neighbour <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr" title="Ringo Starr">Ringo Starr</a> he would go out drinking. It got to the point where he became unreliable and prior to going onstage would have to feel his way along the wall to get there, according to Barry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drink.2Fref_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-drink.2Fref-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> One factor in Maurice's recovery was the active intervention of his brothers, who had recently lost youngest brother Andy. In an interview, Maurice acknowledged that his final years of alcohol abuse had been driven by his failure to reach Andy before his death, and his subsequent guilt. After rehab Maurice started to rediscover his family again, spending quality time with them. To celebrate this, he and Yvonne renewed their wedding vows in 1992. The ceremony was attended not only by many members of their families but many of the friends Gibb made whilst at the rehabilitation centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drink.2Fref_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-drink.2Fref-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_relationships">First relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: First relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gibb met Scottish pop singer <a href="/wiki/Lulu_(singer)" title="Lulu (singer)">Lulu</a> through <a href="/wiki/Colin_Petersen" title="Colin Petersen">Colin Petersen</a>. Lulu recalls about Maurice, "I thought Maurice was cute, so I said, 'In that case, tell him to stop talking about me and take me out', He did just that, I never expected much to come from this, but in fact our relationship grew, after a fashion, Going steady is quite the wrong way to describe what was happening between us. Going unsteady might better sum up the way we fell in and out with each other". According to Lulu, she, Maurice and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stigwood" title="Robert Stigwood">Robert Stigwood</a> watched <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Saville_Theatre" title="Saville Theatre">Saville Theatre</a> in London. Later, after his relationship with Lulu had become public knowledge, he said: "Lulu and I met on the <i>Top of the Pops</i> TV show three months ago, and then at the Saville Theatre in London last month".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-around_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-around-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Later in 1968, Lulu was seen out on the town with <a href="/wiki/Davy_Jones_(musician)" title="Davy Jones (musician)">Davy Jones</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Monkees" title="The Monkees">The Monkees</a>, Jones was also a friend of Maurice's. As Gibb recalled, "Davy Jones was a good friend of mine, I broke it off with Lulu over the phone and Davy called me up and said 'I am going to ask Lu to dinner with some friends, is that all right?' I said, 'Yes, I am not going out with her any more, have a ball' and threw the phone down. They then took so many photos of them and made it look like a six-month romance, but she had only been out with him that one night, she did not even want to go out. She phoned me the next day and she was crying, saying she was sorry". Maurice later regretted not accepting her apology, "We just grew up, that's all," Maurice admitted. "We were miserable apart and when we started going out again, it got so that I didn't want to be with anyone. I used to phone her up from <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> about twice a day. Then she'd call me back. We used to make about 90 minute calls". And on the same year, Gibb was seen around town with a Hungarian singer <a href="/wiki/Sarolta_Zalatnay" title="Sarolta Zalatnay">Sarolta Zalatnay</a>, but he later insisted: "I'm not a Casanova and it's all most unfortunate. I've never been publicised to be available. Because of the stories of my affairs with Lulu and Sarolta, the fans feel, 'Oh, we won't bother about Maurice because he's always got a steady girl'. Gibb recalled the first meeting with his future-in-laws, "I went up to <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> to meet Lu's parents, and I was told that Billy, who is younger than Lu, would be at the station to meet me. I got off the train and was walking along the platform when I saw him. I didn't need to speak, I knew it was Billy and I went up to him and said, 'You're Billy, aren't you?', he said, 'You must be Maurice' and we had met".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-around_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-around-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The couple married in 18 February 1969 and divorced in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Their careers and his heavy drinking forced them apart and they divorced, childless, in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Gibb later stated they both drank: "We didn't have any responsibilities, we'd just party."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drink.2Fref_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-drink.2Fref-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He married his second wife, Yvonne Spenceley Gibb, on 17 October 1975. They had two children, Adam and Samantha Gibb,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> and their marriage lasted until his death. Maurice's alcoholic nadir came in 1991, when he pulled a gun on his wife and kids after a month-long <a href="/wiki/Binge_drinking" title="Binge drinking">bender</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drink.2Fref_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-drink.2Fref-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> She left him and immediately went over to brother Barry's house, refusing to come back until he had done something about his drinking. Maurice went into rehab, calling Yvonne telling her he was going to stay because he really wanted to stop drinking. She said that was the call she had been waiting for.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drink.2Fref_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-drink.2Fref-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gibb died unexpectedly at the age of 53, at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai_Medical_Center_%26_Miami_Heart_Institute" title="Mount Sinai Medical Center &amp; Miami Heart Institute">Mount Sinai Medical Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Miami_Beach,_Florida" title="Miami Beach, Florida">Miami Beach, Florida</a> on 12 January 2003 of a <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a>, while waiting to undergo surgery for a <a href="/wiki/Volvulus" title="Volvulus">twisted intestine</a>, with his wife, children, and brothers at his side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> After his funeral service, he was <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremated</a>. His brothers Barry and Robin ceased performing as a group for a time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> but later decided to perform occasionally under the Bee Gees banner<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> before twin brother Robin died of liver and kidney failure on 20 May 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Barry and Robin Gibb told the BBC about Maurice's death, "The fact that they had to operate on Maurice during the shock of cardiac arrest is questionable". Barry said "None of the sequence of events have yet made sense to us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Robin Gibb spoke to <i>Mojo</i> magazine about Maurice's death in 2003: "We were kids together, and teenagers. We spent the whole of our lives with each other because of our music. I can't accept that he's dead. I just imagine he's alive somewhere else."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tributes">Tributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Tributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jagged_Edge_(group)" title="Jagged Edge (group)">Jagged Edge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> recorded "<a href="/wiki/Too_Much_Heaven" title="Too Much Heaven">Too Much Heaven</a>" in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wyclef_Jean" title="Wyclef Jean">Wyclef Jean</a> recorded "<a href="/wiki/Jive_Talkin%27" title="Jive Talkin'">Jive Talkin'</a>" in 2005 and <a href="/wiki/Sheryl_Crow" title="Sheryl Crow">Sheryl Crow</a> recorded "<a href="/wiki/To_Love_Somebody_(song)" title="To Love Somebody (song)">To Love Somebody</a>" in 2005 as a tribute for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> A tribute to Maurice, entitled <i>Souls Stick Around: A Tale of the Black Hills and Maurice Gibb</i>, was published as an eBook on Amazon.com in March 2012 by Bee Gees fan and author, Dawnette Owens.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His influences were <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Everly_Brothers" title="The Everly Brothers">the Everly Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Richard" title="Cliff Richard">Cliff Richard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Anka" title="Paul Anka">Paul Anka</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mills_Brothers" title="The Mills Brothers">the Mills Brothers</a>. According to Gibb himself when he was a young kid, he listened to the Beatles' early records. He was also influenced by <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a>'s bass playing. Talking about the song "Have You Heard the Word" on which he played bass, "We were just getting ready to do some tracks and we were just doing nothing and I was fartin' around on the bass. I was a big Paul freak. He was a great teacher for me".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-are_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-are-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Gibb continued, "I could play every bass lick he [Paul McCartney] played", especially "<a href="/wiki/Michelle_(song)" title="Michelle (song)">Michelle</a>" on which he says "the bass line, really tasteful stuff, and way ahead, way ahead of his time". Gibb also described "<a href="/wiki/The_Word_(song)" title="The Word (song)">The Word</a>" as a "real John Lennon number" when he listened to the song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-are_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-are-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A recording studio at <a href="/wiki/Chorlton_High_School" title="Chorlton High School">Chorlton High School</a>, one of the schools the brothers attended, commemorates Maurice Gibb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> In honoring Gibb, his brother Barry Gibb noted: "Mo was a real McCartney bass freak, as a lot of us were. He would pick up on all the things that McCartney would [do]. Maurice was very good on different instruments, you know. Good lead guitarist, good bass player, good keyboard player. He was versatile. He loved playing bass more than anything else, I think, at that time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
