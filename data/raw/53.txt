<p><b><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> film trilogy</b> comprises three <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/live_action" title="wikipedia:live action">live action</a> <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/fantasy" title="wikipedia:fantasy">fantasy</a> <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/epic" title="wikipedia:epic">epic films</a>; <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring_(film)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring (film)">The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2001" title="2001">2001</a>), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Two_Towers_(film)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers (film)">The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2002" title="2002">2002</a>) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Return_of_the_King_(film)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King (film)">The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2003" title="2003">2003</a>). For simplicity, the titles are often abbreviated to 'LotR', with 'FotR', 'TTT' and 'RotK' for each of the respective films.
</p><p>Set in <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth" title="Middle-earth">Middle-earth</a>, the three films follow the young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hobbit" title="Hobbit">Hobbit</a> <a href="/wiki/Frodo_Baggins" title="Frodo Baggins">Frodo Baggins</a> as he and theÂ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellowship_of_the_Ring_(characters)" title="Fellowship of the Ring (characters)">Fellowship</a> embark on a quest to destroy the <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">One Ring</a>, and thus ensure the destruction of the Dark Lord <a href="/wiki/Sauron" title="Sauron">Sauron</a>, but the Fellowship becomes broken, and Frodo continues the quest together with his loyal companion <a href="/wiki/Samwise_Gamgee" title="Samwise Gamgee">Sam</a> and the treacherous <a href="/wiki/Gollum" title="Gollum">Gollum</a>. Meanwhile the Wizard <a href="/wiki/Gandalf" title="Gandalf">Gandalf</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aragorn" title="Aragorn">Aragorn</a>, heir in exile to the throne of <a href="/wiki/Gondor" title="Gondor">Gondor</a>, unite and rally the Free Peoples of Middle-earth in several battles cumulating in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Ring" title="War of the Ring">War of the Ring</a>. The Wizard <a href="/wiki/Saruman" title="Saruman">Saruman</a> is defeated, the Ring is destroyed, and Sauron and his forces are vanquished.
</p><p>The movies were directed by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a> and released by <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a>. The trilogy is based on the book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a> and follows its general storyline, despite some deviations. Considered to be one of the biggest movie projects ever undertaken with an overall budget of $280 million, the entire project took eight years, with the filming for all three films done simultaneously and entirely in Jackson's native <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.
</p><p>The trilogy was a large financial success, with the films being the 25<sup>th</sup>, 17<sup>th </sup>and 5<sup style="border-style: initial; border-color: initial; font-style: normal;">th </sup>highest grossing films of alltime respectively, unadjusted for inflation. The films were critically acclaimed, winning 17 Academy Awards in total, as well as wide praise for the cast and innovative practical and digital special effects. Each film in the trilogy also had Special Extended Editions, released a year after the theatrical release on <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DVD" title="wikipedia:DVD">DVD</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit Development section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h2>
<figure class="article-thumb tleft show-info-icon" style="width: 180px"> <a class="image image-thumbnail" href="http://img2.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20150203040819/lotr/images/7/74/LOTRFOTRmovie.jpg"><img alt="LOTRFOTRmovie" class="thumbimage " data-image-key="LOTRFOTRmovie.jpg" data-image-name="LOTRFOTRmovie.jpg" height="263" src="http://img3.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20150203040819/lotr/images/thumb/7/74/LOTRFOTRmovie.jpg/180px-LOTRFOTRmovie.jpg" width="180"/></a> <figcaption> <a class="sprite info-icon" href="/wiki/File:LOTRFOTRmovie.jpg"></a> <p class="caption"><i>The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring</i> movie poster (2001)</p> </figcaption> </figure>
<p>Director <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a> first came into contact with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i> when he saw <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Bakshi" title="Ralph Bakshi">Ralph Bakshi</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(1978_film)" title="The Lord of the Rings (1978 film)">1978 film</a>, which he found confusing. Afterwards, he read a tie-in edition of the book during a twelve-hour train journey from <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> to <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auckland" title="wikipedia:Auckland">Auckland</a> when he was seventeen. Jackson's reaction was, "I can't wait until somebody makes a movie of this book because <i>I'd</i> like to see it!
</p><p>In 1995, Jackson was finishing <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Frighteners" title="wikipedia:The Frighteners">The Frighteners</a></i> and considered <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> as a new project, wondering "why nobody else seemed to be doing anything about it". With the new developments in <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/computer_generated_imagery" title="wikipedia:computer generated imagery">computer generated imagery</a> following <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="wikipedia:Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i>, Jackson set about planning a fantasy film that would be relatively serious and feel "real". By October, he and his partner <a href="/wiki/Fran_Walsh" title="Fran Walsh">Fran Walsh</a> teamed up with <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miramax" title="wikipedia:Miramax">Miramax</a> boss <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvey_Weinstein" title="wikipedia:Harvey Weinstein">Harvey Weinstein</a> to negotiate with <a href="/wiki/Saul_Zaentz" title="Saul Zaentz">Saul Zaentz</a> who had held the rights to the book since the early 1970s, pitching an adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a></i> and two films based on <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>. Negotiations then stalled when <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="wikipedia:Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a> offered Jackson a <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/remake" title="wikipedia:remake">remake</a> of <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Kong_(1933_film)" title="wikipedia:King Kong (1933 film)">King Kong</a></i>. Weinstein was furious, and further problems arose when it turned out Zaentz did not have distribution rights to <i>The Hobbit</i>; <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Artists" title="wikipedia:United Artists">United Artists</a>, which was in the market, did. By April 1996 the rights question was still not resolved. Jackson decided to move ahead with <i>King Kong</i> before filming <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, prompting Universal to enter a deal with Miramax to receive foreign earnings from <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> whilst Miramax received foreign earnings from <i>King Kong</i>.
</p><p>When Universal cancelled <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Kong_(2005_film)" title="wikipedia:King Kong (2005 film)">King Kong</a></i> in 1997, Jackson and Walsh immediately received support from Weinstein and began a six-week process of sorting out the rights. Jackson and Walsh asked <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Costa_Botes" title="wikipedia:Costa Botes">Costa Botes</a> to write a synopsis of the book and they began to re-read the book. Two to three months later, they had written their treatment. The first film would have dealt with what would become <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring_(film)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring (film)">The Fellowship of the Ring</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Two_Towers_(film)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers (film)">The Two Towers</a></i> and the beginning of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Return_of_the_King_(film)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King (film)">The Return of the King</a></i>, ending with the death of <a href="/wiki/Saruman" title="Saruman">Saruman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gandalf" title="Gandalf">Gandalf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peregrin_Took" title="Peregrin Took">Pippin</a> going to <a href="/wiki/Minas_Tirith" title="Minas Tirith">Minas Tirith</a>. In this treatment Gwaihir and Gandalf visit Edoras after escaping Saruman, Gollum attacks Frodo when the Fellowship is still united, and <a href="/wiki/Farmer_Maggot" title="Farmer Maggot">Farmer Maggot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glorfindel" title="Glorfindel">Glorfindel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radagast" title="Radagast">Radagast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elladan_and_Elrohir" title="Elladan and Elrohir">Elladan and Elrohir</a> are present. Bilbo attends the Council of Elrond, Sam looks into Galadriel's mirror, Saruman is redeemed before he dies and the NazgÃ»l just make it into Mount Doom before they fall. They presented their treatment to Harvey and <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Weinstein" title="wikipedia:Bob Weinstein">Bob Weinstein</a>, the latter of whom they focused on impressing with their screenwriting as he had not read the book. They agreed upon two films and a total budget of $75 million.
</p><p>During mid-1997, Jackson and Walsh began writing with Stephen Sinclair. Sinclair's partner, <a href="/wiki/Philippa_Boyens" title="Philippa Boyens">Philippa Boyens</a>, was a major fan of the book and joined the writing team after reading their treatment. It took 13â14 months to write the two film scripts, which were 147 and 144 pages respectively. Sinclair left the project due to theatrical obligations. Amongst their revisions, Sam, Merry and Pippin are caught eavesdropping and forced to go along with Frodo. Gandalf's account of his time at Orthanc was pulled out of flashback and LothlÃ³rien was cut with <a href="/wiki/Galadriel" title="Galadriel">Galadriel</a> attending the Council of Elrond. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denethor" title="Denethor">Denethor</a>, Boromir's father, also attends the Council, and other changes included having <a href="/wiki/Arwen" title="Arwen">Arwen</a> rescue Frodo, and the action sequence involving the cave troll. Arwen was even going to kill the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Witch-king" title="Witch-king">Witch-king</a>. Most significantly, there was an all-new sequence. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ringwraith" title="Ringwraith">Ringwraith</a> kills Saruman and attacks Gandalf at Orthanc. Seeing this from the Seeing Seat, now at <a href="/wiki/Emyn_Muil" title="Emyn Muil">Emyn Muil</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Amon_Hen" title="Amon Hen">Amon Hen</a>, Frodo puts on the Ring and draws him all the way to the Seat on his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fell_beast" title="Fell beast">Fell beast</a>. Frodo manages to save Sam and stabs the wraith in his heart.
</p><p>Trouble struck when Marty Katz was sent to New Zealand. Spending four months there, he told Miramax that the films were more likely to cost $150 million, and with Miramax unable to finance this, and with $15 million already spent, they decided to merge the two films into one. On <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/June_17" title="wikipedia:June 17">June 17</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1998" title="1998">1998</a>, Bob Weinstein presented a treatment of a single two-hour film version of the book. He suggested cutting <a href="/wiki/Bree" title="Bree">Bree</a> and the Battle of Helm's Deep, "losing or using" <a href="/wiki/Saruman" title="Saruman">Saruman</a>, merging <a href="/wiki/Rohan" title="Rohan">Rohan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gondor" title="Gondor">Gondor</a> with <a href="/wiki/%C3%89owyn" title="Ãowyn">Ãowyn</a> as <a href="/wiki/Boromir" title="Boromir">Boromir</a>'s sister, shortening <a href="/wiki/Rivendell" title="Rivendell">Rivendell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moria" title="Moria">Moria</a> as well as having <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ent" title="Ent">Ents</a> prevent the Uruk-hai kidnapping Merry and Pippin. Upset by the idea of "cutting out half the good stuff" Jackson balked, and Miramax declared that any script or <a href="/wiki/Weta_Workshop" title="Weta Workshop">Weta Workshop</a>'s work was theirs. Jackson went around Hollywood for four weeks, showing a thirty-five minute video of their work, before meeting with <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Ordesky" title="wikipedia:Mark Ordesky">Mark Ordesky</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a>. At New Line Cinema, <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Shaye" title="wikipedia:Robert Shaye">Robert Shaye</a> viewed the video, and then asked why they were making two films when the book was published as three volumes; he wanted to make a film trilogy. Now Jackson, Walsh and Boyens had to write three new scripts.
</p><p>The expansion to three films allowed a lot more creative freedom, and Jackson, Walsh and Boyens had to restructure their script accordingly. Each film is not exactly based on each volume of the book, but rather they represent a three-part adaptation, as Jackson takes a more chronological approach to the story, whilst Tolkien retold chunks of his fictional history. Frodo's quest is the main focus, and Aragorn is the main subplot, and many sequences (such as <a href="/wiki/Tom_Bombadil" title="Tom Bombadil">Tom Bombadil</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scouring_of_the_Shire" title="Scouring of the Shire">Scouring of the Shire</a>) that do not contribute directly to those two plots were left out. Much effort was put into creating satisfactory conclusions and making sure exposition did not bog down the pacing. Amongst new sequences, there are also expansions on elements Tolkien kept ambiguous, such as the battles and the creatures.
</p><p>Above all, most characters have been altered for extra drama. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aragorn" title="Aragorn">Aragorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9oden" title="ThÃ©oden">ThÃ©oden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treebeard" title="Treebeard">Treebeard</a> have added or modified elements of self-doubt, whilst <a href="/wiki/Galadriel" title="Galadriel">Galadriel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elrond" title="Elrond">Elrond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Faramir" title="Faramir">Faramir</a> have been darkened. <a href="/wiki/Boromir" title="Boromir">Boromir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gollum" title="Gollum">Gollum</a> are (arguably) relatively more sympathetic, whilst some characters such as <a href="/wiki/Legolas" title="Legolas">Legolas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gimli" title="Gimli">Gimli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saruman" title="Saruman">Saruman</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denethor" title="Denethor">Denethor</a> have been simplified. Some characters, such as <a href="/wiki/Arwen" title="Arwen">Arwen</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89omer" title="Ãomer">Ãomer</a>, are given actions from minor characters such as <a href="/wiki/Glorfindel" title="Glorfindel">Glorfindel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erkenbrand" title="Erkenbrand">Erkenbrand</a>, and generally lines of dialogue are somewhat preserved or switched around between locations or characters depending on suitability of the scenes. New scenes were also added to expand on characterization. In the meantime, during shooting, the screenplays would undergo many daily transformations, due to contributions from cast looking to further explore their characters. Most notable amongst these rewrites was the character <a href="/wiki/Arwen" title="Arwen">Arwen</a>, who was originally planned as a warrior princess, but reverted to her book counterpart, who remains physically inactive in the story (though she sends moral and military support).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_design">Production design</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit Production design section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h2>
<p>Jackson began storyboarding the trilogy with <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Rivers" title="wikipedia:Christian Rivers">Christian Rivers</a> in <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_1997" title="wikipedia:August 1997">August 1997</a> and assigned his crew to begin designing Middle-earth at the same time. Jackson hired longtime collaborator <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Taylor_(movies)" title="wikipedia:Richard Taylor (movies)">Richard Taylor</a> to lead Weta Workshop on five major design elements: armour, weapons, prosthetics/make-up, creatures and miniatures. In November 1997, famed Tolkien illustrators <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lee" title="Alan Lee">Alan Lee</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Howe" title="John Howe">John Howe</a> joined the project. Most of the imagery in the films is based on their various illustrations. Grant Major was charged with the task of converting Lee and Howe's designs into architecture, creating models of the sets, whilst Dan Hennah worked as art director, scouting locations and organizing the building of sets.
</p><p>Jackson's vision of <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth" title="Middle-earth">Middle-earth</a> was described as being "<a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_Harryhausen" title="wikipedia:Ray Harryhausen">Ray Harryhausen</a> meets <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Lean" title="wikipedia:David Lean">David Lean</a>" by Randy Cook. Jackson wanted a gritty realism and historical regard for the fantasy, and attempted to make the world rational and believable. For example, the New Zealand army helped build Hobbiton months before filming began so the plants could really grow. Creatures were designed to be biologically believable, such as the enormous wings of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fell_beast" title="Fell beast">Fell beast</a> to help it fly. In total, Weta Workshop created 48,000 pieces of armour, 500 bows and 10,000 arrows. They also created many prosthetics, such as 1800 pairs of Hobbit feet for the lead actors, as well as many ears, noses and heads for the cast, and around 19,000 costumes were woven and aged. Every prop was specially designed by the Art Department, taking the different scales into account.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit Filming section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h2>
<p><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_photography" title="wikipedia:Principal photography">Principal photography</a> for all three films was conducted concurrently in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> from <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_11" title="wikipedia:October 11">October 11</a>, <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1999" title="wikipedia:1999">1999</a> through to <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/December_22" title="wikipedia:December 22">December 22</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2000" title="2000">2000</a> for 274 days. <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pick-up_(film)" title="wikipedia:Pick-up (film)">Pick-up</a> shoots were conducted annually from 2001 to 2004. The trilogy was shot at over 150 different locations, with seven different units shooting, as well as soundstages around <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> and <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queenstown,_New_Zealand" title="wikipedia:Queenstown, New Zealand">Queenstown</a>. As well as Jackson directing the whole production, other unit directors included John Mahaffie, Geoff Murphy, <a href="/wiki/Fran_Walsh" title="Fran Walsh">Fran Walsh</a>, Barrie Osbourne, Rick Porras and any other assistant director, producer or writer available. Jackson monitored these units with live satellite feeds, and with the added pressure of constant script re-writes and the multiple units interpreting his envisioned result, he only got around four hours of sleep a night. Due to the remoteness of some of New Zealand's untamed landscapes, the crew would also bring survival kits in case helicopters couldn't reach the location to bring them home in time.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_effects">Special effects</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit Special effects section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h2>
<p>The first film has around 540 effects shots, the second 799, and the third 1488 (2730 in total). The total increases to 3420 with the extended editions. 260 visual effects artists began work on the trilogy, and the number doubled by <i>The Two Towers</i>. The crew, led by Jim Rygiel and Randy Cook, worked long and hard hours, often overnight, to produce special effects within a short space of time. Jackson's overactive imagination was a driving force. For example, several major shots of Helm's Deep were produced within the last six weeks of post-production of <i>The Two Towers</i>, and the same happened again within the last six weeks on <i>The Return of the King</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-production">Post-production</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit Post-production section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h2>
<figure class="article-thumb tleft show-info-icon" style="width: 180px"> <a class="image image-thumbnail" href="http://img1.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20130216182515/lotr/images/2/2a/LOTRTTTmovie.jpg"><img alt="LOTRTTTmovie" class="thumbimage lzy lzyPlcHld " data-image-key="LOTRTTTmovie.jpg" data-image-name="LOTRTTTmovie.jpg" data-src="http://img2.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20130216182515/lotr/images/thumb/2/2a/LOTRTTTmovie.jpg/180px-LOTRTTTmovie.jpg" height="267" onload="if(typeof ImgLzy==='object'){ImgLzy.load(this)}" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D" width="180"><noscript><img alt="LOTRTTTmovie" class="thumbimage " data-image-key="LOTRTTTmovie.jpg" data-image-name="LOTRTTTmovie.jpg" height="267" src="http://img2.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20130216182515/lotr/images/thumb/2/2a/LOTRTTTmovie.jpg/180px-LOTRTTTmovie.jpg" width="180"/></noscript></img></a> <figcaption> <a class="sprite info-icon" href="/wiki/File:LOTRTTTmovie.jpg"></a> <p class="caption"><i>The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers</i> movie poster (2002)</p> </figcaption> </figure>
<p>Post-production would have the benefit for a full year on each film before their respective December releases, often finishing in OctoberâNovember, with the crew immediately going to work on the next film. Later on, Jackson would move to <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London" title="wikipedia:London">London</a> to advise the score and continued editing, whilst having a computer feed for discussions to The Dorchester Hotel, and a "fat pipe" of internet connections from <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinewood_Studios" title="wikipedia:Pinewood Studios">Pinewood Studios</a> to look at the special effects. He had a <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycom" title="wikipedia:Polycom">Polycom</a> video link and 5.1 surround sound to organise meetings, and listen to new music and sound effects generally wherever he was. The extended editions also had a tight schedule at the start of each year to complete special effects and music.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Editing">Editing</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit Editing section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>To avoid pressure, Jackson hired a different editor for each film. John Gilbert worked on the first film, Mike Horton and Jabez Olssen on the second and longtime Jackson collaborators Jamie Selkirk and Annie Collins on the third. Daily rushes would often last up to four hours, with scenes being done throughout 1999â2002 for the rough (4 1/2 hours) assemblies of the films. In total, six million feet of film (over 1,800Â km) was edited down to the 11 hours and 23 minutes (683 minutes) of Extended DVD running time. This was the final area of shaping of the films, when Jackson realised that sometimes the best scripting could be redundant on screen, as he picked apart scenes every day from multiple takes.
</p><p>Editing on the first film was relatively easygoing, with Jackson coming up with the concept of an Extended Edition later on, although after a screening to New Line they had to re-edit the beginning for a prologue. <i>The Two Towers</i> was always acknowledged by the crew as the most difficult film to make, as "it had no beginning or end", and had the additional problem of inter-cutting storylines appropriately. Jackson even continued editing the film when that part of the schedule officially ended, resulting in some scenes, including the reforging of <a href="/wiki/And%C3%BAril" title="AndÃºril">AndÃºril</a>, Gollum's back-story, and Saruman's demise, being moved to <i>The Return of the King</i>. Later, Saruman's demise was controversially cut from the cinema edition (but included in the extended edition) when Jackson felt it was not starting the third film effectively enough. As with all parts of the third film's post-production, editing was very chaotic. The first time Jackson actually saw the completed film was at the Wellington premiere.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit Music section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Howard_Shore" title="Howard Shore">Howard Shore</a> composed the trilogy's music. He was hired in August 2000 and visited the set, and watched the assembly cuts of Films 1 and 3. Although the first film had some of its score done in Wellington, the trilogy's score was mostly recorded in <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watford" title="wikipedia:Watford">Watford</a> Town Hall and mixed at <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbey_Road_Studios" title="wikipedia:Abbey Road Studios">Abbey Road Studios</a>. Jackson planned to advise the score for six weeks each year in London, although for <i>The Two Towers</i> he stayed 12. As a <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Beatles" title="wikipedia:The Beatles">Beatles</a> fan, Jackson had a photo tribute done there on the zebra crossing.
</p><p>The soundtrack is primarily played by the <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="wikipedia:London Philharmonic Orchestra">London Philharmonic Orchestra</a>, and many artists such as <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Del_Maestro" title="wikipedia:Ben Del Maestro">Ben Del Maestro</a>, <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enya" title="wikipedia:Enya">Enya</a>, <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renee_Fleming" title="wikipedia:Renee Fleming">Renee Fleming</a>, <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Galway" title="wikipedia:James Galway">Sir James Galway</a> and <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annie_Lennox" title="wikipedia:Annie Lennox">Annie Lennox</a> contributed. Even actors <a href="/wiki/Billy_Boyd" title="Billy Boyd">Billy Boyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viggo_Mortensen" title="Viggo Mortensen">Viggo Mortensen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liv_Tyler" title="Liv Tyler">Liv Tyler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miranda_Otto" title="Miranda Otto">Miranda Otto</a> (extended cuts only for the latter two) and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a> (for a single gong sound in the second film) contributed to the score. Fran Walsh and Philippa Boyens also wrote the lyrics to various music and songs, which David Salo translated into Tolkien's languages. The third film's end song, <i><a href="/wiki/Into_the_West_(song)" title="Into the West (song)">Into the West</a></i>, was a tribute to a young filmmaker Jackson and Walsh befriended named <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameron_Duncan" title="wikipedia:Cameron Duncan">Cameron Duncan</a>, who died of cancer in 2003.
</p><p>Shore composed a main theme for the Fellowship rather than many different character themes, and its strength and weaknesses in volume are depicted at different points in the trilogy. On top of that, individual themes were composed to represent different cultures. Infamously, the amount of music Shore had to write every day for the third film increased dramatically to around seven minutes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sound">Sound</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit Sound section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Sound technicians spent the early part of the year trying to find the right sounds: animal sounds like tigers and walruses were bought. Sometimes human voices were used, such as Fran Walsh as the NazgÃ»l scream and David Farmer as some Warg howls. Some sounds were unexpected: a donkey screech is the Fell Beast, and the <i>mÃ»makil</i> roar comes from the beginning and end of a lion. In addition, there was <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)" title="wikipedia:Dubbing (filmmaking)">ADR</a> for most of the dialogue.
</p><p>The technicians worked with New Zealand locals to get many of the sounds. They re-recorded sounds in abandoned tunnels for an echo-like effect in the Moria sequence. 10,000 New Zealand cricket fans provided the sound of the Uruk-hai army in <i>The Two Towers</i>, with Jackson acting as conductor during a single cricket break. They spent time recording sounds in a graveyard at night, and also had construction workers drop stone blocks for the sounds of boulders firing and landing in <i>The Return of the King</i>. Mixing generally took place between August and November at "The Film Mix", before Jackson commissioned building a new studio in 2003. Annoyingly, the building wasn't fully completed as they started mixing for <i>The Return of the King</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_and_critical_response">Public and critical response</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit Public and critical response section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h2>
<p><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> film trilogy is verified to be the currently highest grossing motion picture trilogy worldwide of all time, besting such other film franchises as the <a class="external text exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Wars">two Star Wars Trilogies</a> and <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Godfather" title="wikipedia:The Godfather">The Godfather</a>. The film trilogy also tied a record for the total number of Academy Awards won.
</p><p>The majority of critics have also praised the trilogy, with <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Turan" title="wikipedia:Kenneth Turan">Kenneth Turan</a> of the <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="wikipedia:Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a> writing that "<i>the trilogy will not soon, if ever, find its equal</i>". In particular, performances from <a href="/wiki/Ian_McKellen" title="Ian McKellen">Ian McKellen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sean_Astin" title="Sean Astin">Sean Astin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sean_Bean" title="Sean Bean">Sean Bean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Serkis" title="Andy Serkis">Andy Serkis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Hill" title="Bernard Hill">Bernard Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viggo_Mortensen" title="Viggo Mortensen">Viggo Mortensen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miranda_Otto" title="Miranda Otto">Miranda Otto</a> stood out for many in audience polls, although the entire cast was well praised and won awards of Best Acting Ensemble. The special effects for the battles and Gollum were also praised. Overall, the films received a positive 93% critics rating on <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/rottentomatoes.com" title="wikipedia:rottentomatoes.com">rottentomatoes.com</a>, (93% for FotR, 94% for RotK and 97% for TTT) a consensus amongst film critics.
</p><p>The trilogy appears in many "Top 10" film lists, such as the <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dallas-Fort_Worth_Film_Critics_Association%27s_Top_10_Films" title="wikipedia:Dallas-Fort Worth Film Critics Association's Top 10 Films">Dallas-Fort Worth Film Critics Association's Top 10 Films</a>, <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_Magazine%27s_All-Time_100_Movies" title="wikipedia:Time Magazine's All-Time 100 Movies">Time Magazine's All-Time 100 Movies</a>, <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Berardinelli" title="wikipedia:James Berardinelli">James Berardinelli</a>'s Top 100, and The Screen Directory's "Top Ten Films of All Time" (considering the trilogy as "one epic film split into three parts"). In 2007, <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USA_Today" title="wikipedia:USA Today">USA Today</a></i> named the trilogy as the most important films of the past 25 years.
</p><p>In 2006, all the three films were in the Top 10 of <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Movie_Database" title="wikipedia:Internet Movie Database">IMDb</a>'s famous <i>Top 250</i> list. As of September 3, 2007, RotK stood in the 10th place, FotR in the 17th place and TTT in the 25th place, a consensus amongst voters. The trilogy is constantly increasing and decreasing its rating due to the many voters, and is the only movie trilogy in the site that has all its three movies in the Top 25 of the list (this feat has since been matched only by Christopher Nolan's Batman Trilogy), a firm display of the Lord of the Rings film trilogy's popularity and reputation as the best trilogy of all time. FotR is the third most voted movie on IMDb (only after <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Shawshank_Redemption" title="wikipedia:The Shawshank Redemption">The Shawshank Redemption</a>Â and <a class="external text exitstitial" href="http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0468569/" rel="nofollow">The Dark Knight</a> </i>), with an average of 814,515 votes as of December 16, 2013. RotK and TTT, respectively, follow closely after.
</p><p>Overall, The Lord of the Rings trilogy is well praised and considered by many the best trilogy of all time and the best movie (considering the trilogy as a whole unique epic) of the Twenty-First Century.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_of_worldwide_box_office_figures">Comparison of worldwide box office figures</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit Comparison of worldwide box office figures section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The following movies were all released with but a few years of each other:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_won">Awards won</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit Awards won section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h3>

<p>The three films together were nominated for a total of 30 Academy Awards, of which they won 17, a record for any movie trilogy. On its own, <i>The Return of the King</i> tied the previous record of eleven Academy Awards and won in every category it was nominated in, an extremely rare feat. Return of the King also tied a record for the total number of Academy Awards won, 11, with <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben-Hur_(1959_film)" title="wikipedia:Ben-Hur (1959 film)">Ben-Hur</a> and <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="wikipedia:Titanic (1997 film)">Titanic</a>. Although the three films failed to win any acting awards from the Academy, <a href="/wiki/Ian_McKellen" title="Ian McKellen">Ian McKellen</a> earned the series its sole Academy acting nomination for the 2001 release of The Fellowship of the Ring.

<p>As well as Academy Awards, each film of the trilogy scored <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MTV_Movie_Awards" title="wikipedia:MTV Movie Awards">MTV Movie Awards</a>' Best Film, and the <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hugo_Awards" title="wikipedia:Hugo Awards">Hugo Awards</a> for Best Dramatic Presentation categories. The first and third films also won the Best Film <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BAFTA" title="wikipedia:BAFTA">BAFTAs</a>. It must also be noted that the soundtrack for the Two Towers did not receive a nomination because of the rule prohibiting a soundtrack including music from a previous soundtrack to be eligible for nomination. This rule was overturned in time for <i>The Return of the King</i> to receive the Oscar for Best Music Score.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_to_changes_in_the_movies_from_the_book">Reactions to changes in the movies from the book</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit Reactions to changes in the movies from the book section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>While the films were generally well received, some readers of the book decried certain changes made in the adaptation, including changes in tone and themes; various changes made to characters such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aragorn" title="Aragorn">Aragorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arwen" title="Arwen">Arwen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denethor" title="Denethor">Denethor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faramir" title="Faramir">Faramir</a> and Gimli, as well as to the main protagonist <a href="/wiki/Frodo_Baggins" title="Frodo Baggins">Frodo</a> himself; changes made to events (such as the Elves participating at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Hornburg" title="Battle of the Hornburg">Battle of Helm's Deep</a>, and Faramir taking the hobbits to <a href="/wiki/Osgiliath" title="Osgiliath">Osgiliath</a>); and the deletion of the penultimate chapter of Tolkien's work, "<a href="/wiki/The_Scouring_of_the_Shire" title="The Scouring of the Shire">The Scouring of the Shire</a>", a part he felt thematically necessary. For example, <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Hammond" title="Wayne Hammond">Wayne G. Hammond</a>, a noted <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tolkien_research" title="wikipedia:Tolkien research">Tolkien scholar</a>, has said of the first two films:
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<dl><dd>"<i>"</i>I find both of the Jackson films to be travesties as adaptations... faithful only on a basic level of plot... Cut and compress as necessary, yes, but don't change or add new material without very good reason... In the moments in which the films succeed, they do so by staying close to what Tolkien so carefully wrote; where they fail, it tends to be where they diverge from him, most seriously in the area of characterization. Most of the characters in the films are mere shadows of those in the book, weak and diminished (notably Frodo) or insulting caricatures (Pippin, Merry, and Gimli)... [T]he filmmakers sacrifice the richness of Tolkien's story and characters, not to mention common sense, for violence, cheap humor, and cheaper thrills... [S]o many of its reviewers have praised it as faithful to the book, or even superior to it, all of which adds insult to injury and is demonstrably wrong...'<b>"</b>
</dd><dd>âWayne Hammond
</dd></dl>
<p>Some fans of the book who disagreed with such changes have released their <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/fan_edit" title="wikipedia:fan edit">fan edits</a> of the films, which removed many of the changes to bring them closer to the original. The theatrical version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Two_Towers:_The_Purist_Edit" title="The Two Towers: The Purist Edit">The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers</a></i> has received this treatment, and a combined 8-hour version of the trilogy exists, called "<a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Two_Towers:_The_Purist_Edit" title="wikipedia:The Two Towers: The Purist Edit">The Lord of the Rings: The Purist Edition</a>".
Supporters of the film trilogy assert that it is a worthy interpretation of the book, most changes stemming from the filmmakers putting the book into a modern context; connected to this is their perceived need for developing characters further. It is important to note that many who worked on the trilogy are fans of the book, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christopher_Lee" title="Christopher Lee">Christopher Lee</a>, who alone among the cast had actually met Tolkien in person, and Boyens once noted that no matter what, it is simply their interpretation of the book. Jackson once said that to simply summarise the story on screen would be a mess, and in his own words, "<i>Sure, it's not really The Lord of the Rings... but it could still be a pretty damn cool movie.</i>" Other fans also claim that despite any changes, they do not matter within the context of stand-alone films, and nonetheless they serve as a tribute to the book and yet appeal to those who have not read it, and even lead some to. The Encyclopedia of Arda's Movie Guide states:
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<dl><dd>"<i>"</i>It seems appropriate to end with a word of acknowledgement of Peter Jackson and everyone else associated with the movie version of The Lord of the Rings. However, of course, they haven't come close to the scope and intricacy of the original story that would be quite impossible; what they have produced is still nothing less than a masterpiece. The film-makers, and of course Peter Jackson in particular, have to be admired merely for having the courage to take on such an immense challenge, let alone to produce such an exceptional result. The complete story of The Lord of the Rings is probably unfilmable, but Peter Jackson has come closer than anyone could have imagined possible.<i>"</i>"
</dd><dd>âEncyclopedia of Arda's Movie Guide
</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Three_films_or_one.3F">Three films or one?</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit Three films or one? section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Because the films were shot together and then edited into three separate films released theatrically over a span of three successive years, a significant number of fans and critics have come to regard the trilogy as a single film. They argue that as with the book, which was meant to be a single novel but was first released in three parts for marketing and budget reasons (leading to the common but erroneous label of "trilogy"); Jackson's trilogy is one long 10-hour film. When <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_magazine" title="wikipedia:Time magazine">Time magazine</a> placed the trilogy in its <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_magazine_top_100" title="wikipedia:Time magazine top 100">top 100 list</a> it was done under a single heading.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="editsection"><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy?action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit Legacy section"><img class="sprite edit-pencil" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D"/>Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The release of the films saw a surge of interest in <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> and Tolkien's other works, vastly increasing his impact on popular culture. For example, in 2003, the BBC conducted a poll to find the U.K.'s favourite book, and <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> won, at the height of anticipation for the third film. Despite higher sales, it was rumoured that the Tolkien family became split on the trilogy, with <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Tolkien" title="Christopher Tolkien">Christopher Tolkien</a> and <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Tolkien" title="wikipedia:Simon Tolkien">Simon Tolkien</a> feuding over whether or not it was a good idea to adapt. Christopher Tolkien has since denied these claims saying, "My own position is that <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> is peculiarly unsuitable to transformation into visual dramatic form. The suggestions that have been made that I 'disapprove' of the films, vent to the extent of thinking ill of those with whom I may differ, are wholly without foundation." He added that he had never "expressed any such feeling". Capitalizing on the trilogy's success, a musical adaptation of the book was launched in Toronto in 2006, but it closed after mostly poor reviews. The success of the films has also spawned the production of video games and many other kinds of merchandise.
</p><p>Jackson has become his own mogul like <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="wikipedia:Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>, and has befriended some industry heavyweights like <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bryan_Singer" title="wikipedia:Bryan Singer">Bryan Singer</a>, <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Darabont" title="wikipedia:Frank Darabont">Frank Darabont</a> and <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Cameron" title="wikipedia:James Cameron">James Cameron</a>. He founded his own film production company <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wingnut_Films" title="wikipedia:Wingnut Films">Wingnut Films</a>, and <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wingnut_Interactive" title="wikipedia:Wingnut Interactive">Wingnut Interactive</a>, a video game company. He was also finally given a chance to remake <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Kong_(2005_film)" title="wikipedia:King Kong (2005 film)">King Kong</a></i> in 2005; although it was not as successful, it nevertheless still received critical acclaim. On a personal level, he found it hard to leave the trilogy and still keeps the Bag End set (as a guesthouse) and Rivendell miniatures. He has also become a "favourite son" of New Zealand. Howard Shore also found leaving difficult, and in 2004 toured with <i>The Lord of the Rings Symphony</i>, consisting of two hours of the score.
</p><p>The trilogy has also renewed interest in the fantasy film genre. Around the same time, fellow New Zealand director <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Adamson" title="wikipedia:Andrew Adamson">Andrew Adamson</a> began <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Chronicles_of_Narnia_(film_series)" title="wikipedia:The Chronicles of Narnia (film series)">The Chronicles of Narnia<i> film series</i></a>, credited by many to be stylistically influenced by <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, being also shot in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and having art direction from Weta Workshop, as well as its own extended edition. <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MGM" title="wikipedia:MGM">MGM</a> wishes to make an adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a></i> in co-operation with <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a>, although Jackson is not signed on due to a dispute with the studio.
</p><p><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_capture" title="wikipedia:Motion capture">Motion capture</a> was used for characters in <i>King Kong</i>, <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I,_Robot_(film)" title="wikipedia:I, Robot (film)">I, Robot</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pirates_of_the_Caribbean:_Dead_Man%27s_Chest" title="wikipedia:Pirates of the Caribbean: Dead Man's Chest">Pirates of the Caribbean</a></i>. <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Heaven_(film)" title="wikipedia:Kingdom of Heaven (film)">Kingdom of Heaven</a></i> is one of many epics to use the <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massive_(software)" title="wikipedia:Massive (software)">MASSIVE</a> technology. In non-filmic terms, tourism for <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> is up, possibly due to its exposure in the trilogy, with the tourism industry in the country waking up to an audience's familiarity.
</p><p>In summary, The Lord of the Rings film trilogy is a myth on its own, and will perhaps be a major influence upon other films in the future.
</p>
