<p><i><b>Avatar: The Last Airbender</b></i> (<i><b>Avatar: The Legend of Aang</b></i> in some regions) is an American television series that aired for <a href="/wiki/List_of_Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_episodes" title="List of Avatar: The Last Airbender episodes">three seasons</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickelodeon_(TV_channel)" title="Nickelodeon (TV channel)">Nickelodeon</a> from 2005 to 2008. <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> is set in an Asiatic-like world<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NickMagInterview1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NickMagInterview1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> in which some people are able to manipulate the <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">classical elements</a> by use of <a href="/wiki/Psychokinesis" title="Psychokinesis">psychokinetic</a> variants of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial arts</a>, known as "bending". The show combined the styles of <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modern_animation_in_the_United_States" title="Modern animation in the United States">American cartoons</a>, and relied on the imagery of various East Asian, <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a>, Southeast Asian, South Asian, and New World societies. Therefore, the series regularly enters the conversation regarding its consideration as an anime work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The series follows the adventures of protagonist twelve-year-old <a href="/wiki/Aang" title="Aang">Aang</a> and his friends, who must bring peace and unity to the world by ending the Fire Lord's war against the other three nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DemographicandPlot_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DemographicandPlot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The pilot episode first aired on February 21, 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and the series concluded with a widely praised <a href="/wiki/Sozin%27s_Comet:_The_Final_Battle" title="Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle">two-hour episode</a> on July 19, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The show is obtainable from various sources, including DVD, the <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes Store</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zune_Marketplace" title="Zune Marketplace">Zune Marketplace</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xbox_Live_Marketplace" title="Xbox Live Marketplace">Xbox Live Marketplace</a>, the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Store" title="PlayStation Store">PlayStation Store</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Instant_Video" title="Amazon Instant Video">Amazon Instant Video</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicktoons_Network" title="Nicktoons Network">Nicktoons Network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout its run, <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> was universally acclaimed by audiences and critics alike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Praises went to the art direction, humor, cultural references, characters, and themes. It was also commercially successful, garnering 5.6 million viewers on its best-rated showing and receiving high ratings in the <a href="/wiki/Nicktoons" title="Nicktoons">Nicktoons</a> lineup, even outside of its 6- to 11-year-old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demographic" title="Demographic">demographic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DemographicandPlot_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-DemographicandPlot-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The series has been nominated for and won awards from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annie_Awards" title="Annie Awards">Annie Awards</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Genesis_Awards" title="Genesis Awards">Genesis Awards</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award" title="Primetime Emmy Award">Primetime Emmy Award</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Award" title="Peabody Award">Peabody Award</a>, among others. The first season's success prompted Nickelodeon to order second<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-secondseason_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-secondseason-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and third<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-as_S3airdate_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-as_S3airdate-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> seasons.</p>
<p>In other media, the series has spawned a critically panned, but financially successful, live-action film, titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Airbender" title="The Last Airbender">The Last Airbender</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/M._Night_Shyamalan" title="M. Night Shyamalan">M. Night Shyamalan</a>; scaled <a href="/wiki/Action_figure" title="Action figure">action figures</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_Trading_Card_Game" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender Trading Card Game">trading card game</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> three video games; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stuffed_animal" title="Stuffed animal">stuffed animals</a> distributed by <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Parks" title="Paramount Parks">Paramount Parks</a> and two <a href="/wiki/Lego" title="Lego">Lego</a> <a href="/wiki/Lego_Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender" title="Lego Avatar: The Last Airbender">sets</a>. An art book was also released in mid-2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> A sequel series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Korra" title="The Legend of Korra">The Legend of Korra</a></i>, aired from 2012 to 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Series_overview">Series overview</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_episodes" title="List of Avatar: The Last Airbender episodes">List of Avatar: The Last Airbender episodes</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Avatar_world_map.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="331" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/39/Avatar_world_map.jpg/260px-Avatar_world_map.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/39/Avatar_world_map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/39/Avatar_world_map.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>

<p><i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> takes place in a world home to humans and hybrid animals, adjacent to a parallel Spirit World. Human civilization is divided into four nations: the Water Tribes, the Earth Kingdom, the Fire Nation, and the Air Nomads. Each nation has a distinct society, wherein people known as "benders" have the ability to manipulate and control the <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">element</a> of their nation using the physical motions of <a href="/wiki/Martial_arts" title="Martial arts">martial arts</a>. The show's creators based each bending style on an existing <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial art</a>, leading to clear visual and physical differences in the techniques used by Waterbenders (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%27ai_chi_ch%27uan" title="T'ai chi ch'uan">T'ai chi ch'uan</a>), Earthbenders (<a href="/wiki/Hung_Ga" title="Hung Ga">Hung Ga</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kung_fu" title="Kung fu">kung fu</a>, or Southern Praying Mantis Style), Firebenders (<a href="/wiki/Northern_Shaolin_(martial_art)" title="Northern Shaolin (martial art)">Northern Shaolin</a> kung fu) and Airbenders (<a href="/wiki/Baguazhang" title="Baguazhang">Baguazhang</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avatar2.2Findex.jhtml_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-avatar2.2Findex.jhtml-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At any given time, only one person in the world is capable of bending all four elements: the Avatar, who serves as an international arbiter. When the Avatar dies, the Avatar spirit is <a href="/wiki/Reincarnation" title="Reincarnation">reincarnated</a> into the next one of the four nations in the Avatar Cycle: the Fire Nation, Air Nomads, Water Tribe, Earth Kingdom, in order. An Avatar incarnation is born male or female, and is required to master each bending art in seasonal order, starting with its native land's element. Additionally, the Avatar can enter a trance known as the Avatar State: normally a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_mechanism" title="Defense mechanism">defense mechanism</a> until placed, by mental discipline, under control, wherein the current Avatar briefly gains the knowledge and abilities of all past Avatars. If an Avatar were to be killed while in the Avatar State, the reincarnation cycle would end and the Avatar would never again be reborn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Through the various incarnations, the Avatar maintains a relative equality among the nations while serving as mediator between humans and spirits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avatar2.2Findex.jhtml_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-avatar2.2Findex.jhtml-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Spirit_World_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Spirit_World-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The origin of the Avatar cycle is revealed in the sequel series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Korra" title="The Legend of Korra">The Legend of Korra</a></i>, as the result of a young man named Wan combining his soul with the spirit Raava to imprison her opponent, Vaatu. The sequel series also revealed that bending was originally bestowed by the immense Lion Turtles, before these giant creatures renounced their roles as mankind's protectors upon the creation of the Avatar.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Backstory">Backstory</span></h3>
<p>The backstory of the series (told largely through <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Flashback" title="Flashback">flashback</a> sequences) extends backward to over 100 year before the time period in which the bulk of the series takes place, but these prelude events are revealed slowly in a fragmented, non-linear manner. More than a century before the opening events of the series, Fire Lord Sozin, ruler of the Fire Nation, planned a <a href="/wiki/World_war" title="World war">world war</a> to expand his nation's territory and influence, but was prevented him from carrying out his plans by the Avatar Roku. After Roku's death, the Avatar was reincarnated as an Airbender, named <a href="/wiki/Aang" title="Aang">Aang</a>, who learned of his Avatar status when twelve years old. Aang, frightened of his new responsibilities and of separation from his <a href="/wiki/Mentor" title="Mentor">mentor</a> Gyatso, fled his home on his flying bison, <a href="/wiki/Appa" title="Appa">Appa</a>. The two were forced into the ocean by a storm, with Aang entering the Avatar State and encasing them both in an <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">iceberg</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Suspended_animation" title="Suspended animation">suspended animation</a>, for one hundred years. Sozin, knowing the nature of the Avatar's reincarnation cycle, carried out <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> of the Air Nomads during the passing of a <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a> that increased the Firebenders' firepower, with the comet later renamed after him as a result, and spent his remaining days searching for the eponymous "Last Airbender". As the Fire Nation's Hundred Year War continued, Sozin was succeeded as Fire Lord by his son, Azulon, who in turn passed the title to his youngest son, Ozai, who becomes the primary <a href="/wiki/Antagonist" title="Antagonist">antagonist</a> of the series.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_One_.28Book_One:_Water.29">Season One (<i>Book One: Water</i>)</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_(season_1)" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender (season 1)">Avatar: The Last Airbender (season 1)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Katara_(Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender)" title="Katara (Avatar: The Last Airbender)">Katara</a>, a fourteen-year-old Southern Tribe Waterbender, and her older brother, <a href="/wiki/Sokka" title="Sokka">Sokka</a>, find and revive Aang and Appa. Aang learns of the war occurring in his absence, and the siblings join him to reach the Northern Water Tribe at the North Pole, so that he and Katara can learn Waterbending from a master. Aang's return attracts the attention of prince <a href="/wiki/Zuko" title="Zuko">Zuko</a>, the exiled son of Ozai, who pursues the three thereafter. Aang is also pursued by Zhao, a Fire Nation militant who intends to win Ozai's favor and rob Zuko of his redemption.</p>
<p><i>En route</i> to the North Pole, Aang meets the Kyoshi Warriors (established by an eponymous previous Avatar) and his childhood friend, King Bumi, attempts to learn Firebending from the deserter Firebending master, Jeong Jeong, and gains a traveling companion in a winged lemur he names <a href="/wiki/Momo_(Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender)" title="Momo (Avatar: The Last Airbender)">Momo</a>. Aang discovers the genocide of his people while visiting the Southern Air Temple. During the winter solstice, Aang meets his predecessor, Roku. At the Northern Water Tribe, Aang and Katara learn advanced Waterbending from its Master, Pakku, while Sokka falls in love with Princess Yue, the tribal chief's daughter. Zhao lays siege to the Northern Water Tribe, seizing the mortal forms of the Ocean and Moon Spirits, the source of waterbending, and thus causing a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipse</a>. When urged to desist by General Iroh (Ozai's elder brother) Zhao kills the moon spirit to render the Waterbenders powerless. Aang uses his Avatar State and combines with the Ocean Spirit to drive off the enemy's fleet while Princess Yue sacrifices her life to revive the Moon Spirit. When Ozai hears of Iroh's resistance to Zhao, he sends his daughter, Azula, to capture Iroh and Zuko.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_Two_.28Book_Two:_Earth.29">Season Two (<i>Book Two: Earth</i>)</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_(season_2)" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender (season 2)">Avatar: The Last Airbender (season 2)</a></div>
<p>After leaving the Northern Water Tribe, Aang continues to master Waterbending under Katara's tutelage as the group searches for an Earthbending teacher. Their search brings them to <a href="/wiki/Toph_Beifong" title="Toph Beifong">Toph Beifong</a>, a twelve-year-old blind tomboy and Earthbending prodigy who wants independence from her upper-class family. Pursued by Azula, Zuko and Iroh lead new lives in the Earth Kingdom, first as wanderers and later as refugees in the capital of Ba Sing Se. At a library guarded by the spirit Wan Shi Tong, Aang and his group learn that an imminent <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipse</a> could allow them to stop the Fire Nation before Sozin's Comet arrives. Their journey to Ba Sing Se to inform the Earth King of this is complicated when Appa is kidnapped. At the city, they find the Earth King Kuei and Ba Sing Se manipulated by Long Feng, the leader of the Dai Li <a href="/wiki/Secret_police" title="Secret police">secret police</a>.</p>
<p>After Aang's group finds Appa and exposes Long Feng, Toph is captured, but escapes by 'Metalbending', while Aang attempts to consciously access the Avatar state. The Dai Li join Azula to instigate a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a> of Ba Sing Se; and Zuko, having tried to ignore his past obsession, relapses when the Avatar appears to rescue Katara. As Aang tries to enter the Avatar state, Azula nearly kills him. With Ba Sing Se and the Earth Kingdom now under Fire Nation rule, the group escapes thanks to Iroh, who is imprisoned for betraying the Fire Nation, and Kuei goes into hiding.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_Three_.28Book_Three:_Fire.29">Season Three (<i>Book Three: Fire</i>)</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_(season_3)" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender (season 3)">Avatar: The Last Airbender (season 3)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote boilerplate seealso">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sozin%27s_Comet:_The_Final_Battle" title="Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle">Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle</a></div>
<p>Aang recovers from a long coma to find his friends and allies disguised as soldiers on a Fire Nation ship, preparing invasion of the Fire Nation. Despite receiving credit for the Avatar's apparent death, Zuko regrets his role therein. Although the invasion meets great success at first, Aang and his friends are unable to find Ozai and are forced to retreat with many of their allies captured. At the same time, having learned of his father's intention to destroy the Earth Kingdom at the time of Sozin's Comet, Zuko leaves the palace to teach Aang Firebending. As the comet approaches, Aang becomes reluctant to kill Ozai, and goes alone to consult his predecessors' ghosts. Katara and the others unsuccessfully search for Aang, and find the newly escaped Iroh, joined by the other members of a secret society called the Order of the White Lotus: Bumi, Master Pakku, Jeong-Jeong, and Master Piandao (a master swordsman who taught Sokka). Together, the Order of the White Lotus liberates Ba Sing Se. Sokka, Toph, and Kyoshi Warrior Suki hinder the Fire Nation's airships, while Zuko and Katara prevent Azula from becoming the new Fire Lord. As Sozin's Comet arrives, Aang confronts Ozai, but finds himself in a losing battle until Ozai mistakenly re-establishes Aang's connection to the Avatar State. Thus enabled, Aang strips Ozai of his fire-bending powers, instead of killing him. Soon after, Zuko is crowned the new Fire Lord and initiates an <a href="/wiki/Armistice" title="Armistice">armistice</a>. The series ends in a meeting of all the protagonists at Ba Sing Se, where Aang and Katara hug and kiss beneath the sunset.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">Characters</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_characters" title="List of Avatar: The Last Airbender characters">List of Avatar: The Last Airbender characters</a></div>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aang" title="Aang">Aang</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Zach_Tyler_Eisen" title="Zach Tyler Eisen">Zach Tyler Eisen</a>) is the twelve-year-old, fun-loving, Airbending protagonist of the series. Although averse to fighting, Aang is fiercely protective of his friends. He is the current incarnation of the planet's Avatar Spirit, and is therefore required to act as arbiter among the various people. According to DiMartino and Konietzko, the arrow-like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tattoos" title="Tattoos">tattoos</a> on his forehead and arms mark Aang as an Airbending master; Aang being the youngest Airbender in history to have earned them. His mentor, Monk Gyatso, was the greatest Airbender who had ever lived, according to Aang.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Katara_(Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender)" title="Katara (Avatar: The Last Airbender)">Katara</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Mae_Whitman" title="Mae Whitman">Mae Whitman</a>) is the fourteen-year-old last Waterbender of the Southern Water Tribe. She and her brother Sokka accompany Aang on his quest to defeat the Fire Lord. Eventually she becomes his Waterbending master. Katara is one of the only two surviving waterbenders of the Southern Water Tribe (the other being Hama) and one of only two Waterbenders able to manipulate and control human bodies by bending the water and liquids therein, known as bloodbending (an ability used twice in the series). Katara is usually kind-hearted and generous, but is deeply hurt, hostile, and often angered by the slightest <a href="/wiki/Betrayal" title="Betrayal">betrayal</a> or ingratitude.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Sokka" title="Sokka">Sokka</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Jack_DeSena" title="Jack DeSena">Jack DeSena</a>) is a fifteen-year-old non-bender warrior of the Southern Water Tribe. With his younger sister Katara, he accompanies Aang on his quest to defeat the Fire Lord. Sokka describes himself as "meat-loving" and "sarcastic",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bitter_Work_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bitter_Work-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and is often a source of <a href="/wiki/Comic_relief" title="Comic relief">comic relief</a>. Sokka's first love interest is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_major_secondary_characters" title="List of Avatar: The Last Airbender major secondary characters">Suki</a>, the leader of the Kyoshi Island Warriors. At the end of season one, he falls in love with Princess Yue of the Northern Water Tribe. Following her transformation into the moon spirit, he mourns for her, but returns to Suki. Unlike his companions, Sokka does not have any bending ability; his skill lies largely in mechanics and the sword. His chief weapons are a metallic <a href="/wiki/Boomerang" title="Boomerang">boomerang</a>, a blunt metal battle club, a Water Tribe machete, and later a black <a href="/wiki/Jian" title="Jian">jian</a> created from the metals of a <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorite</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Toph_Beifong" title="Toph Beifong">Toph Beifong</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Jessie_Flower" title="Jessie Flower">Jessie Flower</a>), a twelve-year-old blind female Earthbending <a href="/wiki/Child_prodigy" title="Child prodigy">prodigy</a>, and Aang's Earthbending instructor. Being blind, Toph "sees" by feeling vibrations in the ground through her feet using Earthbending. This ability also enables her to feel the pulses of one's heart, allowing her to tell if someone is lying. She is later shown developing a method of 'Metalbending' by detecting impurities in the metal. She was strictly taught only the basics of Earthbending by a tutor but learned its original form from badger-moles, the original Earthbenders. Toph also has a crush on Sokka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Serpent.27s_Pass_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Serpent.27s_Pass-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Zuko" title="Zuko">Zuko</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Dante_Basco" title="Dante Basco">Dante Basco</a>) is the sixteen-year-old Firebender, exiled prince of the Fire Nation. He is the original <a href="/wiki/Antagonist" title="Antagonist">antagonist</a> of the first season, an <a href="/wiki/Antihero" title="Antihero">antihero</a> in the second season, and a protagonist in third season. He is determined and strong-willed, and rarely shows compassion until the third season. During season two, Zuko struggles to deal with his anger, self-pity, and complex familial relationships, as well as the choice between good and evil. He takes on the vigilante identity of the "Blue Spirit" throughout season one and season two. In season three, he defects from the Fire Nation to become Aang's firebending master. At the end of the series, he is crowned ruler of the Fire Nation.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Appa" title="Appa">Appa</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Dee_Bradley_Baker" title="Dee Bradley Baker">Dee Bradley Baker</a>) is Aang's flying <a href="/wiki/Bison" title="Bison">bison</a>, who serves as the protagonists' <a href="/wiki/Mode_of_transport" title="Mode of transport">mode of transport</a> around the world. He remains in suspended animation with Aang for one hundred years and shares a strong bond with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Storm_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Storm-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> He possesses the ability to fly and can use his tail to create powerful gusts of air. According to Aang, the flying bison were the first Airbenders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Southern_Air_Temple_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Southern_Air_Temple-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Momo_(Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender)" title="Momo (Avatar: The Last Airbender)">Momo</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Dee_Bradley_Baker" title="Dee Bradley Baker">Dee Bradley Baker</a>) is an intelligent and curious winged <a href="/wiki/Lemur" title="Lemur">lemur</a> discovered by Aang at the Southern Air Temple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Southern_Air_Temple_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Southern_Air_Temple-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> He often picks fights with other winged and smaller creatures and with Appa over food. He can understand Aang's speech, but less so of others'. In "Tales of Ba Sing Se", Momo's name was written as 模模 (mó mó). Momo means "peach" in Japanese (he grabbed a peach out of Sokka's hand just as Aang was about to name him), but it is written 桃 (táo).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Iroh" title="Iroh">Iroh</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Mako_Iwamatsu" title="Mako Iwamatsu">Mako Iwamatsu</a>/<a href="/wiki/Greg_Baldwin" title="Greg Baldwin">Greg Baldwin</a>) is a former general of the Fire Nation, known as the Dragon of the West, and Prince Zuko's paternal uncle and <a href="/wiki/Mentor" title="Mentor">mentor</a>. Iroh was the heir to the Fire Nation throne until Ozai usurped the throne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> On the surface, Iroh is a cheerful, kind, optimistic, eccentric old man, but remains a powerful warrior and a devoted surrogate parent to Zuko. Iroh is a Grand Master of the Order of the White Lotus, a secret society of men from all nations. Unlike most Firebenders, Iroh does not use <a href="/wiki/Anger" title="Anger">anger</a> as the source of his strength, relying instead on Firebending secrets learned from the <a href="/wiki/Dragon" title="Dragon">Dragons</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Azula" title="Azula">Azula</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Grey_DeLisle" title="Grey DeLisle">Grey DeLisle</a>) is the princess of the Fire Nation, Zuko's younger sister and one of the primary <a href="/wiki/Antagonist" title="Antagonist">antagonists</a> of the series. Although she is much older in appearance, it is confirmed by the official website that she is intended to be 14, making her two years younger than her brother. Azula is a Firebending <a href="/wiki/Child_prodigy" title="Child prodigy">prodigy</a> and is one of the few living Firebenders capable of casting <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>. She is a cunning master at manipulation and a great leader, using fear to control her subjects and friends alike, reserving loyalty solely for her father. She is meticulous and calculating, but also a perfectionist. At the end of season three, she loses her sanity altogether as a result of a fragile mental state broken by the betrayal of her friends, the loss of her father, and her elevation to a position of power. She is defeated by Zuko and Katara in a duel.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_characters#Kyoshi" title="List of Avatar: The Last Airbender characters">Suki</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Jennie_Kwan" title="Jennie Kwan">Jennie Kwan</a>) is the leader of the young and exclusively female Kyoshi Warriors, a sect established by Avatar Kyoshi. She is an exceptionally skilled fighter and staunch ally of the protagonists. She was imprisoned by the Fire Nation after the Kyoshi Warriors were defeated by Azula, but she was ultimately released by Sokka and Zuko. She remained with the protagonists thereafter and fought with Toph and Sokka to disable the Fire Nation's air force. She was Sokka's love interest immediately following the end of the War.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_major_secondary_characters" title="List of Avatar: The Last Airbender major secondary characters">Mai</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Cricket_Leigh" title="Cricket Leigh">Cricket Leigh</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup>) is the best friend of Ty Lee. Mai herself lacks bending, but she is agile, swift, and skilled in throwing darts and knives. She assists Azula throughout most of her role, but she ultimately decides to abandon Azula. She is also Zuko's girlfriend.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ty_Lee" title="Ty Lee">Ty Lee</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Olivia_Hack" title="Olivia Hack">Olivia Hack</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TV_Guide_Cast_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-TV_Guide_Cast-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup>) is an acrobat who fights alongside Azula against the protagonists, notable for her appearance of vivacity, innocence, and youth, and for her ability to disable enemies by temporarily obstructing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ch%27i" title="Ch'i">chi</a> in their limbs. Having abandoned Azula, she joins the Kyoshi Warriors, whom she had earlier impersonated.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_Lord_Ozai" title="Fire Lord Ozai">Fire Lord Ozai</a></b> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Hamill" title="Mark Hamill">Mark Hamill</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TV_Guide_Cast_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-TV_Guide_Cast-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup>) ruler of the Fire Nation, is the father of <a href="/wiki/Zuko" title="Zuko">Zuko</a> and <a href="/wiki/Azula" title="Azula">Azula</a>, younger brother of <a href="/wiki/Iroh" title="Iroh">Iroh</a>, and the supreme antagonist of the series. Ozai is depicted as a cruel and merciless leader, and is described as "the worst father in the history of fathers" by his son Zuko,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> of whom he reportedly thought banishment too light a punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> He favors Azula over Zuko, because he sees her as a firebending prodigy and sees his own beliefs embodied in her. In "<a href="/wiki/Sozin%27s_Comet:_The_Final_Battle" title="Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle">Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle</a>", he renames himself the Phoenix King, ruler of the world, and appoints Azula to watch over the Fire Nation alone; but is defeated by Aang, who removes Ozai's ability to firebend.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mike_Dante_DiMartino.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="470" data-file-width="510" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Mike_Dante_DiMartino.JPG/220px-Mike_Dante_DiMartino.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Mike_Dante_DiMartino.JPG/330px-Mike_Dante_DiMartino.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Mike_Dante_DiMartino.JPG/440px-Mike_Dante_DiMartino.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
<p><i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> was co-created and produced by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dante_DiMartino" title="Michael Dante DiMartino">Michael Dante DiMartino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Konietzko" title="Bryan Konietzko">Bryan Konietzko</a> at Nickelodeon Animation Studios in <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank, California</a>. Animation work was mostly done by three animation studios in South Korea: JM Animation, <a href="/wiki/DR_Movie" title="DR Movie">DR Movie</a>, and MOI Animation. According to Bryan Konietzko, the program was conceived in the spring of 2001 when he took an old sketch of a balding, middle-aged man and re-imagined the character as a child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NickMagInterview1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NickMagInterview1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Konietzko drew the character herding bison in the sky, and showed the sketch to Mike DiMartino. At the time, DiMartino was studying a documentary about explorers trapped in the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a>. Konietzko described their early development of the concept:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">We thought, "There's an air guy along with these water people trapped in a snowy wasteland... and maybe some fire people are pressing down on them..."</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>The co-creators successfully pitched the idea to Nickelodeon vice president and executive producer Eric Coleman just two weeks later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The series was first revealed to the public in a teaser reel at <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Comic-Con_International" title="San Diego Comic-Con International">Comic-Con</a> 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> and aired February 21, 2005. In the United States, the first two episodes of the series were shown together in a one-hour premiere event. A second twenty-episode season ran from March 17, 2006, through December 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-secondseason_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-secondseason-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> A third and final season, beginning September 21, 2007, featured twenty-one episodes rather than the usual twenty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-as_S3airdate_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-as_S3airdate-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The final four episodes were packaged as a two-hour movie.</p>
<p><i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> is notable for borrowing extensively from <a href="/wiki/History_of_Eastern_art" title="History of Eastern art">East Asian art</a> and mythology to create its <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>. The series' character designs are heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Chinese_art" title="Chinese art">Chinese art</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">Yoga</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Traditional <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calligraphy" title="Chinese calligraphy">Chinese calligraphy</a> styles represent nearly all the writing in the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calligraphy.html_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-calligraphy.html-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> For each instance of calligraphy, an appropriate style is used, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Seal_script" title="Seal script">seal script</a> (more archaic) to <a href="/wiki/Clerical_script" title="Clerical script">clerical script</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calligraphy.html_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-calligraphy.html-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The show employed a cultural consultant, Edwin Zane, and calligrapher Siu-Leung Lee as consultants for the series' cultural influences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> The choreographed martial art bending moves were affected by Asian cinema.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NickMagInterview1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-NickMagInterview1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In an interview, Bryan revealed that, "Mike and I were really interested in other epic 'Legends &amp; Lore' properties, like <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">Lord of the Rings</a></i>, but we knew that we wanted to take a different approach to that type of genre. Our love for Japanese anime, Hong Kong action and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kung_fu" title="Kung fu">kung fu</a> cinema, yoga, and Eastern philosophies led us to the initial inspiration for <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The show's character designs are influenced by <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a>; but the show is not considered an example of such.</p>
<p>All music and sound used in the series was done by <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Zuckerman" title="Jeremy Zuckerman">Jeremy Zuckerman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Wynn" title="Benjamin Wynn">Benjamin Wynn</a>, who formed The Track Team. They experimented with use of a wide range of different instruments, such as the <a href="/wiki/Guzheng" title="Guzheng">guzheng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pipa" title="Pipa">pipa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Duduk" title="Duduk">duduk</a>, to compose background music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Avatar" title="Avatar">Avatar</a>" comes from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> (<span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">अवतार</span>), wherein means "descent"; its roots are <i>ava</i>, "down," and <i>tri</i>, "to pass". In the Hindu scriptures, <i>avatar</i> signifies the mortal incarnation of a god (usually <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese characters</a> apparent at the top of the show's title card mean "the divine medium who has descended upon the mortal world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calligraphy.html_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-calligraphy.html-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> According to the plot, <a href="/wiki/Aang" title="Aang">Aang</a> unknowingly revealed he was the Avatar when by choosing four toys out of thousands, each of which were the childhood toys of previous Avatars. In <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a>, there is a similar test for reincarnations of a <a href="/wiki/Tulku" title="Tulku">Tulku</a> <a href="/wiki/Lama" title="Lama">Lama</a>. In <i>Magic and Mystery in Tibet</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandra_David-Neel" title="Alexandra David-Neel">Alexandra David-Neel</a> writes that "a number of objects such as <a href="/wiki/Rosary" title="Rosary">rosaries</a>, ritualistic implements, books, tea-cups, etc., are placed together, and the child must pick out those which belonged to the late <i>tulku</i>, thus showing that he recognizes the things which were <i>theirs</i> in their previous life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Each successor is expected to show signs of continuity with the previous Avatar, such as being born within a week of the death.</p>
<p><i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> draws on the four <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">classical elements</a> for its bending arts: <a href="/wiki/Water_(classical_element)" title="Water (classical element)">Water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth_(classical_element)" title="Earth (classical element)">Earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fire_(classical_element)" title="Fire (classical element)">Fire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Air_(classical_element)" title="Air (classical element)">Air</a>. Although each has its own variation, most ancient philosophies incorporate these four elements: examples include the classical <a href="/wiki/Tattva" title="Tattva">Hindu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mah%C4%81bh%C5%ABta" title="Mahābhūta">Buddhist</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_elements#Classical_elements_in_Greece" title="Classical elements">Greek</a> elemental traditions. In the show's opening, each element is accompanied by two Chinese characters: an ancient Chinese <a href="/wiki/Seal_script" title="Seal script">seal script</a> character on the left representing the element being shown and a modern Chinese character on the right describing some feature of the element. The character 水 (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i>shuǐ</i>), which stands for <a href="/wiki/Water_(classical_element)" title="Water (classical element)">water</a>, is shown with 善 (pinyin: <i>shàn</i>), which means good and benevolent. The character 土 (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i>tǔ</i>), which stands for <a href="/wiki/Earth_(classical_element)" title="Earth (classical element)">earth</a>, is shown with 強 (pinyin: <i>qiáng</i>), which means strong and powerful. The character 火 (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i>huǒ</i>), which stands for <a href="/wiki/Fire_(classical_element)" title="Fire (classical element)">fire</a>, is shown with 烈 (pinyin: <i>liè</i>), which means intense and fierce. Finally, the character 氣 (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i>qì</i>), which stands for <a href="/wiki/Air_(classical_element)" title="Air (classical element)">air</a>, is shown with 和 (pinyin: <i>hé</i>), which means peace/peaceful and harmony/harmonious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cally_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-cally-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the use of four classical elements in the series, the fighting styles associated with each element are derived from different styles of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial arts</a>, for which the film-makers employed Sifu Kisu of the Harmonious Fist Chinese Athletic Association as a consultant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Each fighting style was chosen to represent the element it projected. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%27ai_chi_ch%27uan" title="T'ai chi ch'uan">T'ai chi</a> was used for "Waterbending" in the series, which focuses on alignment, body structure, breath, and visualization. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hung_Gar" title="Hung Gar">Hung Gar</a> was used for "Earthbending" in the series, and was chosen for its firmly rooted stances and powerful strikes to present the solid nature of earth. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Shaolin" title="Northern Shaolin">Northern Shaolin</a>, which uses strong arm and leg movements was used to represent "Firebending". <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ba_Gua_Zhang" title="Ba Gua Zhang">Ba Gua</a>, which uses dynamic circular movements and quick directional changes, was used for "Airbending".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avatar2.2Findex.jhtml_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-avatar2.2Findex.jhtml-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The only exception to these styles is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toph" title="Toph">Toph</a>, who can be seen practicing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Praying_Mantis_(martial_art)#Chu_Gar" title="Southern Praying Mantis (martial art)">Chu Gar Southern Praying Mantis</a> style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-con_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-con-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ratings">Ratings</span></h3>
<p>When the series debuted, it was rated the best animated television series in its <a href="/wiki/Demographics" title="Demographics">demographic</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animation_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-animation-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> new episodes averaged 3.1 million viewers each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animation_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-animation-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Many people regard it as a major animated series, and it has gained somewhat of a cult following. A one-hour special showing of "The Secret of the Fire Nation" which aired on September 15, 2006, consisting of "The Serpent's Pass" and "The Drill", gathered an audience of 5.1 million viewers. According to the <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Media_Research" title="Nielsen Media Research">Nielsen Media Research</a>, the special was the best performing cable television show airing in that week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2007, <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> was syndicated to more than 105 countries worldwide, and was one of Nickelodeon's top rated programs. The series was ranked first on Nickelodeon in Germany, Indonesia, Malaysia, The Netherlands, Belgium, and Colombia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The four-part series finale, <i><a href="/wiki/Sozin%27s_Comet:_The_Final_Battle" title="Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle">Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle</a></i>, received the highest ratings of the series. Its premiere averaged 5.6 million viewers, 95% more viewers than Nickelodeon had received in mid-July 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> During the week of July 14, it ranked as the most-viewed program for the under-14 demographic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle</i> also appeared on <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a>' top ten list of best-selling television episodes during that same week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle</i> '​s popularity affected online media as well; "Rise of the Phoenix King", a <a href="/wiki/Nick.com" title="Nick.com">Nick.com</a> online game based on <i>Sozin's Comet: The Final Battle</i>, generated almost 815,000 game plays within three days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> listed the complete series as 35th in its list of Top 100 Animated TV Shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_media">Other media</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_and_comic_books">Art and comic books</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_(comics_series)" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender (comics series)">Avatar: The Last Airbender (comics series)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dark_Horse_Comics" title="Dark Horse Comics">Dark Horse Comics</a> released an art book titled <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender – The Art of the Animated Series</i>, on June 2, 2010, which contains 184 pages of the original art and creation behind the <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> animated series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Several comic book short stories were published in <a href="/wiki/Nickelodeon_Magazine" title="Nickelodeon Magazine">Nickelodeon Magazine</a>, and on June 15, 2011, Dark Horse released a collection of these and new comics in a single volume, <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender – The Lost Adventures</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darkhorse1_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-darkhorse1-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Dark Horse also publishes a graphic novel series by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gene_Yang" title="Gene Yang">Gene Yang</a> that continues Aang's story after the Hundred Years' War. <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_%E2%80%93_The_Promise" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender – The Promise">Avatar: The Last Airbender – The Promise</a></i>, published in three volumes in 2012, is about the fate of the Fire Nation colonies that eventually become <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Korra" title="The Legend of Korra">The Legend of Korra</a></i> '​s United Republic. A second set of three comic books, <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_%E2%80%93_The_Search" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender – The Search">Avatar: The Last Airbender – The Search</a></i>, focuses on Zuko and Azula and the fate of their mother, Ursa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBR_25_June_2012_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBR_25_June_2012-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> The third set, <a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_%E2%80%93_The_Rift" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender – The Rift"><i>Avatar: The Last Airbender - The Rift</i></a>, shifts the focus to Aang and the process of creating Republic City, as well as Toph's relationship with her family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Avatar: The Last Airbender'</i>s success has led to some promotional advertising with third-party companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Burger_King" title="Burger King">Burger King</a> and <a href="/wiki/Upper_Deck_Company" title="Upper Deck Company">Upper Deck Entertainment</a>. <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i>-themed <a href="/wiki/Roller_coaster" title="Roller coaster">roller coasters</a> at <a href="/wiki/Nickelodeon_Universe" title="Nickelodeon Universe">Nickelodeon Universe</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mall_of_America" title="Mall of America">Mall of America</a> and one formerly at <a href="/wiki/Kings_Island" title="Kings Island">Kings Island</a> also appeared. During the show's runtime, Nickelodeon published two special issues of <i><a href="/wiki/Nickelodeon_Magazine" title="Nickelodeon Magazine">Nick Mag Presents</a></i> dedicated entirely to the show. Various members of the <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> staff and cast appeared at the 2006 San Diego Comic-Con International convention, while Michael Dante DiMartino and Bryan Konietzko appeared with Martial Arts Consultant Sifu Kisu at the Pacific Media Expo on October 28, 2006. <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> also has its own line of T-shirts, <a href="/wiki/Lego" title="Lego">LEGO</a> <a href="/wiki/Lego_Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender" title="Lego Avatar: The Last Airbender">playsets</a>, toys, a trading card game,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cine-manga" title="Cine-manga">cine-manga</a>, and three video games, as well as an <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_game" title="Massively multiplayer online game">MMO</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fisher-Price" title="Fisher-Price">Fisher-Price</a>-produced action figure toy line generated some controversy with its exclusion of any female characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> Mattel came to release information stating that they have taken account of Katara's increased role within the program, and that she would be included in the figure assortment for a mid-2007 release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> The figure ultimately went unreleased, however, as the entire line was canceled before she could be produced.</p>
<p>Nickelodeon executives have since released optimistic plans for upcoming marketing strategies in regards to <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i>. Nickelodeon President <a href="/wiki/Cyma_Zarghami" title="Cyma Zarghami">Cyma Zarghami</a> openly stated her belief that the franchise "could become their <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a></i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-becomeharrypotter_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-becomeharrypotter-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span></h3>
<p>A video game trilogy about <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> has been created. <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_(video_game)" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender (video game)">Avatar: The Last Airbender</a></i>, the video game, was released on October 10, 2006. <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_%E2%80%93_The_Burning_Earth" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender – The Burning Earth">Avatar: The Last Airbender – The Burning Earth</a></i> was released on October 16, 2007. <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar:_The_Last_Airbender_%E2%80%93_Into_the_Inferno" title="Avatar: The Last Airbender – Into the Inferno">Avatar: The Last Airbender – Into the Inferno</a></i> was released on October 13, 2008. The three games were loosely based on seasons one, two and three, respectively. Players can select characters and complete quests to gain experience and advance the storyline. The games were met with mixed to positive reviews (the ratings improved with each game), and the games did extremely well commercially; for example, <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i> was <a href="/wiki/THQ" title="THQ">THQ</a>'s top selling Nickelodeon game in 2006 and even reached <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> CEA's "Greatest Hits" status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Avatar: Legends of the Arena</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_role-playing_game" title="Massively multiplayer online role-playing game">massively multiplayer online role-playing game</a> (MMORPG) for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, was launched on September 25, 2008, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickelodeon_(TV_Channel)" title="Nickelodeon (TV Channel)">Nickelodeon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avatar_spirit_media_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Avatar_spirit_media-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> Each user is able to create his or her own character, choose a nation, and interact with others across the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avatar_spirit_media_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-Avatar_spirit_media-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> Since the 2012 holiday season, the game's servers have been closed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Airbender" title="The Last Airbender">The Last Airbender</a></div>
<p>The first season of the show became the basis for the 2010 live-action film <i>The Last Airbender</i>, written and directed by <a href="/wiki/M._Night_Shyamalan" title="M. Night Shyamalan">M. Night Shyamalan</a>. It is claimed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to be the first movie of a planned trilogy from each of the television 3 seasons. But the film's reception was overwhelmingly negative from both critics and fans alike, earning the film a 6% rating on <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> and five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Raspberry_Award" title="Golden Raspberry Award">Razzies</a> in <a href="/wiki/31st_Golden_Raspberry_Awards" title="31st Golden Raspberry Awards">2011</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Golden_Raspberry_Award_for_Worst_Picture" title="Golden Raspberry Award for Worst Picture">Worst Picture</a>. The film originally shared the title of the television series, but it was changed to <i>The Last Airbender</i> because producers feared it would be confused with the <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i>. The movie received harsh reviews by the critics and the fans of the show. The film version stars <a href="/wiki/Noah_Ringer" title="Noah Ringer">Noah Ringer</a> as Aang, <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Peltz" title="Nicola Peltz">Nicola Peltz</a> as Katara, <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Rathbone" title="Jackson Rathbone">Jackson Rathbone</a> as Sokka, <a href="/wiki/Dev_Patel" title="Dev Patel">Dev Patel</a> as Zuko, and <a href="/wiki/Shaun_Toub" title="Shaun Toub">Shaun Toub</a> as Iroh.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequel_.28The_Legend_of_Korra.29">Sequel (<i>The Legend of Korra</i>)</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Korra" title="The Legend of Korra">The Legend of Korra</a></div>
<p><i>The Legend of Korra</i>, a sequel series to <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i>, was announced at the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Comic-Con_International" title="San Diego Comic-Con International">Comic-Con</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> on July 22, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-date_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-date-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> It is written and produced by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dante_DiMartino" title="Michael Dante DiMartino">Michael Dante DiMartino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Konietzko" title="Bryan Konietzko">Bryan Konietzko</a>, the creators and producers of the original series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToonZone_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-ToonZone-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> Initially titled <i>Avatar: Legend of Korra</i>, then <i>The Last Airbender: Legend of Korra</i>, it takes place seventy years after the end of <i>Avatar: The Last Airbender</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> The first season of 12 episodes aired from April to June 2012, and a second season of 14 episodes aired from September 2013 to November 2013; a third season of 13 episodes aired from June 2014 to August 2014; and a fourth and final season is in post production and scheduled to be released in October 2014. Nickelodeon also awarded an additional 26 episodes to <i>The Legend of Korra</i>, re-announced by co-creator Konietzko at the 2012 Comic-Con in San Diego.</p>
<p>The series' protagonist is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korra_(The_Legend_of_Korra)" title="Korra (The Legend of Korra)">Korra</a>, a 17-year-old girl from the Southern Water Tribe and the reincarnation of the Avatar after Aang's death. The character was partly inspired by Avatar Kyoshi of the original series, whom the creators say was popular among fans. To avoid repetition of Aang's adventures during the original series, the creators wanted to root the show in one place: Republic City. A concept drawing of the city, released with the announcement of the series, shows the city's design as inspired by <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> with a steampunk vibe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> In the show's first season, Korra has to learn airbending from Tenzin, the youngest son of Aang and Katara, and contend with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amon_(The_Legend_of_Korra)" title="Amon (The Legend of Korra)">Amon</a>'s anti-bender revolution taking place in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interview_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-interview-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> The show's second season also provides the origins of the Avatar Cycle.</p>
