<p><i><b>And Then There Were None</b></i> is a 1943 play by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crime_writer" title="Crime writer">crime writer</a> <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a>. The play, like the 1939 <a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None" title="And Then There Were None">book on which it is based</a>, was originally titled and performed in the UK as <i><b>Ten Little Niggers</b></i>. It was also performed under the name <i><b>Ten Little Indians</b></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None_(play)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Christie had been pleased with the book, stating in her autobiography "I wrote the book after a tremendous amount of planning, and I was pleased with what I made of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The book was very well received upon publication and soon after Christie received a request from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reginald_Simpson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reginald Simpson (page does not exist)">Reginald Simpson</a> to be allowed to dramatise it. Christie refused as she relished the challenge herself<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> although she was intermittently some two years in carrying out the task. She knew the ending would have to be changed as all of the characters die in the book and therefore "I must make two of the characters innocent, to be reunited at the end and come safe out of the ordeal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The original <a href="/wiki/Nursery_rhyme" title="Nursery rhyme">nursery rhyme</a> on which the book was based had an alternative ending of...</p>
<dl>
<dd>"He got married and then there were none"</dd>
</dl>
<p>...which allowed Christie to portray a different conclusion on stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After the play had been written, most people she discussed it with considered it impossible to produce. She received some encouragement from Charles Cochrane but he was unable to find financial backers. Finally, Bertie Mayer who had produced the 1928 play <i><a href="/wiki/Alibi_(play)" title="Alibi (play)">Alibi</a></i> agreed to stage it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-An_Autobiography._Page_472_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-An_Autobiography._Page_472-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After a try-out at the <a href="/wiki/New_Wimbledon_Theatre" title="New Wimbledon Theatre">Wimbledon Theatre</a> starting on 20 September 1943, the play opened in the West End at the <a href="/wiki/St_James%27s_Theatre" title="St James's Theatre">St James's Theatre</a> on 17 November. It gained good reviews and ran for 260 performances until 24 February 1944 when the theatre was bombed. It then transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Theatre" title="Cambridge Theatre">Cambridge Theatre</a> opening on 29 February and running at that venue until 6 May. It then transferred back to the restored St James' on 9 May and finally closed on 1 July.</p>
<p>Although she didn't feel it to be her best play, Christie did declare it was her best piece of "craftsmanship". She also considered it to be the play which formally started her career as a playwright, despite the success of <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Coffee_(play)" title="Black Coffee (play)">Black Coffee</a></i> in 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-An_Autobiography._Page_472_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-An_Autobiography._Page_472-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scenes">Scenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None_(play)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scene of the play is the living-room of the house on Indian Island (Note: Nigger Island in the 1943 UK production), off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>. The time – the present.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_of_London_production">Reception of London production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None_(play)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception of London production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ivor_Brown" title="Ivor Brown">Ivor Brown</a> reviewed the play in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a>'</i>s issue of 21 November 1943 when he said, "Miss Agatha Christie does not stint things. Like Hotspur, who could kill six dozen Scots at breakfast, complain of his quiet life, and then ask for work, she is not one to be concerned about a mere singleton corpse. But she can add quality to quantity in her domestic morgue. In <i>Ten Little Niggers</i> she shows an intense ingenuity in adapting that very lethal rhyme (so oddly deemed a nursery matter) to modern conditions." Mr. Brown concluded that Henrietta Watson's portrayal of Emily Brent was, "the most authentic member of a house party with 'no future in it.' as the airmen say. That gently lugubrious phrase certainly does not hold of the play."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadway_production">Broadway production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None_(play)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Broadway production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A production in New York opened at the <a href="/wiki/Broadhurst_Theatre" title="Broadhurst Theatre">Broadhurst Theatre</a> under the title <i><b>Ten Little Indians</b></i> on 27 June 1944. On 6 January 1945, it transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Schoenfeld_Theatre" title="Gerald Schoenfeld Theatre">Plymouth Theatre</a> where it ran from 9 January until 30 June 1945. The total run on Broadway was 426 performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_and_further_adaptations">Publication and further adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None_(play)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Publication and further adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The play was first published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_French_Ltd" title="Samuel French Ltd">Samuel French Ltd</a> as a paperback in 1944. It was first published in hardback in <i>The Mousetrap and Other Plays</i> by <a href="/wiki/Dodd,_Mead_and_Company" title="Dodd, Mead and Company">Dodd, Mead and Company</a> in 1978 (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0396076319">ISBN 0-39-607631-9</a>) and in the UK by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harper_Collins" title="Harper Collins">Harper Collins</a> in 1993 (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/000224344X">ISBN 0-00-224344-X</a>).</p>
<p>At some point after the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, a survivor of <a href="/wiki/Buchenwald_concentration_camp" title="Buchenwald concentration camp">Buchenwald concentration camp</a> contacted Christie and told her that the inmates had staged their own production there, undoubtedly writing their own script as they would not have had access to the Christie version. Christie was told that they found that it had "sustained them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2007, Lakota East High School in <a href="/wiki/West_Chester_Township,_Butler_County,_Ohio" title="West Chester Township, Butler County, Ohio">West Chester, OH</a>, was set to perform the play but plans were cancelled after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAACP" title="NAACP">NAACP</a> protested about the production because of the original title of the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Lakota East High School officials subsequently revised their plans and decided to perform the play on 29 November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005_production">2005 production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None_(play)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2005 production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 14 October 2005 a new version of play, written by <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Elyot" title="Kevin Elyot">Kevin Elyot</a> and directed by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Pimlott" title="Steven Pimlott">Steven Pimlott</a> opened at the <a href="/wiki/Gielgud_Theatre" title="Gielgud Theatre">Gielgud Theatre</a> in London. For this version, Elyot returned to the book version of story and restored the original ending where both Vera and Lombard die and Wargrave commits suicide. The version of the rhyme and island name used was "Ten Little Soldiers" and "Soldier Island" as per current printings of the novel. Despite very positive reviews, the play closed on 14 January 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
