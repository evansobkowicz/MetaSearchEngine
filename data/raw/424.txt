<p><b>Simba</b> (from the <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Swahili" title="Swahili">Swahili</a> word for <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a>) is a fictional character who appears in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_(franchise)" title="The Lion King (franchise)">The Lion King</a></i> franchise. Introduced in <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Animation_Studios" title="Walt Disney Animation Studios">Disney Animation's</a> 32nd animated feature film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King" title="The Lion King">The Lion King</a></i> (1994), the character subsequently appears in its sequels <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride" title="The Lion King II: Simba's Pride">The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</a></i> (1998) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_1%C2%BD" title="The Lion King 1½">The Lion King 1½</a></i> (2004).</p>
<p>Simba was created by screenwriters <a href="/wiki/Irene_Mecchi" title="Irene Mecchi">Irene Mecchi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Roberts_(writer)" title="Jonathan Roberts (writer)">Jonathan Roberts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linda_Woolverton" title="Linda Woolverton">Linda Woolverton</a>. While <a href="/wiki/Mark_Henn" title="Mark Henn">Mark Henn</a> served as Simba's supervising animator as a cub, <a href="/wiki/Ruben_A._Aquino" title="Ruben A. Aquino">Ruben A. Aquino</a> animated the character as he appears as an adult.</p>
<p>Although considered an original character, Simba was inspired by the character <a href="/wiki/Bambi_(character)" title="Bambi (character)">Bambi</a> from Disney's <i><a href="/wiki/Bambi" title="Bambi">Bambi</a></i> (1942), as well as the stories of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_(son_of_Jacob)" title="Joseph (son of Jacob)">Joseph</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>. Additionally, several similarities have been drawn between Simba and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Hamlet" title="Prince Hamlet">Prince Hamlet</a> from <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">tragedy of the same name</a>. In 1997, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King" title="The Lion King">The Lion King</a></i> was adapted into <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_(musical)" title="The Lion King (musical)">Broadway musical</a>, with actors <a href="/wiki/Scott_Irby-Ranniar" title="Scott Irby-Ranniar">Scott Irby-Ranniar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jason_Raize" title="Jason Raize">Jason Raize</a> originating the roles of the cub and adult Simbas, respectively. Simba has received a mixed reception from film and entertainment critics, who tended to compliment his design and Broderick's portrayal, but felt that the character was unoriginal and uninteresting.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conception">Conception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:300px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Simba was framed for this terrible murder, and on the one hand you can say it wasn’t his fault, but he wasn’t a stand-up guy, so a little bit of the theme of the movie is you have to stand up for yourself if you know you’re right. So that idea of redemption, that idea of that day in your life that you have to take responsibility for yourself, that you’re no longer a child, you’re an adult, all those themes resonated with us when we were making the movie and, thankfully, the audience appreciated them, as well.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;">— Producer <a href="/wiki/Don_Hahn" title="Don Hahn">Don Hahn</a> on Simba's role in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>The idea for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King" title="The Lion King">The Lion King</a></i> originated from Disney CEO <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Katzenberg" title="Jeffrey Katzenberg">Jeffrey Katzenberg</a> in 1989<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and was originally conceived under the title <i>King of the Jungle</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The story, which has been compared to <i><a href="/wiki/Bambi" title="Bambi">Bambi</a></i> (1942),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> was jokingly referred to as "<i>Bambi</i> in Africa" because of the similarities between the two films and their respective main characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Co-director <a href="/wiki/Rob_Minkoff" title="Rob Minkoff">Rob Minkoff</a> said that both films are "more true-life adventure than mythical epic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Though considered an original<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coming-of-age_film" title="Coming-of-age film">coming-of-age</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> story that follows the life of Simba as he grows up and "tak[es] on the responsibility of adulthood," co-directors <a href="/wiki/Roger_Allers" title="Roger Allers">Roger Allers</a> and Minkoff drew inspiration from other sources. In particular, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblical" title="Biblical">biblical</a> figures <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_(son_of_Jacob)" title="Joseph (son of Jacob)">Joseph</a> served as creative inspiration for the character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:8-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Producer <a href="/wiki/Don_Hahn" title="Don Hahn">Don Hahn</a> said that, like them, Simba is "born into royalty, is then exiled, and has to return to claim [his] kingdom."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:9-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Several film and entertainment critics have noted similarities and parallels between the stories of <i>The Lion King</i> and <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s tragedy <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamlet_(play)" title="Hamlet (play)">Hamlet</a></i>, and their <a href="/wiki/Prince_Hamlet" title="Prince Hamlet">protagonists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:10-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:11_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:11-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:13-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Allers said that these similarities were not initially intentional and came as a surprise to the filmmakers themselves; they noticed the similarities only after the story was established and they eventually decided to pursue it. According to Hahn, "When we first pitched the revised outline of the movie ... someone in the room announced that its themes and relationships were similar to <i>Hamlet</i>. Everyone responded favorably to the idea that we were doing something Shakespearean, so we continued to look for ways to model our film on that all-time classic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:9-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Screenwriter <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Roberts_(writer)" title="Jonathan Roberts (writer)">Jonathan Roberts</a> said that, in a <a href="/wiki/Musical_film" title="Musical film">musical</a>, songs are used to convey a character's emotions and "I wants." Composer <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> and lyricist <a href="/wiki/Tim_Rice" title="Tim Rice">Tim Rice</a> wrote the song "<a href="/wiki/I_Just_Can%27t_Wait_to_Be_King" title="I Just Can't Wait to Be King">I Just Can't Wait to Be King</a>" in order to give Simba a medium through which he can express his desire to become King of the Pride Lands. Roberts said, "It's a way for storytellers to move the story and deliver the direction of the character."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:14_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:14-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Matthew_Broderick.jpg"><img alt="Matthew Broderick.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Matthew_Broderick.jpg/175px-Matthew_Broderick.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Matthew_Broderick.jpg/263px-Matthew_Broderick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Matthew_Broderick.jpg/350px-Matthew_Broderick.jpg 2x" width="175"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Matthew_Broderick.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Matthew_Broderick" title="Matthew Broderick">Matthew Broderick</a> provided the speaking voice of Adult Simba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:16_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:16-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The first actor to be assigned to <i>The Lion King</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Broderick learned of the role while he was on vacation in Ireland, where he received a telephone call from his agent informing him that the directors were interested in casting him as Simba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:16_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:16-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> At the time, Broderick was well known for portraying the title character in <i><a href="/wiki/Ferris_Bueller%27s_Day_Off" title="Ferris Bueller's Day Off">Ferris Bueller's Day Off</a></i> (1986). The directors decided to cast him as Simba because they felt that he was "perfect" for the role; according to producer Don Hahn, Broderick's voice resembled "the kind of character who could be irresponsible and likeable, but you also felt that he could come back in a very heroic way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:16_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:16-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Taylor_Thomas" title="Jonathan Taylor Thomas">Jonathan Taylor Thomas</a>, who was starring as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Randy_Taylor" title="Randy Taylor">Randy Taylor</a> on the television sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Home_Improvement_(TV_series)" title="Home Improvement (TV series)">Home Improvement</a></i> at the time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:17_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-:17-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:17_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-:17-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> was cast as the speaking voice of Young Simba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:18_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:18-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> His appearance and personality would later serve as creative inspiration for supervising animator <a href="/wiki/Mark_Henn" title="Mark Henn">Mark Henn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As neither Broderick nor Thomas are singers, <a href="/wiki/Toto_(band)" title="Toto (band)">Toto</a> lead singer <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Williams_(musician)" title="Joseph Williams (musician)">Joseph Williams</a> and actor <a href="/wiki/Jason_Weaver" title="Jason Weaver">Jason Weaver</a> were hired to <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">dub</a> their respective singing voices. Williams' voice is heard on the song "<a href="/wiki/Can_You_Feel_the_Love_Tonight" title="Can You Feel the Love Tonight">Can You Feel the Love Tonight</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:19_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:19-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Impressed by Weaver's performance as a young <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Miniseries" title="Miniseries">miniseries</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jacksons:_An_American_Dream" title="The Jacksons: An American Dream">The Jacksons: An American Dream</a></i>, songwriters Elton John and Tim Rice recruited him to record "I Just Can't Wait to Be King" and "<a href="/wiki/Hakuna_Matata_(song)" title="Hakuna Matata (song)">Hakuna Matata</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:20_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-:20-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> while the film was still in its early stages of production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:21_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:21-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> As directors, Roger Allers and Rob Minkoff worked closely with the actors in order to ensure credible performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> As is frequently done in animated films, the filmmakers videotaped the actors while they recorded their dialogue, allowing the animators to incorporate their specific mannerisms into the designs of their characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characterization_and_animation">Characterization and animation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Characterization and animation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <i>The Lion King</i> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green-lit" title="Green-lit">green-lit</a>, its concept and story were not well received by studio employees. To guarantee the release of at least one successful film, Disney CEO Jeffrey Katzenberg divided the studio into two separate projects: <i>The Lion King</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_(1995_film)" title="Pocahontas (1995 film)">Pocahontas</a></i> (1995), with <i>Pocahontas</i> expected to be the more successful of the two. Because of this assumption, the majority of the studio's more seasoned animators gravitated towards it because <i>The Lion King</i> was deemed a "risk," while less experienced animators were assigned to work on <i>The Lion King</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:16_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-:16-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Co-director Rob Minkoff received this positively, saying that this decision "gave a lot of newer animators a chance to step up to leadership roles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-:22-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:300px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>You can't just use your house cat as a model, thinking, "I can just draw him, only bigger." You need to know why a lion is a lion, the difference in movements between a lion, a tiger, a leopard or your house cat. We looked at this film as our <i>Bambi</i>. They had the same approach. Look at the way the deer are drawn in <i>Snow White</i> and the way they drew the deer in <i>Bambi</i> just a few years later. They look like real deer because the artists did their homework.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;">— Supervising animator <a href="/wiki/Mark_Henn" title="Mark Henn">Mark Henn</a> on animating Simba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orlando_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-orlando-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>The role of animating Simba was divided between Mark Henn and <a href="/wiki/Ruben_A._Aquino" title="Ruben A. Aquino">Ruben A. Aquino</a>. While Henn served as the supervising animator of Simba as a cub, credited as Young Simba,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Aquino was placed in charge of animating the character as he appears as an adult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Lion King</i> was Disney's first animated feature film to feature a cast of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadrupeds" title="Quadrupeds">quadrupeds</a> since <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver_%26_Company" title="Oliver &amp; Company">Oliver &amp; Company</a></i> (1988). According to Aquino, animating four-legged creatures is difficult because the artists are faced with the task of drawing "twice as many legs ... as you do with human characters" and must also attribute to them both human and animal-like qualities. For assistance, Aquino drew influence from previous animated films that feature four-legged creatures as their main characters, citing <i>Bambi</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_and_the_Tramp" title="Lady and the Tramp">Lady and the Tramp</a></i> (1955) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jungle_Book_(1967_film)" title="The Jungle Book (1967 film)">The Jungle Book</a></i> (1967) as his main sources of inspiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tlkiisp-simba.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="179" data-file-width="300" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0f/Tlkiisp-simba.png/250px-Tlkiisp-simba.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/0/0f/Tlkiisp-simba.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/0/0f/Tlkiisp-simba.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tlkiisp-simba.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Simba as he appears as an adult in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride" title="The Lion King II: Simba's Pride">The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to <i>The Lion King</i>, Henn's experience as a supervising animator was limited to predominantly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Female_lead" title="Female lead">female characters</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> he had just recently completed work on <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(The_Little_Mermaid)" title="Ariel (The Little Mermaid)">Ariel</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Mermaid_(1989_film)" title="The Little Mermaid (1989 film)">The Little Mermaid</a></i> (1989), <a href="/wiki/Belle_(Disney)" title="Belle (Disney)">Belle</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Beauty_and_the_Beast_(1991_film)" title="Beauty and the Beast (1991 film)">Beauty and the Beast</a></i> (1991) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jasmine_(Aladdin)" title="Jasmine (Aladdin)">Jasmine</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Aladdin_(1992_Disney_film)" title="Aladdin (1992 Disney film)">Aladdin</a></i> (1992).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orlando_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-orlando-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> When he became involved with <i>The Lion King</i>, Henn initially expressed interest in animating the film's villain, <a href="/wiki/Scar_(The_Lion_King)" title="Scar (The Lion King)">Scar</a>, because he wanted to do "something different."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> However, producer <a href="/wiki/Don_Hahn" title="Don Hahn">Don Hahn</a> felt that he was better suited for animating Simba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:8-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Henn approaches animating new characters by "put[ting himself] into the character’s situation." Simba proved to be a challenge because Henn was faced with the task of creating an animated character who would both appear and behave like a real lion cub. To achieve this, Henn visited zoos, sketched and studied live lion cubs that were brought into the studio for research, and frequently consulted with wildlife experts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When it came time to animate Simba during the "I Just Can't Wait to Be King" musical sequence, Henn felt it essential that the character remain on all fours at all times, despite the fact that he is meant to be dancing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> In terms of personality, Henn aimed to depict Simba as a "cocky, confident character" at the beginning of the film, who must eventually mature and learn to take responsibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The animators would often observe and document the voice actors while they recorded their dialogue, using their movements and mannerisms as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_aid" title="Visual aid">visual aid</a>. Actor <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Taylor_Thomas" title="Jonathan Taylor Thomas">Jonathan Taylor Thomas</a>, who provided the voice of Young Simba, served as inspiration for the design and personality of Simba. Henn said, "I loved watching Jonathan Taylor Thomas when he was a boy on <i>Home Improvement</i>, and getting to meet him and observe him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-:5-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Although Aquino was responsible for animating the majority of Simba's adult sequences, Henn animated the character's first appearance as an adult that occurs near the end of the "Hakuna Matata" musical number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearances">Appearances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Appearances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lion_King"><i>The Lion King</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Lion King">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King" title="The Lion King">The Lion King</a></div>
<p>Released in theaters in 1994, <i>The Lion King</i> marks Simba's first appearance. All the animals in the Pride Lands gather at the foot of Pride Rock to commemorate the birth of Simba, who will eventually succeed to the throne and take his father <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mufasa" title="Mufasa">Mufasa</a>'s place as king. Furious by the fact that he is no longer next in line, Simba's jealous uncle <a href="/wiki/Scar_(The_Lion_King)" title="Scar (The Lion King)">Scar</a> refuses to attend the ceremony. While Simba grows into a rambunctious lion cub who frequently boasts about the fact that he will someday rule over the Pride Lands, Scar secretly plots against him.</p>
<p>Scar plots <a href="/wiki/Regicide" title="Regicide">regicide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Familicide" title="Familicide">familicide</a> against Simba and Mufasa by luring Simba into a vast gorge, where he triggers a <a href="/wiki/Wildebeest" title="Wildebeest">wildebeest</a> <a href="/wiki/Stampede" title="Stampede">stampede</a>. Notified by Scar that Simba is in danger, Mufasa rushes to his aid and manages to place him safely on a ledge. Weakened and unable to pull himself up the steep slope to safety, Mufasa asks his brother for assistance. However, Scar's true nature is revealed and he betrays Mufasa, throwing him into the gorge where he is killed by the fall.</p>
<p>Convinced by Scar that he is responsible for his father's death, Simba runs away to a distant jungle where he is befriended by <a href="/wiki/Timon_and_Pumbaa" title="Timon and Pumbaa">Timon and Pumbaa</a>, who teach him to ignore his past and avoid his responsibilities. There, he grows into a handsome young lion, while Scar wreaks havoc on the Pride Lands. When Simba is discovered by his childhood friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nala_(The_Lion_King)" title="Nala (The Lion King)">Nala</a> several years later, she confronts him, warning him of Scar's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyranny" title="Tyranny">tyranny</a> and ordering him to return home. Afraid of facing his past, Simba refuses until a wise mandrill named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rafiki" title="Rafiki">Rafiki</a> leads him to Mufasa's ghost, who convinces him to return home and reclaim his kingdom from Scar.</p>
<p>Simba returns to the Pride Lands and finds them barren because their <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> have been squandered and abused by Scar. After witnessing Scar strike his mother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarabi" title="Sarabi">Sarabi</a>, Simba orders Scar to resign. At first thrown by the fact that he is alive, Scar soon regains composure and forces Simba to admit to the pride that he is responsible for Mufasa's death, while cornering him at the edge of Pride Rock, hoping to subject him to a similar fate as his father. Having grown overconfident, Scar finally reveals to Simba that he, in fact, killed Mufasa. Enraged, Simba tackles Scar with unexpected fury and forces him into announcing this to the pride, initiating a battle between Simba's pride of lionesses and Scar's army of <a href="/wiki/Hyena" title="Hyena">hyenas</a>. After a grueling fight with Scar, Simba eventually defeats him and takes his rightful place as king. When the kingdom returns to its former glory, the animals welcome the birth of King Simba and Queen Nala's first born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-:8-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lion_King_II:_Simba.27s_Pride"><i>The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Lion King II: Simba's Pride">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride" title="The Lion King II: Simba's Pride">The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">direct-to-video</a> sequel released in 1998, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simba%27s_Pride" title="Simba's Pride">Simba's Pride</a></i> takes place shortly after the events of the first film, depicting Simba and Nala as king and queen of the Pride Lands. In a ceremony at Pride Rock, the Pride Lands commemorate the birth of Simba and Nala's daughter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kiara_(The_Lion_King)" title="Kiara (The Lion King)">Kiara</a>, of whom Simba is overprotective. He discovers that Kiara has disobeyed him by visiting the forbidden Outlands, home to an enemy pride of Scar's followers known as the Outsiders, and befriending a young member of the pride named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kovu_(The_Lion_King)" title="Kovu (The Lion King)">Kovu</a>. After a close confrontation with Kovu's mother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zira_(The_Lion_King)" title="Zira (The Lion King)">Zira</a>, the leader of the Outsiders and Scar's most loyal follower, Simba separates the two and reminds Kiara of her responsibilities as a princess. Meanwhile, Zira plots to manipulate Kovu in order to gain revenge on Simba for killing Scar (Not knowing that the Hyenas killed Scar).</p>
<p>Several years later, Simba grants a young adult Kiara permission to embark on her first hunting excursion. Despite the fact that he promised her independence, Simba assigns Timon and Pumbaa to follow and watch her. Angered by her father's overprotectiveness, Kiara decides to rebel and pursue her hunt outside of the Pride Lands, where she nearly falls victim to a <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a>. Kiara is rescued by Kovu, who returns her to the Pride Lands, all secretly part of Zira's plan to overthrow Simba. Claiming to have left the Outsiders, Kovu asks Simba for permission to join his pride. Simba reluctantly agrees, but does not trust Kovu because of his prior association with and likeness to Scar, and continues to treat him coldly. That night Simba dreams about attempting to save Mufasa from falling into the stampede but is stopped by Scar who turns into Kovu and throws Simba off the cliff into the stampede.</p>
<p>While Kiara and Kovu's friendship continues to grow, Simba, encouraged by Nala, attempts to show Kovu kindness by spending a day with him. Realizing that Kovu is beginning to side with Simba because of his love for Kiara, Zira ambushes and attacks Simba. Convinced by Zira that Kovu is responsible, Simba exiles him and warns Kiara not to see him, but she ignores this and leaves to find Kovu. When a battle ensues between the Pride Landers and the Outsiders, Kiara and Kovu arrive and stops them. When a furious Zira attacks Simba, she is intercepted by Kiara, causing the two to fall over the edge of a cliff. Kiara, who has landed safely on a ledge, offers to help Zira, who is struggling to hang on. However, Zira, consumed by her hatred towards Simba, refuses to accept her assistance and falls into the raging water below. Simba finally approves of Kiara's love for Kovu and reconciles with his daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lion_King_1.C2.BD"><i>The Lion King 1½</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Lion King 1½">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_1%C2%BD" title="The Lion King 1½">The Lion King 1½</a></div>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_1%C2%BD" title="The Lion King 1½">The Lion King 1½</a></i>, a direct-to-video <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interquel" title="Interquel">interquel</a> released in 2004, Simba appears as a less prominent character because the film's primary focus is on Timon and Pumbaa's behind-the-scenes role and involvement in <i>The Lion King</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> in which they appear as <a href="/wiki/Supporting_character" title="Supporting character">supporting characters</a>. Although the two films technically share the same story and timeline, the plot of <i>The Lion King 1½</i> focuses more on Timon and Pumbaa. The <a href="/wiki/Meerkat" title="Meerkat">meerkat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warthog" title="Warthog">warthog</a> unknowingly coexist alongside Simba, and the story fills in the two characters' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backstories" title="Backstories">backstories</a> and events that lead up to their long-lasting friendship, coinciding with and often initiating the events that affect Simba's life during the first film. These events include the commemorative bow that occurs during the opening "<a href="/wiki/Circle_of_Life" title="Circle of Life">Circle of Life</a>" musical number and the collapsing of the animal tower that takes place during "<a href="/wiki/I_Just_Can%27t_Wait_to_Be_King" title="I Just Can't Wait to Be King">I Just Can't Wait to Be King</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The film also explores, in further detail, the relationship among the three characters as Timon and Pumbaa struggle to raise Simba as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adoptive_parent#Example_of_Terms_used_in_Honest_Adoption_Language" title="Adoptive parent">adoptive "parents"</a> and disapprove of his relationship with Nala, portraying Simba as he grows from an energetic young lion cub, into an incorrigible teenager and, finally, an independent young lion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timon_.26_Pumbaa_and_television"><i>Timon &amp; Pumbaa</i> and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Timon &amp; Pumbaa and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Timon_%26_Pumbaa_(TV_series)" title="Timon &amp; Pumbaa (TV series)">Timon &amp; Pumbaa (TV series)</a></div>
<p>The success of <i>The Lion King</i> and popularity of its characters led to the production of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timon_and_Pumbaa_(TV_series)" title="Timon and Pumbaa (TV series)">Timon &amp; Pumbaa</a></i>, an animated television series starring <a href="/wiki/Timon_and_Pumbaa" title="Timon and Pumbaa">Timon and Pumbaa</a>. Simba makes several brief appearances, including one episode in which Timon drags him out to try to revive Pumbaa's lost memory.</p>
<p>In the episode "Congo On Like This," Timon and Pumbaa suspect that Simba has reverted to his carnivorous nature. The episode "Shake Your Djibouti" again features Simba, when Timon and Pumbaa are forced to train him to protect them from a laboratory monster. Another episode, entitled "Rome Alone," shows Simba being captured by Romans and forced into <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiatorial</a> battle with another lion named Claudius. Simba also appears in a music video of "<a href="/wiki/The_Lion_Sleeps_Tonight" title="The Lion Sleeps Tonight">The Lion Sleeps Tonight</a>" starring Timon and Pumbaa.</p>
<p>Simba was featured as a guest in the animated series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Mouse" title="House of Mouse">House of Mouse</a></i>, in which he alternates between cub and adult.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lion_Guard"><i>The Lion Guard</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Lion Guard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_Guard_(TV_series)" title="The Lion Guard (TV series)">The Lion Guard (TV series)</a></div>
<p>In November 2015 a new series called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_Guard_(TV_series)" title="The Lion Guard (TV series)">The Lion Guard</a></i> is due to be released. Set after the timeline of <i>The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</i>, it will feature a new cub named Kion who is the son of Simba and Nala.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadway_musical">Broadway musical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Broadway musical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_(musical)" title="The Lion King (musical)">The Lion King (musical)</a></div>
<p>The success of <i>The Lion King</i> led to the production of a <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_(musical)" title="The Lion King (musical)">Broadway musical based on the film</a>. Directed by <a href="/wiki/Julie_Taymor" title="Julie Taymor">Julie Taymor</a>, with a book by <a href="/wiki/Irene_Mecchi" title="Irene Mecchi">Irene Mecchi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Allers" title="Roger Allers">Roger Allers</a>, <i>The Lion King</i> premiered at the <a href="/wiki/New_Amsterdam_Theatre" title="New Amsterdam Theatre">New Amsterdam Theatre</a> on November 13, 1997, where it ran for nine years until being moved to the <a href="/wiki/Minskoff_Theatre" title="Minskoff Theatre">Minskoff Theatre</a> on June 13, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The role of Simba was originated by <a href="/wiki/Scott_Irby-Ranniar" title="Scott Irby-Ranniar">Scott Irby-Ranniar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jason_Raize" title="Jason Raize">Jason Raize</a>, with Irby-Ranniar portraying Young Simba and Raize portraying Adult Simba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Raize auditioned for the role of Adult Simba after hearing that Taymor was looking to cast an actor who was of "unidentifiable ethnicity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Raize revealed in an interview that there was a lot of competition for the role because the musical required "triple-threat work -- singing, dancing and acting -- that you don't get to such an extent in other shows. It was more the sense of who can take the challenge and not be daunted by the task." Raize, who instantly felt that he "had a connection with Simba," eventually won the role with the approval of Taymor and choreographer <a href="/wiki/Garth_Fagan" title="Garth Fagan">Garth Fagan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> with Fagan admiring the fact that Raize was "willing to try, to fail, and then to try again."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-:7-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Once cast, Raize found it difficult to maintain Taymor's "sense of duality" because Simba is "both man and beast." He said, "The tendency is to sacrifice one for the other, but you can't."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Although hundreds of children auditioned for the role of Young Simba,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> the casting process was far less grueling for Irby-Ranniar who, according to Taymor, simply "walked in and he had the part."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Merchandising_and_video_games">Merchandising and video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Merchandising and video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As part of the <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_(franchise)" title="The Lion King (franchise)">franchise</a>'s merchandising, Simba has appeared in various <i>The Lion King</i>-related products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> The character's likeness has been used in and adapted into a variety of items, including plush toys and figurines, clothing, bedding, household decor and appliances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> The success of the Broadway musical has also lead to its own line of merchandising,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> including the Simba beanbag doll, based on the character's appearance and costume in the Broadway show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Since the film's 1994 debut, Simba has appeared as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playable_character" title="Playable character">playable character</a> in a variety of video game releases, both directly and indirectly associated with the franchise. The character's first appearance as a <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">video game character</a> was in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_(video_game)" title="The Lion King (video game)">The Lion King</a></i>, which was released by <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Interactive" title="Virgin Interactive">Virgin Interactive</a> on November 1, 1994, for the video game platforms <a href="/wiki/Super_Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Super Nintendo Entertainment System">Super Nintendo Entertainment System</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sega_Game_Gear" title="Sega Game Gear">Sega Game Gear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">Nintendo Entertainment System</a> and <a href="/wiki/PC_game" title="PC game">PC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> The game follows the plot of the original film and features Simba as both a cub and an adult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On December 28, 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a> released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King:_Simba%27s_Mighty_Adventure" title="The Lion King: Simba's Mighty Adventure">The Lion King: Simba's Mighty Adventure</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">Game Boy Color</a> and <a href="/wiki/PlayStation" title="PlayStation">PlayStation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> The game encompasses 10 levels and incorporates the plot of both <i>The Lion King</i> and <i>The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</i> as "Simba ... matures from a precocious cub to an adult lion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Simba also appears as a playable character in <a href="/wiki/Disney_Interactive_Studios" title="Disney Interactive Studios">Disney Interactive Studios</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Disney%27s_Extreme_Skate_Adventure" title="Disney's Extreme Skate Adventure">Disney's Extreme Skate Adventure</a></i>, released on September 3, 2003, for <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a>, <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/GameCube" title="GameCube">GameCube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox" title="Xbox">Xbox</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Disney_Friends" title="Disney Friends">Disney Friends</a></i> , released for <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a> on February 26, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, Simba appears in the <a href="/wiki/Square_Enix" title="Square Enix">Square Enix</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hearts" title="Kingdom Hearts">Kingdom Hearts</a></i> video game franchise as a friend and ally of the series' main character, <a href="/wiki/Sora_(Kingdom_Hearts)" title="Sora (Kingdom Hearts)">Sora</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Walt_Disney_Parks_and_Resorts">Walt Disney Parks and Resorts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Walt Disney Parks and Resorts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Live versions of Simba appear in the <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_(musical)" title="The Lion King (musical)"><i>Lion King</i> musical</a> and in the parades and shows at the <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Parks_and_Resorts" title="Walt Disney Parks and Resorts">Walt Disney Parks and Resorts</a>.</p>
<p>Simba was also the main character in "Legend of the Lion King," a former <a href="/wiki/Fantasyland" title="Fantasyland">Fantasyland</a> attraction in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World_Resort" title="Walt Disney World Resort">Walt Disney World</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Magic_Kingdom" title="Magic Kingdom">Magic Kingdom</a>, which retold the story of the film using fully articulated puppets. Other Disney attractions that have featured Simba include the <a href="/wiki/Mickey%27s_PhilharMagic" title="Mickey's PhilharMagic">Mickey's PhilharMagic</a> 3D show and the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Disneyland" title="Hong Kong Disneyland">Hong Kong Disneyland</a> version of <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_a_Small_World" title="It's a Small World">It's a Small World</a></i>.</p>
<p>He appears as one of the main characters at Epcot's <a href="/wiki/The_Land_(Epcot)" title="The Land (Epcot)">Land Pavilion</a> 12-minute <a href="/wiki/Educational_entertainment" title="Educational entertainment">edutainment</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Circle_of_Life:_An_Environmental_Fable" title="Circle of Life: An Environmental Fable">Circle of Life: An Environmental Fable</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_legacy">Reception and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Reception and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Matthew_Broderick_portrait_2009.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2142" data-file-width="1514" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Matthew_Broderick_portrait_2009.jpg/175px-Matthew_Broderick_portrait_2009.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Matthew_Broderick_portrait_2009.jpg/263px-Matthew_Broderick_portrait_2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Matthew_Broderick_portrait_2009.jpg/350px-Matthew_Broderick_portrait_2009.jpg 2x" width="175"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Matthew_Broderick_portrait_2009.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Actor <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Broderick" title="Matthew Broderick">Matthew Broderick</a> was praised for his performance as Simba.</div>

<p>Although <i>The Lion King</i> itself has garnered largely positive reviews from film critics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> reception towards Simba has been generally mixed. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Christian_Science_Monitor" title="The Christian Science Monitor">The Christian Science Monitor</a></i> '​s <a href="/wiki/David_Sterritt" title="David Sterritt">David Sterritt</a> hailed Simba as "a superbly realized character," specifically praising the scene in which the character "faces discipline by his dad after his adventure with the hyenas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Owen_Gleiberman" title="Owen Gleiberman">Owen Gleiberman</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> wrote that Simba "has been given a marvelously expressive face" to the point of which "He seems more human than the <a href="/wiki/Ken_(doll)" title="Ken (doll)">Ken</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbie_doll" title="Barbie doll">Barbie</a> types featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Aladdin_(1992_Disney_film)" title="Aladdin (1992 Disney film)">Aladdin</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Mermaid_(1989_film)" title="The Little Mermaid (1989 film)">The Little Mermaid</a></i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Travers" title="Peter Travers">Peter Travers</a> of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone_(magazine)" title="Rolling Stone (magazine)">Rolling Stone</a></i> described "the father-son relationship" shared by Simba and Mufasa as "movingly rendered,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> while About.com's David Nusair wrote, "it’s the touching father/son stuff that lies at the heart of the movie that cements <i>The Lion King</i> '​s place as an utterly timeless piece of work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Berardinelli" title="James Berardinelli">James Berardinelli</a> of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ReelViews" title="ReelViews">ReelViews</a></i> enjoyed the fact that the film focuses more on the story of Simba himself as opposed to the romantic relationship developing between the character and <a href="/wiki/Nala" title="Nala">Nala</a>. However, Berardinelli criticized <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Broderick" title="Matthew Broderick">Matthew Broderick</a>'s vocal performance, describing it as "nondescript."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> Rob Humanick of <a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a> hailed the fact that "it's never laid on [Simba] that his time as king will directly correspond with the eventual passing of his father" as one of the film's "most important facets." However, he criticized <i>The Lion King</i> '​s characters, describing them as well-designed but "lazy and troublesome."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Austin_Chronicle" title="The Austin Chronicle">The Austin Chronicle</a></i> '​s Robert Faires felt that Simba and the other <i>Lion King</i> characters, though "true", were simply unoriginal retreads of preceding animated characters who were "swiped from other Disney cartoons."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Hal Hinson of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> gave the character a negative review. Labeling Timon and Pumbaa the only interesting characters in <i>The Lion King</i>, Hinson questioned Simba's role as the film's hero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Turan" title="Kenneth Turan">Kenneth Turan</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> agreed, "A movie's heroes may have their names above the title, but often as not it's the sidekicks who get the real work done." Turan went on to pan Simba, describing him as "irritatingly callow."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> Chris Hick of the <i><a href="/wiki/Deseret_News" title="Deseret News">Deseret News</a></i> complained about the fact that Simba and the other "characters in <i>The Lion King</i> are not as warm and fuzzy as other Disney animated features," crediting this with making "the film a bit tougher to warm [up] to."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ComingSoon.net" title="ComingSoon.net">ComingSoon.net</a> strongly panned Simba as a lead character, writing, "typically for Disney animated fare, it's the hero who is the weak link being both blandly designed and blandly performed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> Acknowledging the character's Shakespearean origins, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Baltimore_Sun" title="The Baltimore Sun">The Baltimore Sun</a></i> '​s Stephen Hunter gave Simba a negative review, writing, "Alas ...Simba stands in for Hamlet, but he's a lot less complicated; in fact, he's less complicated than <a href="/wiki/Morris_the_Cat" title="Morris the Cat">Morris the Cat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sylvester" title="Sylvester">Sylvester</a>." Hunter continued, "Simba the Exile is even less interesting than Simba the Prince."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Null" title="Christopher Null">Christopher Null</a> of <a href="/wiki/Contactmusic.com" title="Contactmusic.com">Contactmusic.com</a> was critical of Weaver's performance as the singing voice of Young Simba, writing, "If there's anything annoying about the film, it's the singing. Young Simba sounds like a young <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> ... You almost don't want him to succeed." However, Null reacted more positively towards Broderick's performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the character's mixed reception, several critics have awarded specific praise to Broderick for his portrayal of Simba, including the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> '​s Peter Stack and <i>The Washington Post</i> '​s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desson_Howe" title="Desson Howe">Desson Howe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> Annette Basile of <i><a href="/wiki/Filmink" title="Filmink">Filmink</a></i> described Broderick's performance as "excellent,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bradshaw" title="Peter Bradshaw">Peter Bradshaw</a> of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Guardian_(newspaper)" title="The Guardian (newspaper)">The Guardian</a></i> called it "sumptuous." <a href="/wiki/Digital_Spy" title="Digital Spy">Digital Spy</a>'s Mayer Nissim described Broderick's portrayal of Simba as "wonderful."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_and_popularity">Impact and popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Impact and popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the film's opening number, "<a href="/wiki/Circle_of_Life" title="Circle of Life">Circle of Life</a>", <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rafiki" title="Rafiki">Rafiki</a> introduces a newborn Simba to the crowd of animals gathered at the foot of Pride Rock by holding him high above their heads while parents Mufasa and Sarabi look on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-:8-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Since the film's 1994 release, this scene has grown to iconic status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> In November 2002, singer Michael Jackson sparked controversy by holding his son over the protective railing of a hotel balcony in Berlin. The event was witnessed by a large crowd of spectators who were watching from below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> Some sources have claimed that Jackson was harmlessly attempting to emulate the scene from <i>The Lion King.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kate_Middleton" title="Kate Middleton">Kate Middleton, Duchess of Cambridge</a> went into labor with hers and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_William" title="Prince William">Prince William</a>'s baby in July 2013, the idea that the couple should reenact the famous scene from <i>The Lion King</i> became quite popular among <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> Radio journalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darren_Simpson" title="Darren Simpson">Darren Simpson</a> reportedly tweeted, "when your baby arrives please re-enact the scene from the <i>Lion King</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> Shortly after Middleton gave birth to a boy, England native Tommy Peto initiated a petition asking the couple to welcome their baby by having the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a> emulate the scene by holding him over the balcony of <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Palace" title="Buckingham Palace">Buckingham Palace</a>. Ultimately, the idea was deemed "outside the responsibility of the government" and was declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The scene has found itself the subject of both reference and parody in various forms of media, such as in the film <i><a href="/wiki/George_of_the_Jungle_(film)" title="George of the Jungle (film)">George of the Jungle</a></i> (1997).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> In what is almost an exact replica of the scene, George, portrayed by actor <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Fraser" title="Brendan Fraser">Brendan Fraser</a>, takes the place of both Rafiki and Mufasa by standing at the tip of Pride Rock and presenting his young son to a crowd of onlooking animals, accompanied by wife Ursula, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Mann" title="Leslie Mann">Leslie Mann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Since the release of <i>The Lion King</i> in 1994, the name "Simba" has increased in use and popularity among dog and cat owners. According to <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a> in 2010, the use of Simba as a dog name reemerged in popularity in 2009 after experiencing a noticeable decline in 2001, ranking the name ninth out of 10 on its list of "Top 10 Trendiest Dog Names of the Year."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> In May 2013, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Lifestyle" title="Yahoo! Lifestyle">Yahoo! Lifestyle</a> included the name on its list of "Trendiest Dog Names."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> According to YouPet, Simba is the 17th most popular cat name out of 100 candidates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> Care2 included Simba in its article "All-around Cool Cat Names,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> while DutchNews.nl reported that Simba ranks among the country's most popular cat names as of July 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> In its list of "Top Popular Pet Names," BabyNames.com placed Simba at number 64 on its list of most popular dog names out of the 100 that were considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
