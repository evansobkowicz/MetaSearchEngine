<p><i><b>The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring</b></i> is a 2001 <a href="/wiki/Epic_film" title="Epic film">epic</a> <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_film" title="Fantasy film">fantasy film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring" title="The Fellowship of the Ring">first volume</a> of <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i> (1954–1955).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It is the first installment in the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(film_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (film series)">The Lord of the Rings film series</a></i>, and was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Two_Towers" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers">The Two Towers</a></i> (2002) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Return_of_the_King" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King">The Return of the King</a></i> (2003), based on the second and third volumes of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>.</p>
<p>Set in <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth" title="Middle-earth">Middle-earth</a>, the story tells of the Dark Lord <a href="/wiki/Sauron" title="Sauron">Sauron</a>, who is seeking the <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">One Ring</a>. The Ring has found its way to the young <a href="/wiki/Hobbit" title="Hobbit">hobbit</a> <a href="/wiki/Frodo_Baggins" title="Frodo Baggins">Frodo Baggins</a> (<a href="/wiki/Elijah_Wood" title="Elijah Wood">Elijah Wood</a>). The fate of Middle-earth hangs in the balance as Frodo and eight companions who form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellowship_of_the_Ring_(characters)" title="Fellowship of the Ring (characters)">Fellowship of the Ring</a> begin their journey to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Doom" title="Mount Doom">Mount Doom</a> in the land of <a href="/wiki/Mordor" title="Mordor">Mordor</a>, the only place where the Ring can be destroyed.</p>
<p>Released on 10 December 2001, the film was highly acclaimed by critics and fans alike who considered it to be a landmark in film-making and an achievement in the fantasy film genre. It has continued to be featured on critics' lists of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_considered_the_best" title="List of films considered the best">greatest fantasy films ever made</a> as of 2013. The film was a massive box office success, earning over $871 million worldwide, and becoming the second highest-grossing film of 2001 in the U.S. and worldwide (behind <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone_(film)" title="Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone (film)">Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone</a></i>) which made it the fifth highest-grossing film ever at the time.</p>
<p>As of August 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, it is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_highest-grossing_films" title="List of highest-grossing films">36th highest-grossing film</a> of all time worldwide. It was nominated for thirteen <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Oscars</a> at the <a href="/wiki/74th_Academy_Awards" title="74th Academy Awards">74th Academy Awards</a> ceremony, winning four for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Cinematography" title="Academy Award for Best Cinematography">Best Cinematography</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Makeup" title="Academy Award for Best Makeup">Best Makeup</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Score" title="Academy Award for Best Original Score">Best Original Score</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Best Visual Effects">Best Visual Effects</a>. It won also four <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_Film_Awards" title="British Academy Film Awards">British Academy Film Awards</a>, including Best Film and Best Director BAFTA awards. The <i>Special Extended Edition</i> was released to DVD on 12 November 2002 and to <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a> on 28 June 2011. In 2007, <i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> was voted <abbr title="Number">No.</abbr> 50 on the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a>'s list of <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Movies_(10th_Anniversary_Edition)" title="AFI's 100 Years...100 Movies (10th Anniversary Edition)">100 greatest American films</a>. The AFI also voted it the second greatest fantasy film of all time during their <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_10_Top_10" title="AFI's 10 Top 10">10 Top 10</a> special.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Second_Age" title="Second Age">Second Age</a>, the Dark Lord <a href="/wiki/Sauron" title="Sauron">Sauron</a> attempts to conquer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle-Earth" title="Middle-Earth">Middle-Earth</a> using his <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">One Ring</a>. In battle against the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Middle-earth_wars_and_battles#War_of_the_Last_Alliance" title="List of Middle-earth wars and battles">Elves and Men</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isildur" title="Isildur">Isildur</a> cuts the ring from Sauron's hand, destroying his physical form and vanquishing his army. However, Sauron's "life force" is bound to the Ring, allowing him to survive while the Ring remains. Isildur, corrupted by the Ring's power, refuses to destroy it. When Isildur is killed by <a href="/wiki/Orc_(Middle-earth)" title="Orc (Middle-earth)">Orcs</a>, the Ring is lost in the river for 2,500 years. The Ring is found by <a href="/wiki/Gollum" title="Gollum">Gollum</a>, who possesses it for centuries until it is taken by the <a href="/wiki/Hobbit" title="Hobbit">Hobbit</a> <a href="/wiki/Bilbo_Baggins" title="Bilbo Baggins">Bilbo Baggins</a>.</p>
<p>Sixty years later, Bilbo leaves the Ring to his nephew, <a href="/wiki/Frodo_Baggins" title="Frodo Baggins">Frodo Baggins</a>. Upon learning that the Ring belonged to Sauron, the <a href="/wiki/Wizard_(Middle-earth)" title="Wizard (Middle-earth)">Wizard</a> <a href="/wiki/Gandalf" title="Gandalf">Gandalf the Grey</a> warns Frodo that Sauron's forces will come for him. Gandalf catches Frodo's gardener <a href="/wiki/Samwise_Gamgee" title="Samwise Gamgee">Samwise Gamgee</a> eavesdropping by a window and sends him with Frodo to leave the Shire. Gandalf rides to <a href="/wiki/Isengard" title="Isengard">Isengard</a> to meet with the head of his order, <a href="/wiki/Saruman" title="Saruman">Saruman the White</a>, who reveals that Sauron's servants, the <a href="/wiki/Nazg%C3%BBl" title="Nazgûl">Nazgûl</a>, have been sent to capture the Ring. Saruman reveals himself to be in service to Sauron, and imprisons Gandalf atop his tower. Saruman commands Sauron's Orcs to construct weapons of war and produce a new breed of Orc fighters: the <a href="/wiki/Uruk-hai" title="Uruk-hai">Uruk-hai</a>.</p>
<p>While traveling to <a href="/wiki/Bree_(Middle-earth)" title="Bree (Middle-earth)">Bree</a>, Frodo and Sam are joined by <a href="/wiki/Meriadoc_Brandybuck" title="Meriadoc Brandybuck">Merry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peregrin_Took" title="Peregrin Took">Pippin</a>, and are nearly captured by the Nazgûl. At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inn_of_the_Prancing_Pony" title="Inn of the Prancing Pony">Inn of the Prancing Pony</a>, Frodo meets the mysterious ranger <a href="/wiki/Aragorn" title="Aragorn">Aragorn</a>, who hides them from their pursuers and agrees to lead them to <a href="/wiki/Rivendell" title="Rivendell">Rivendell</a>. During the journey they are attacked by the Nazgûl. Aragorn fights off the creatures, but Frodo is wounded by their <a href="/wiki/List_of_Middle-earth_weapons_and_armour#Morgul-blade" title="List of Middle-earth weapons and armour">morgul blade</a>, which will cause him to turn into a wraith if not attended to with the proper care. While chased by the Nazgûl, Frodo is taken by the <a href="/wiki/Elf_(Middle-earth)" title="Elf (Middle-earth)">Elf</a> <a href="/wiki/Arwen" title="Arwen">Arwen</a> to Rivendell, and healed by her father, <a href="/wiki/Elrond" title="Elrond">Elrond</a>. Arwen uses her magic to summon a surge of water that sweeps away the pursuing Nazgûl. Gandalf escapes Saruman's tower with the aid of an <a href="/wiki/Eagle_(Middle-earth)" title="Eagle (Middle-earth)">eagle</a> and travels to Rivendell. Elrond calls a <a href="/wiki/The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring#Book_II:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring" title="The Fellowship of the Ring">council</a> of the races still loyal to Middle-Earth to decide what should be done with the Ring. It is revealed that the Ring can only be destroyed by throwing it into the fires of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Doom" title="Mount Doom">Mount Doom</a>, where it was forged. Frodo volunteers to take the Ring to Mount Doom, accompanied by Samwise, Merry, Pippin, Gandalf, and Aragorn. They are joined by the Elf <a href="/wiki/Legolas" title="Legolas">Legolas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_(Middle-earth)" title="Dwarf (Middle-earth)">Dwarf</a> <a href="/wiki/Gimli_(Middle-earth)" title="Gimli (Middle-earth)">Gimli</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Boromir" title="Boromir">Boromir</a>, heir to the stewardship of <a href="/wiki/Gondor" title="Gondor">Gondor</a>, to form the <a href="/wiki/The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring#Members_of_the_Fellowship_of_the_Ring" title="The Fellowship of the Ring">Fellowship of the Ring</a>.</p>
<p>The Fellowship set out but are hindered by Saruman's magic. They travel through the dwarf <a href="/wiki/Moria_(Middle-earth)" title="Moria (Middle-earth)">Mines of Moria</a>. Inside, they find that Orcs have overrun the mines and slain the Dwarfs; they also discover that Gollum is following them, determined to reclaim the Ring. The Fellowship is attacked by the Orcs and a <a href="/wiki/Balrog" title="Balrog">Balrog</a>, an ancient <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">demon</a> of fire and shadow. Gandalf confronts the Balrog, allowing the others to escape, but both Gandalf and the Balrog fall into an abyss. Mourning Gandalf's death, the group flees to <a href="/wiki/Lothl%C3%B3rien" title="Lothlórien">Lothlórien</a>, where they are sheltered by its rulers, the Elves <a href="/wiki/Galadriel" title="Galadriel">Galadriel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celeborn" title="Celeborn">Celeborn</a>. That night, Frodo meets Galadriel, who informs Frodo that it is his destiny to destroy the Ring. The Fellowship continue their journey. Meanwhile, Saruman assembles a force of Uruk-hai to hunt the Fellowship.</p>
<p>After arriving at Parth Galen, Boromir gives in to the Ring's corruption and tries to take it from Frodo, believing that it is the only way to save his realm. Frodo manages to escape by putting the Ring on his finger, becoming invisible. Boromir realizes what he has done, and attempts to find Frodo, who decides to continue his journey alone, heeding Galadriel's warning that the Ring will eventually corrupt the other members of the Fellowship. The Uruk-hai arrive and Aragorn, Legolas, and Gimli attempt to hold them off while Frodo escapes. Merry and Pippin lead the Orcs away from Frodo and are nearly killed before Boromir appears and intervenes, in the process suffering mortal wounds. The Uruk-Hai capture Merry and Pippin. Aragorn tends to a dying Boromir, and promises him that the world of men will not fall. Boromir dies with Aragorn, Gimli and Legolas by his side.</p>
<p>Samwise finds Frodo, who reluctantly lets Samwise join him. Aragorn, Legolas and Gimli set out to rescue Merry and Pippin. As Frodo and Sam gaze out at the land before them, they grieve over the others. Frodo says that he is glad that Sam is with him and the two Hobbits set off towards Mordor.</p>

<p>Before filming began on 11 October 1999, the principal actors trained for six weeks in sword fighting (with <a href="/wiki/Bob_Anderson_(fencer)" title="Bob Anderson (fencer)">Bob Anderson</a>), riding and boating. Jackson hoped such activities would allow the cast to bond so chemistry would be evident on screen as well as getting them used to life in <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-cast-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> They were also trained to pronounce Tolkien's verses properly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-officialguide_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-officialguide-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> After the shoot, the nine cast members playing the Fellowship got a tattoo, the Elvish symbol for the number nine, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/John_Rhys-Davies" title="John Rhys-Davies">John Rhys-Davies</a>, whose stunt double got the tattoo instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-master_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-master-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The film is noted for having an <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_cast" title="Ensemble cast">ensemble cast</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and some of the cast and their respective characters include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elijah_Wood" title="Elijah Wood">Elijah Wood</a> as <a href="/wiki/Frodo_Baggins" title="Frodo Baggins">Frodo Baggins</a>: a young <a href="/wiki/Hobbit" title="Hobbit">hobbit</a> who inherits the <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">One Ring</a> from his uncle Bilbo. Wood was the first actor to be cast on 7 July 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Wood was a fan of the book, and he sent in an audition dressed as Frodo, reading lines from the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Wood was selected from 150 actors who auditioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bumps_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-bumps-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ian_McKellen" title="Ian McKellen">Ian McKellen</a> as <a href="/wiki/Gandalf" title="Gandalf">Gandalf the Grey</a>: an <a href="/wiki/Wizard_(Middle-earth)" title="Wizard (Middle-earth)">Istari wizard</a> and mentor to Frodo. <a href="/wiki/Sean_Connery" title="Sean Connery">Sean Connery</a> was approached for the role, but did not understand the plot,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Stewart" title="Patrick Stewart">Patrick Stewart</a> turned it down as he disliked the script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Before being cast, McKellen had to sort his schedule with <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> as there was a two-month overlap with <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men_(film)" title="X-Men (film)">X-Men</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bumps_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-bumps-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> He enjoyed playing Gandalf the Grey more than his transformed state in the next two films,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-master_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-master-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> and based his accent on Tolkien. Unlike his on-screen character, McKellen did not spend much time with the actors playing the Hobbits; instead he worked with their scale doubles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-cast-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Viggo_Mortensen" title="Viggo Mortensen">Viggo Mortensen</a> as <a href="/wiki/Aragorn" title="Aragorn">Aragorn "Strider" Elessar II</a>: a <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BAnedain" title="Dúnedain">Dúnedain</a> <a href="/wiki/Ranger_(Middle-earth)" title="Ranger (Middle-earth)">ranger</a> and heir to <a href="/wiki/Gondor" title="Gondor">Gondor</a>'s throne. <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Day-Lewis" title="Daniel Day-Lewis">Daniel Day-Lewis</a> was offered the part at the beginning of pre-production, but turned it down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDL_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-DDL-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Cage" title="Nicolas Cage">Nicolas Cage</a> also received an offer, declining because of "family obligations",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Vin_Diesel" title="Vin Diesel">Vin Diesel</a>, a fan of the book, auditioned for Aragorn. <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Townsend" title="Stuart Townsend">Stuart Townsend</a> was cast in the role, before being replaced during filming when Jackson realised he was too young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Russell_Crowe" title="Russell Crowe">Russell Crowe</a> was considered as a replacement, but he turned it down after taking what he thought to be a similar role in <i><a href="/wiki/Gladiator_(2000_film)" title="Gladiator (2000 film)">Gladiator</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Day-Lewis was offered the role for a second time, but declined again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DDL_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-DDL-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Executive Producer <a href="/wiki/Mark_Ordesky" title="Mark Ordesky">Mark Ordesky</a> saw Mortensen in a play. Mortensen's son, a fan of the book, convinced him to take the role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-cast-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Mortensen read the book on the plane, received a crash course lesson in fencing from Bob Anderson and began filming the scenes on Weathertop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filming_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-filming-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Mortensen became a hit with the crew by patching up his costume<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-costumes_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-costumes-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> and carrying his "hero" sword around with him off-camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-cast-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sean_Astin" title="Sean Astin">Sean Astin</a> as <a href="/wiki/Samwise_Gamgee" title="Samwise Gamgee">Samwise "Sam" Gamgee</a>: a hobbit gardener and Frodo's best friend. Astin, who had recently become a father, bonded with the 18-year-old Wood in a protective manner, which mirrored Sam's relationship with Frodo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-cast-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liv_Tyler" title="Liv Tyler">Liv Tyler</a> as <a href="/wiki/Arwen" title="Arwen">Arwen Undomiel</a>: an <a href="/wiki/Elf_(Middle-earth)" title="Elf (Middle-earth)">elven</a> princess of Rivendell and Aragorn's lover. The filmmakers approached Tyler after seeing her performance in <i><a href="/wiki/Plunkett_%26_Macleane" title="Plunkett &amp; Macleane">Plunkett &amp; Macleane</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a> leaped at the opportunity of having one Hollywood star in the film. Actress <a href="/wiki/Helena_Bonham_Carter" title="Helena Bonham Carter">Helena Bonham Carter</a> had expressed interest in the role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Tyler came to shoot on short occasions, unlike the rest of the actors. She was one of the last actors to be cast, on 25 August 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cate_Blanchett" title="Cate Blanchett">Cate Blanchett</a> as <a href="/wiki/Galadriel" title="Galadriel">Galadriel</a>: the elven co-ruler of <a href="/wiki/Lothl%C3%B3rien" title="Lothlórien">Lothlórien</a> alongside her husband Celeborn.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Rhys-Davies" title="John Rhys-Davies">John Rhys-Davies</a> as <a href="/wiki/Gimli_(Middle-earth)" title="Gimli (Middle-earth)">Gimli</a>: a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_(Middle-earth)" title="Dwarf (Middle-earth)">dwarf</a> warrior who accompanies the Fellowship to Mordor after they set out from <a href="/wiki/Rivendell" title="Rivendell">Rivendell</a>. <a href="/wiki/Billy_Connolly" title="Billy Connolly">Billy Connolly</a>, who was considered for the part of Gimli, later portrayed <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1in_II_Ironfoot" title="Dáin II Ironfoot">Dáin II Ironfoot</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson's</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit_(film_series)" title="The Hobbit (film series)"><i>The Hobbit</i> film trilogy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Rhys-Davies wore heavy prosthetics to play Gimli, which limited his vision, and eventually he developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eczema" title="Eczema">eczema</a> around his eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-cast-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Rhys-Davies also played Gimli's father <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gl%C3%B3in" title="Glóin">Glóin</a> during the scene where the fellowship is forged.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Billy_Boyd_(actor)" title="Billy Boyd (actor)">Billy Boyd</a> as <a href="/wiki/Peregrin_Took" title="Peregrin Took">Peregrin "Pippin" Took</a>: a hobbit who travels with the Fellowship on their journey to Mordor.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dominic_Monaghan" title="Dominic Monaghan">Dominic Monaghan</a> as <a href="/wiki/Meriadoc_Brandybuck" title="Meriadoc Brandybuck">Meriadoc "Merry" Brandybuck</a>: a distant cousin of Frodo. Monaghan was cast as Merry after auditioning for Frodo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Orlando_Bloom" title="Orlando Bloom">Orlando Bloom</a> as <a href="/wiki/Legolas" title="Legolas">Legolas Greenleaf</a>: a prince of the elves' <a href="/wiki/Mirkwood" title="Mirkwood">Woodland Realm</a> and a skilled archer. Bloom initially auditioned for <a href="/wiki/Faramir" title="Faramir">Faramir</a>, who appears in the second film, a role which went to <a href="/wiki/David_Wenham" title="David Wenham">David Wenham</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lee" title="Christopher Lee">Christopher Lee</a> as <a href="/wiki/Saruman" title="Saruman">Saruman the White</a>: the fallen head of the Istari Order who succumbs to Sauron's will through his use of the <i><a href="/wiki/Palant%C3%ADr" title="Palantír">palantír</a></i>. Lee is a major fan of the book, and reads it once a year. He has also met <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filming_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-filming-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> He originally auditioned for Gandalf, but was judged too old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hugo_Weaving" title="Hugo Weaving">Hugo Weaving</a> as <a href="/wiki/Elrond" title="Elrond">Elrond</a>: the elven Lord of Rivendell who leads the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Elrond" title="Council of Elrond">Council of Elrond</a>, which ultimately decides to destroy the Ring. <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> expressed interest in the role, but Jackson stated, "To have a famous, beloved character and a famous star colliding is slightly uncomfortable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bumps_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-bumps-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sean_Bean" title="Sean Bean">Sean Bean</a> as <a href="/wiki/Boromir" title="Boromir">Boromir</a>: a prince of the <a href="/wiki/Stewards_of_Gondor" title="Stewards of Gondor">Stewards of Gondor</a> who journeys with the Fellowship towards Mordor. <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Willis" title="Bruce Willis">Bruce Willis</a>, a fan of the book, expressed interest in the role, while <a href="/wiki/Liam_Neeson" title="Liam Neeson">Liam Neeson</a> was sent the script, but passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ian_Holm" title="Ian Holm">Ian Holm</a> as <a href="/wiki/Bilbo_Baggins" title="Bilbo Baggins">Bilbo Baggins</a>: Frodo's uncle who gives him the Ring after he decides to retire to Rivendell. Holm previously played Frodo in a <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(1981_radio_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (1981 radio series)">1981 radio adaption of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i></a>, and was cast as Bilbo after Jackson remembered his performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circus_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-circus-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_McCoy" title="Sylvester McCoy">Sylvester McCoy</a>, who would later play Radagast the Brown Wizard in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit_(film_series)" title="The Hobbit (film series)">The Hobbit</a></i>, was contacted about playing the role, and was kept in place as a potential Bilbo for six months before Jackson went with Holm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andy_Serkis" title="Andy Serkis">Andy Serkis</a> as <a href="/wiki/Gollum" title="Gollum">Gollum</a>: a wretched hobbit-like creature whose mind was poisoned by the Ring after bearing it for centuries; voice and <a href="/wiki/Motion_capture" title="Motion capture">motion capture</a>. This character appears briefly in the prologue. In <a href="/wiki/Mordor" title="Mordor">Mordor</a>, you can only hear his voice shouting and in <a href="/wiki/Moria_(Middle-earth)" title="Moria (Middle-earth)">Moria</a>, only his eyes and his nose appear.</li>
</ul>

<li><a href="/wiki/Marton_Csokas" title="Marton Csokas">Marton Csokas</a> as <a href="/wiki/Celeborn" title="Celeborn">Celeborn the Wise</a>: the elven Lord of <a href="/wiki/Lothl%C3%B3rien" title="Lothlórien">Lothlórien</a> and Galadriel's husband;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Makoare" title="Lawrence Makoare">Lawrence Makoare</a> as <a href="/wiki/List_of_Middle-earth_Orcs#Lurtz" title="List of Middle-earth Orcs">Lurtz</a>: the commander of Saruman's Orc forces;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Craig_Parker" title="Craig Parker">Craig Parker</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haldir_of_L%C3%B3rien" title="Haldir of Lórien">Haldir</a>: the leader of the Galadhrim warriors guarding the border of Lothlórien;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mark_Ferguson" title="Mark Ferguson">Mark Ferguson</a> as <a href="/wiki/Gil-galad" title="Gil-galad">Ereinion Gil-galad</a>, the last Elven-King of <a href="/wiki/Noldor" title="Noldor">Noldor</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_McKenzie_(actor)" title="Peter McKenzie (actor)">Peter McKenzie</a> as <a href="/wiki/Elendil" title="Elendil">Elendil the Tall</a>: the last High King of Arnor and Gondor;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Harry_Sinclair" title="Harry Sinclair">Harry Sinclair</a> as <a href="/wiki/Isildur" title="Isildur">Isildur</a>: Elendil's son and Aragorn's ancestor who originally defeated Sauron.</li>

<p>Jackson, Walsh and Boyens made numerous changes to the story, for purposes of pacing and character development. Jackson said his main desire was to make a film focused primarily on Frodo and the Ring, the "backbone" of the story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Story_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Story-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The prologue condenses Tolkien's backstory, in which The Last Alliance's seven-year siege of the <a href="/wiki/Barad-d%C3%BBr" title="Barad-dûr">Barad-dûr</a> is a single battle, where Sauron is shown to explode, though Tolkien only said his spirit flees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Letters_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Letters-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Events at the beginning of the film are condensed or omitted altogether. In the book the time between Gandalf leaving the Ring to Frodo and returning to reveal its inscription, which is 17 years, is compressed for timing reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DirectorCommentary_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-DirectorCommentary-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Frodo also spends a few months preparing to move to Buckland, on the eastern border of the Shire. This move is omitted and combined with him setting out for Bree. Also compressed is the time between Frodo and Sam leaving Bag End and their meeting Merry and Pippin. Characters such as <a href="/wiki/Tom_Bombadil" title="Tom Bombadil">Tom Bombadil</a> and the incidents in the old forest and the barrow downs are left out to simplify the plot and increase the threat of the <a href="/wiki/Nazg%C3%BBl" title="Nazgûl">Ringwraiths</a>. Such sequences are left out to make time to introduce Saruman, who in the book doesn't appear until Gandalf's account at the Council of Elrond. While some characters are left out, some are referenced such as Tom, Bert, and William to show how "The Hobbit" and the "The Lord of the Rings" series intertwine. Saruman's role is enhanced: he is to blame for the blizzard on Caradhras, a role taken from Sauron and/or Caradhras itself in the book. Gandalf's capture by Saruman is also expanded with a fight sequence.</p>
<p>The role of <a href="/wiki/Barliman_Butterbur" title="Barliman Butterbur">Barliman Butterbur</a> at the Prancing Pony is largely removed for time and dramatic flow.</p>
<p>The events at Weathertop were also altered. The location of the fight against the Ringwraiths was changed to the ruins on top of the hill rather than a campsite at its base. When Frodo was stabbed in the book, the party spent two weeks travelling to Rivendell, but in the film this is shortened to less than a week, with Frodo's condition worsening at a commensurately greater rate. <a href="/wiki/Arwen" title="Arwen">Arwen</a> was given a greater role in the film, accompanying Frodo all the way to Rivendell, while in the book Frodo faced the Ringwraiths alone at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruinen" title="Bruinen">Ford of Bruinen</a>. The character of <a href="/wiki/Glorfindel" title="Glorfindel">Glorfindel</a> was omitted entirely and his scenes were also given to Arwen. She was tacitly credited with the river rising against the Ringwraiths, which was the work of her father <a href="/wiki/Elrond" title="Elrond">Elrond</a> with aid from <a href="/wiki/Gandalf" title="Gandalf">Gandalf</a> in the book.</p>
<p>A significant new addition is Aragorn's self-doubt, which causes him to hesitate to claim the kingship of Gondor. This element is not present in the book, where Aragorn intends to claim the throne at an appropriate time. In the book Narsil is reforged immediately when he joins the Fellowship, but this event is held over until <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Return_of_the_King" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King">Return of the King</a></i> in film to symbolically coincide with his acceptance of his title. These elements were added because Peter Jackson believed that each character should be forced to grow or change over the course of the story.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Elrond" title="Elrond">Elrond</a>'s character gained an adversarial edge; he expresses doubts in the strength of Men to resist Sauron's evil after Isildur's failure to destroy the ring as depicted in the prologue. Jackson also shortens the Council of Elrond by spreading its exposition into earlier parts of the film. Elrond's counsellor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erestor" title="Erestor">Erestor</a>—who suggested the Ring be given to <a href="/wiki/Tom_Bombadil" title="Tom Bombadil">Tom Bombadil</a>—was completely absent from this scene. Gimli's father, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gl%C3%B3in" title="Glóin">Glóin</a>, was also deemed unnecessary. In addition, the movie makes it seem by chance that the Fellowship is made of nine companions, whereas in the book Elrond suggests there be nine in the fellowship in response to the nine <a href="/wiki/Nazg%C3%BBl" title="Nazgûl">Nazgûl</a>.</p>
<p>The tone of the Moria sequence was altered. Although in the book the Fellowship realise the Dwarves are all dead only once they reach <a href="/wiki/Balin_(Middle-earth)" title="Balin (Middle-earth)">Balin</a>'s tomb, the filmmakers chose to use <a href="/wiki/Foreshadowing" title="Foreshadowing">foreshadowing</a> devices instead. Gandalf says to Gimli he would prefer not to enter Moria, and Saruman is shown to be aware of Gandalf's reticence, and also reveals an illustration of the Balrog in one of his books. The corpses of the dwarves are instantly shown as the Fellowship enter Moria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> One detail that many critics commented upon is the fact that, in the novel, Pippin tosses a mere pebble into the well in Moria ("They then hear what sounds like a hammer tapping in the distance"), whereas in the film, he knocks an entire skeleton in ("Next, the skeleton ... falls down the well, also dragging down a chain and bucket. The noise is incredible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In terms of dramatic structure, the book simply ends; there is no climax, because Tolkien wrote the "trilogy" as a single story published in three volumes. Jackson's version incorporates the first chapter of '"The Two Towers" and makes its events, told in real time instead of flashback, simultaneous with the Breaking of the Fellowship. This finale is played as a climactic battle, into which he introduces the Uruk-hai referred to as Lurtz in the script. In the book, Boromir is unable to tell Aragorn which hobbits were kidnapped by the orcs before he dies. From there, Aragorn deduces Frodo's intentions when he notices that a boat is missing and Sam's pack is gone. In the film, Aragorn and Frodo have a scene together in which Frodo's intentions are explicitly stated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a> began working with <a href="/wiki/Christian_Rivers" title="Christian Rivers">Christian Rivers</a> to <a href="/wiki/Storyboard" title="Storyboard">storyboard</a> the series in August 1997, as well as getting <a href="/wiki/Richard_Taylor_(filmmaker)" title="Richard Taylor (filmmaker)">Richard Taylor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weta_Workshop" title="Weta Workshop">Weta Workshop</a> to begin creating his interpretation of Middle-earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Jackson told them to make Middle-earth as plausible and believable as possible, to think of Middle-earth in a historical manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Design_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Design-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In November,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Design_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Design-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lee_(illustrator)" title="Alan Lee (illustrator)">Alan Lee</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Howe_(illustrator)" title="John Howe (illustrator)">John Howe</a> became the film trilogy's primary conceptual designers, having had previous experience as illustrators for the book and various other tie-ins. Lee worked for the Art Department creating places such as <a href="/wiki/Rivendell" title="Rivendell">Rivendell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isengard" title="Isengard">Isengard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moria_(Middle-earth)" title="Moria (Middle-earth)">Moria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lothl%C3%B3rien" title="Lothlórien">Lothlórien</a>, giving <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">art nouveau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> influences to the Elves and Dwarves respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Design_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Design-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigature_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-bigature-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Though Howe contributed with Bag End and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argonath" title="Argonath">Argonath</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Design_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Design-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigature_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-bigature-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> he focused working on armour having studied it all his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Weta and the Art Department continued to design, with <a href="/wiki/Grant_Major" title="Grant Major">Grant Major</a> turning the Art Department's designs into architecture, and <a href="/wiki/Dan_Hennah" title="Dan Hennah">Dan Hennah</a> scouting locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Design_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-Design-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> On 1 April 1999, <a href="/wiki/Ngila_Dickson" title="Ngila Dickson">Ngila Dickson</a> joined the crew as costume designer. She and 40 seamstresses would create 19,000 costumes, 40 per version for the actor and their doubles, ageing and wearing them out for impression of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-costumes_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-costumes-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_effects">Special effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Special effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> makes extensive use of digital, practical and make-up special effects throughout. One noticeable illusion that appears in almost every scene involves setting a proper scale so that the characters are all the correct height. Elijah Wood, who plays Frodo, is 5 ft 6 in (1.68 m) tall in real life, but the character of Frodo Baggins is barely four feet in height. Many different tricks were used to depict the hobbits (and Gimli the Dwarf) as being of diminutive stature. (In a happy coincidence, John-Rhys Davies — who played Gimli — is as tall compared to the Hobbit actors as his character needed to be compared to theirs, so he did not need to be filmed separately as a third variation of height, and is quite taller than Orlando Bloom, who played Legolas.) Large- and small-scale doubles were used in certain scenes, while entire duplicates of certain sets (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bag_End" title="Bag End">Bag End</a> in Hobbiton) were built at two different scales, so that the characters would appear to be the appropriate size. At one point in the film, Frodo runs along a corridor in Bag End, followed by Gandalf. Elijah Wood and Ian McKellen were filmed in separate versions of the same corridor, built at two different scales, and a fast camera pan conceals the edit between the two. <a href="/wiki/Forced_perspective" title="Forced perspective">Forced perspective</a> was also employed, so that it would look as though the short Hobbits were interacting with taller <a href="/wiki/Man_(Middle-earth)" title="Man (Middle-earth)">Men</a> and Elves. Even the simple use of kneeling down, to the filmmakers' surprise, turned out to be an effective method in creating the illusion.</p>
<p>For the battle between the Last Alliance and Sauron's forces that begins the film, an elaborate <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">CGI</a> animation system, called <a href="/wiki/MASSIVE_(software)" title="MASSIVE (software)">MASSIVE</a>, was developed by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Regelous" title="Stephen Regelous">Stephen Regelous</a>; it allowed thousands of individual animated "characters" in the program to act independently. This helped give the illusion of realism to the battle sequences. The "Making of" Lord of the Rings DVD reports some interesting initial problems: in the first execution of a battle between groups of characters, the wrong groups attacked each other. In another early demo, some of the warriors at the edge of the field could be seen running away. They were initially moving in the wrong direction, and had been programmed to keep running until they encountered an enemy.</p>
<p>The digital creatures were important due to Jackson's requirement of biological plausibility. Their surface was scanned from large <a href="/wiki/Maquette" title="Maquette">maquettes</a> before numerous digital details of their skeletons and muscles were added. In the case of the Balrog, Gray Horsfield created a system that copied recorded imagery of fire.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Score">Score</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Score">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring_(Soundtrack)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring (Soundtrack)">The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring (Soundtrack)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote boilerplate seealso">See also: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_The_Lord_of_the_Rings_film_series" title="Music of The Lord of the Rings film series">Music of The Lord of the Rings film series</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">musical score</a> for <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> films was composed by <a href="/wiki/Howard_Shore" title="Howard Shore">Howard Shore</a>. It was performed by the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Symphony_Orchestra" title="New Zealand Symphony Orchestra">New Zealand Symphony Orchestra</a>, the <a href="/wiki/London_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="London Philharmonic Orchestra">London Philharmonic Orchestra</a>, the <a href="/wiki/London_Voices" title="London Voices">London Voices</a>, and featured several vocal soloists. Two original songs, "<a href="/wiki/An%C3%ADron" title="Aníron">Aníron</a>" and the end title theme "<a href="/wiki/May_It_Be" title="May It Be">May It Be</a>", were composed and sung by <a href="/wiki/Enya" title="Enya">Enya</a>, who allowed her label, <a href="/wiki/Reprise_Records" title="Reprise Records">Reprise Records</a>, to release the soundtrack to this and its two sequels. In addition to this, Shore composed "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_Dreams_(Howard_Shore_song)" title="In Dreams (Howard Shore song)">In Dreams</a>", which was sung by Edward Ross of the London Oratory School Schola.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote boilerplate seealso">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_considered_the_best" title="List of films considered the best">List of films considered the best</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> was released on 19 December 2001 in 3,359 cinemas where it grossed $47.2 million on its opening weekend. The World premiere was held at the <a href="/wiki/Odeon_Leicester_Square" title="Odeon Leicester Square">Odeon Leicester Square</a> in London. It went on to make $314.7 million in North America and $555.9 million in the rest of the world for a worldwide total of $870.7 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boxoffice_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-boxoffice-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> received universal critical acclaim from major film critics and was one of 2001's best reviewed films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The film holds a 92% "Fresh" rating on aggregate review site <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, based on 225 reviews, with an average score of 8.2/10. The site's main consensus reads "Full of eye-popping special effects, and featuring a pitch-perfect cast, <i>The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring</i> brings J.R.R. Tolkien's classic to vivid life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> The film holds a score of 92 out of 100 on <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, based on 34 reviews, indicating "Universal Acclaim".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> gave the film three out of four stars and wrote, "Peter Jackson... has made a work for, and of, our times. It will be embraced, I suspect, by many Tolkien fans and take on aspects of a cult. It is a candidate for many Oscars. It is an awesome production in its daring and breadth, and there are small touches that are just right".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> also gave the film three out of four stars and wrote, "this movie version of a beloved book should please devotees as well as the uninitiated".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Puig_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Puig-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> In his review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Mitchell" title="Elvis Mitchell">Elvis Mitchell</a> wrote, "The playful spookiness of Mr. Jackson's direction provides a lively, light touch, a gesture that doesn't normally come to mind when Tolkien's name is mentioned".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> magazine gave the film an "A" rating and Lisa Schwarzbaum wrote, "The cast take to their roles with becoming modesty, certainly, but Jackson also makes it easy for them: His <i>Fellowship</i> flows, never lingering for the sake of admiring its own beauty ... Every detail of which engrossed me. I may have never turned a page of Tolkien, but I know enchantment when I see it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarzbaum_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwarzbaum-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In her review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, Rita Kempley praised the cast, in particular, "Mortensen, as Strider, is a revelation, not to mention downright gorgeous. And McKellen, carrying the burden of thousands of years' worth of the fight against evil, is positively Merlinesque".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kempley_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kempley-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine's Richard Corliss praised Jackson's work: "His movie achieves what the best fairy tales do: the creation of an alternate world, plausible and persuasive, where the young — and not only the young — can lose themselves. And perhaps, in identifying with the little Hobbit that could, find their better selves".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corliss_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corliss-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> In his review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/J._Hoberman" title="J. Hoberman">J. Hoberman</a> wrote, "Peter Jackson's adaptation is certainly successful on its own terms".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoberman_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoberman-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's <a href="/wiki/Peter_Travers" title="Peter Travers">Peter Travers</a> wrote, "It's emotion that makes <i>Fellowship</i> stick hard in the memory ... Jackson deserves to revel in his success. He's made a three-hour film that leaves you wanting more".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travers_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Travers-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> However, in his review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, Peter Bradshaw wrote, "there is a strange paucity of plot complication, an absence of anything unfolding, all the more disconcerting because of the clotted and indigestible mythic back story that we have to wade through before anything happens at all".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradshaw_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradshaw-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/CinemaScore" title="CinemaScore">CinemaScore</a> polls conducted during the opening weekend, cinema audiences gave the film an average grade of "A-" on an A+ to F scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_accolades_received_by_The_Lord_of_the_Rings_film_series" title="List of accolades received by The Lord of the Rings film series">List of accolades received by The Lord of the Rings film series</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/74th_Academy_Awards" title="74th Academy Awards">2002</a>, the film won four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Awards</a> from thirteen nominations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oscars2002_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oscars2002-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> The winning categories were for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Cinematography" title="Academy Award for Best Cinematography">Best Cinematography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Best Visual Effects">Best Visual Effects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Makeup_and_Hairstyling" title="Academy Award for Best Makeup and Hairstyling">Best Makeup</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Score" title="Academy Award for Best Original Score">Best Original Score</a>. It was also nominated for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor">Best Actor in a Supporting Role</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ian_McKellen" title="Ian McKellen">Ian McKellen</a>), <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Production_Design" title="Academy Award for Best Production Design">Best Art Direction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Best Director</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Film_Editing" title="Academy Award for Best Film Editing">Best Film Editing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Song" title="Academy Award for Best Original Song">Best Original Song</a> (<a href="/wiki/Enya" title="Enya">Enya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicky_Ryan" title="Nicky Ryan">Nicky Ryan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roma_Ryan" title="Roma Ryan">Roma Ryan</a> for "<a href="/wiki/May_It_Be" title="May It Be">May It Be</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Best Picture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Sound" title="Academy Award for Best Sound">Best Sound</a> (<a href="/wiki/Christopher_Boyes" title="Christopher Boyes">Christopher Boyes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Semanick" title="Michael Semanick">Michael Semanick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gethin_Creagh" title="Gethin Creagh">Gethin Creagh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hammond_Peek" title="Hammond Peek">Hammond Peek</a>), <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Costume_Design" title="Academy Award for Best Costume Design">Best Costume Design</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Writing_(Adapted_Screenplay)" title="Academy Award for Best Writing (Adapted Screenplay)">Best Adapted Screenplay</a>.</p>
<p>As of January 2014, it is the 31st highest-grossing film worldwide, with US$871,530,324 in worldwide theatrical box office receipts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The film won the 2002 <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Dramatic_Presentation" title="Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation">Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation</a>. It also won <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">Empire</a></i> readers' Best Film award, as well as five <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">BAFTAs</a>, including <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Film" title="BAFTA Award for Best Film">Best Film</a>, the <a href="/wiki/David_Lean" title="David Lean">David Lean</a> Award for <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Direction" title="BAFTA Award for Best Direction">Best Direction</a>, the Audience Award (voted for by the public), <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Special_Visual_Effects" title="BAFTA Award for Best Special Visual Effects">Best Special Effects</a>, and <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Makeup_and_Hair" title="BAFTA Award for Best Makeup and Hair">Best Make-up</a>. The film was nominated for an <a href="/wiki/MTV_Movie_Award_for_Best_Fight" title="MTV Movie Award for Best Fight">MTV Movie Award for Best Fight</a> between Gandalf and Saruman.</p>
<p>In June 2008, AFI revealed its "10 Top 10"—the ten best films in ten "classic" American film genres—after polling over 1,500 people from the creative community. <i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> was acknowledged as the second best film in the fantasy genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatrical_and_extended_release">Theatrical and extended release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Theatrical and extended release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> on 6 August 2002.</p>
<p>On 12 November 2002, an extended edition was released on VHS and DVD, with 30 minutes of new material, added special effects and music, plus 20 minutes of fan-club credits, totalling to 219 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> The DVD set included four commentaries and over three hours of supplementary material.</p>
<p>On 29 August 2006, a limited edition of <i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> was released on DVD. The set included both the film's theatrical and extended editions on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DVD-9" title="DVD-9">double-sided disc</a> along with all-new bonus material.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blu-ray_edition">Blu-ray edition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Blu-ray edition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The theatrical <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> version of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> was released in the United States on 6 April 2010. There were two separate sets: one with digital copies and one without.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> The individual Blu-ray disc of <i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> was released on 14 September 2010 with the same special features as the complete trilogy release, except there was no digital copy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The extended Blu-ray editions were released in the U.S. on 28 June 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> This version has a runtime of 228 minutes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> (the extended editions include the names of all fan club members at the time of their release; the additional 9 minutes in the Blu-ray version are because of expanded member rolls, not any additional story material).</p>
