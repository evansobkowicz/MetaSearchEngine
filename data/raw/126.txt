<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><span dir="auto"><i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> in popular culture</span></h1>

<p>The 1952 novel <i><b><a href="/wiki/The_Catcher_in_the_Rye" title="The Catcher in the Rye">The Catcher in the Rye</a></b></i> by <a href="/wiki/J._D._Salinger" title="J. D. Salinger">J. D. Salinger</a> has had a lasting influence<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> as it remains both a <a href="/wiki/Bestseller" title="Bestseller">bestseller</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinn_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guinn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_most_commonly_challenged_books_in_the_U.S." title="List of most commonly challenged books in the U.S.">frequently challenged book</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinn_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guinn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Numerous works in <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a> have referenced the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SB_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SB-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Factors contributing to the novel's mystique and impact include its portrayal of protagonist <a href="/wiki/Holden_Caulfield" title="Holden Caulfield">Holden Caulfield</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WP-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> its tone of sincerity;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-WP-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> its themes of familial neglect,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> tension between teens and society,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinn_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guinn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and rebellion;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> its previous banned status;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and Salinger's reclusiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-WP-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> has inspired "rewrites" which have been said to form their own genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Menand_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Menand-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> On the other hand, there are examples of similarities between the novel and other works that were not intended by their authors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> which suggests that the novel is "present, at least spiritually, in ... any story line that involves quirky young people struggling to find their places in a society prone to reward conformity and condemn individuality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinn_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guinn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While the novel is linked to several murders and murder attempts, it has been claimed that the novel's overall effect on society is "far more positive than negative."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinn_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Guinn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The novel also helped popularize the slang verb "screw up".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In recent years there has been a discussion of depression as exhibited in Holden Caulfield. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shootings">Shootings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Catcher_in_the_Rye_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Shootings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most well-known event associated with <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> is arguably <a href="/wiki/Mark_David_Chapman" title="Mark David Chapman">Mark David Chapman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Death_of_John_Lennon" title="Death of John Lennon">shooting of John Lennon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Chapman identified with the novel's narrator to the extent that he wanted to change his name to Holden Caulfield. On the night he shot Lennon, Chapman was found with a copy of the book in which he had written "<u>This</u> is my statement" and signed Holden's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Later, he read a passage from the novel to address the court during his sentencing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitfield.2C_571.E2.80.93572_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitfield.2C_571.E2.80.93572-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Daniel Stashower speculated that Chapman had wanted Lennon's innocence to be preserved by death, inspired by Holden's wish to preserve children's innocence despite Holden's later realization that children should be left alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitfield.2C_571.E2.80.93572_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whitfield.2C_571.E2.80.93572-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/John_Hinckley,_Jr." title="John Hinckley, Jr.">John Hinckley, Jr.</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_assassination_attempt" title="Reagan assassination attempt">assassination attempt</a> on <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> in 1981, police found <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> among half a dozen other books in his hotel room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_John_Bardo" title="Robert John Bardo">Robert John Bardo</a>, who murdered <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Schaeffer" title="Rebecca Schaeffer">Rebecca Schaeffer</a>, was carrying the book when he visited Schaeffer's apartment in Hollywood on July 18, 1989 and murdered her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Films">Films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Catcher_in_the_Rye_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Salinger had refused a <a href="/wiki/Film_adaptation" title="Film adaptation">film adaptation</a>, many Hollywood films have based characters on Holden Caulfield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNT_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-PNT-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Holden has been identified as "one of the most reproduced characters on film." Furthermore, many such films reference each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onstad_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Onstad-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Anthony Caputi, a specialist in dramatic literature at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, claims that the novel inspires both "variations" and "imitations", comparing it with several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coming-of-age" title="Coming-of-age">coming-of-age</a> films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Collector" title="The Collector">The Collector</a></i> (1965), which is based on the <a href="/wiki/John_Fowles" title="John Fowles">John Fowles</a> novel, Clegg cannot understand why Miranda likes the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In the 1990 play and 1993 film <i><a href="/wiki/Six_Degrees_of_Separation_(film)" title="Six Degrees of Separation (film)">Six Degrees of Separation</a></i>, the impostor Paul gives an analysis on the novel in a monologue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> According to him, the novel, a "manifesto of hate" against phonies, would have been the excuse<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> or defense<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> for Chapman and Hinckley's shootings.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Chasing_Amy" title="Chasing Amy">Chasing Amy</a></i> (1997) has two clear connections with the novel, the main character (played by <a href="/wiki/Ben_Affleck" title="Ben Affleck">Ben Affleck</a>) is named Holden. In addition to his name, he also shares the angsty qualities of Holden Caulfield. The other connection comes from his best friend in the film (played by <a href="/wiki/Jason_Lee_(actor)" title="Jason Lee (actor)">Jason Lee</a>), who is named Banky Edwards. His name derives from Ed Banky, a coach at Pencey Prep that gives his car to athletes so they can use it to have sex with their girlfriends.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_Theory_(film)" title="Conspiracy Theory (film)">Conspiracy Theory</a></i> (1997), Mel Gibson's character is programmed to buy the novel whenever he sees it, though he never actually reads it. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chasing_Holden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chasing Holden (page does not exist)">Chasing Holden</a></i> (2001) is named after Holden Caulfield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The protagonist Neil relates his life to Holden's, skips class to go to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, goes on a road trip to <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> to find Salinger, and contemplates killing Salinger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Screenwriter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mike_White_(scriptwriter)" title="Mike White (scriptwriter)">Mike White</a> regards the novel as "part of a literary trend that goes back to Goethe's '<a href="/wiki/The_Sorrows_of_Young_Werther" title="The Sorrows of Young Werther">The Sorrows of Werther</a>' (1774) ... I don't think Salinger discovered it. He just did the quintessential American version."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> He thought the influence of the novel may rise in Hollywood,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and two of his 2002 films reflect this. In <i><a href="/wiki/Orange_County_(film)" title="Orange County (film)">Orange County</a></i>, protagonist Shaun searches for the professor who wrote the book that changed his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Good_Girl" title="The Good Girl">The Good Girl</a></i>, protagonist Thomas Worther calls himself Holden and is seen reading the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNT_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-PNT-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Igby_Goes_Down" title="Igby Goes Down">Igby Goes Down</a></i> (2002), originally intended to be a novel, has been interpreted as being inspired by <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNT_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-PNT-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onstad_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Onstad-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> but director and screenwriter <a href="/wiki/Burr_Steers" title="Burr Steers">Burr Steers</a> said it is not a direct influence and the story is more of an autobiography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> On the influence of the book, Steers "liken[s] it to being a musician and being influenced by the music ingrained in you, like <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Chapter_27" title="Chapter 27">Chapter 27</a></i> (2007), a biographical film depicting the assassination of John Lennon by Mark David Chapman.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Catcher_in_the_Rye_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The main plot for the Japanese anime television series <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_in_the_Shell:_Stand_Alone_Complex" title="Ghost in the Shell: Stand Alone Complex">Ghost in the Shell: Stand Alone Complex</a></i> (2002) hinges on elements from <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> from which a character uses a digital logo representing Holden Caulfield, and the quote "I thought what I'd do was, I'd pretend I was one of those deaf-mutes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> These themes and some additional quotes are also expressed in <i>Stand Alone Complex 2nd GIG</i> (2004), although the characters and plot have changed.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Scrotie_McBoogerballs" title="The Tale of Scrotie McBoogerballs">The Tale of Scrotie McBoogerballs</a>" (2010) makes particular reference to the <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> and shows that its once controversial use of vocabulary has no impact among young readers nowadays. It also references its connection towards the series of high-profile assassination attempts in the 1980s, with character <a href="/wiki/Butters_Stotch" title="Butters Stotch">Butters Stotch</a> expressing a desire to kill John Lennon after reading the book until his father assures him that Lennon was already killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Catcher_in_the_Rye_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Action-adventure_game" title="Action-adventure game">action-adventure game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Bully_(video_game)" title="Bully (video game)">Bully</a></i> (2006), the main protagonist, Jimmy Hopkins, shares a lot of traits with Holden Caulfield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Postal_2" title="Postal 2">Postal 2</a></i> (1997), Postal Dude's wife asked the Dude to retrieve a book initialed "Catch Her in the Rye" written by B.J. Dillinger this is a obviously a parody on the book and its writer, J.D. Salinger.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Catcher_in_the_Rye_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Fowles" title="John Fowles">John Fowles</a>'s 1963 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Collector" title="The Collector">The Collector</a></i> uses <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> as "one of the most brilliant examples of adolescence" in popular culture, possibly under a moral light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> In it, Miranda encourages her kidnapper Clegg to read <i>Catcher</i>, thinking he might relate to Holden's alienation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> However, Clegg finds Holden's actions unrealistic given Holden's wealth and status, and "[doesn't] see much point in it." In the film adaptation of <i>The Collector</i>, this conversation and Clegg's attitude toward the novel and popular culture is subdued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> The novel has itself been linked to several serial killers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Block" title="Lawrence Block">Lawrence Block</a> wrote a novel called <i>Burglar in the Rye</i> (1999) in his series on burglar <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Rhodenbarr" title="Bernie Rhodenbarr">Bernie Rhodenbarr</a>. The plot focuses on an auction of a reclusive writer's letters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> and Bernie works to track down the character based on J. D. Salinger.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Galt_Niederhoffer" title="Galt Niederhoffer">Galt Niederhoffer</a>'s novel <i>A Taxonomy of Barnacles</i> (2005), Bridget and Billy think about Holden's question as to the whereabouts of ducks during winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/King_Dork" title="King Dork">King Dork</a></i> (2006) is a young adult novel by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Portman" title="Frank Portman">Frank Portman</a> in which protagonist and disaffected slacker Tom Henderson copes with what he terms the cult of <i>Catcher in the Rye</i>, among other annoyances; discovering that his deceased father possessed a copy along with <i><a href="/wiki/Brighton_Rock_(novel)" title="Brighton Rock (novel)">Brighton Rock</a></i>, he is led to explore his fathers life and death.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_David_California" title="John David California">John David California</a> wrote <i>60 Years Later: Coming Through the Rye</i> (2009), an unauthorized sequel in which seventy-six-year-old Holden escapes a retirement home for a journey in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_strips">Comic strips</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Catcher_in_the_Rye_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Comic strips">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Frazz" title="Frazz">Frazz</a></i> character Caulfield is named after Holden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Catcher_in_the_Rye_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Ataris" title="The Ataris">The Ataris</a>' song "If You Really Want to Hear About It" from their album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End_is_Forever" title="End is Forever">End is Forever</a></i> takes its title from the novel's opening sentence. The final lines paraphrase those of the book with "Don't ever tell anyone anything or else you'll wind up missing everybody." Several other specific references are made within the lyrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Billy_Joel" title="Billy Joel">Billy Joel</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/We_Didn%27t_Start_the_Fire" title="We Didn't Start the Fire">We Didn't Start the Fire</a>" mentions the novel as a historic item of note during his lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Guns_N%27_Roses" title="Guns N' Roses">Guns N' Roses</a> released a song with the same name on their 2008 album <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Democracy" title="Chinese Democracy">Chinese Democracy</a>.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Retrospective_Soundtrack_Players&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Retrospective Soundtrack Players (page does not exist)">The Retrospective Soundtrack Players</a> released a concept album based around the novel in 2012.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">Green Day</a> released the song "Who Wrote Holden Caulfield?" on their 1992 album <i><a href="/wiki/Kerplunk_(album)" title="Kerplunk (album)">Kerplunk</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Screeching_Weasel" title="Screeching Weasel">Screeching Weasel</a> released the song "I Wrote Holden Caulfield" on their 1994 album <i><a href="/wiki/How_to_Make_Enemies_and_Irritate_People" title="How to Make Enemies and Irritate People">How to Make Enemies and Irritate People</a></i> as a response to the Green Day song "Who Wrote Holden Caulfield?".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Belle_and_Sebastian" title="Belle and Sebastian">Belle and Sebastian</a>'s song "Le pastie de la bourgeoisie" finishes with this line: "Give yourself up to the allure of 'Catcher in The Rye', the future's swathed in stars and stripes."</li>
</ul>
