<div class="body " itemprop="articleBody">
<figure class="inline-image clearfix ">
<img alt="Bee Gees (Pic:Rex)" class="captify" height="409" src="http://i1.mirror.co.uk/incoming/article204207.ece/alternates/s615/bee-gees-pic-rex-image-2-812686876.jpg" title="" width="615"/>
</figure>
<p>Bad blood, bust-ups and bitter barneys – if you thought the Gallaghers had a monopoly on unbrotherly love within bands you’re doing an injustice to the Bee Gees.</p>
<p>Long before Oasis had even plugged in a guitar, the world-famous Gibb brothers had won a reputation for terrible in-fighting which at one point caused the group to split.</p>
<p>Today, in the second of our Bee Gees series to celebrate their 50 years in music, Barry and Robin admit their feuding at times drove their helpless parents to despair.</p>
<p><strong>Warring</strong></p>
<p>They reveal how they eventually put aside the egotism which was destroying them. And they talk about why, almost seven years on, it is crucial for them to pick up the pieces after Maurice’s death and re-form as a duo.</p>
<p>Speaking exclusively to the Mirror at Miami’s Criteria Studios, where they feel so at home, it is clear that the pair have put any personal differences to one side.</p>
<p>Barry and Robin chat, banter and laugh at shared memories. But for years these two set the template for the sort of feuding seen between the warring Gallaghers.</p>
<p>Understandably, the recent Oasis split has resonated with the Gibbs. And they have some advice for Liam and Noel, who have always cited the early Bee Gees albums as a huge musical influence.</p>
<p>“They’re just copying us,” says Robin, 59. “Oh, we’ve been through all that. To be honest, I’m surprised they stayed together for as long as they did and I think they’ve gone as far as they can together.”</p>
<p>Do the Gallaghers resemble the Gibbs?</p>
<p>‘They remind us of ourselves, but on a more physically violent level,” admits Barry, 63. “We’ve been there and we came full circle.”</p>
<p>These days, Barry and Robin’s differences are a source of creativity rather than conflict.</p>
<p>Barry says: “Robin and I are both individuals but together we fire each other up. And if we can ignore the differences we have apart from music, we get the job done.</p>
<p>“Apart, I don’t know who I am.”</p>
<p>Robin adds: “I don’t know who I am either. But I know who I am when we’re together performing. It’s just there.</p>
<p>“Whatever happens, how Barry thinks and feels will always be important to me because he’s part of my life. We created all of this.”</p>
<p>It’s a pity it has taken them so long to come to such an understanding. Their first big fall-out was in 1969 when the Bee Gees were writing their operatic masterpiece, Odessa.</p>
<p>At the time they were enjoying international fame and fortune following their hit albums, Bee Gees’ 1st, Horizontal and Idea. They were young – Barry was not long out of his teens while twins Robin and Maurice were 19 – and success went to their heads.</p>
<p>Producer and mentor Robert Stigwood warned them to stay grounded but it wasn’t long before egos clashed and the once-close brothers had reached a bitter deadlock.</p>
<p>Something had to give. So Robin quit.</p>
<p>Barry says: “It wasn’t until Odessa that the rot really set in. The cracks began to show. It was impossible to keep our feet on the ground because we really were making it.</p>
<p>“We were young but we were wearing ourselves pretty thin and we were worn out.</p>
<p>“We wanted to do this rock opera but it ran into a brick wall. Mentally and emotionally, we were exhausted. We had stopped communicating and things went downhill.</p>
<p>“We were becoming increasingly fragmented as personalities and more and more individual as opposed to three brothers glued together. Each of us were very alike but we’ve also always been very much unalike.</p>
<p>“We were developing relationships and beginning families and that changed our perspective. And just as we would later experience in the 70s, we had too much.”</p>
<p>Through the media they began a public slanging match. Barry accused Robin of having a “very, very big persecution complex”. Robin said he would never entertain “on any account” the idea of a reunion. Maurice was caught between his more petulant twin and his hot-headed elder brother. When he suggested the</p>
<p>Bee Gees could one day reunite, Barry declared: “If he [Robin] walks back into the group, I walk out.”</p>
<p>Today, Barry and Robin can (just about) laugh it all off. “The Beatles had just split,” grins Barry. “So that became the thing to do.”</p>
<p>But at the time it was no laughing matter. Least of all for mum Barbara and dad Hugh.</p>
<p>“Of course it upset our parents,” says Robin. “We have always been the subject of great concern to our parents. They couldn’t understand because what was a huge problem for us seemed like nothing to them. They thought we should all get on.</p>
<p>“We’re talking about three very different guys here who had total disregard for what was important, a total disregard for money. We wanted to be doing our own thing.”</p>
<p>And that’s what they did. Robin’s debut solo single, Saved By The Bell, peaked at No.2 in the spring of 1969. Barry and Maurice also had a No.2 hit with Don’t Forget to Remember.</p>
<p>Barry recalls an awkward meeting in a BBC corridor when he and Maurice (as the Bee Gees) and Robin (as a soloist) were filming Top of the Pops. They barely said hello.</p>
<p>After 18 months apart, hostilities had calmed and Robin approached Barry about the possibility of getting back together.</p>
<p>He says: “It wasn’t the same going solo. It was horrible, a sham. I was miserable. I could never envisage composing with anyone but Barry. He brings out the best in me.”</p>
<p><strong>Disco</strong></p>
<p>The initial reunion proved to be a purple patch, spawning How Can You Mend a Broken Heart? which became the Bee Gees’ first US No.1. Then a move to Miami led to a change in musical direction, under producer Arif Mardin, towards a blend of soulful disco and R&amp;B. It led to Saturday Night Fever – still the biggest selling soundtrack of all time.</p>
<p>“Perhaps we needed that time away,” says Robin. “The split happened organically. We were getting all this attention and something had to give. Nature is what happened.”</p>
<p>While the brothers’ mutual respect and admiration is clear, you suspect that they will never be bosom buddies. But together in the studio, their chemistry is indisputable.</p>
<p>Currently working on new material, they are keen to recapture the first flush of energy which produced such perfect pop.</p>
<p>Barry says: “We’ll be really happy again together if we can find the place we were at, mentally and emotionally, before we met Robert Stigwood.</p>
<p>“And that’s not a criticism of Robert. But that was a time when we still had our naiveté and innocence.”</p>
<p>Barry and Robin aren’t too old – or too famous – to be put in their place by their mum. Barbara Gibb, 89 this month, is still a formidable force.</p>
<p>“She’s not bungee jumping but we wouldn’t put it past her,” says Robin. “She’s very quick-witted and sharp. Tough as old boots.”</p>
<p>Barry adds: “To mum we’re still kids. She’ll say: ‘Why don’t you talk to each other? Just give him a call!’ She can still tell us off.” we’re mummy’s boys</p>
</div>
