<p><b>Lewis Brian Hopkin Jones</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> (28 February 1942 – 3 July 1969) was the founder and original <a href="/wiki/Bandleader" title="Bandleader">bandleader</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">the Rolling Stones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wyman_.26_Coleman_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wyman_.26_Coleman-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Jones was a multi-instrumentalist, with his main instruments being the guitar, harmonica and keyboards. His innovative use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_music" title="Traditional music">traditional or folk instruments</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sitar" title="Sitar">sitar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marimba" title="Marimba">marimba</a>, was integral to the changing sound of the band.</p>
<p>Although he was originally the leader of the group, Jones's fellow band members <a href="/wiki/Mick_Jagger" title="Mick Jagger">Mick Jagger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keith_Richards" title="Keith Richards">Keith Richards</a> soon overshadowed him, especially after they became a successful <a href="/wiki/Jagger/Richards" title="Jagger/Richards">songwriting team</a>. He developed a serious drug problem over the years and his role in the band steadily diminished. He was asked to leave the Rolling Stones in June 1969 and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Mick_Taylor" title="Mick Taylor">Mick Taylor</a> took his place in the group. Jones died less than a month later by drowning in the swimming pool at his home on Cotchford Farm in <a href="/wiki/Hartfield" title="Hartfield">Hartfield</a>, East Sussex.</p>
<p>Original Rolling Stones bassist <a href="/wiki/Bill_Wyman" title="Bill Wyman">Bill Wyman</a> said of Jones, "He formed the band. He chose the members. He named the band. He chose the music we played. He got us gigs. ... Very influential, very important, and then slowly lost it – highly intelligent – and just kind of wasted it and blew it all away."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_paternity">Early life and paternity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and paternity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jones was born in the Park Nursing Home in <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>, Gloucestershire, on 28 February 1942. An attack of <a href="/wiki/Croup" title="Croup">croup</a> at the age of four left him with <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, which lasted for the rest of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp10p16_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp10p16-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> His middle-class parents, Lewis Blount Jones and Louisa Beatrice Jones (née Simmonds) were of Welsh descent. Brian had two sisters: Pamela, who was born on 3 October 1943 and who died on 14 October 1945 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leukaemia" title="Leukaemia">leukaemia</a>; and Barbara, born on 22 August 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp10p16_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp10p16-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Both Jones's parents were interested in music: his mother Louisa was a piano teacher, and in addition to his job as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeronautical_engineer" title="Aeronautical engineer">aeronautical engineer</a>, Lewis Jones played piano and <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organ</a> and led the choir at the local church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp10p16_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp10p16-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1957 Jones first heard <a href="/wiki/Cannonball_Adderley" title="Cannonball Adderley">Cannonball Adderley</a>'s music, which inspired his interest in jazz. Jones persuaded his parents to buy him a saxophone, and two years later his parents gave him his first acoustic guitar as a 17th birthday present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp23_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp23-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Jones attended local schools, including <a href="/wiki/Dean_Close_School" title="Dean Close School">Dean Close School</a>, from September 1949 to July 1953 and <a href="/wiki/Pate%27s_Grammar_School" title="Pate's Grammar School">Cheltenham Grammar School for Boys</a>, which he entered in September 1953 after passing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleven-plus" title="Eleven-plus">Eleven-plus</a> exam. He enjoyed badminton and diving at school and attained first <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a> in the school orchestra. In 1957 he reportedly obtained seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O-levels" title="O-levels">O-level</a> passes, then he continued into the <a href="/wiki/Sixth_form" title="Sixth form">sixth form</a> and obtained a further two O-levels. He also took three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-levels" title="A-levels">A-levels</a> in Physics, Chemistry and Biology and passed in Physics and Chemistry, but failed in Biology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp19_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp19-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Jones had an IQ of 135 and was able to perform well in exams despite a lack of academic effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp19_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp19-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> However, he found school regimented and disliked conforming. He disliked the school uniforms and angered teachers with his behaviour, though he was popular with classmates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp19_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp19-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Jones himself said: "When I made the sixth form I found myself accepted by the older boys; suddenly I was in."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp19_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp19-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>His hostility to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authority_figure" title="Authority figure">authority figures</a> resulted in his suspension from school on two occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp19_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp19-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Dick Hattrell, a childhood friend: "He was a rebel without a cause, but when examinations came he was brilliant."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp19_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp19-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In late summer 1959, Jones's 17-year-old girlfriend, a Cheltenham schoolgirl named Valerie Corbett, became pregnant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Although Jones is said to have encouraged her to have an abortion, she carried the child to term and placed baby Barry David (later Simon) for adoption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp19_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp19-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Jones quit school in disgrace and left home, travelling for a summer through Northern Europe and Scandinavia. During this period, he lived a <a href="/wiki/Bohemianism" title="Bohemianism">bohemian</a> lifestyle, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busking" title="Busking">busking</a> with his guitar on the streets for money, and living off the charity of others. Eventually, he ran short of money and returned to England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp28_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp28-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Jones listened to classical music as a child, but preferred <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Elmore_James" title="Elmore James">Elmore James</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Johnson_(musician)" title="Robert Johnson (musician)">Robert Johnson</a>. He began performing at local blues and jazz clubs, while busking and working odd jobs. He reportedly stole small amounts of money from work to pay for cigarettes, for which he was fired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp35-36_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp35-36-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1959, Jones went to the Wooden Bridge Hotel in <a href="/wiki/Guildford" title="Guildford">Guildford</a> to see a band perform. He met a young married woman named Angeline, and the two had a one-night stand that resulted in her pregnancy. Angeline and her husband decided to raise the baby, Belinda, born on 4 August the following year. Jones never knew about her birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp28_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp28-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961, Jones applied for a scholarship to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Art_College" title="Cheltenham Art College">Cheltenham Art College</a>. He was initially accepted into the programme, but two days later the offer was withdrawn after an unidentified acquaintance wrote to the college, calling Jones an irresponsible drifter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp29_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp29-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On 23 October 1961, Jones's girlfriend Pat Andrews gave birth to his third child, Julian Mark Andrews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp30-31_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp30-31-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Jones sold his record collection to buy flowers for Pat and clothes for the newborn. He lived with them for a while. On 23 July 1964, another woman, Linda Lawrence, gave birth to Jones's fourth child, named Julian Brian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> In early October 1964, an occasional girlfriend of Brian's, Dawn Molloy, announced to Brian and the band's management that she was pregnant by Brian. She received a cheque for £700 from Andrew Loog Oldham, LTD. In return, she signed an agreement that the matter was now closed and she would make no statement about Brian Jones or the child to the public or the press. The undated statement was signed by Malloy and witnessed by Mick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In March 1965 Dawn gave birth to Brian's fifth child Paul Molloy, renamed John Maynard by his adoptive parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forming_the_Rolling_Stones">Forming the Rolling Stones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forming the Rolling Stones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jones left Cheltenham and moved to London where he became friends with fellow musicians <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Korner" title="Alexis Korner">Alexis Korner</a>, future <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Mann" title="Manfred Mann">Manfred Mann</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Paul_Jones_(singer)" title="Paul Jones (singer)">Paul Jones</a>, future <a href="/wiki/Cream_(band)" title="Cream (band)">Cream</a> bassist <a href="/wiki/Jack_Bruce" title="Jack Bruce">Jack Bruce</a> and others who made up the small London <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a> and jazz scene there. He became a blues musician, for a brief time calling himself "Elmo Lewis", and playing <a href="/wiki/Slide_guitar" title="Slide guitar">slide guitar</a>. Jones also started a group with Paul Jones called the <i>Roosters</i> and in January 1963, after both Brian and Paul left the group, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">Eric Clapton</a> took over Brian's position as guitarist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Jones placed an advertisement in <i>Jazz News</i> (a Soho club information sheet) of 2 May 1962 inviting musicians to audition for a new R&amp;B group at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bricklayer%27s_Arms" title="Bricklayer's Arms">Bricklayer's Arms</a> pub; pianist <a href="/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(musician)" title="Ian Stewart (musician)">Ian "Stu" Stewart</a> was the first to respond. Later singer <a href="/wiki/Mick_Jagger" title="Mick Jagger">Mick Jagger</a> also joined this band; Jagger and his childhood friend <a href="/wiki/Keith_Richards" title="Keith Richards">Keith Richards</a> had met Jones when he and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Jones_(singer)" title="Paul Jones (singer)">Paul Jones</a> were playing <a href="/wiki/Elmore_James" title="Elmore James">Elmore James</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Dust_My_Broom" title="Dust My Broom">Dust My Broom</a>" with Korner's band at the <a href="/wiki/Ealing_Jazz_Club" title="Ealing Jazz Club">Ealing Jazz Club</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp32_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp32-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Jagger brought guitarist Richards to rehearsals; Richards then joined the band. Jones's and Stewart's acceptance of Richards and the <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Berry" title="Chuck Berry">Chuck Berry</a> songs he wanted to play coincided with the departure of blues purists <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Bradford_(musician)" title="Geoff Bradford (musician)">Geoff Bradford</a> and Brian Knight, who had no tolerance for Chuck Berry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp35-36_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp35-36-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As Keith Richards tells it, Jones came up with the name the "Rollin' Stones" (later with the 'g') while on the phone with a venue owner. "The voice on the other end of the line obviously said, 'What are you called?' Panic. <i>The Best of Muddy Waters</i> album was lying on the floor—and track five, side one was 'Rollin' Stone'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaggerp0_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-jaggerp0-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2010)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Rollin' Stones played their first gig on 12 July 1962 in the <a href="/wiki/Marquee_Club" title="Marquee Club">Marquee Club</a> in London with Jagger, Richards, Jones, Stewart, <a href="/wiki/Bassist" title="Bassist">bass player</a> <a href="/wiki/Dick_Taylor" title="Dick Taylor">Dick Taylor</a> (later of <a href="/wiki/The_Pretty_Things" title="The Pretty Things">the Pretty Things</a>) and drummer <a href="/wiki/Tony_Chapman" title="Tony Chapman">Tony Chapman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karnbach-p57-58_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-karnbach-p57-58-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>From September 1962 to September 1963 Jones, Jagger and Richards shared a flat (referred to by Richards as "a beautiful dump")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaggerp37_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-jaggerp37-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> at 102 Edith Grove, <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>, with James Phelge, a future photographer whose name was used in some of the group's early "Nanker/Phelge" writing credits. Jones and Richards spent day after day playing guitar while listening to blues records (notably <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Reed" title="Jimmy Reed">Jimmy Reed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muddy_Waters" title="Muddy Waters">Muddy Waters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willie_Dixon" title="Willie Dixon">Willie Dixon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Howlin%27_Wolf" title="Howlin' Wolf">Howlin' Wolf</a>). During this time, Jones also taught Jagger how to play harmonica.</p>
<p>The four Rollin' Stones went searching for a bassist and drummer, finally settling on <a href="/wiki/Bill_Wyman" title="Bill Wyman">Bill Wyman</a> on bass because he had a spare <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VOX_AC30" title="VOX AC30">VOX AC30</a> <a href="/wiki/Guitar_amplifier" title="Guitar amplifier">guitar amplifier</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keith_Richards_.27Life_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keith_Richards_.27Life-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> and always had cigarettes, as well as a bass guitar that he had built himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> After playing with <a href="/wiki/Mick_Avory" title="Mick Avory">Mick Avory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Chapman" title="Tony Chapman">Tony Chapman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Little" title="Carlo Little">Carlo Little</a>, in January 1963 they finally persuaded jazz-influenced <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Watts" title="Charlie Watts">Charlie Watts</a> to join them. At the time, Watts was considered by fellow musicians to be one of the better drummers in London; he had played with (among others) Alexis Korner's group <a href="/wiki/Blues_Incorporated" title="Blues Incorporated">Blues Incorporated</a>.</p>
<p>Watts described Jones's role in these early days: "Brian was very instrumental in pushing the band at the beginning. Keith and I would look at him and say he was barmy. It was a crusade to him to get us on the stage in a club and be paid half-a-<a href="/wiki/Crown_(British_coin)" title="Crown (British coin)">crown</a> and to be billed as an R&amp;B band".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaggerp0_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-jaggerp0-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2010)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>While acting as the band's business manager, Jones received £5 more than the other members, which did not sit well with the rest of the band and created resentment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaggerp0_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-jaggerp0-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2010)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Keith Richards has said that both he and Mick were surprised to learn that Brian considered himself the leader and was receiving the extra £5, especially as other people, like <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Gomelsky" title="Giorgio Gomelsky">Giorgio Gomelsky</a>, appeared to be doing the booking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_contributions">Musical contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Musical contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rolling_Stones_band_1965.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="549" data-file-width="800" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Rolling_Stones_band_1965.jpg/275px-Rolling_Stones_band_1965.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Rolling_Stones_band_1965.jpg/413px-Rolling_Stones_band_1965.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Rolling_Stones_band_1965.jpg/550px-Rolling_Stones_band_1965.jpg 2x" width="275"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rolling_Stones_band_1965.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Jones's main guitar in the early years was a <a href="/wiki/Harmony_Company" title="Harmony Company">Harmony</a> Stratotone, which he replaced with a <a href="/wiki/Gretsch" title="Gretsch">Gretsch</a> Double Anniversary in two-tone green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1964 and 1965, he often used a teardrop-shaped prototype <a href="/wiki/Vox_(musical_equipment)" title="Vox (musical equipment)">Vox Mark III</a>. From late 1965 until his death, Jones used <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Guitar_Corporation" title="Gibson Guitar Corporation">Gibson</a> models (various Firebirds, ES-330, and a <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Les_Paul" title="Gibson Les Paul">Les Paul model</a>), as well as two <a href="/wiki/Rickenbacker" title="Rickenbacker">Rickenbacker</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_string_guitar" title="Twelve string guitar">12-string</a> models. He can also be seen playing a Fender Telecaster in the 1968 "<a href="/wiki/Jumpin%27_Jack_Flash" title="Jumpin' Jack Flash">Jumpin' Jack Flash</a>" promo video.</p>
<p>Examples of Jones's contributions are his slide guitar on "<a href="/wiki/I_Wanna_Be_Your_Man" title="I Wanna Be Your Man">I Wanna Be Your Man</a>" (1963), "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_a_King_Bee" title="I'm a King Bee">I'm a King Bee</a>" (1964, on the <i>Rolling Stones</i>), "<a href="/wiki/Little_Red_Rooster" title="Little Red Rooster">Little Red Rooster</a>" (1964), "I Can't Be Satisfied" (1965, on <i>Rolling Stones No. 2</i>), "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I%27m_Movin%27_On_(Hank_Snow_song)" title="I'm Movin' On (Hank Snow song)">I'm Movin' On</a>" (1965, on the EP <i>Got Live If You Want It!</i>), "Doncha Bother Me" (1966, on <i>Aftermath</i>) and "<a href="/wiki/No_Expectations" title="No Expectations">No Expectations</a>" (1968, on <i>Beggars Banquet</i>). Jones can also be heard playing Bo Diddley-style rhythm guitar on "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Need_You_Baby_(Mona)" title="I Need You Baby (Mona)">I Need You Baby</a>", the guitar riff in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Last_Time_(song)" title="The Last Time (song)">"The Last Time"</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> sitar on "<a href="/wiki/Street_Fighting_Man" title="Street Fighting Man">Street Fighting Man</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paint_It,_Black" title="Paint It, Black">Paint It, Black</a>"; organ on "<a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Spend_the_Night_Together" title="Let's Spend the Night Together">Let's Spend the Night Together</a>"; <a href="/wiki/Marimba" title="Marimba">marimba</a> on "<a href="/wiki/Under_My_Thumb" title="Under My Thumb">Under My Thumb</a>", "Out of Time" and "<a href="/wiki/Yesterday%27s_Papers" title="Yesterday's Papers">Yesterday's Papers</a>"; <a href="/wiki/Recorder_(musical_instrument)" title="Recorder (musical instrument)">recorder</a> on "<a href="/wiki/Ruby_Tuesday_(song)" title="Ruby Tuesday (song)">Ruby Tuesday</a>" and "All Sold Out"; trumpet on "Child of the Moon", saxophone on "Citadel"; <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_dulcimer" title="Appalachian dulcimer">Appalachian dulcimer</a> on "I Am Waiting" and "<a href="/wiki/Lady_Jane_(song)" title="Lady Jane (song)">Lady Jane</a>", <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">mellotron</a> on "<a href="/wiki/She%27s_a_Rainbow" title="She's a Rainbow">She's a Rainbow</a>", "<a href="/wiki/We_Love_You" title="We Love You">We Love You</a>", "Stray Cat Blues", "<a href="/wiki/2000_Light_Years_from_Home" title="2000 Light Years from Home">2000 Light Years from Home</a>, and "Citadel"; and (for his final recording as a Rolling Stone) the <a href="/wiki/Autoharp" title="Autoharp">autoharp</a> on "<a href="/wiki/You_Got_the_Silver" title="You Got the Silver">You Got the Silver</a>".</p>
<p>Jones also played harmonica on many of the Rolling Stones' early songs. Examples of Jones's playing are on "<a href="/wiki/Stoned_(The_Rolling_Stones_song)" title="Stoned (The Rolling Stones song)">Stoned</a>" (1963), "<a href="/wiki/Not_Fade_Away_(song)" title="Not Fade Away (song)">Not Fade Away</a>" (1964), "<a href="/wiki/I_Just_Want_to_Make_Love_to_You" title="I Just Want to Make Love to You">I Just Want to Make Love to You</a>", "Now I've Got A Witness" (1964)" (from <i>The Rolling Stones</i>), "Good Times, Bad Times" (1964), "<a href="/wiki/2120_South_Michigan_Avenue" title="2120 South Michigan Avenue">2120 South Michigan Avenue</a>" (1964) (from E.P. <i>Five By Five</i>), "The Under Assistant West Coast Promotion Man", "One More Try" (1965) (from <i>Out of Our Heads</i>), "High and Dry" and "<a href="/wiki/Goin%27_Home_(The_Rolling_Stones_song)" title="Goin' Home (The Rolling Stones song)">Goin' Home</a>" (1966) (from <i>Aftermath</i>), "Who's Driving Your Plane?" (1966), "Cool Calm and Collected", "Who's Been Sleeping Here" (1967) (from <i>Between The Buttons</i>), and "<a href="/wiki/Dear_Doctor_(song)" title="Dear Doctor (song)">Dear Doctor</a>" and "Prodigal Son" (1968) (from <i>Beggars Banquet</i>).</p>
<p>In the early years, Jones often served as a backing vocalist. Notable examples are "<a href="/wiki/Come_On_(Chuck_Berry_song)" title="Come On (Chuck Berry song)">Come On</a>", "I Wanna Be Your Man", "I Just Wanna Make Love to You", "<a href="/wiki/Walking_the_Dog" title="Walking the Dog">Walking the Dog</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Money_(That%27s_What_I_Want)" title="Money (That's What I Want)">Money</a>", "I'm Alright", "<a href="/wiki/You_Better_Move_On_(song)" title="You Better Move On (song)">You Better Move On</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_All_Over_Now" title="It's All Over Now">It's All Over Now</a>". He contributed backing vocals as late as 1968 on "<a href="/wiki/Sympathy_for_the_Devil" title="Sympathy for the Devil">Sympathy for the Devil</a>". He is also responsible for the whistling on "Walking the Dog".</p>
<p>Richards maintains that what he calls "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_guitar_players&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Multiple guitar players (page does not exist)">guitar weaving</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> emerged from this period, from listening to <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Reed" title="Jimmy Reed">Jimmy Reed</a> albums: "We listened to the teamwork, trying to work out what was going on in those records; how you could play together with two guitars and make it sound like four or five".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaggerp0_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-jaggerp0-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2010)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Jones's and Richards's guitars became a signature of the sound of the Rolling Stones, with both guitarists playing rhythm and lead without clear boundaries between the two roles.</p>
<p>His aptitude for playing a wide variety of instruments is particularly evident on the albums <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aftermath_(Rolling_Stones_album)" title="Aftermath (Rolling Stones album)">Aftermath</a></i> (1966), <i><a href="/wiki/Between_the_Buttons" title="Between the Buttons">Between the Buttons</a></i> (1967) and <i><a href="/wiki/Their_Satanic_Majesties_Request" title="Their Satanic Majesties Request">Their Satanic Majesties Request</a></i> (1967).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Estrangement_from_bandmates">Estrangement from bandmates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Estrangement from bandmates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Loog_Oldham" title="Andrew Loog Oldham">Andrew Loog Oldham</a>'s arrival marked the beginning of Jones's slow estrangement. Oldham recognised the financial advantages of bandmembers' writing their own songs, as exemplified by <a href="/wiki/Lennon%E2%80%93McCartney" title="Lennon–McCartney">Lennon–McCartney</a>, and that playing covers would not sustain a band in the limelight for long. Further, Oldham wanted to make Jagger's charisma and flamboyance a focus of live performances. Jones saw his influence over the Stones' direction slide as their repertoire comprised fewer of the blues covers that he preferred; more <a href="/wiki/Jagger/Richards" title="Jagger/Richards">Jagger/Richards</a> originals developed, and Oldham increased his own managerial control, displacing Jones from yet another role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp76_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp76-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to Oldham in his book <i>Stoned</i>, Jones was an outsider from the beginning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> When the first tours were arranged in 1963, he travelled separately from the band, stayed at different hotels, and demanded extra pay. According to Oldham, Jones was very emotional and felt alienated because he was not a prolific song writer and his management role had been taken away. He "resisted the symbiosis demanded by the group lifestyle, and so life was becoming more desperate for him day by day. None of us were looking forward to Brian totally cracking up".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The toll from days on the road, the money and fame, and the feeling of being alienated from the group resulted in Jones's overindulgence in alcohol and other drugs. These excesses had a debilitative effect on his physical health and, according to Oldham, Jones became unfriendly and asocial at times.</p>
<p>Jones was arrested for drug possession on 10 May 1967, shortly after the "Redlands" incident at Richards' Sussex home. Authorities found <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marijuana" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methamphetamine" title="Methamphetamine">methamphetamine</a> in his flat. He confessed to marijuana use but claimed he did not use hard drugs. Reacting as fans did at the arrests of Jones's band mates, protesters appeared outside court demanding that Jones be freed. He was not kept in jail but was fined, given <a href="/wiki/Probation" title="Probation">probation</a>, and ordered to see a counselor.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In June 1967, Jones attended the <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Pop_Festival" title="Monterey Pop Festival">Monterey Pop Festival</a>. There he met <a href="/wiki/Frank_Zappa" title="Frank Zappa">Frank Zappa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Hopper" title="Dennis Hopper">Dennis Hopper</a>, and went on stage to introduce the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix_Experience" title="Jimi Hendrix Experience">Jimi Hendrix Experience</a> which was not well known yet in the USA. One review referred to Jones as "the unofficial 'king' of the festival".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Hostility grew between Jones, Jagger, and Richards, alienating Jones further from the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Although many noted that Jones could be friendly and outgoing, Wyman, Richards, and Watts have commented that he could also be cruel and difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> By most accounts, Jones's attitude changed frequently; he was one minute caring and generous, the next making an effort to anger everyone. As Wyman observed in <i>Stone Alone</i>: "There were at least two sides to Brian's personality. One Brian was introverted, shy, sensitive, deep-thinking. The other was a preening peacock, gregarious, artistic, desperately needing assurance from his peers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> "He pushed every friendship to the limit and way beyond".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In March 1967, <a href="/wiki/Anita_Pallenberg" title="Anita Pallenberg">Anita Pallenberg</a>, Jones's girlfriend of two years, left him for Richards when Jones was hospitalised during a trip the three made to Morocco,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> further damaging the already strained relations between Jones and Richards. As tensions and Jones's substance use increased, his musical contributions became sporadic. He became bored with the guitar and sought exotic instruments to play, and he was increasingly absent from recording sessions. In <a href="/wiki/Peter_Whitehead_(filmmaker)" title="Peter Whitehead (filmmaker)">Peter Whitehead's</a> promotional film for "<a href="/wiki/We_Love_You#Promotional_film" title="We Love You">We Love You</a>", made in July 1967, he appears groggy.</p>
<p>Jones's last substantial sessions with the Stones occurred in spring and summer of 1968 when the Stones produced "<a href="/wiki/Jumpin%27_Jack_Flash" title="Jumpin' Jack Flash">Jumpin' Jack Flash</a>" and the <i><a href="/wiki/Beggars_Banquet" title="Beggars Banquet">Beggars Banquet</a></i> album. He can be seen in the <a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Godard" title="Jean-Luc Godard">Jean-Luc Godard</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Sympathy_for_the_Devil_(film)" title="Sympathy for the Devil (film)">One Plus One</a></i> playing acoustic guitar and chatting and sharing cigarettes with Richards, although Jones is neglected in the music-making. The film chronicles the making of "<a href="/wiki/Sympathy_for_the_Devil" title="Sympathy for the Devil">Sympathy for the Devil</a>".</p>
<p>Where once Jones played multiple instruments on many tracks, he now played only minor roles on a few pieces. Jones's last formal appearance was in the December 1968 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones_Rock_and_Roll_Circus" title="The Rolling Stones Rock and Roll Circus">The Rolling Stones Rock and Roll Circus</a></i>, a part concert, part circus-act film organised by the band. It went unreleased for 25 years because Jagger was unhappy with the band's performance compared to others in the film such as <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taj_Mahal_(musician)" title="Taj Mahal (musician)">Taj Mahal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rnrcircus_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-rnrcircus-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Commentary included as bonus material indicated that almost everyone at the concert sensed that the end of Jones's time with the Rolling Stones was near, and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Daltrey" title="Roger Daltrey">Roger Daltrey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pete_Townshend" title="Pete Townshend">Pete Townshend</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a> thought it would be Jones's last live musical performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rnrcircus_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-rnrcircus-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Departure_from_the_band">Departure from the band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Departure from the band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jones was arrested a second time on 21 May 1968, for possession of cannabis, which Jones said had been left by previous tenants of the flat. He was facing a long jail sentence if found guilty owing to his probation. Wyman commented, "The fact that the police had secured a warrant with no evidence showed the arrest was part of a carefully orchestrated plan. Brian and the Stones were being targeted in an effort to deter the public from taking drugs".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The jury found him guilty but the judge had sympathy for Jones; instead of jailing him he fined him £50 plus £105 in costs and told him: "For goodness sake, don't get into trouble again or it really <i>will</i> be serious".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp311_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp311-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Jones's legal troubles, estrangement from his bandmates, <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">substance abuse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mood_swing" title="Mood swing">mood swings</a> became too much of an obstacle to active participation in the band. The Rolling Stones wanted to tour the United States in 1969 for the first time in three years but Jones was not in fit condition to tour and his second arrest exacerbated problems with acquiring a US work visa. In addition, Jones's attendance of rehearsals and recording sessions had become erratic; and when he did appear he rarely contributed anything musically or his bandmates would switch off his guitar leaving Richards playing nearly all the guitars. According to author Gary Herman, Jones was "literally incapable of making music; when he tried to play harmonica his mouth started bleeding".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This behaviour was problematic during the <i>Beggar's Banquet</i> sessions and had worsened by the time the band commenced recording <i>Let It Bleed</i>. In March 1969, Jones borrowed the group's Jaguar and went shopping in Pimlico Road. After the parked car was towed by police Jones hired a chauffeur car to get home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp323_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp323-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> In May 1969, Jones crashed his motorcycle into a shop window and was secretly taken to a hospital under an assumed name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp323_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp323-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> From this point, Jones was still attending recording sessions, but no longer a major contributor to the band's music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp323_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp323-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> By May, he had made two contributions to the work in progress: autoharp on "<a href="/wiki/You_Got_the_Silver" title="You Got the Silver">You Got the Silver</a>" and percussion on "<a href="/wiki/Midnight_Rambler" title="Midnight Rambler">Midnight Rambler</a>". Jagger duly informed Jones that he would be fired from the band if he did not turn up to a photo session. Looking frail, he nonetheless showed up and his last photo session as a Rolling Stone took place on Wednesday, 21 May 1969, first at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Katherine_Docks" title="St. Katherine Docks">St. Katherine Dock</a>s, Tower Bridge, London and then at Ethan Russell's photographic studio in <a href="/wiki/South_Kensington" title="South Kensington">South Kensington</a> London. The photos would appear on the album <i>Through The Past Darkly (Big Hits Vol.2)</i> in September 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Stones decided that following the release of the <i>Let it Bleed</i> album (scheduled for a July 1969 release in the US) they would start a North American tour in November 1969. However, the Stones management was informed that because of his drug convictions Jones would not receive a work permit. At the suggestion of pianist and road manager <a href="/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(musician)" title="Ian Stewart (musician)">Ian Stewart</a>, the Stones decided to add a new guitarist and on 8 June 1969, Jones was visited by Mick Jagger, Keith Richards, and Charlie Watts where he was told that the group he had formed would continue without him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp324-326_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp324-326-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>To the public it appeared as if Jones had left voluntarily; the other band members told him that although he was being asked to leave it was his choice how to break it to the public. Jones released a statement on 9 June 1969, announcing his departure. In this statement he said, among other things, that "I no longer see eye-to-eye with the others over the discs we are cutting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp326_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp326-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Jones was replaced by 20-year-old guitarist <a href="/wiki/Mick_Taylor" title="Mick Taylor">Mick Taylor</a> (formerly of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Mayall%27s_Bluesbreakers" title="John Mayall's Bluesbreakers">John Mayall's Bluesbreakers</a>).</p>
<p>During the period of his decreasing involvement in the band Jones was living at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cotchford_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cotchford Farm (page does not exist)">Cotchford Farm</a> in East Sussex, the residence formerly owned by <i><a href="/wiki/Winnie-the-Pooh" title="Winnie-the-Pooh">Winnie-the-Pooh</a></i> author <a href="/wiki/A._A._Milne" title="A. A. Milne">A. A. Milne</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teleg2012_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-teleg2012-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> which Jones had purchased in November 1968. There is uncertainty as to the mental and physical state Jones was in. The last known photographs of Jones, taken by schoolgirl Helen Spittal on 23 June 1969, shortly after his departure from the Stones are not flattering; he appears bloated with deep-set eyes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Korner" title="Alexis Korner">Alexis Korner</a>, who visited in late June only shortly after the Spittal photos were taken, noted that Jones seemed "happier than he had ever been".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp329_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp329-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Jones is known to have contacted Korner, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(musician)" title="Ian Stewart (musician)">Ian Stewart</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitch_Mitchell" title="Mitch Mitchell">Mitch Mitchell</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jimmy_Miller_(producer)" title="Jimmy Miller (producer)">Jimmy Miller</a> about intentions to put together another band. Jones had apparently demoed a few of his own songs in the weeks before his death, including 'Has Anybody Seen My Baby?' and 'Chow Time.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aeppli.ch_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-aeppli.ch-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brian_Jones_of_the_Rolling_Stones_-_geograph.org.uk_-_670236.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Brian_Jones_of_the_Rolling_Stones_-_geograph.org.uk_-_670236.jpg/220px-Brian_Jones_of_the_Rolling_Stones_-_geograph.org.uk_-_670236.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Brian_Jones_of_the_Rolling_Stones_-_geograph.org.uk_-_670236.jpg/330px-Brian_Jones_of_the_Rolling_Stones_-_geograph.org.uk_-_670236.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Brian_Jones_of_the_Rolling_Stones_-_geograph.org.uk_-_670236.jpg/440px-Brian_Jones_of_the_Rolling_Stones_-_geograph.org.uk_-_670236.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brian_Jones_of_the_Rolling_Stones_-_geograph.org.uk_-_670236.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Brian Jones's headstone, located in Cheltenham Cemetery.</div>

<p>At around midnight on the night of 2–3 July 1969, Jones was discovered motionless at the bottom of his swimming pool at Cotchford Farm. His Swedish girlfriend, Anna Wohlin, was convinced Jones was alive when he was taken out of the pool insisting he still had a pulse. However, by the time the doctors arrived it was too late and he was pronounced dead. The coroner's report stated "death by misadventure" and noted his liver and heart were heavily enlarged by drug and alcohol abuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp329_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp329-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Upon Jones's death, <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Pete_Townshend" title="Pete Townshend">Pete Townshend</a> wrote a poem titled "A Normal Day for Brian, A Man Who Died Every Day" (printed in <i>The Times</i>), <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a> dedicated a song to him on US television, and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Morrison" title="Jim Morrison">Jim Morrison</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">The Doors</a> published a poem entitled "Ode to L.A. While Thinking of Brian Jones, Deceased".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Hendrix and Morrison both died within the following two years, both <a href="/wiki/27_Club" title="27 Club">aged 27</a>, the same as Jones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27C_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-27C-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27C_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-27C-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Rolling Stones performed at a free concert in <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park</a> on 5 July 1969, two days after Jones's death. The concert had been scheduled weeks earlier as an opportunity to present the Stones' new guitarist, <a href="/wiki/Mick_Taylor" title="Mick Taylor">Mick Taylor</a>, and the band decided to dedicate the concert to Jones. Before the Rolling Stones' set Jagger read excerpts from "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adonais" title="Adonais">Adonais</a>", a poem by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percy_Shelley" title="Percy Shelley">Percy Shelley</a> about the death of his friend <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a>, and stagehands released hundreds of white butterflies as part of the tribute. The band opened with a <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Winter" title="Johnny Winter">Johnny Winter</a> song that was one of Jones's favourites, "I'm Yours and I'm Hers".</p>
<p>Jones was reportedly buried 10 feet (3.0 m) deep in <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a> Cemetery (to prevent exhumation by trophy hunters). His body was embalmed, hair bleached white, and was placed in an air-tight metal casket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-youtube.com_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-youtube.com-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Watts and Wyman were the only Rolling Stones who attended the funeral. Mick Jagger and <a href="/wiki/Marianne_Faithfull" title="Marianne Faithfull">Marianne Faithfull</a> were travelling to Australia to begin the filming of <i><a href="/wiki/Ned_Kelly_(1970_film)" title="Ned Kelly (1970 film)">Ned Kelly</a></i>; they stated that their contracts did not allow them to delay the trip to attend the funeral.</p>
<p>When asked if he felt guilty about Jones's death Mick Jagger told <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> in 1995: "No, I don't really. I do feel that I behaved in a very childish way, but we were very young, and in some ways we picked on him. But, unfortunately, he made himself a target for it; he was very, very jealous, very difficult, very <a href="/wiki/Psychological_manipulation" title="Psychological manipulation">manipulative</a>, and if you do that in this kind of a group of people you get back as good as you give, to be honest. I wasn't understanding enough about his drug addiction. No one seemed to know much about drug addiction. Things like LSD were all new. No one knew the harm. People thought cocaine was good for you."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaggerremembers_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-jaggerremembers-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Murder_claims">Murder claims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Murder claims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Theories surrounding Jones's death developed soon afterwards with associates of the Stones claiming to have information that he was murdered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phil_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-phil-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> According to rock biographer <a href="/wiki/Philip_Norman_(author)" title="Philip Norman (author)">Philip Norman</a>, "the murder theory would bubble back to the surface every five years or so".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phil_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-phil-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1993 it was reported that Jones was murdered by Frank Thorogood, who was doing some construction work on the property. He was the last person to see Jones alive. Thorogood allegedly confessed the murder to the Rolling Stones' driver, Tom Keylock, who later denied this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp328_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp328-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> The Thorogood theory was dramatised in the 2005 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Stoned_(film)" title="Stoned (film)">Stoned</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2009, Sussex Police decided to review Jones's death for the first time since 1969, after new evidence was handed to them by Scott Jones, an investigative journalist in the UK. Scott Jones had traced many of the people who were at Brian Jones's house the night he died plus unseen police files held at the National Archives. According to Jones' researcher, Trevor Hobley, a neighbor saw a large bonfire on Jones' estate in which documentation was being burned on the morning of Jones' death as he was leaving for work at 7 a.m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-youtube.com_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-youtube.com-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i>Mail on Sunday</i> in November 2008, Scott Jones said Frank Thorogood killed Brian Jones in a fight and the senior police officers covered up the true cause of death. Following the review the Sussex police stated that they would not be reopening the case. They asserted that "this has been thoroughly reviewed by Sussex Police's Crime Policy and Review Branch but there is no new evidence to suggest that the coroner's original verdict of 'death by misadventure' was incorrect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Songwriting_credits">Songwriting credits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Songwriting credits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unsure and insecure as a composer, Jones was not a prolific songwriter. The 30-second "Rice Krispies" jingle for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kellogg_Company" title="Kellogg Company">Kellogg's</a>, co-written with the <a href="/wiki/JWT" title="JWT">J. Walter Thompson</a> advertising agency in 1963 and performed by the Rolling Stones incognito was credited to Jones; this did not sit well with the rest of the band, who felt it was a group effort and all should benefit equally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wymanp90_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-wymanp90-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> Jones was also included in the "<a href="/wiki/Nanker_Phelge" title="Nanker Phelge">Nanker/Phelge</a>" songwriting credit, a pseudonym used on fourteen tracks that were composed by the entire band and Andrew Oldham.</p>
<p>According to Andrew Oldham, the main reason for Jones's not writing songs was that Jones, being a blues purist, did not love simple pop music enough. Oldham tried to establish a songwriting partnership between Jones and <a href="/wiki/Gene_Pitney" title="Gene Pitney">Gene Pitney</a> after "becoming bored senseless by Jones's bleating about the potential of half-finished melodies that by no means deserved completion", but after two days of sessions "the results remain best to be unheard, even by Stones' completists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When asked in 1965 if he had written songs, Jones replied: "Always tried. I've written quite a few, but mostly in blues style".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> Many years later after his death, Keith Richards stated: "No, no, absolutely not. That was the one thing he would never do. Brian wouldn't show them to anybody within the Stones. Brian as far as I know never wrote a single finished song in his life; he wrote bits and pieces but he never presented them to us. No doubt he spent hours, weeks, working on things, but his paranoia was so great that he could never bring himself to present them to us".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2010)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Bill Wyman has stated that Jones was "an incredibly gifted musician, but not a song writer";<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and in 1995, Mick Jagger told <i>Rolling Stone</i> that Jones had been jealous of the Jagger/Richards songwriting team, and added: "To be honest, Brian had no talent for writing songs. None. I've never known a guy with less talent for songwriting."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaggerremembers_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-jaggerremembers-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marianne_Faithfull" title="Marianne Faithfull">Marianne Faithfull</a> reported that Brian Jones wrote an early version of the melody for "<a href="/wiki/Ruby_Tuesday_(song)" title="Ruby Tuesday (song)">Ruby Tuesday</a>" and presented it to the group. <a href="/wiki/Victor_Bockris" title="Victor Bockris">Victor Bockris</a> reported that <a href="/wiki/Keith_Richards" title="Keith Richards">Keith Richards</a> and Brian Jones worked out the final melody in the studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, Jones is credited (along with Keith Richards) for the instrumental piece "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hear_It&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hear It (page does not exist)">Hear It</a>". However, in 1966 Jones composed, produced, and played on the soundtrack to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mord_und_Totschlag" title="Mord und Totschlag">Mord und Totschlag</a></i> (English title: <i>A Degree Of Murder</i>), an avant-garde German film with Anita Pallenberg, adding the majority of the instrumentation to the soundtrack.</p>
<p>In 1990, <a href="/wiki/Carla_Olson" title="Carla Olson">Carla Olson</a> was given permission from Jones's estate to put one of his poems to music and thus created the Jones/Olson song "Thank You For Being There". It appeared on the album <i>True Voices</i>, performed by Krysia Kristianne and <a href="/wiki/Robin_Williamson" title="Robin Williamson">Robin Williamson</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_contributions">Other contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In summer 1968, Jones recorded the Morocco-based ensemble, the <a href="/wiki/Master_Musicians_of_Joujouka" title="Master Musicians of Joujouka">Master Musicians of Joujouka</a>, as the name of the Moroccan mountain village was spelled at the time, the more correct spelling of the Arabic name being Jajouka, which was later used by the band; the recording was released in 1971 as <i><a href="/wiki/Brian_Jones_Presents_the_Pipes_of_Pan_at_Joujouka" title="Brian Jones Presents the Pipes of Pan at Joujouka">Brian Jones Presents the Pipes of Pan at Joujouka</a></i>. Jagger and Richards visited <a href="/wiki/Jajouka" title="Jajouka">Jajouka</a> in 1989 after recording "Continental Drift" for the Rolling Stones album <i><a href="/wiki/Steel_Wheels" title="Steel Wheels">Steel Wheels</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/The_Master_Musicians_of_Jajouka_led_by_Bachir_Attar" title="The Master Musicians of Jajouka led by Bachir Attar">The Master Musicians of Jajouka led by Bachir Attar</a> in Tangier. A homage to Jones entitled "Brian Jones Joujouka very Stoned", painted by <a href="/wiki/Mohamed_Hamri" title="Mohamed Hamri">Mohamed Hamri</a>, who had brought Jones to Jajouka in 1967, appeared on the cover of <a href="/wiki/Joujouka_Black_Eyes" title="Joujouka Black Eyes">Joujouka Black Eyes</a> by the Master Musicians of Joujouka in 1995, this being a splinter group created by an Irish friend of the former and estranged Moroccan manager, Mohamed Hamri.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (October 2008)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>Brian Jones Presents the Pipes of Pan at Jajouka</i> was rereleased in co-operation with Bachir Attar and Philip Glass in 1995. The executive producers were Philip Glass, Kurt Munkasci and Rory Johnston, with notes by <a href="/wiki/Bachir_Attar" title="Bachir Attar">Bachir Attar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bowles" title="Paul Bowles">Paul Bowles</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_S._Burroughs" title="William S. Burroughs">William S. Burroughs</a>, Stephen Davis, Brian Jones, <a href="/wiki/Brion_Gysin" title="Brion Gysin">Brion Gysin</a> and David Silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> and included additional graphics, more extensive notes by David Silver and William S. Burroughs, and a second CD, produced by Cliff Mark, with two "full-length remixes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nzentgraf_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-nzentgraf-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> In mid-May 1967, Jones played oboe on The Beatles' "<a href="/wiki/Baby,_You%27re_a_Rich_Man" title="Baby, You're a Rich Man">Baby, You're a Rich Man</a>". Jones played <a href="/wiki/Alto_saxophone" title="Alto saxophone">alto saxophone</a> on The Beatles song "<a href="/wiki/You_Know_My_Name_(Look_Up_the_Number)" title="You Know My Name (Look Up the Number)">You Know My Name</a>", which was released in March 1970, eight months after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nzentgraf_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-nzentgraf-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_image_and_legend">Public image and legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Public image and legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anita Pallenberg has stated in an interview that he wanted to look like <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7oise_Hardy" title="Françoise Hardy">Françoise Hardy</a>, he loved "dressing up and posing about" and that he would ask her to do his hair and make-up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnitaP_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnitaP-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bo_Diddley" title="Bo Diddley">Bo Diddley</a> described Brian as "a little dude that was trying to pull the group ahead. I saw him as the leader. He didn't take no mess. He was a fantastic cat; he handled the group beautifully."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Jones's death at 27 was the first of the 1960s rock movement; <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Janis_Joplin" title="Janis Joplin">Janis Joplin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Morrison" title="Jim Morrison">Jim Morrison</a> found their own drug-related deaths at the same age within two years (Morrison died two years to the day after Jones). The coincidence of ages has been described as the "<a href="/wiki/27_Club" title="27 Club">27 Club</a>". When Alastair Johns, who owned Cotchford Farm for over 40 years after Jones's death, refurbished the pool, he sold the original tiles to Jones's fans for £100 each, which paid for half of the work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teleg2012_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-teleg2012-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Johns noted that Cotchford Farm remained for decades an attraction for Jones' fans.</p>
<p>Jagger wrote The Stones' "<a href="/wiki/Shine_a_Light_(The_Rolling_Stones_song)" title="Shine a Light (The Rolling Stones song)">Shine a Light</a>" after Jones's death. It depicts Jones's behaviour and remoteness from the band, and asks God to shine a light on his soul.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> Several other songs have been written about Jones: Morrison originally wrote The Doors' song "Tightrope Ride" for Jones, but after Morrison's death <a href="/wiki/Ray_Manzarek" title="Ray Manzarek">Ray Manzarek</a> rewrote some of the lyrics so that they apply to both musicians. The <a href="/wiki/Psychic_TV" title="Psychic TV">Psychic TV</a> song "Godstar" is about Jones's death, as are <a href="/wiki/Robyn_Hitchcock" title="Robyn Hitchcock">Robyn Hitchcock</a>'s "Trash", <a href="/wiki/The_Drovers" title="The Drovers">The Drovers</a>' "She's as Pretty as Brian Jones Was", <a href="/wiki/Ted_Nugent" title="Ted Nugent">Ted Nugent</a>'s "Death by Misadventure", and <a href="/wiki/Salmonblaster" title="Salmonblaster">Salmonblaster</a>'s "Brian Jones". <a href="/wiki/Toy_Love" title="Toy Love">Toy Love</a>'s song "Swimming Pool" lists several dead rock icons including Jones (the others are Morrison, Hendrix, and <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bolan" title="Marc Bolan">Marc Bolan</a>); Jones is also mentioned in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Phazz" title="De Phazz">De Phazz</a>'s song "Something Special". <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Master_Musicians_of_Joujouka" title="The Master Musicians of Joujouka">The Master Musicians of Joujouka</a> song "Brian Jones Joujouka Very Stoned" was released in 1974 and 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> The band <a href="/wiki/Tigers_Jaw" title="Tigers Jaw">Tigers Jaw</a> heavily references Jones and his death in their song "I Saw Water".</p>
<p>Many of his contemporaries admit to idolising him as young musicians, including <a href="/wiki/Noel_Redding" title="Noel Redding">Noel Redding</a>, who, according to <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Des_Barres" title="Pamela Des Barres">Pamela Des Barres</a>'s book <i>I'm With the Band</i>, contemplated suicide after hearing about his death.</p>
<p>The 2005 film <i><a href="/wiki/Stoned_(film)" title="Stoned (film)">Stoned</a></i> is a fictional account of Jones and his role in the Rolling Stones. The part of Brian was played by English actor <a href="/wiki/Leo_Gregory" title="Leo Gregory">Leo Gregory</a>.</p>
<p>A fictionalised version of Jones and the tribute concert to him appears in <a href="/wiki/Alan_Moore" title="Alan Moore">Alan Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_O%27Neill_(comics)" title="Kevin O'Neill (comics)">Kevin O'Neill</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_League_of_Extraordinary_Gentlemen,_Volume_III:_Century" title="The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen, Volume III: Century">The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen, Volume III: Century</a></i> in its second issue, "Paint it Black".</p>
<p>His exceptional musicianship as well as contribution to the band is featured heavily in the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Crossfire_Hurricane" title="Crossfire Hurricane">Crossfire Hurricane</a></i>.</p>
