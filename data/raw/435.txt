<span class="bookmark hide"><a class="url entry-title" href="yourhoustonnews.com/memorial/news/musical-teens-help-premiere-soon-to-release-school-editions-of/article_432d39c4-8bc5-515a-9d5b-3a858cbb01b7.html" rel="bookmark">Musical teens help premiere soon-to-release school editions of ‘Lion King’</a></span>
<span class="author vcard"><span class="fn">By Cynthia Lescalleet<br>Special to HCN</br></span></span>
<span class="hide source-org vcard"><span class="org fn">Houston Community Newspapers</span></span>
</p>
<div class="entry-content" id="blox-story-text">
<div class="container" id="paging_container">
<div class="content">
<span class="first-paragraph">
<span class="paragraph-0"><p>When iTheatrics’ Junior Theater Festival convened at the Cobb Center in Atlanta earlier this month, an accomplished group of students from TUTS’ Humphreys School of Musical Theatre didn’t just perform with 4,800 peers in attendance, they performed for them.</p></span>
</span>
<span class="paragraph-1"><p>HSMT was one of four groups – out of a 101 -- that festival producers iTheatrics, MTI and Disney had picked to showcase new materials for accredited schools to produce. Houston’s group premiered costumed and choreographed selections from newly minted “The Lion King Jr.” and “The Lion King Kids.” Both versions trim the iconic show but boost the educational outreach. (As examples, affiliated lesson plans translate the Swahili lacing the powerful “Circle of Life” and instruct how to play an African drum.)</p></span>
<p>Rehearsals here began in September, soon after Disney tapped HSMT for the rollout role, said Shay Rodgers, HMST education manager. It’s the program’s fourth time to premiere a show, she said.</p>
<p>While not exactly the musical theater equivalent of the Olympics, Rodgers said, the festival does provide a rare opportunity for the nation’s musical theater juniors to come together, this year from 26 states and two countries. The skill-boosting weekend combined intensive workshops, critiques by panels of theater notables and just hanging out.</p>
<p>Students might not realize it, Rodgers said, but another festival takeaway is sizing up future competitors (though not everyone who attends aspires to Broadway).</p>
<p>Of the 90 MSMT students who auditioned for the showcase and festival, 38 made the cut -- and the trip by plane and bus. Expense is one reason students attend every other year, she said. Another is to give them something to shoot for.</p>
<h5>THE MEMORIAL BUNCH</h5>
<p>Scott Fonseka, 12, roared in the premiere as young Simba. A bit lion-hearted in real life, the Memorial Middle School student said he likes everything about performing, “especially the feeling you get when people see the show.”</p>
<p>The festival’s audience was like-minded. “They’re really supportive,” he said. “They know what we go through so if we mess up, they understand.”</p>
<p>Scott said he followed some friends to HSMT about four years ago and appreciates the ongoing training.</p>
<p>Daniel Fonseka, a sophomore at Memorial High School, started at HMST with his younger brother and has also appeared in MHS’s polished productions. Classes and performances have made him a more confidant public speaker, he says, and shown him how to work with a team -- or rock an interview, which is like an audition.</p>
<p>College? Daniel might be interested in music. First, though, he wants to get his driver’s license. Oh, the irony. Rehearsals cut into the limited free time he has for driver’s ed so he can drive – to rehearsals.</p>
<p>Lauren Dodd came to musical theater via singing. “It feels right,” said the Memorial Middle School eighth-grader. “There’s something magical about being on the stage.” Broadway beckons, but first, there’s Stratford High School‘s award-winning musical program awaiting.</p>
<p>Hannah Keller, also bound for Stratford High School from Memorial Middle School, came away from the recent junior theater festival all charged up: “It’s cool to go into a room and see all those people who have the same interests.”</p>
<p>Lindsay Bates of MMS speaks of HMST training as a way to better express herself.</p>
<p>Ella Ducharme, a Kinkaid School sixth-grader with a quick mind for improv, likes how performing takes her to another world. “You get to be anyone you want to be,” she said. Her dream role? It’s Elle Woods in “Legally Blonde,” perhaps not a stretch given Ella’s charm and humor.</p>
<p>Nicole Fish of St. Cecilia Catholic School says her extensive dance training initially landed her a slot at HMST. Voice and acting bugs are catching up, however. Now, she says, “I do everything equally.” Nicole likes being in lots of HSMT shows and venues throughout the year instead of in a culminating recital.</p>
<p>Other HMST students who performed at the festival and showcase include: Mallory Bechtel, Jenna Bobbora, Cole Bracey, Lauren Breath, Alaina Brownfield, Chase Callahan, Peyton Crenshaw, Gerra Gistand, Preston Granger, Kennedy Haygood, Fernell Hogan, Fallon Jones, Katelyn Kapocsi, Sarah Klein, Dylan Lambert, London Lane, Sam Linda, Kelly Lomonte, Alexa Magro, McKenna Marmolejo, Miles Marmolejo, Savannah May, Lauren McDonald, Lily Ohanian, Joi Pierre, Courtney Robertson, Carlie Sachs, Hanna Seay, Matthew Smith, Michelle Smith, Jacqueline Touchet and Fallon Williams.</p>
