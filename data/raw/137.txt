<div class="quoteright" style="width:336px;"><img alt="" border="0" class="embeddedimage" src="http://static.tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pub/images/catcher_rye_7408.jpg"/></div><div class="acaptionright" style="width:336px;"> A strangely creepy cover.</div><p></p><div class="indent"><em>You know what I'd do, if I had a goddamn choice? I'd be <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/TitleDrop" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/TitleDrop">the catcher in the rye</a> and all.</em></div><p></p><em>The Catcher in the Rye</em> is a 1951 novel by the late, reclusive author <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Creator/JDSalinger" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Creator/JDSalinger">J. D. Salinger</a>.<p></p>The story concerns Holden Caulfield, a smart but troubled kid who, after being expelled from his boarding school in December 1949, spends his time wandering <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/BigApplesauce" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/BigApplesauce">New York City</a>, mourning for the loss of innocence in children, and failing to understand the people that surround him. Holden himself can come off as a <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/JerkWithAHeartOfGold" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/JerkWithAHeartOfGold">Jerk with a Heart of Gold</a> as he <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/Wangst" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/Wangst">unkindly judges almost everyone</a>, but as the book explores his underlying psychological issues and reaches its <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/BittersweetEnding" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/BittersweetEnding">Bittersweet Ending</a>, Holden's true nature becomes apparent.<p></p>The book is considered one of the best novels of all time, is practically the textbook for <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/PointOfView" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/PointOfView">First-Person Narration</a>, and is regularly found in critical lists of the greatest English works of fiction. The novel is a frequent target of the <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/MoralGuardians" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/MoralGuardians">Moral Guardians</a> for its offensive language and nihilistic attitude. It is the most popular novel never to have been adapted into a movie.<p></p><hr/><h2>Contains examples of:</h2><p></p><ul><li> <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/AmbiguousSituation" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/AmbiguousSituation">Ambiguous Situation</a>: The scene where <span class="spoiler" title="you can set spoilers visible by default on your profile"> Mr Antolini strokes Holden's sleeping head. It could be (as Holden thinks) a <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/HoYay" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/HoYay">sexual pass</a> or simply a misguided attempt at paternal affection.</span></li><li> <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/AntiHero" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/AntiHero">Anti-Hero</a>: Holden is a <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/ClassicalAntiHero" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/ClassicalAntiHero">Classical Anti-Hero</a>.</li><li> <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/AuthorAvatar" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/AuthorAvatar">Author Avatar</a>: Holden. J.D. Salinger stated that he would have allowed a stage adaptation of the work on the condition that he be allowed to play Holden, <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/DawsonCasting" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/DawsonCasting">despite being significantly older than Holden</a> by the time this was a possibility.<ul><li> Although this may have been simply because Salinger didn't want a stage play made at all, as he implied he would allow a film adaptation to happen only upon his death, partly to provide for his children, and partly so he wouldn't have to see it. In his later novella "Seymour: An Introduction," narrator Buddy Glass implies authorship of <em>Catcher</em> and emphatically denies Holden is based on Buddy's elder brother Seymour. Both Seymour and Buddy have been suggested as the more likely <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/AuthorAvatar" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/AuthorAvatar">Author Avatar</a> of Salinger himself.</li></ul></li><li> <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/BerserkButton" title="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/BerserkButton">Berserk Button</a>: Holden seeing anyone engaging in "phony" behavior- mainly blatant hypocrisy- he complains about them at length in the narration.</li><li> <a class="twikilink" href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/BigApplesauce"
