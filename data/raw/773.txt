<p><b>Barry Alan Crompton Gibb</b>, <span style="font-size:100%;"><a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a></span> (born 1 September 1946) is a musician, singer, songwriter, and record producer who rose to worldwide fame as a founder member of the <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a>, one of the most commercially successful and critically acclaimed groups in the history of popular music. With his brothers, <a href="/wiki/Robin_Gibb" title="Robin Gibb">Robin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Gibb" title="Maurice Gibb">Maurice</a>, he formed a <a href="/wiki/Barry,_Robin_%26_Maurice_Gibb" title="Barry, Robin &amp; Maurice Gibb">songwriting partnership</a> from 1966.</p>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and raised in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> where he became involved in the <a href="/wiki/Skiffle#Skiffle_in_Britain" title="Skiffle">skiffle craze</a>, forming his first band, <a href="/wiki/The_Rattlesnakes_(1955_band)" title="The Rattlesnakes (1955 band)">the Rattlesnakes</a>, which evolved into the Bee Gees in 1960 when they moved to <a href="/wiki/Redcliffe,_Queensland" title="Redcliffe, Queensland">Redcliffe, Queensland</a>, Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> They returned to England where they achieved worldwide fame. He was also known for his high-pitched <a href="/wiki/Falsetto" title="Falsetto">falsetto</a> singing voice. Gibb shares the record with <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> for consecutive <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> number ones as a writer with six. <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i> lists Gibb as the second most successful songwriter in history behind Sir Paul McCartney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Gibb's career has spanned over fifty years. In 1994, he was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Songwriters_Hall_of_Fame" title="Songwriters Hall of Fame">Songwriters Hall of Fame</a> with his brothers. In 1997, as a member of the Bee Gees, he was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> and received the <a href="/wiki/Brit_Awards" title="Brit Awards">Brit Award</a> for Outstanding Contribution to Music. Barry is a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Songwriters,_Composers_and_Authors" title="British Academy of Songwriters, Composers and Authors">British Academy of Songwriters, Composers and Authors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fellow_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fellow-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1946.E2.80.9354:_Early_years">1946–54: Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1946–54: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Gibb" title="Hugh Gibb">Hugh Gibb</a></div>
<p>Barry Alan Crompton Gibb was born in Jane Crookall Maternity Home at <a href="/wiki/Douglas,_Isle_of_Man" title="Douglas, Isle of Man">Douglas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Gibb" title="Hugh Gibb">Hugh</a>, an English drummer, and Barbara Gibb (née Pass).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> He has an older sister, Lesley Evans. He was named Barry Alan Crompton Gibb after his father's youngest brother Alan, who died in infancy. According to Hugh, in a mix of fact and fiction, the third name was given to Barry to honor the Gibbs' illustrious ancestor, Isaac Crompton. When Gibb was born, his father was busy with his music working at various hotels in Douglas, while his mother stayed at home looking after the children. Later, the Gibbs moved to Chapel House on Strang Road. When he was almost two years old, he was badly burned. His mother had just made tea which she had put on the table; he climbed up and pulled the tea pot down and got the tea all over him. He was in Nobles Hospital for about two-and-a-half months.</p>
<p>In 1949, the Gibb family relocated to 50 St. Catherine's Drive. Later on 22 December, his two younger brothers <a href="/wiki/Robin_Gibb" title="Robin Gibb">Robin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Gibb" title="Maurice Gibb">Maurice</a> were born. When the twins were young, they moved to Smedley Cottage, Spring Valley, also in Douglas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Gibb started school on 4 September 1951, three days after his fifth birthday, attending Braddan school. In 1952, the Gibb family relocated to 43 Snaefell Road, Willaston, which became their home for the next two years. The same year, he went to Tynwald Street Infants School. On his seventh birthday in 1953, he went to Desmesne Road Boys School.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1955.E2.80.932012:_The_Rattlesnakes_to_the_Bee_Gees">1955–2012: The Rattlesnakes to the Bee Gees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1955–2012: The Rattlesnakes to the Bee Gees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/The_Rattlesnakes_(1955_band)" title="The Rattlesnakes (1955 band)">The Rattlesnakes (1955 band)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry,_Robin_%26_Maurice_Gibb" title="Barry, Robin &amp; Maurice Gibb">Barry, Robin &amp; Maurice Gibb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stigwood" title="Robert Stigwood">Robert Stigwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Bee_Gees%27_concerts_in_1967_and_1968" title="The Bee Gees' concerts in 1967 and 1968">The Bee Gees' concerts in 1967 and 1968</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1955.E2.80.9358:_Formation.2C_Australian_days_and_songwriting_career">1955–58: Formation, Australian days and songwriting career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1955–58: Formation, Australian days and songwriting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1955, Gibb formed the <a href="/wiki/Skiffle" title="Skiffle">skiffle group</a>, The Rattlesnakes. Consisted of himself on vocals and guitar, Robin and Maurice on vocals and friends/neighbours Paul Frost and Kenny Horrocks also providing vocals. By 1957, The Rattlesnakes played numbers with songs by <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Richard" title="Cliff Richard">Cliff Richard</a>. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Anka" title="Paul Anka">Paul Anka</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Everly_Brothers" title="The Everly Brothers">The Everly Brothers</a>. Gibb himself said that their first song ever played was Paul Anka's "I Love You, Baby", however in 1968, he remembered it as the Everly Brothers' "<a href="/wiki/Wake_Up_Little_Susie" title="Wake Up Little Susie">Wake Up Little Susie</a>". The Rattlesnakes' first professional gig happened on 28 December 1957 in Gaumont Cinema. Around 1958, Gibb's guitar was accidentally broke by Frost, while Frost himself described the incident as "broken in the middle".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> By May 1958 when the Gibbs moved in Northern Grove it signalled the departure of Frost and Horrocks. The brothers later changed the name of the group to Wee Johnny Hayes and the Blue Cats. Under that name, they performed regularly in <i>Minor 15</i>, a talent contest for under-fifteens held on Thursday nights from 7:00 to 9:00 PM at Princess Club in <a href="/wiki/Chorlton-cum-Hardy" title="Chorlton-cum-Hardy">Chorlton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the beginning of August 1958, the Gibb family set sail for Australia from Southampton on a ship called <i>Fairsea</i>. Also aboard was <a href="/wiki/Red_Symons" title="Red Symons">Red Symons</a>, future guitarist of Skyhooks as well as <a href="/wiki/Kylie_Minogue" title="Kylie Minogue">Kylie Minogue</a>'s mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> They later changed their name to Bee Gees. In 1959, the brothers began singing at the Redcliffe Speedway between races to earn money, their vocal talent brought them to the attention of Bill Gates, a radio deejay. Gates also interested on Gibb's original material including "Let Me Love You" and "(Underneath the) Starlight of Love". After hearing those songs, Gates asked him for more original material for the tape. Gibb quit school in September 1961 and the Gibbs moved to the Surfers Paradise. The brothers spent the summer of 1961 and 1962 performing at hotels and clubs in the Gold Coast area. By September 1962, Gibb managed to audition songs to <a href="/wiki/Col_Joye" title="Col Joye">Col Joye</a>. And the Gibb family finally moved in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> at the start of 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Around the same time, the Bee Gees were signed to <a href="/wiki/Festival_Records" title="Festival Records">Festival</a> but they were assigned to <a href="/wiki/Leedon_Records" title="Leedon Records">Leedon</a>. Their first single, "<a href="/wiki/The_Battle_of_the_Blue_and_the_Grey" title="The Battle of the Blue and the Grey">The Battle of the Blue and the Grey</a>" was written by Gibb himself. The Bee Gees' original singles from 1963 to 1966 is written by Gibb. While between 1963 and 1966, Gibb's songs were recorded by numerous Australian recording artists including <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Gordon" title="Trevor Gordon">Trevor Gordon</a>, Michelle Rae, Noeleen Batley, Bip Addison, Sandy Summers, Anne Shelton, April Byron, <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Burns_(singer)" title="Ronnie Burns (singer)">Ronnie Burns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lori_Balmer" title="Lori Balmer">Lori Balmer</a>, Barrington Davis, Jenene and Jon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> One of them is his composition "One Road" hit No. 2 in New South Wales Chart (Australia) for Jimmy Little in 1964. And his another composition "I Just Don't Like to Be Alone" by Bryan Davies hit No. 25 also in New South Wales Chart in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Returning_in_England.2C_commercial_break-out_and_worldwide_popularity">Returning in England, commercial break-out and worldwide popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Returning in England, commercial break-out and worldwide popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Bee Gees returned in England in early 1967. On 24 February, the group passed a live audition with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stigwood" title="Robert Stigwood">Robert Stigwood</a> and later signed to Robert Stigwood Organization also the same day that "Spicks and Specks" was released by Polydor as a single in England. Stigwood became the manager of the Bee Gees. <a href="/wiki/Colin_Petersen" title="Colin Petersen">Colin Petersen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vince_Melouney" title="Vince Melouney">Vince Melouney</a> later joined the band as drummer and lead guitarist. The result was their first international LP <i><a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees%27_1st" title="Bee Gees' 1st">Bee Gees' 1st</a></i> which featured three singles. The second single "<a href="/wiki/To_Love_Somebody_(song)" title="To Love Somebody (song)">To Love Somebody</a>" (on which Gibb provided the lead vocal) has become a standard song as covered by hundreds of artists consistently throughout the years since. Around the same time, the Gibb brothers contributed backing vocals on the track "<a href="/wiki/Cowman,_Milk_Your_Cow" title="Cowman, Milk Your Cow">Cowman, Milk Your Cow</a>", a song written by Gibb and Robin. The guitar work is by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Green_(musician)" title="Peter Green (musician)">Peter Green</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fleetwood_Mac" title="Fleetwood Mac">Fleetwood Mac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> At the end of 1967, fame was taking its toll and the group were at odds with each other. At the start of 1968, the Gibbs were vying for influence in determining the musical direction of the group, creating tension in the studio and on stage. Also in 1968, to promote <i><a href="/wiki/Horizontal_(album)" title="Horizontal (album)">Horizontal</a></i> the group made their first appearance on US television on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Smothers_Brothers_Show" title="The Smothers Brothers Show">The Smothers Brothers Show</a></i> as well as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ed_Sullivan_Show" title="The Ed Sullivan Show">The Ed Sullivan Show</a></i>. The group later filmed a TV special <i>Frankie Howerd Meets the Bee Gees</i> with <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Howerd" title="Frankie Howerd">Frankie Howerd</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> His 1968 composition "<a href="/wiki/Only_One_Woman" title="Only One Woman">Only One Woman</a>" (UK #5) was <a href="/wiki/The_Marbles_(duo)" title="The Marbles (duo)">the Marbles</a>' most successful single up to date.</p>
<p>In 1969, the Bee Gees released "<a href="/wiki/First_of_May_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="First of May (Bee Gees song)">First of May</a>" as the A-side, with the flip side being "Lamplight" on which Robin sang the lead. Robert Stigwood chose "First of May" to be the A-side. No other songs were released from the album <i><a href="/wiki/Odessa_(Bee_Gees_album)" title="Odessa (Bee Gees album)">Odessa</a></i>. Shortly after Robin announced his solo plans, the Bee Gees released their first single without him, "<a href="/wiki/Tomorrow_Tomorrow_(Bee_Gees_song)" title="Tomorrow Tomorrow (Bee Gees song)">Tomorrow Tomorrow</a>". Barry also worked with <a href="/wiki/Samantha_Sang" title="Samantha Sang">Samantha Sang</a>, co-writing and producing two songs "The Love of a Woman" and "Don't Let It Happen Again". He also produced songs for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P.P._Arnold" title="P.P. Arnold">P.P. Arnold</a> including "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bury_Me_Down_By_the_River" title="Bury Me Down By the River">Bury Me Down By the River</a>", which were originally recorded by the Bee Gees for their 1970 album. In the summer of 1969, drummer <a href="/wiki/Colin_Petersen" title="Colin Petersen">Colin Petersen</a> left the group which now only featured Barry and Maurice as the Bee Gees. <a href="/wiki/Pentangle_(band)" title="Pentangle (band)">Pentangle</a> drummer <a href="/wiki/Terry_Cox" title="Terry Cox">Terry Cox</a> replaced Petersen to complete another 12 songs, but only five songs were released in the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The film <i><a href="/wiki/Cucumber_Castle_(film)" title="Cucumber Castle (film)">Cucumber Castle</a></i>, featuring only two Bee Gees, although the film was originally included Petersen. When the group announced that they had split at the start of December 1969, he spent writing songs in January 1970 to record it on the following month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_work_and_return">Solo work and return</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Solo work and return">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>In February 1970, he started to record his first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kid%27s_No_Good" title="The Kid's No Good">The Kid's No Good</a></i>; he released his first solo single, "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Kiss_Your_Memory" title="I'll Kiss Your Memory">I'll Kiss Your Memory</a>", but the rest of the songs that were supposed to be on the album were never released. In April 1970 the album <i><a href="/wiki/Cucumber_Castle" title="Cucumber Castle">Cucumber Castle</a></i> was released four months after the break-up, featuring only Barry and Maurice, as Robin was working on his solo career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-session_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-session-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> For the first time as a solo artist, he appeared on the panel of judges and performed "<a href="/wiki/One_Bad_Thing" title="One Bad Thing">One Bad Thing</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/The_Day_Your_Eyes_Meet_Mine" title="The Day Your Eyes Meet Mine">The Day Your Eyes Meet Mine</a>" on <i>Miss Teen Princess of the World</i> pageant in Germany, his reaction was "I've not appeared on stage for at least a year and three months, and I really miss people". In July 1970, Gibb traveled to Australia to act as compere for <i>Go-Set</i>, and on that occasion, he gave an interview on the magazine which revealed that sixteen months after the Bee Gees' break-up, but he was still not ready to argue with his brothers, "Obviously, we're still brothers, but we are no longer as a group".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/One_Bad_Thing" title="One Bad Thing">One Bad Thing</a>", which he considered as the second single from his debut album and Atco pressed some copies of it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and was distributed to the radio stations in Canada and elsewhere but was not continued and it was decided to focus on a new Bee Gees release (in favor of the Bee Gees' reunion single "<a href="/wiki/Lonely_Days" title="Lonely Days">Lonely Days</a>") and was released around August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-session_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-session-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Gibb claimed that had not only had he been focusing on writing and recording songs, but he has still hoped to be an actor, "I have already had offers to play different parts but without appearing swell headed, I haven't found the right part yet".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In August, the Bee Gees would reunite and record together again, writing "<a href="/wiki/Lonely_Days" title="Lonely Days">Lonely Days</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/How_Can_You_Mend_a_Broken_Heart" title="How Can You Mend a Broken Heart">How Can You Mend a Broken Heart</a>" at their first reunion session.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-session_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-session-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Two of Gibb's singles were omitted after the group had reunited, "The Day Your Eyes Meet Mine" was withdrawn at the last minute in the US while Polydor planned to release "One Bad Thing" (2 October 1970).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971, the group released "How Can You Mend a Broken Heart" which was their first American #1 hit. In October 1971, Gibb recorded three songs for his fan club called <i>Barry Gibb Fan Club</i>; one of the songs, "<a href="/wiki/King_Kathy" title="King Kathy">King Kathy</a>" was released as a single on Lyntone Records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Gibb had remained a constant creative force for the group and, by 1972, had set aside the idea of a parallel solo career and was ready to devote full attention to the band. Bill Shepherd who had worked with the group since 1964. Also in 1972, Gibb moved to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> to record in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Record_Plant" title="The Record Plant">The Record Plant</a> studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1973, the Bee Gees moved to Robert Stigwood's <a href="/wiki/RSO_Records" title="RSO Records">RSO Records</a> and released their first singles for Stigwood, "<a href="/wiki/Saw_a_New_Morning" title="Saw a New Morning">Saw a New Morning</a>" and <i><a href="/wiki/Life_in_a_Tin_Can" title="Life in a Tin Can">Life in a Tin Can</a></i>. Stigwood was not ready to give up on the group, but he did not believe in the musical direction they were taking. At the suggestion of <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Wexler" title="Jerry Wexler">Jerry Wexler</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmet_Ertegun" title="Ahmet Ertegun">Ahmet Ertegun</a> of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records" title="Atlantic Records">Atlantic Records</a>, Stigwood sent them to work with Atlantic producer and arranger <a href="/wiki/Arif_Mardin" title="Arif Mardin">Arif Mardin</a> who produced the next Bee Gees album in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> After a United States tour early in 1974, the band started to performed at small clubs in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_disco.2FR.26B_era">The disco/R&amp;B era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The disco/R&amp;B era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At the start of 1975, the band moved to Miami to work on their new album with <a href="/wiki/Arif_Mardin" title="Arif Mardin">Arif Mardin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Criteria_Studios" title="Criteria Studios">Criteria Studios</a>. The first song featuring Gibb's falsetto was "<a href="/wiki/Nights_on_Broadway" title="Nights on Broadway">Nights on Broadway</a>". He explains on <i>The Larry King Show</i> in 2002:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"It came to me in a dream, there was a request by Arif Mardin, who was like an uncle to us, he was a great record producer during the song 'Nights On Broadway,' for the <i>Main Course</i> album, which is previous to the 'Fever' syndrome. And he said, 'Can any of you scream, scream in falsetto.' So, you know, give us an ad lib or a scream at the end. So from screaming, it turned into things like 'blaming it all'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>At the start of 1976, Gibb recorded songs for the Bee Gees' album <i><a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_World" title="Children of the World">Children of the World</a></i>. Also in that year Gibb wrote "<a href="/wiki/I_Just_Want_to_Be_Your_Everything" title="I Just Want to Be Your Everything">I Just Want to Be Your Everything</a>", <a href="/wiki/Andy_Gibb" title="Andy Gibb">Andy Gibb</a>'s first US No. 1, and provided backup vocals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1977, Gibb took the lead vocals on the songs "<a href="/wiki/How_Deep_Is_Your_Love" title="How Deep Is Your Love">How Deep Is Your Love</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Stayin%27_Alive" title="Stayin' Alive">Stayin' Alive</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Night_Fever" title="Night Fever">Night Fever</a>"; these songs all reached No. 1 in the US. Gibb co-wrote "<a href="/wiki/Emotion_(Samantha_Sang_song)" title="Emotion (Samantha Sang song)">Emotion</a>", which remains <a href="/wiki/Samantha_Sang" title="Samantha Sang">Samantha Sang</a>'s best-known hit. Around the same time, Gibb and Galuten wrote "<a href="/wiki/Save_Me,_Save_Me" title="Save Me, Save Me">Save Me, Save Me</a>" as recorded by the group Network from New York City. In May to September 1977, Gibb along with Robin and Maurice started to record songs for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper%27s_Lonely_Hearts_Club_Band_(film)" title="Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band (film)">Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band</a></i>, produced by <a href="/wiki/George_Martin" title="George Martin">George Martin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1978, Andy Gibb recorded his second album <i><a href="/wiki/Shadow_Dancing_(album)" title="Shadow Dancing (album)">Shadow Dancing</a></i>; the <a href="/wiki/Shadow_Dancing_(song)" title="Shadow Dancing (song)">song of the same name</a>, which was credited to all four brothers, was also a US No. 1 single. Barry sang backup vocals, arranged and produced the album. In 1978, Gibb wrote "<a href="/wiki/Grease_(song)" title="Grease (song)">Grease</a>" for the stage musical <a href="/wiki/Grease_(musical)" title="Grease (musical)">of the same name</a>; later the song was recorded by <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Valli" title="Frankie Valli">Frankie Valli</a>. Also in February 1978, Gibb participated with <a href="/wiki/Teri_DeSario" title="Teri DeSario">Teri DeSario</a> recording "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ain%27t_Nothing_Gonna_Keep_Me_From_You" title="Ain't Nothing Gonna Keep Me From You">Ain't Nothing Gonna Keep Me From You</a>" which he wrote in 1977. In 1979, the <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a>' 1979 album <i><a href="/wiki/Spirits_Having_Flown" title="Spirits Having Flown">Spirits Having Flown</a></i> reached No. 1 in the US and other countries, with most of the songs performed by Barry. After the Bee Gees' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1979_Spirits_Having_Flown_Tour" title="1979 Spirits Having Flown Tour">1979 Spirits Having Flown Tour</a>, he started to record demos for <a href="/wiki/Barbra_Streisand" title="Barbra Streisand">Barbra Streisand</a>. Also in that year, he sang backup vocals, played guitar and synthesizer and produced <a href="/wiki/Andy_Gibb" title="Andy Gibb">Andy Gibb</a>'s 1980 album <i><a href="/wiki/After_Dark_(Andy_Gibb_album)" title="After Dark (Andy Gibb album)">After Dark</a></i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakaway_from_disco_sound_and_hiatus">Breakaway from disco sound and hiatus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Breakaway from disco sound and hiatus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In September 1980, Gibb produced the new songs—including a cover version of "<a href="/wiki/Will_You_Love_Me_Tomorrow" title="Will You Love Me Tomorrow">Will You Love Me Tomorrow</a>"—on Andy Gibb's first compilation album, <i><a href="/wiki/Andy_Gibb%27s_Greatest_Hits" title="Andy Gibb's Greatest Hits">Greatest Hits</a></i>. In October 1980, Gibb produced <a href="/wiki/Barbra_Streisand" title="Barbra Streisand">Barbra Streisand</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Guilty_(Barbra_Streisand_album)" title="Guilty (Barbra Streisand album)">Guilty</a></i>. Songs from the album include the hits "<a href="/wiki/What_Kind_of_Fool" title="What Kind of Fool">What Kind of Fool</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Guilty_(Barbra_Streisand_and_Barry_Gibb_song)" title="Guilty (Barbra Streisand and Barry Gibb song)">title track</a>, both of which were vocal duets between Streisand and Gibb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1981, the Bee Gees released <i><a href="/wiki/Living_Eyes_(Bee_Gees_album)" title="Living Eyes (Bee Gees album)">Living Eyes</a></i>. The two singles from the album were not in the disco genre. The album was not a commercial success. In 1982, <a href="/wiki/Clive_Davis" title="Clive Davis">Clive Davis</a> intervened and asked Gibb to write solely for <a href="/wiki/Dionne_Warwick" title="Dionne Warwick">Dionne Warwick</a>, who was on his Arista label. Around February, Gibb recorded the 12 demos for Dionne Warwick, but eight were used by Warwick and included on Gibb's demo album in 2006. As he produced Warwick's 1982 album <i><a href="/wiki/Heartbreaker_(Dionne_Warwick_album)" title="Heartbreaker (Dionne Warwick album)">Heartbreaker</a></i>, the songs were all written with Gibb except "<a href="/wiki/Our_Day_Will_Come" title="Our Day Will Come">Our Day Will Come</a>". About August, after Gibb met <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Rogers" title="Kenny Rogers">Kenny Rogers</a> and asked for some songs, Gibb recorded his first demo for Rogers entitled "<a href="/wiki/Eyes_That_See_in_the_Dark_(song)#Barry_Gibb_version" title="Eyes That See in the Dark (song)">Eyes That See in the Dark</a>". While at that time, Robin working on his <i><a href="/wiki/How_Old_Are_You%3F_(album)" title="How Old Are You? (album)">How Old Are You?</a></i> album with Maurice playing instruments. The other songs intended for Kenny Rogers were written in late 1982,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> then at the start of 1983, he continued to record the demos until April 1983, while also recording songs with the Bee Gees for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Staying_Alive_(1983_film)" title="Staying Alive (1983 film)">Staying Alive</a></i>. In August 1983, he produced Rogers' new album <i><a href="/wiki/Eyes_That_See_in_the_Dark" title="Eyes That See in the Dark">Eyes That See in the Dark</a></i>, which includes the song "<a href="/wiki/Islands_in_the_Stream_(song)" title="Islands in the Stream (song)">Islands in the Stream</a>" by Rogers and <a href="/wiki/Dolly_Parton" title="Dolly Parton">Dolly Parton</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_solo_work">Second solo work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Second solo work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In August 1983, Irving Azoff signed Gibb to the MCA Records for North America. Gibb was signed for a few million dollars to a multi-album deal. Polydor still had rights to Gibb's songs outside North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> In September 1984, his album <i><a href="/wiki/Now_Voyager" title="Now Voyager">Now Voyager</a></i> was released. Two singles from the album include "<a href="/wiki/Shine,_Shine" title="Shine, Shine">Shine, Shine</a>" and the dance number "<a href="/wiki/Fine_Line_(Barry_Gibb_song)" title="Fine Line (Barry Gibb song)">Fine Line</a>". A film of the same name was directed by Storm Thorgerson, in which Gibb is the protagonist, with actor <a href="/wiki/Michael_Hordern" title="Michael Hordern">Michael Hordern</a> as his guide through a confusing world between life and death. The film includes a music video of "Fine Line" featuring Gibb without his trademark beard and was filmed in black and white.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1985, Gibb started to record demos for <a href="/wiki/Diana_Ross" title="Diana Ross">Diana Ross</a>, for her album <i><a href="/wiki/Eaten_Alive_(album)" title="Eaten Alive (album)">Eaten Alive</a></i>. Also in 1985, Gibb co-wrote some songs on Robin Gibb's album <i><a href="/wiki/Walls_Have_Eyes" title="Walls Have Eyes">Walls Have Eyes</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In late-1985 and early-1986, Gibb wrote new songs for his next album, though his third album <i><a href="/wiki/Moonlight_Madness_(Barry_Gibb_album)" title="Moonlight Madness (Barry Gibb album)">Moonlight Madness</a></i> was not released and most of the songs on that album was later released in 1988. Producer <a href="/wiki/Randy_Jackson" title="Randy Jackson">Randy Jackson</a> plays bass on all of the tracks. Gibb co-wrote, "When Two Worlds Collide", "Lost in the Crowd" and "Spread Your Wings" for Swedish singer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carola_(singer)" title="Carola (singer)">Carola</a> on her album <i><a href="/wiki/Runaway_(Carola_album)" title="Runaway (Carola album)">Runaway</a></i>. Also in 1985, Gibb participated with the Bunburys with David English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Comeback_and_continued_popularity">Comeback and continued popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Comeback and continued popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In early 1987, the Bee Gees started to record their first album in six years. In June and July 1987, Gibb and Maurice produced Andy Gibb's four new songs; one of them is "<a href="/wiki/Arrow_Through_the_Heart" title="Arrow Through the Heart">Arrow Through the Heart</a>" which was later released in 2010. In 1987, Gibb co-wrote "Up the Revolution" by <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> And in 1988, Gibb recorded two new songs for the <i><a href="/wiki/Hawks_(film)" title="Hawks (film)">Hawks</a></i> film. In September 1988, the <a href="/wiki/Hawks_(soundtrack)" title="Hawks (soundtrack)">film soundtrack</a> of the film was released by Polydor only in the UK. The songs were all performed by Gibb except "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chain_Reaction_(song)" title="Chain Reaction (song)">Chain Reaction</a>" (Diana Ross). The single from the soundtrack, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Childhood_Days_(song)" title="Childhood Days (song)">Childhood Days</a>", reached only No. 60 in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Around 1990, the Bee Gees recorded <i><a href="/wiki/High_Civilization" title="High Civilization">High Civilization</a></i>. In September 1990, Gibb played guitar and produced "Born to Be Loved by You" by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kelli_Wolfe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kelli Wolfe (page does not exist)">Kelli Wolfe</a>, which was released as an unreleased B-side in August 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Around 1992, Gibb played guitar on <a href="/wiki/Lulu_(singer)" title="Lulu (singer)">Lulu</a>'s "Let Me Wake Up in Your Arms", released in 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1993, the Bee Gees recorded and released <i><a href="/wiki/Size_Isn%27t_Everything" title="Size Isn't Everything">Size Isn't Everything</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1994, the Bee Gees and <a href="/wiki/Polydor_Records" title="Polydor Records">Polydor</a> planned a tour to promote <i>Size Isn't Everything</i>, but it was off in February, due to Gibb's trouble with arthritis in the back, right hand and right knee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Gibb co-wrote "I Will Be There", which was recorded as a demo for <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a> and released on Turner's album <i><a href="/wiki/Twenty_Four_Seven_(Tina_Turner_album)" title="Twenty Four Seven (Tina Turner album)">Twenty Four Seven</a></i>. In 2001, Gibb co-wrote "I Cannot Give You My Love" with Ashley Gibb, which was intended for <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Richard" title="Cliff Richard">Cliff Richard</a>. In September 2001, the Bee Gees re-recorded "<a href="/wiki/Islands_in_the_Stream_(song)" title="Islands in the Stream (song)">Islands in the Stream</a>", which became their last session as a group, as Gibb did not seem to be present on the session in Middle Ear Studios, Miami Beach, Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2002, Gibb and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> recorded "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All_In_Your_Name_(Barry_Gibb_song)" title="All In Your Name (Barry Gibb song)">All In Your Name</a>". Also in 2002, Gibb sings background vocals on <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bubl%C3%A9" title="Michael Bublé">Michael Bublé</a>'s version of the 1971 song "<a href="/wiki/How_Can_You_Mend_a_Broken_Heart" title="How Can You Mend a Broken Heart">How Can You Mend a Broken Heart</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2003, ten months after Maurice's death, Gibb produced and contributed background vocals and guitar to two songs performed by Cliff Richard, "I Cannot Give You My Love" and "How Many Sleeps?"; Maurice Gibb's keyboard work from a 2001 demo version was included in this 2003 version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> On 2 May 2004, Barry and Robin Gibb received the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> award at <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Palace" title="Buckingham Palace">Buckingham Palace</a>; their nephew Adam accepted his father Maurice's posthumous award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brothersgibb_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-brothersgibb-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Also in 2004, Gibb co-wrote and sang background vocals on his son <a href="/wiki/Steve_Gibb" title="Steve Gibb">Steve Gibb</a>'s solo single "Living in the Rain". In January 2005, along with many artists, Gibb and his brother Robin recorded vocal parts for the charity single "Grief Never Grows Old" on behalf of victims of the <a href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami">tsunami</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> on 26 December 2004. In April and May 2005, Barbra Streisand recorded songs for her new album produced by Gibb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> In August 2006, two Gibb singles, "Doctor Mann" and "Underworld", were released on <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a>. "Underworld" was featured on the film soundtrack of <i><a href="/wiki/Arctic_Tale" title="Arctic Tale">Arctic Tale</a></i> but not in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> On 7 December 2006, Gibb joined 4,500 other musicians in a full-page advertisement in the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i> newspaper, calling for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Government" title="British Government">British Government</a> to extend the existing 50-year copyright protection of sound recordings in the United Kingdom. The <a href="/wiki/Fair_play_for_musicians" title="Fair play for musicians">fair play for musicians</a> advertisement proposed that the copyright be extended to the American standard of 95 years and was a direct response to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gowers_Review" title="Gowers Review">Gowers Review</a> (published by the British Government on 6 December 2006), which recommended the retention of the 50 year protection for sound recordings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, "<a href="/wiki/Drown_On_the_River" title="Drown On the River">Drown On the River</a>" was released as a single on <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a>. The song later appeared on the soundtrack of <i><a href="/wiki/Deal_(2008_film)" title="Deal (2008 film)">Deal</a></i>. Also in the same year, Gibb sang background vocals on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jamie_Jo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jamie Jo (page does not exist)">Jamie Jo</a>'s song "U Turn Me On"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> and wrote the theme music for <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Grease_Is_the_Word" title="Grease Is the Word">Grease Is the Word</a></i>. Also in 2007 Gibb appeared as a mentor in season six of <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idol_(season_6)" title="American Idol (season 6)">American Idol</a></i>. On 14 March 2009, Gibb teamed with <a href="/wiki/Olivia_Newton-John" title="Olivia Newton-John">Olivia Newton-John</a> to present the one-hour finale performance at a star-studded 12-hour live concert at Sydney's <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Cricket_Ground" title="Sydney Cricket Ground">Sydney Cricket Ground</a>, part of <a href="/wiki/Sound_Relief" title="Sound Relief">Sound Relief</a>, a fundraiser to aid victims of the February 2009 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victorian_Bushfires" title="Victorian Bushfires">Victorian Bushfires</a> that devastated large tracts of heavily wooded and populated south-eastern Australia, where the Gibb family once lived. The concert was televised live nationally across Australia on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MAX_(Channel)" title="MAX (Channel)">Max TV</a> cable network. On 10 July 2009, Gibb was made a Freeman of the Borough of Douglas (Isle of Man). The award was also bestowed upon his brother Robin and posthumously upon his brother Maurice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iomtoday1_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-iomtoday1-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Also in 2009, Barry and Linda Gibb became US citizens; they retain their British citizenship. In late 2009, Barry and Robin announced plans to record and perform together once again as the Bee Gees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_News_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> In late 2009, Barry and Robin announced plans to record and perform together once again as the Bee Gees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_News_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, Gibb withdrew from a planned appearance on the Gorillaz album <i>Plastic Beach</i> which was released in March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> In December 2011, his two songs, "Grey Ghost" and "Daddy's Little Girl" were released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> On 21 February 2012, Gibb performed his first solo concert in the US at the Seminole <a href="/wiki/Hard_Rock_Cafe" title="Hard Rock Cafe">Hard Rock Cafe</a> in Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> He sang "<a href="/wiki/How_Can_You_Mend_a_Broken_Heart" title="How Can You Mend a Broken Heart">How Can You Mend a Broken Heart</a>" with Maurice's daughter, Samantha Gibb, who is a singer in her own band. Barry's son Steve was also on stage as lead guitarist and sang a Maurice composition, "<a href="/wiki/On_Time_(song)" title="On Time (song)">On Time</a>". On 20 May 2012, Robin Gibb died, making Barry the sole surviving Gibb brother.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012.E2.80.93present:_Solo_career">2012–present: Solo career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2012–present: Solo career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gibb made his debut performance at the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Ole_Opry" title="Grand Ole Opry">Grand Ole Opry</a> on 27 July 2012, performing three songs with <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Skaggs" title="Ricky Skaggs">Ricky Skaggs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Skaggs later recorded "<a href="/wiki/Soldier%27s_Son" title="Soldier's Son">Soldier's Son</a>" on which Gibb added vocals for Skaggs' album <i>Music to My Ears</i> released in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>He commenced a world tour in 2013 in Australia called <a href="/wiki/Mythology_Tour" title="Mythology Tour">Mythology Tour</a>, featuring the Bee Gees anthology set <a href="/wiki/Mythology_(Bee_Gees_album)" title="Mythology (Bee Gees album)">album of the same name</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> He was joined on stage by his son Steve and Maurice's daughter Samantha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> On that tour, for the first time on stage, Gibb performed "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playdown" title="Playdown">Playdown</a>" (1966), "<a href="/wiki/Every_Christian_Lion_Hearted_Man_Will_Show_You" title="Every Christian Lion Hearted Man Will Show You">Every Christian Lion Hearted Man Will Show You</a>" (1967), "<a href="/wiki/I_Started_a_Joke" title="I Started a Joke">I Started a Joke</a>" (1969) and "With the Sun in My Eyes" (1968). Also, for the first time, the music video of the Bee Gees' 2001 song "Technicolor Dreams", written by Gibb, was shown before the show began. This short tour inspired <a href="/wiki/Festival_Records" title="Festival Records">Festival Records</a> to release a box set of the Bee Gees' three Australian albums, <i>The Festival Albums Collection, 1965-1967</i>, and a 'best of' compilation of the group's Australian era songs titled <i>Morning of My Life</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On 27 October 2013, Gibb appeared at the Country Music Hall of Fame with Kenny Rogers, Bobby Bare and Cowboy Jack Clement; Gibb performed "Islands in the Stream" with Kelly Lang. In October, Gibb performed at the Grand Ole Opry for the second time with Ricky Skaggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> On 21 December 2013, Barry Gibb made a surprise appearance on the US television show <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> at the end of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barry_Gibb_Talk_Show" title="Barry Gibb Talk Show">Barry Gibb Talk Show</a>" skit with <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Fallon" title="Jimmy Fallon">Jimmy Fallon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justin_Timberlake" title="Justin Timberlake">Justin Timberlake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>. Also appearing that night was Sir <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> who shared the stage with Gibb during the closing credits where they embraced each other.</p>
<p>On 27 January 2014, Gibb appeared on the American television show <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Night_with_Jimmy_Fallon" title="Late Night with Jimmy Fallon">Late Night with Jimmy Fallon</a></i> to announce the start of his first solo tour of the US. He performed "You Should Be Dancing" with help from Fallon's backing band, The Roots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> Gibb and Fallon sang some Everly Brothers songs, including "<a href="/wiki/Bye_Bye_Love_(The_Everly_Brothers_song)" title="Bye Bye Love (The Everly Brothers song)">Bye Bye Love</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Wake_Up_Little_Susie" title="Wake Up Little Susie">Wake Up Little Susie</a>". Gibb also sang "<a href="/wiki/To_Love_Somebody_(song)" title="To Love Somebody (song)">To Love Somebody</a>" as a bonus online performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> On 28 January, Gibb was interviewed by the CNN about his brothers and, in that same interview, Gibb talks about <a href="/wiki/Justin_Bieber" title="Justin Bieber">Justin Bieber</a>, saying he is "heading for a brick wall".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Gibb appeared on a Paul McCartney tribute album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_McCartney" title="The Art of McCartney">The Art of McCartney</a></i> released in 18 November 2014 performing "<a href="/wiki/When_I%27m_Sixty-Four" title="When I'm Sixty-Four">When I'm Sixty-Four</a>".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gibb's first marriage was to Maureen Bates, whom he married on 22 August 1966, when he was 19 years old. The couple lived together for only a short time and were divorced in July 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> During the taping of the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">Top of the Pops</a></i> in London, Gibb met the former Miss Edinburgh, Linda Gray. On 1 September 1970 (his 24th birthday), Gibb married Gray. Together, they have five children - Stephen (born 1973), Ashley (born 1977), Travis (born 1981), Michael (born 1984) and Alexandra (born 1991) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> - and seven grandchildren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After many stresses on their personal and professional lives, both Barry and Robin collapsed from nervous exhaustion on a flight from Australia to Turkey in 1967. On 23 December 1967, Barry and Robin left England for Australia. As Gibb explained: "But due to time difference we arrived on Christmas Day, we missed Christmas Eve altogether!". The pair celebrated Christmas with their manager <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stigwood" title="Robert Stigwood">Robert Stigwood</a>'s family as Gibb said: "We went on to Sydney".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The three Gibb brothers were appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">Commanders of the Order of the British Empire</a> (CBE) in 2002. On 2 May 2004, Barry and Robin received their awards at <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Palace" title="Buckingham Palace">Buckingham Palace</a>, along with their nephew Adam, who collected the award on behalf of his father Maurice, who had died in January 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brothersgibb_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-brothersgibb-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2006, Gibb purchased the former home of country singers <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a> and <a href="/wiki/June_Carter_Cash" title="June Carter Cash">June Carter Cash</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hendersonville,_Tennessee" title="Hendersonville, Tennessee">Hendersonville, Tennessee</a>, intending to restore it and turn it into a songwriting retreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> The house was destroyed by fire on 10 April 2007 while under renovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> On 10 July 2009, Gibb was made a Freeman of the Borough of Douglas (Isle of Man). The award was also bestowed upon his brother Robin and posthumously upon his brother Maurice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iomtoday1_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-iomtoday1-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Also in 2009, Barry and Linda Gibb became US citizens. They have dual citizenship. Barry has homes in <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, Florida and in <a href="/wiki/Beaconsfield" title="Beaconsfield">Beaconsfield</a>, Buckinghamshire, set in 90 acres of English countryside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gibb's influences when he was in the Rattlesnakes were <a href="/wiki/The_Everly_Brothers" title="The Everly Brothers">The Everly Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Anka" title="Paul Anka">Paul Anka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Richard" title="Cliff Richard">Cliff Richard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-first-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bee_Gees" title="Bee Gees">Bee Gees</a> acknowledged that they would sing in the style of the Everlys and then add a third harmony; the result was "<a href="/wiki/New_York_Mining_Disaster_1941" title="New York Mining Disaster 1941">New York Mining Disaster 1941</a>" (1967). When Gibb heard <a href="/wiki/Roy_Orbison" title="Roy Orbison">Roy Orbison</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Crying_(Roy_Orbison_song)" title="Crying (Roy Orbison song)">Crying</a>", he said: "That was it. To me that was the voice of God."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-hall-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Gibb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gibb had a highly successful career as a member of the Bee Gees, a group near the top of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_music_artists" title="List of best-selling music artists">all-time top-sellers list</a>. When the group was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> in 1997, their citation read "Only <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garth_Brooks" title="Garth Brooks">Garth Brooks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> have outsold the Bee Gees".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoF_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-HoF-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> The trio's contribution to <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Fever" title="Saturday Night Fever">Saturday Night Fever</a></i> pushed the film's soundtrack past the 40 million mark in sales. It reigned as the top-selling album until Michael Jackson's <i><a href="/wiki/Thriller_(Michael_Jackson_album)" title="Thriller (Michael Jackson album)">Thriller</a></i>. They are the only group in pop history to write, produce and record six straight No.1 hits. They have 16 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy" title="Grammy">Grammy</a> nominations and nine Grammy wins.</p>
<p>Barry Gibb is also a prolific and successful songwriter and a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Songwriters,_Composers_and_Authors" title="British Academy of Songwriters, Composers and Authors">British Academy of Songwriters, Composers and Authors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fellow_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fellow-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1977, Gibb saw five of his songs simultaneously enter the Top Ten of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and, for one week in March, four of the top five songs were written by him. His songs were No. 1 for 27 out of 37 weeks from 24 December 1977 to 2 September 1978. Gibb also holds a very unusual record, in that he is the only songwriter in history to write four successive US number one hits: in 1978, the Bee Gees' "Stayin' Alive" was replaced at number one by Andy Gibb's single, "Love Is Thicker Than Water", followed by the Bee Gees' "Night Fever" for their longest run, seven weeks. This was then replaced by Yvonne Elliman's "If I Can't Have You".</p>
<p>As a songwriter Gibb has had No. 1 songs in the 1960s, 1970s, 1980s 1990s and 2000s, when "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/(Barry)_Islands_in_the_Stream" title="(Barry) Islands in the Stream">Islands in the Stream</a>" became No. 1 in the UK as the comic relief single for 2009. His songs have been recorded by numerous artists, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jose_Feliciano" title="Jose Feliciano">Jose Feliciano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celine_Dion" title="Celine Dion">Celine Dion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al_Green" title="Al Green">Al Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wyclef_Jean" title="Wyclef Jean">Wyclef Jean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Janis_Joplin" title="Janis Joplin">Janis Joplin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Little" title="Jimmy Little">Jimmy Little</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Manilow" title="Barry Manilow">Barry Manilow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olivia_Newton-John" title="Olivia Newton-John">Olivia Newton-John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roy_Orbison" title="Roy Orbison">Roy Orbison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Rogers" title="Kenny Rogers">Kenny Rogers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diana_Ross" title="Diana Ross">Diana Ross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nina_Simone" title="Nina Simone">Nina Simone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbra_Streisand" title="Barbra Streisand">Barbra Streisand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samantha_Sang" title="Samantha Sang">Samantha Sang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conway_Twitty" title="Conway Twitty">Conway Twitty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Valli" title="Frankie Valli">Frankie Valli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luther_Vandross" title="Luther Vandross">Luther Vandross</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarah_Vaughn" title="Sarah Vaughn">Sarah Vaughn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Warnes" title="Jennifer Warnes">Jennifer Warnes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dionne_Warwick" title="Dionne Warwick">Dionne Warwick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andy_Williams" title="Andy Williams">Andy Williams</a>. Gibb has also produced albums for <a href="/wiki/Andy_Gibb" title="Andy Gibb">Andy Gibb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Rogers" title="Kenny Rogers">Kenny Rogers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diana_Ross" title="Diana Ross">Diana Ross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbra_Streisand" title="Barbra Streisand">Barbra Streisand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dionne_Warwick" title="Dionne Warwick">Dionne Warwick</a>.</p>
