<p>In motion picture technology—either <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a> or <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a>—<b>high frame rate</b> (<b>HFR</b>) refers to higher <a href="/wiki/Frame_rate" title="Frame rate">frame rates</a> than typical prior practice.</p>
<p>The frame rate for motion picture film cameras was typically 24 frames per second with multiple shuttering on each frame to prevent flicker during projection. Television video historically operated at 50 or 60 images per second using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interlaced_scanning" title="Interlaced scanning">interlaced scanning</a>. (A video "frame" is actually two images, hence PAL 25fps looks nothing like film's 24fps.) Frame rates higher than 24 Hz for feature motion pictures and frame rates higher than 30 Hz for other applications is an emerging trend in the 21st century.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_frame_rates_in_cinema">History of frame rates in cinema</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_frame_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of frame rates in cinema">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early cinema history, there was no standard <a href="/wiki/Frame_rate" title="Frame rate">frame rate</a> established. Edison's early films were shot at 40 frames per second (fps), while the Lumière Brothers used 16 fps. This had to do with a combination of the use of a hand crank rather than a motor, which created variable frame rates because of the inconsistency of the cranking of the film through the camera. After the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Sound_film" title="Sound film">synch sound</a> recording, 24 fps became the industry standard frame rate for capture and projection of motion pictures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> 24 fps was chosen because it was the best frame rate for sound clarity – lower frame rates produced sound tracks with too much surface noise.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A few film formats have experimented with frame rates higher than the 24 fps standard. The original 3-strip <a href="/wiki/Cinerama" title="Cinerama">Cinerama</a> features of the 1950s ran at 26 fps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The first two <a href="/wiki/Todd-AO" title="Todd-AO">Todd-AO</a> 70mm features, <i><a href="/wiki/Oklahoma!_(1955_film)" title="Oklahoma! (1955 film)">Oklahoma!</a></i> (1955) and <i><a href="/wiki/Around_the_World_in_80_Days_(1956_film)" title="Around the World in 80 Days (1956 film)">Around the World in 80 Days</a></i> (1956) were shot and projected at 30 fps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Trumbull" title="Douglas Trumbull">Douglas Trumbull's</a> 70mm <a href="/wiki/Showscan" title="Showscan">Showscan</a> film format operated at 60 fps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IMAX_HD" title="IMAX HD">IMAX HD</a> (high definition in this case meaning high definition <a href="/wiki/Film_stock" title="Film stock">film stock</a>, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/70mm" title="70mm">70mm</a> IMAX is the highest resolution motion picture image in the world) film <i><a href="/wiki/Momentum_(IMAX_film)" title="Momentum (IMAX film)">Momentum</a></i>, presented at <a href="/wiki/Seville_Expo_%2792" title="Seville Expo '92">Seville Expo '92</a>, was shot and projected at 48 fps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NFB_Seville_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NFB_Seville-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> IMAX HD has also been used in ridefilms, including the Disney theme park attraction <a href="/wiki/Soarin%27_Over_California" title="Soarin' Over California">Soarin' Over California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The proposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxivision_48" title="Maxivision 48">Maxivision 48</a> format ran 35mm film at 48 fps, but was never commercially deployed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Digital_Cinema_Initiatives" title="Digital Cinema Initiatives">Digital Cinema Initiatives</a> has published a document outlining recommended practice for high frame rate digital cinema.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> This document outlines the frame rates and resolutions that can be used in high frame rate digital theatrical presentations with currently available equipment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_in_the_film_industry">Usage in the film industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_frame_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage in the film industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit_(film_series)" title="The Hobbit (film series)"><i>The Hobbit</i> film series</a>, beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit:_An_Unexpected_Journey" title="The Hobbit: An Unexpected Journey">The Hobbit: An Unexpected Journey</a></i> in December 2012, used a shooting and projection frame rate of 48 frames per second, becoming the first feature film with a <a href="/wiki/Wide_release" title="Wide release">wide release</a> to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Its 2013 sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit:_The_Desolation_of_Smaug" title="The Hobbit: The Desolation of Smaug">The Hobbit: The Desolation of Smaug</a></i> and 2014 sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit:_The_Battle_of_the_Five_Armies" title="The Hobbit: The Battle of the Five Armies">The Hobbit: The Battle of the Five Armies</a></i>, followed suit. All films also have versions which are converted and projected at 24 fps.</p>
<p>RocketJump's <a href="/wiki/Video_Game_High_School" title="Video Game High School">Video Game High School</a> was the first web series to use HFR, and the first content shot and edited in a mixed frame rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The series, which follows the lives of high school students in a world where gamers are revered as pro-athletes, adopted HFR in its second season, using the standard 24 frames per second for real world interactions, and 48 frames per second for "in-game" action sequences. Although the content is available on YouTube and Netflix, it can only be viewed in mixed frame rate using a special player on RocketJump's website.</p>
<p>Other film-makers who intend to use the high frame rate format include <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a> in his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avatar_(film_series)" title="Avatar (film series)"><i>Avatar</i> sequels</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Andy_Serkis" title="Andy Serkis">Andy Serkis</a> in his adaptation of <a href="/wiki/George_Orwell" title="George Orwell">George Orwell</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Animal_Farm" title="Animal Farm">Animal Farm</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The difference between these films and the historical processes is that they are to be shot digitally rather than on <a href="/wiki/Film_stock" title="Film stock">film</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Out_of_the_cinema">Out of the cinema</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_frame_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Out of the cinema">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some media players are capable of showing HFR content and almost all computers and smart devices can handle this format as well. In the last years some TV screens has the ability to take normal 24fps videos and "convert" them to HFR content by <a href="/wiki/Motion_interpolation" title="Motion interpolation">interpolating the motion</a> of the picture, effectively creating new computer generated frames between each two key frames and running them at higher refresh rate. Some computer programs (such as SVP, "<a href="/wiki/SmoothVideo_Project" title="SmoothVideo Project">SmoothVideo Project</a>") allow for that as well but with higher precision and better quality as the computing power of the PC has grown.</p>
<p>Motion interpolation might cause some artifacts as a reason of the computer "guessing" the frames wrong (when low on computing power and using less precise methods) but they are rare and usually the content is smooth and steady.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_concerns">Criticism and concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_frame_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism and concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Criticisms of the format include assertions that the "cinematic look" is lost with the use of high frame rates. Film critics have noted that the much sharper image looks akin to video games, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDTV" title="HDTV">HDTV</a>, live theater or a cheap home movie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Another claim is that the HFR content has the "soap opera" feel to it (due to soap operas being shot in 30fps and up) but some claim it is better than the standard 24fps and is felt "Weird" only to people who are not used to watching HFR content. Some even consider the "stuttery" feel of the 24 frames per second format a bigger annoyance than the "soap opera" effect and prefer HFR over the classic 24 fps format.</p>
<p>If the use of high frame rates were to become the norm, many cinemas would need to upgrade most of their projection equipment, incurring high costs per screen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> albeit most of these costs were already incurred in theatres that have switched to digital projection.</p>
