<p><i><b>The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</b></i> (later retitled <i><b>The Lion King 2: Simba's Pride</b></i>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">direct-to-video</a> <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animated</a> <a href="/wiki/Musical_film" title="Musical film">musical</a> <a href="/wiki/Romance_film" title="Romance film">romantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy-drama" title="Comedy-drama">comedy-drama</a> film produced by <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Pictures" title="Walt Disney Pictures">Walt Disney Pictures</a> and <a href="/wiki/DisneyToon_Studios" title="DisneyToon Studios">Walt Disney Animation Australia</a> released on October 27, 1998, by <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Studios_Home_Entertainment" title="Walt Disney Studios Home Entertainment">Walt Disney Home Video</a> (aka Buena Vista Home Entertainment). The film is the sequel to the 1994 Disney animated film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King" title="The Lion King">The Lion King</a></i>. It was re-released twice: first as a special edition DVD on August 31, 2004, and second as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> alongside <i>The Lion King</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_1%C2%BD" title="The Lion King 1½">The Lion King 1½</a></i>. The Blu-ray edition was placed into the "<a href="/wiki/Disney_Vault" title="Disney Vault">Disney Vault</a>" on April 30, 2013.</p>
<p>The film centers around Simba and Nala's daughter, Kiara. She falls in love with Kovu, a male lion who was raised in a pride called the Outsiders, which was banished by Simba for being loyal to his evil uncle, Scar. Desperate to be together, Kiara and Kovu must overcome the two obstacles that are keeping them apart: Kovu's mother, Zira, and Simba's prejudice against the Outsiders. This prejudice leads to another conflict: Kiara and Simba have a troubled father-daughter relationship because Kiara has a desire to take care of herself and to be on her own while still under her father's protection.</p>
<p>Most of the original cast returned to their roles from the first film, apart from <a href="/wiki/Rowan_Atkinson" title="Rowan Atkinson">Rowan Atkinson</a>, who was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hibbert" title="Edward Hibbert">Edward Hibbert</a> as the voice of Zazu and its follow-up/predecessor <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_1%C2%BD" title="The Lion King 1½">The Lion King 1½</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Madge_Sinclair" title="Madge Sinclair">Madge Sinclair</a>, the voice of Simba's mother Sarabi in the first film, was unable to continue her role due to her death in 1995 after the release of the first film. As a result, her character was written out.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The animals of the <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">Pridelands</a> gather to witness the celebration of the newborn cub, Kiara, daughter of King Simba and Queen Nala. Simba's father, Mufasa, proudly watches over the ceremony from the Heavens above. As she grows older, Kiara becomes annoyed with her father's overprotective parenting. Simba assigns Timon and Pumbaa to watch her. Kiara ignores the duo's attention and enters the "Outlands," where she meets Kovu, a cub who, unknown to her, was chosen by Simba's uncle Scar to be his heir. When Kovu retaliates to Kiara's playing, Simba confronts the young cub just as Zira, Kovu's mother, confronts Simba. Zira reminds Simba of how he exiled her and the other Outsiders, and she informs him that Kovu was hand-chosen to be Scar's successor. After returning to the Pride Lands, Simba sends Nala and the rest of the pride back to Pride Rock while he warns Kiara about the danger posed by the Outsiders. Simba then tells her that they are a part of each other.</p>
<p>Meanwhile in the Outlands, Zira reminds Kovu that Simba killed Scar and was the one who exiled the lions who respect Scar. Kovu explains that he does not think it is so bad to have Kiara as his good friend, but Zira realizes she can use Kovu's friendship with Kiara to exact revenge on Simba.</p>
<p>Many years later, Kiara, now a young adult, heads out for her first solo hunt. As part of Zira's plan, Kovu's siblings Nuka and Vitani trap Kiara in a fire, allowing Kovu to rescue her. Simba, unwilling to thank the young rogue, is forced to accept Kovu's asylum now that Kovu saved his daughter. Later that night, Simba dreams about attempting to save Mufasa from falling into the stampede but is stopped by Scar who then turns into Kovu and throws Simba off the cliff into the stampede. The next day, Kovu contemplates attacking Simba as he was instructed to, but he goes out to teach Kiara how to hunt instead and hardly realizes the love he has for her at this point. Kovu attempts to confess his mission to Kiara, but Rafiki interrupts and leads them to the jungle, where he introduces them to "upendi" (which in the film means <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_love" title="Romantic love">love</a>), and the two lions ultimately fall in love.</p>
<p>Kovu's guilt drives him to confess his mission to Kiara. Before he can do so, Simba takes him around the Pride Lands and tells him the real story of Scar, which Kovu had never heard. They fall into an ambush set up by Zira, who orders Kovu to kill Simba, but Kovu runs away instead. Nuka tries to capture and kill Simba but is lethally crushed by logs in a nearby dam. Zira blames Kovu for his brother's death and claws him across his eye in anger and frustration, leaving a scar. Kovu attempts to return to the Pride Lands and pleads Simba for his forgiveness but is promptly exiled. Furious at Simba, Kiara exclaims that Simba will never be like Mufasa, before fleeing to find Kovu. The two young lions later find each other and confess their love. Kiara convinces Kovu to return with her to the Pridelands.</p>
<p>The Outsiders and Pridelanders clash. Zira and Simba confront each other, but Kovu and Kiara intervene and tell them to stop their hostilities. Vitani and the Outsiders side with the Pridelanders. Zira fails in an attempt to attack Simba, and Kiara pushes her over a cliff, dangling over a storm-swollen river. Kiara offers Zira her help, but Zira refuses and falls to her death. Simba helps Kiara back up the cliff, and they, along with Nala, allow the Outsiders and Kovu to rejoin the Pridelands at Pride Rock. Simba accepts Kovu as his son-in-law and the future king and Kiara the future queen. Simba looks up at the sky to hear the approval of his father, who says he is proud of him.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice_cast">Voice cast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Voice cast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Lion_King_characters" title="List of The Lion King characters">List of The Lion King characters</a></div>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matthew_Broderick" title="Matthew Broderick">Matthew Broderick</a> as Simba, the King of the Pride Lands, son of Mufasa, mate of Nala and father of Kiara. <a href="/wiki/Cam_Clarke" title="Cam Clarke">Cam Clarke</a> provides his singing voice. Ian Harrowell served as the supervising animator for Simba.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Moira_Kelly" title="Moira Kelly">Moira Kelly</a> as Nala, the Queen of the Pride Lands, mate of Simba and mother of Kiara. Ian Harrowell served as the supervising animator for Nala.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neve_Campbell" title="Neve Campbell">Neve Campbell</a> as Kiara, Simba and Nala's daughter. As a cub, she is voiced by <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Horn" title="Michelle Horn">Michelle Horn</a>, with Charity Sanoy providing her child singing voice as a cub, <a href="/wiki/Ashley_Edner" title="Ashley Edner">Ashley Edner</a> providing her lion growls as a cub and <a href="/wiki/Liz_Callaway" title="Liz Callaway">Liz Callaway</a> providing her adult singing voice. Lianne Hughes served as the supervising animator for Kiara.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Pleshette" title="Suzanne Pleshette">Suzanne Pleshette</a> as Zira, the leader of the Outsiders and Scar's most loyal follower and the mother of Nuka, Vitani and Kovu. Kevin Peaty served as the supervising animator for Zira.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jason_Marsden" title="Jason Marsden">Jason Marsden</a> as Kovu, Zira's son and Nuka and Vitani's younger brother. Gene Miller provides Kovu's singing voice. As a cub, he is voiced by <a href="/wiki/Ryan_O%27Donohue" title="Ryan O'Donohue">Ryan O'Donohue</a>. Andrew Collins served as the supervising animator for Kovu.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andy_Dick" title="Andy Dick">Andy Dick</a> as Nuka, Zira's son, Vitani and Kovu's older brother and the oldest male of Zira's family. Ian Harrowell served as the supervising animator for Nuka.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nathan_Lane" title="Nathan Lane">Nathan Lane</a> as Timon, Simba's <a href="/wiki/Meerkat" title="Meerkat">meerkat</a> best friend, royal adviser and Kiara's guardian. Bob Baxter served as the supervising animator for Timon.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ernie_Sabella" title="Ernie Sabella">Ernie Sabella</a> as Pumbaa, Simba's <a href="/wiki/Warthog" title="Warthog">warthog</a> best friend, royal adviser and Kiara's guardian. Bob Baxter served as the supervising animator for Pumbaa.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_Guillaume" title="Robert Guillaume">Robert Guillaume</a> as Rafiki, the mandrill shaman of The Pride Lands. Bob Baxter served as the supervising animator for Rafiki.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Lien" title="Jennifer Lien">Jennifer Lien</a> as Vitani, Zira's daughter and Nuka and Kovu's sister. As a cub, she is voiced by <a href="/wiki/Lacey_Chabert" title="Lacey Chabert">Lacey Chabert</a>, with Crysta Macalush providing her singing voice. Kevin Peaty served as the supervising animator for Vitani.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Edward_Hibbert" title="Edward Hibbert">Edward Hibbert</a> as Zazu (replacing <a href="/wiki/Rowan_Atkinson" title="Rowan Atkinson">Rowan Atkinson</a>), Simba's hornbill adviser and childhood guardian. Bob Baxter served as the supervising animator for Zazu.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/James_Earl_Jones" title="James Earl Jones">James Earl Jones</a> as Mufasa, the spirit of Simba's late father, the older brother of Scar, Kiara's grandfather, and the previous King of the Pride Lands.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jim_Cummings" title="Jim Cummings">Jim Cummings</a> as Scar (replacing <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Irons" title="Jeremy Irons">Jeremy Irons</a>), Mufasa's younger brother, Simba's evil uncle and Kiara's great uncle. Though Scar does not actually appear in the main body of the movie itself (due to him being eaten alive by the hyenas in the first film), he appears briefly in Simba's nightmare and also makes a brief cameo appearance when Kovu (right after being exiled by Simba) looks in a pool of water and watches his reflection change into Scar's.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Discussion began about the possibility of a sequel to <i>The Lion King</i> before the first film even hit theaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In January 1995, it was reported that a <i>Lion King</i> sequel was to be released "in the next twelve months".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> However, it was delayed, and then it was reported in May 1996 that it would be released in "early next year" of 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1996, producer Jeannine Roussel and director <a href="/wiki/Darrell_Rooney" title="Darrell Rooney">Darrell Rooney</a> signed on board to produce and direct the sequel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In December 1996, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Broderick" title="Matthew Broderick">Matthew Broderick</a> was confirmed to be returning as Simba while his wife, <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Jessica_Parker" title="Sarah Jessica Parker">Sarah Jessica Parker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Aniston" title="Jennifer Aniston">Jennifer Aniston</a> were in talks to voice Aisha, Simba's daughter. <a href="/wiki/Andy_Dick" title="Andy Dick">Andy Dick</a> was also confirmed to have signed on to voice Nuka, the son of Scar, who attempts to romance Aisha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Ultimately, the character was renamed Kiara, and voiced by <a href="/wiki/Neve_Campbell" title="Neve Campbell">Neve Campbell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Nuka was renamed Kovu, and voiced by <a href="/wiki/Jason_Marsden" title="Jason Marsden">Jason Marsden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_Times_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Then-Disney CEO <a href="/wiki/Michael_Eisner" title="Michael Eisner">Michael Eisner</a> urged for Kovu's relationship to Scar to be changed during production as being Scar's son would make him Kiara's second cousin. According to Rooney, the final draft gradually became a variation of <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Romeo and Juliet</a></i>. "It's the biggest love story we have," he explained. "The difference is that you understand the position of the parents in this film in a way you never did in the Shakespeare play."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-production-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Because none of the original animators were involved in the production, the majority of the animation was done by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Television_Animation" title="Walt Disney Television Animation">Walt Disney Television Animation</a>'s studio in Sydney, Australia. However, all storyboarding and pre-production work was done at the Feature Animation studio in Burbank, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-production-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> By March 1998, Disney confirmed the sequel would be released on October 27, 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film was first released on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> in the United States on October 27, 1998 and on <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> as a limited issue on November 23, 1999. The DVD release featured the film in a letterboxed 1.66:1 aspect ratio, the trailer for the movie, and a music video of "Love Will Find A Way" performed by <a href="/wiki/Heather_Headley" title="Heather Headley">Heather Headley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Lattimore" title="Kenny Lattimore">Kenny Lattimore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLK_on_home_video_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TLK_on_home_video-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1998, Disney believed that <i>The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</i> would be so popular that it shipped 15 million copies to stores for the October 27 release date. In March 2001, it was reported that in its first three day, 3.5 million VHS copies were sold, and ultimately about thirteen million copies were sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In September 2001, it was reported that <i>Simba's Pride</i> had sold more than 15 million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Overall, consumer spending on <i>The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</i> accumulated about $300 million — roughly the same figure of its predecessor's theatrical release at that time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> and continues to be one of the top-selling direct-to-video release of all time, with $464.5 million worldwide in sales and rentals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On August 31, 2004, the film was re-released on VHS and a 2-Disc Special Edition DVD. The DVD edition featured optional pop-up informational commentary, interactive games (the "Virtual Safari") featuring Timon and Pumbaa and Rafiki, five humorous "Find Out Why" shorts, an animated short based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebo_M" title="Lebo M">Lebo M</a>'s "One By One", and a "Proud of Simba's Pride" featurette.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The Special Edition version featured changes made to the film such as Kovu in the water being inexplicably re-animated as well as other alterations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLK_on_home_video_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-TLK_on_home_video-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> A DVD boxed set of the three <i>The Lion King</i> films (in two-disc Special Edition formats) was released on December 6, 2004. In January 2005, the film, along with the sequels, went back into moratorium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outofprint_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-outofprint-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On October 4, 2011, <i>Simba's Pride</i> was included in an eight-disc <a href="/wiki/Box_set" title="Box set">box set</a> trilogy set with the other two films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The Blu-ray edition for the film was released as a separate version on March 6, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blu-ray_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-blu-ray-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The Blu-ray edition has three different versions, a 2-disc Blu-ray/DVD combo pack, a 1-disc edition, and a digital download. The Blu-ray edition has also been attached with a new Timon &amp; Pumbaa short, in which the two friends gaze at the night sky as the star constellations resemble their favorite meal, insects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blu-ray_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-blu-ray-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The Blu-ray edition of <i>The Lion King II</i>, along with the other two films in the series, was placed into moratorium on April 30, 2013.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Critical response aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gave the film an approval rating of 33% based on six critics with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean">average score</a> of 5.6/10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/At_the_Movies_(U.S._TV_series)" title="At the Movies (U.S. TV series)">Siskel &amp; Ebert</a></i> gave the film a "two-thumbs up" and said it was a "satisfactory sequel to one of the most popular films of all time, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King" title="The Lion King">The Lion King</a></i>". However, they also said it was best that it went to video, citing that the music was lacking and not remotely equal to the original's soundtrack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">TV Guide</a></i> gave the film 2½ stars out of four, claiming that, despite being of slightly higher quality than Disney's previous direct-to-video animated sequels, "comes nowhere near the level of its big-screen predecessor", either musically or artistically. The review later went on to say that "Though most of the original characters and their voices are back, they all sound bored, apart from the zesty addition of Suzanne Pleshette as the scheming Zira. The overall result is OK for kids, who will enjoy the low humor provided by the comical meerkat Timon and the flatulent warthog Pumbaa, but it could have been so much better."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Leydon" title="Joe Leydon">Joe Leydon</a> commented in his review "In marked contrast to most of the studio's small screen sequels to bigscreen animated hits, the new pic isn't merely kids' stuff. Not unlike its predecessor, <i>Lion King II</i> has enough across-the-board appeal to entertain viewers of all ages."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Caryn James of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> concluded her review with "It's the rare sequel that matches the creative flair of an original, of course. <i>The Lion King II</i> may be derivative, but it is also winning on its own."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_Times_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> critic Stephen Witty, who graded the sequel a C+, wrote, "Despite its drawbacks, <i>The Lion King II</i> could make a decent rental for undemanding under-7 fans of the original, who won't be overburdened by the psychodrama. For true believers who've already watched and rewound their copies to shreds, it might even make a good buy. And for them, hey, hakuna matata. But for the rest of us, caveat emptor might be a better motto."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> James Plath of Movie Metropolis gave the film 6/10, saying that, "Simply put, we've seen it all before."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Felix Vasquez Jr. of <i>Cinema Crazed</i> derided, "the sequel is as predictable a sequel as can be. It takes from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fox_and_the_Hound" title="The Fox and the Hound">The Fox and the Hound</a></i> with shades of <i>Romeo and Juliet</i> and side steps the interesting Simba in favor of his bland daughter Kiara, and Timon and Pumba [<i>sic</i>]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Songs">Songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The songwriters were <a href="/wiki/Marty_Panzer" title="Marty Panzer">Marty Panzer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Snow_(songwriter)" title="Tom Snow (songwriter)">Tom Snow</a>, Kevin Quinn, Randy Petersen, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Mancina" title="Mark Mancina">Mark Mancina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jay_Rifkin" title="Jay Rifkin">Jay Rifkin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebo_M" title="Lebo M">Lebo M</a>, Jack Feldman, Scott Warrender, and <a href="/wiki/Joss_Whedon" title="Joss Whedon">Joss Whedon</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/He_Lives_in_You" title="He Lives in You">He Lives in You</a>" – Sung by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebo_M" title="Lebo M">Lebo M</a> and his African choir. This song represents Kiara's birth and is the equivalent of "<a href="/wiki/Circle_of_Life" title="Circle of Life">Circle of Life</a>". The song is a direct reference to when Rafiki told Simba in the first film that Mufasa "lives" in him. Also appears in the <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_(musical)" title="The Lion King (musical)">Broadway version</a> of the first film. The end title is performed by <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>.</li>
<li>"We Are One" – Sung by <a href="/wiki/Cam_Clarke" title="Cam Clarke">Cam Clarke</a> and Charity Sanoy. Following Kiara's encounter with Kovu and Zira in which she endangers herself, Simba explains how important she is to the pride and that the pride is one. It can be seen as the musical equivalent to the first film's talk about the Great Kings of the Past with Mufasa and Simba.</li>
<li>"My Lullaby" – Sung by <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Pleshette" title="Suzanne Pleshette">Suzanne Pleshette</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Dick" title="Andy Dick">Andy Dick</a>, and Crysta Macalush. Zira's lullaby to Kovu, which outlines her plot for him to kill Simba and how proud it would make her. The equivalent to "<a href="/wiki/Be_Prepared_(song)" title="Be Prepared (song)">Be Prepared</a>" as both songs detail the murder plots of the two villains. There are several allusions to the first film, including the similarities in the ending sequences of both songs and Zira's treatment of Nuka during the song, which resembles Scar's abuse of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shenzi,_Banzai_and_Ed" title="Shenzi, Banzai and Ed">Shenzi, Banzai and Ed</a>.</li>
<li>"Upendi" (Swahili for "love") – Sung by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Guillaume" title="Robert Guillaume">Robert Guillaume</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liz_Callaway" title="Liz Callaway">Liz Callaway</a>, Gene Miller, and <a href="/wiki/Ladysmith_Black_Mambazo" title="Ladysmith Black Mambazo">Ladysmith Black Mambazo</a>. Rafiki's song to Kiara and Kovu about love, friendship, and happiness. Sung by Rafiki and his animal friends. Also the equivalent to "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hakuna_Matata" title="Hakuna Matata">Hakuna Matata</a>", from the first film, as well as "<a href="/wiki/I_Just_Can%27t_Wait_to_Be_King" title="I Just Can't Wait to Be King">I Just Can't Wait to Be King</a>" visually.</li>
<li>"Not One of Us" – Sung following Kovu being exiled by Simba after he wrongfully accuses Kovu of betraying him. This was the first time the animals other than lions outside of the main characters (excluding <i>Lion King 1½</i>) have been seen talking. This is the only song to not have an equivalent to the first film, but may lyrically reflect on Mufasa's or Scar's death.</li>
<li>"Love Will Find a Way" – Sung by <a href="/wiki/Liz_Callaway" title="Liz Callaway">Liz Callaway</a> and Gene Miller. A romantic love song that includes of Kiara and Kovu's first encounter following Kovu's banishment. The pair concludes that their mutual feelings for each other are too strong and true for their differences to keep them apart. Similar to "<a href="/wiki/Can_You_Feel_the_Love_Tonight" title="Can You Feel the Love Tonight">Can You Feel the Love Tonight</a>". The end title version is performed by R&amp;B artists <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Lattimore" title="Kenny Lattimore">Kenny Lattimore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heather_Headley" title="Heather Headley">Heather Headley</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtrack">Soundtrack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Soundtrack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An audio CD entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Return_to_Pride_Rock:_Songs_Inspired_by_Disney%27s_The_Lion_King_II:_Simba%27s_Pride" title="Return to Pride Rock: Songs Inspired by Disney's The Lion King II: Simba's Pride">Return to Pride Rock: Songs Inspired by Disney's The Lion King II: Simba's Pride</a></i> was released on September 8, 1998. Although not promoted as a soundtrack to the film, it contained all the songs from the film and some additional songs inspired by it by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebo_M" title="Lebo M">Lebo M</a>.</p>
