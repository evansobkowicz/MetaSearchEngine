<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><span dir="auto">Comin' Thro' the Rye</span></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote">For the 1923 film starring Alma Taylor, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comin%27_Thro_the_Rye_(film)" title="Comin' Thro the Rye (film)">Comin' Thro the Rye (film)</a>.</div>
<p>"<b>Comin' Thro' the Rye</b>" is a poem written in 1782 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a> (1759–96). It is well known as a traditional <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_song" title="Children's song">children's song</a>, with the words put to the melody of the Scottish Minstrel <i>Common' Frae The Town</i>. This is a variant of the tune to which <i><a href="/wiki/Auld_Lang_Syne" title="Auld Lang Syne">Auld Lang Syne</a></i> is usually sung—the melodic shape is almost identical, the difference lying in the tempo and rhythm.</p>
<p>G. W. Napier, in an 1876 <i><a href="/wiki/Notes_and_Queries" title="Notes and Queries">Notes and Queries</a></i>, wrote that,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The original words of "Comin' thro' the rye" cannot be satisfactorily traced. There are many different versions of the song. The version which is now to be found in the <i>Works</i> of Burns is the one given in <a href="/wiki/Scots_Musical_Museum" title="Scots Musical Museum">Johnson's <i>Museum</i></a>, which passed through the hands of Burns; but the song itself, in some form or other, was known long before Burns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The protagonist, "Jenny", is not further identified, but there has been reference to a "Jenny from Dalry" and a longstanding legend in the town of <a href="/wiki/Dalry,_North_Ayrshire" title="Dalry, North Ayrshire">Dalry, North Ayrshire</a>, holds that "comin thro' the rye" describes crossing a <a href="/wiki/Ford_(crossing)" title="Ford (crossing)">ford</a> through the <a href="/wiki/River_Garnock#Rye_Water" title="River Garnock">Rye Water</a> at <a href="/wiki/Drakemyre,_North_Ayrshire" title="Drakemyre, North Ayrshire">Drakemyre</a> to the north of the town, downstream from Ryefield House and not far from the confluence of the Rye with the River Garnock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cairney2011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cairney2011-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Folksinger_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Folksinger-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> When this story appeared in the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Herald_(Glasgow)" title="The Herald (Glasgow)">Glasgow Herald</a></i> in 1867, it was soon disputed with the assertion that everyone understood the rye to be a field of <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, wet with <a href="/wiki/Dew" title="Dew">dew</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns1871_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burns1871-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> An alternative suggestion is that "the rye" was a long narrow <a href="/wiki/Cobblestone" title="Cobblestone">cobblestone</a> paved lane, prone to puddles of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cairney2011_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cairney2011-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While the original poem is already full of sexual imagery, an alternative version makes this more explicit. It has a different chorus, referring to a phallic "staun o' staunin' graith", "kiss" is replaced by "<a href="/wiki/Fuck" title="Fuck">fuck</a>", and Jenny's "thing" in stanza four is identified as her "<a href="/wiki/Cunt" title="Cunt">cunt</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>O, Jenny's a' weet,<sup class="reference" id="ref_AA"><a href="#endnote_AA">[A]</a></sup> poor body,<br/>
Jenny's seldom dry:<br/>
She draigl't<sup class="reference" id="ref_BB"><a href="#endnote_BB">[B]</a></sup> a' her petticoatie,<br/>
Comin thro' the rye!<br/>
<br/>
Chorus:<br/>
Comin thro' the rye, poor body,<br/>
Comin thro' the rye,<br/>
She draigl't a' her petticoatie,<br/>
Comin thro' the rye!<br/>
<br/>
Gin<sup class="reference" id="ref_CC"><a href="#endnote_CC">[C]</a></sup> a body meet a body<br/>
Comin thro' the rye,<br/>
Gin a body kiss a body,<br/>
Need a body cry?<sup class="reference" id="ref_DD"><a href="#endnote_DD">[D]</a></sup><br/>
<br/>
(chorus)<br/>
<br/>
Gin a body meet a body<br/>
Comin thro' the glen<br/>
Gin a body kiss a body,<br/>
Need the warl'<sup class="reference" id="ref_EE"><a href="#endnote_EE">[E]</a></sup> ken?<sup class="reference" id="ref_FF"><a href="#endnote_FF">[F]</a></sup><br/>
<br/>
(chorus)<br/>
<br/>
Gin a body meet a body<br/>
Comin thro' the grain;<br/>
Gin a body kiss a body,<br/>
The thing's a body's ain.<sup class="reference" id="ref_GG"><a href="#endnote_GG">[G]</a></sup><br/>
<br/>
(chorus)<br/>
<br/>
Ilka lassie has her laddie,<br/>
Nane, they say, ha’e I<br/>
Yet all the lads they smile on me,<br/>
When comin' thro' the rye.</p>
</div>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Catcher_in_the_Rye"><i>The Catcher in the Rye</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comin%27_Thro%27_the_Rye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Catcher in the Rye">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The title of the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Catcher_in_the_Rye" title="The Catcher in the Rye">The Catcher in the Rye</a></i> (1951) by J. D. Salinger comes from the poem's name. Holden Caulfield, the protagonist, misinterprets a part of this poem to mean "if a body catch a body" rather than "if a body meet a body." He keeps picturing children playing in a field of rye near the edge of a cliff, and him catching them when they start to fall off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cover_versions">Cover versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comin%27_Thro%27_the_Rye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cover versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The song was covered by <a href="/wiki/Marian_Anderson" title="Marian Anderson">Marian Anderson</a> in 1944</li>
<li>The song was covered by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Haley_%26_His_Comets" title="Bill Haley &amp; His Comets">Bill Haley &amp; His Comets</a> in 1956 as ‘Rockin' Through The Rye’. Bill Haley had updated the lyrics to a more 1950's hip slang (included the lyrics, "All the lassies rock with me when rockin' through the rye"). In Sept 1956, when the record was climbing the UK charts, the single was banned by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> from its playlist because they felt the song went against traditional British standards. The record, was at No.5 on the UK charts.</li>
<li>The song was covered by <a href="/wiki/Alvin_and_the_Chipmunks" title="Alvin and the Chipmunks">Alvin and the Chipmunks</a> for their 1960 album <i><a href="/wiki/Around_the_World_with_The_Chipmunks" title="Around the World with The Chipmunks">Around the World with The Chipmunks</a></i>.</li>
<li>The song was covered by <a href="/wiki/The_Real_McKenzies" title="The Real McKenzies">The Real McKenzies</a> for their 2005 album "<a href="/wiki/10,000_Shots" title="10,000 Shots">10,000 Shots</a>."</li>
<li>The song was covered by Ava Gardner in the 1953 John Ford film "Mogambo." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The song was parodied by <a href="/wiki/Allan_Sherman" title="Allan Sherman">Allan Sherman</a> on his 1963 album <i><a href="/wiki/My_Son,_the_Celebrity" title="My Son, the Celebrity">My Son, the Celebrity</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
