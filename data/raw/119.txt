<p>The official motion picture soundtrack for <i><b><a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code_(film)" title="The Da Vinci Code (film)">The Da Vinci Code</a></b></i> with <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bowes_(violinist)" title="Thomas Bowes (violinist)">Thomas Bowes (violinist)</a>, King's Consort Choir, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Marsh" title="Hugh Marsh">Hugh Marsh</a>, Orchestra, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Harvey" title="Richard Harvey">Richard Harvey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hila_Plitmann" title="Hila Plitmann">Hila Plitmann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Tillman" title="Martin Tillman">Martin Tillman</a> was released on May 9, 2006 via Decca label. The film's music was composed by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Zimmer" title="Hans Zimmer">Hans Zimmer</a>, whose work resulted in a nomination for the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Original_Score" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Original Score">Best Original Score</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code_(soundtrack)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For a soundscape that was religious to the core, Zimmer used a massive orchestra and chorus to create a dramatic '<a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">stained glass</a> <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a>' feeling. While the score has more in common with Zimmer's previous work for <i><a href="/wiki/Hannibal_(film)" title="Hannibal (film)">Hannibal</a></i>, there is also a solid mixture of the motifs used for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thin_Red_Line_(1998_film)" title="The Thin Red Line (1998 film)">The Thin Red Line</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Begins" title="Batman Begins">Batman Begins</a></i>. The thirteenth cue, "Chevaliers de Sangreal", is the most bombastic; powerfully underscoring the 'discovery' scene in the film.</p>
<p>Like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_Ventures" title="Media Ventures">Media Ventures</a> protégé <a href="/wiki/Harry_Gregson-Williams" title="Harry Gregson-Williams">Harry Gregson-Williams</a>, who composed the soundtrack for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronicles_of_Narnia:_The_Lion,_the_Witch_and_the_Wardrobe_(soundtrack)" title="The Chronicles of Narnia: The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe (soundtrack)">The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe</a></i>, Zimmer used <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road_Studios" title="Abbey Road Studios">Abbey Road Studios</a> to help create his music for <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>. Additional sections were recorded at London's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIR_Studios" title="AIR Studios">AIR Studios</a>, atop <a href="/wiki/Rosslyn_Hill" title="Rosslyn Hill">Rosslyn Hill</a>.</p>
<p>Director <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a> commented that "Like every other facet of this movie, the score for <i>The Da Vinci Code</i> demanded a range of textures that recognized and reinforced the layers of ideas and emotion, which unfold as the basic story does." Claiming that Zimmer was "inspired", Howard added that "Hans Zimmer has given us extraordinarily memorable music to appreciate within the framework of a film or completely on its own, where you can let the sounds carry you on your own private journey."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-album_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-album-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It was rumored that the song <a href="/wiki/A-ha" title="A-ha">A-ha</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Celice_(song)" title="Celice (song)">Celice</a> would be in the soundtrack to the film so that song is a double entendre for the torture device, the <a href="/wiki/Cilice" title="Cilice">cilice</a>, and the name of a woman named "Celice" whose presence seems to torture the men, but this did not occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code_(soundtrack)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Da Vinci Code</i> '​s director, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a>, said that the soundtrack was "powerful, fresh and wonderfully effective"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-album_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-album-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and most film music reviewers agreed with him. Soundtrack.Net and Scorereviews rated the score highly. The music was nominated for a 2007 Golden Globe Award for Best Original Score but lost to <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Desplat" title="Alexandre Desplat">Alexandre Desplat</a>'s work for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Painted_Veil_(2006_film)" title="The Painted Veil (2006 film)">The Painted Veil</a></i>.</p>
