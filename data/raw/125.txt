<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><span dir="auto">Holden Caulfield</span></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Holden Caulfield</b> (born <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">c.</a>1933) is the fictional teenage <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrator" title="Narrator">narrator</a> of author <a href="/wiki/J._D._Salinger" title="J. D. Salinger">J. D. Salinger</a>'s 1951 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Catcher_in_the_Rye" title="The Catcher in the Rye">The Catcher in the Rye</a></i>. Since the book's publication, Holden has become an icon for teenage <a href="/wiki/Rebellion" title="Rebellion">rebellion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angst" title="Angst">angst</a>, and now stands among the most important characters of 20th-century American literature. The name Holden Caulfield was used in an unpublished short story written in 1942 and first appeared in print in 1945.</p>
<p>Although it has been conjectured that J. D. Salinger got the name for Holden Caulfield in <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> when he saw a marquee for <i><a href="/wiki/Dear_Ruth" title="Dear Ruth">Dear Ruth</a></i> (1947), starring <a href="/wiki/William_Holden" title="William Holden">William Holden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joan_Caulfield" title="Joan Caulfield">Joan Caulfield</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Salinger's first Holden Caulfield story, "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_Crazy" title="I'm Crazy">I'm Crazy</a>," appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Collier%27s" title="Collier's">Collier's</a></i> on December 22, 1945, a year and a half before this movie was released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance_and_personality">Appearance and personality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holden_Caulfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Appearance and personality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Holden is naive and at the same time resentful of the adult world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> One of Holden's most striking and quintessential qualities is his powerful revulsion for "phony" qualities, a catch-all term for all the perceived hypocrisy that irritates Holden. It is this <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(contemporary)" title="Cynicism (contemporary)">cynicism</a> that causes him to distance himself from other people. Holden is very much a character of contradiction; at sixteen years of age he is six feet two-and-a-half inches tall, well above the adult male average, and already has some grey hair - though he himself admits that he acts more like a 13-year-old than an adult. He continually fails classes and calls himself "dumb," yet he shows intelligence through his exceptionally articulate narration. This idea in the book may be Holden's criticism of a society that is unable to acknowledge his hidden intelligence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_The_Catcher_in_the_Rye">In <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holden_Caulfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In The Catcher in the Rye">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Holden Caulfield is the narrator and main character of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Catcher_in_the_Rye" title="The Catcher in the Rye">The Catcher in the Rye</a></i>. The novel recounts Holden's week in New York City during <a href="/wiki/School_holiday" title="School holiday">Christmas break</a> following his expulsion from Pencey Prep, a preparatory school based loosely on Salinger's alma mater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valley_Forge_Military_Academy" title="Valley Forge Military Academy">Valley Forge Military Academy</a>. Holden Caulfield tells his story from a hospital in California in a cynical and jaded language, often swearing and using profanity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_works">In other works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holden_Caulfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In other works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The character, as <b>Holden Morrisey Caulfield</b>, also appears in Salinger's "<a href="/wiki/Slight_Rebellion_off_Madison" title="Slight Rebellion off Madison">Slight Rebellion off Madison</a>", published in the December 21, 1946 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>. An earlier version of this story, titled "Are You Banging Your Head Against a Wall?" was accepted for publication by <i>The New Yorker</i> in October 1941, but was not published then because editors found the tone to be too desolate for its readership. An edited version of this <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short story</a> later became the basis of several chapters in the middle-late section of <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> dealing with Caulfield's date with Sally Hayes, during which he confesses his desire to run away with her, he meets Carl Luce for drinks, and he makes a drunken phone call to the Hayes home. Unlike the similar sequence in the novel, Caulfield is on a <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> break from school, and, in the story, the interlude with Sally is split into two occurrences. Also, the meeting with Carl Luce is considerably briefer in the story than in the novel.</p>
<p>Caulfield also figures as a character in the short story "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_Crazy" title="I'm Crazy">I'm Crazy</a>", published in <i>Colliers</i> (December 22, 1945), and other members of the Caulfield family are featured in "<a href="/wiki/Last_Day_of_the_Last_Furlough" title="Last Day of the Last Furlough">Last Day of the Last Furlough</a>", published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Saturday_Evening_Post" title="The Saturday Evening Post">The Saturday Evening Post</a></i> (July 15, 1944) and the unpublished short stories "<a href="/wiki/The_Last_and_Best_of_the_Peter_Pans" title="The Last and Best of the Peter Pans">The Last and Best of the Peter Pans</a>" (c. 1942) and "<a href="/wiki/The_Ocean_Full_of_Bowling_Balls" title="The Ocean Full of Bowling Balls">The Ocean Full of Bowling Balls</a>" (c. 1945). "I'm Crazy" is closely related to the first chapter of <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i>. It begins with Caulfield standing on a hill at "Pencey Prep" watching a football game below, and develops as Holden visits with his history teacher, Mr. Spencer, for a talk about his expulsion from school and his future. Several other details match those found in the first chapter of <i>Catcher</i>, including a reference to the mother of one of Caulfield's schoolmates and to his own mother sending him a gift of ice skates, but the story ends with his returning home instead of running away from school. Once home, he is not shown confronting his parents, who, according to the maid, are playing bridge. Instead, he goes to speak to Phoebe. Their dialogue is similar to that which appears in the later chapters of <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i>. The other notable feature of the story is that his sister Viola gets her first, and only, mention in the Caulfield saga.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/This_Sandwich_Has_No_Mayonnaise" title="This Sandwich Has No Mayonnaise">This Sandwich Has No Mayonnaise</a>" (<i><a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)">Esquire</a></i>, 1945) reveals that Holden went missing in action during the war.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Caulfield_family_in_other_works">Caulfield family in other works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holden_Caulfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Caulfield family in other works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Last Day of the Last Furlough" relates the final day of Babe Gladwaller before he leaves to fight in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Gladwaller spends part of the day with his little sister before Vincent Caulfield (later renamed D.B. in the novel) arrives. At that point Vincent is a fellow soldier about to leave for the war. Vincent announces that his brother, Holden, has been declared missing in action. Gladwaller's relationship with his younger sister can be seen as a parallel to Caulfield's relationship with Phoebe.</p>
<p>"The Last and Best of the Peter Pans" relates the story of Vincent's (D.B.) <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">draft</a> questionnaire being hidden by his mother. The events occur just after the death of Kenneth (later renamed Allie) and reveal the anxiety of Mary Moriarity, an actress and Caulfield's mother. The story is notable for the appearance of Phoebe and Vincent's statements about a child crawling off a cliff.</p>
<p>In "The Ocean Full of Bowling Balls," Vincent (D.B.) recalls the day his brother Kenneth (Allie) died. The story is set at the Caulfield summer home on <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a>. Several details make their way from this story into <i>Catcher</i>, including the characterization of Allie; Allie's poetry-inscribed left-handed baseball mitt; Vincent's girlfriend, Helen, who keeps her kings in the back row (like Jane Gallagher); and Caulfield's critical view of others. While the cause of death in "Catcher" is <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a>, here it is due to an unspecified heart condition. Toward the end of the story, Kenneth and Vincent are on the beach. Kenneth decides to go swimming and is knocked out by a wave. Holden, just home from camp, is waiting on the porch with his suitcases as Vincent comes back with Kenneth's unconscious body. Kenneth passes away later the same night. The story was reportedly sold to a magazine, only to be taken back by Salinger before publication.</p>
<p>Another short story of note with relationship to Caulfield is "The Boy in the People Shooting Hat," which was submitted to <i>The New Yorker</i> sometime between 1948 and 1949 but was never published. It focuses on a fight between two characters named Bobby and Stradlater over Bobby's feelings about Jane Gallagher. This story appears to form the basis for several key scenes in the first several chapters of <i>The Catcher in the Rye</i>.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Raise_High_the_Roof_Beam,_Carpenters_and_Seymour:_An_Introduction#Seymour:_An_Introduction" title="Raise High the Roof Beam, Carpenters and Seymour: An Introduction">Seymour: An Introduction</a></i> a Curtis Caulfield is mentioned in passing as "an exceptionally intelligent and likable boy" who appeared on the same radio show as Seymour and the other <a href="/wiki/Glass_family" title="Glass family">Glass children</a>. He is reportedly "killed during one of the landings in the Pacific."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_impact">Cultural impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holden_Caulfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultural impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Holden Caulfield is one of the most enduring characters in 20th-century <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fiction</a>. It has been suggested that Salinger himself related so closely to Holden that he was protective of the character and this was the reason he was unwilling to allow filming of the book or use of the character by other writers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>The Catcher in the Rye</i> is required reading in many <a href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a> English courses today, although it has been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banned_books" title="Banned books">banned</a> from some school libraries by parent and teacher groups opposed to its use of profanity and perceived glorification of rebellion.</li>
<li>Holden was influential in the life of <a href="/wiki/Mark_David_Chapman" title="Mark David Chapman">Mark David Chapman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> the deranged ex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beatles" title="Beatles">Beatles</a> fan who murdered <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a> in 1980.</li>
<li>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Family_Guy" title="Family Guy">Family Guy</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Jerome_Is_the_New_Black" title="Jerome Is the New Black">Jerome Is the New Black</a>", primary character Brian is remarked upon as being fond of Caulfield as proof of his pretentious and immature personality by Quagmire, who describes Caulfield as "a spoiled brat". An unnamed character obsessed with "phoniness" also appears on the show, spray-painting "PHONY" on Peter's car after seeing him pretending to play a keyboard in a toy store. The character is listed as "Holden Caulfield" in the show's credits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">Green Day</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Kerplunk_(album)" title="Kerplunk (album)">Kerplunk</a></i> (1992), there is a song called 'Who Wrote Holden Caulfield?'</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Screeching_Weasel" title="Screeching Weasel">Screeching Weasel</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/How_to_Make_Enemies_and_Irritate_People" title="How to Make Enemies and Irritate People">How to Make Enemies and Irritate People</a></i>, there is a song called "I Wrote Holden Caulfield".</li>
<li>In the comic strip <i><a href="/wiki/Frazz" title="Frazz">Frazz</a></i>, Frazz's best friend is an 8-year-old named Caulfield, who emulates the character of Holden Caulfield by always reading books that are above his grade level, such as <i>Catch-22</i> and <i>The Old Man and the Sea</i>. He also asks odd questions in class (e.g. "If horses don't really like spurs, why do barbed wires keep them in?").</li>
<li>In the <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Scrotie_McBoogerballs" title="The Tale of Scrotie McBoogerballs">The Tale of Scrotie McBoogerballs</a>", the fourth grade class is assigned to read the novel, which they take interest in once they hear it was only recently taken off the list of banned books. However, the boys are in no way surprised by the novel's controversial content, prompting the story for the remainder of the episode: to write their own novel as vulgar and offensive as possible to get it on the banned books list</li>
<li>In the 2002 black comedy-drama film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Good_Girl" title="The Good Girl">The Good Girl</a></i>, the film's protagonist, Thomas "Holden" Worther (portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Jake_Gyllenhaal" title="Jake Gyllenhaal">Jake Gyllenhaal</a>), constantly has his nose in “<a href="/wiki/The_Catcher_in_the_Rye" title="The Catcher in the Rye">The Catcher in the Rye</a>” and claims that his own life parallels that of Holden Caulfield.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Streetlight_Manifesto" title="Streetlight Manifesto">Streetlight Manifesto</a>'s song "Here's to Life," mentions Holden Caulfield as one of the several suicidal people included in the song.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ben_Affleck" title="Ben Affleck">Ben Affleck</a>'s character in <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Smith" title="Kevin Smith">Kevin Smith</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Chasing_Amy" title="Chasing Amy">Chasing Amy</a></i> is named Holden after Holden Caulfield.</li>
<li>In <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grow_Up_(Brooks_novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grow Up (Brooks novel) (page does not exist)"><i>Grow Up</i></a>, the protagonist, Jasper, says "I am Holden Caulfield, only less reckless, and more attractive" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Five_Iron_Frenzy" title="Five Iron Frenzy">Five Iron Frenzy</a>'s song "Superpowers" quips "I sometimes feel like I'm Holden Caulfield, sometimes Jack Keroac."</li>
</ul>
