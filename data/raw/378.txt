mischievous grin. While most often celebrated in "Alice"-related contexts, the Cheshire Cat has transcended the context of literature and become enmeshed in popular culture, appearing in various forms of media, from political cartoons to television, as well as cross-disciplinary studies, from business to science. One of its distinguishing features is that from time to time its body disappears, the last thing visible being its iconic grin.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of the Cheshire Cat begin in the 18th century. <i>A classical dictionary of the vulgar tongue</i> (1788) by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Grose" title="Francis Grose">Francis Grose</a> (The Second Edition, Corrected and Enlarged, London) contains the following entry: "CHESHIRE CAT. He grins like a Cheshire cat; said of any one who shows his teeth and gums in laughing."</p>
<p>The phrase appears again in print in <a href="/wiki/John_Wolcot" title="John Wolcot">John Wolcot</a>'s pseudonymous <a href="/wiki/John_Wolcot" title="John Wolcot">Peter Pindar</a>'s <i>Pair of Lyric Epistles</i> (1792): "Lo, like a Cheshire cat our court will grin." The "Cheshire Cat" is also the name of a character in <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s famous novel, <a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dairy_farming">Dairy farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dairy farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A possible origin of the phrase "Grinning like a Cheshire Cat" is one favoured by the people of <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a>, a county in England which boasts numerous dairy farms; hence the cats grin because of the abundance of milk and cream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cheese_moulds">Cheese moulds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cheese moulds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_Dictionary_of_Phrase_and_Fable" title="Brewer's Dictionary of Phrase and Fable">Brewer's Dictionary of Phrase and Fable</a></i> says <i>grinning like a Cheshire cat</i> is "an old <a href="/wiki/Simile" title="Simile">simile</a>, popularised by Lewis Carroll". According to <i>Brewer's Dictionary</i>, "The phrase has never been satisfactorily accounted for, but it has been said that cheese was formerly sold in Cheshire moulded like a cat that looked as though it was grinning".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The cheese was cut from the tail end, so that the last part eaten was the head of the smiling cat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Church_carvings">Church carvings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Church carvings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many reports that Carroll found inspiration for the name and expression of the Cheshire Cat in the 16th century sandstone carving of a grinning cat, on the west face of <a href="/wiki/St_Wilfrid%27s_Church,_Grappenhall" title="St Wilfrid's Church, Grappenhall">St. Wilfrid's Church</a> tower in <a href="/wiki/Grappenhall" title="Grappenhall">Grappenhall</a>, a village adjacent to his birthplace in <a href="/wiki/Daresbury" title="Daresbury">Daresbury</a>, Cheshire.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Lewis Carroll's father, Reverend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Dodgson_(priest)" title="Charles Dodgson (priest)">Charles Dodgson</a>, was Rector of Croft and Archdeacon of Richmond in North Yorkshire, England, from 1843 to 1868; Carroll lived here from 1843 to 1850.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Historians believe Lewis Carroll's Cheshire Cat in the book <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> was inspired by a carving in Croft church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, members of the Lewis Carroll Society attributed it to a <a href="/wiki/Gargoyle" title="Gargoyle">gargoyle</a> found on a pillar in St. Nicolas Church, <a href="/wiki/Cranleigh" title="Cranleigh">Cranleigh</a>, where Carroll used to travel frequently when he lived in Guildford (though this is doubtful as he moved to Guildford some three years after <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> had been published) and a carving in a church in the village of <a href="/wiki/Croft-on-Tees" title="Croft-on-Tees">Croft-on-Tees</a>, in the north east of England, where his father had been <a href="/wiki/Rector_(ecclesiastical)" title="Rector (ecclesiastical)">rector</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Carroll is believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2013)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to have visited <a href="/wiki/St_Christopher%27s_Church,_Pott_Shrigley" title="St Christopher's Church, Pott Shrigley">St. Christopher's church</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pott_Shrigley" title="Pott Shrigley">Pott Shrigley</a>, Cheshire, which has a stone sculpture most closely resembling the pictorial cat in the book.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heraldic_lion">Heraldic lion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Heraldic lion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1853, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Maunder" title="Samuel Maunder">Samuel Maunder</a> explains:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"This phrase owes its origin to the unhappy attempts of a sign painter of that country to represent a lion rampant, which was the crest of an influential family, on the sign-boards of many of the inns. The resemblance of these lions to cats caused them to be generally called by the more ignoble name. A similar case is to be found in the village of Charlton, between Pewsey and Devizes, Wiltshire. A public-house by the roadside is commonly known by the name of The Cat at Charlton. The sign of the house was originally a lion or tiger, or some such animal, the crest of the family of Sir Edward Poore."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lewis_Carroll.27s_character">Lewis Carroll's character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lewis Carroll's character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Cheshire Cat is largely associated with the character of the same name in Lewis Carroll's novel, <a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a>. Alice first encounters the Cheshire Cat at <a href="/wiki/Duchess_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Duchess (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">the Duchess</a>' house in her kitchen, and then later outside on the branches of a tree, where it appears and disappears at will, engaging Alice in amusing but sometimes vexing conversation. The cat sometimes raises philosophical points that annoy or baffle <a href="/wiki/Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Alice (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Alice</a>. It does, however, appear to cheer her up when it turns up suddenly at the <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Queen of Hearts (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Queen of Hearts</a>' <a href="/wiki/Croquet" title="Croquet">croquet</a> field, and when sentenced to death baffles everyone by having made its head appear without its body, sparking a massive argument between the executioner and the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="King of Hearts (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">King</a> and Queen of Hearts about whether something that does not have a body can indeed be <a href="/wiki/Decapitation" title="Decapitation">beheaded</a>.</p>
<p>At one point, the cat disappears gradually until nothing is left but its grin, prompting Alice to remark that she has often seen a cat without a grin but never a grin without a cat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Screen_media_adaptations">Screen media adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Screen media adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cheshire Cat character has been re-depicted by other creators, as well as used as the inspiration for new characters, primarily in screen media (film, television, video games) and print media (literature, comics, art). Other non-media contexts that embrace the Cheshire Cat include music, business, and science.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_adaptations">Film adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Film adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Disney.27s_1951_animated_film.2C_Alice_in_Wonderland">Disney's 1951 animated film, <i>Alice in Wonderland</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Disney's 1951 animated film, Alice in Wonderland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the 1951 Disney animated film, <i><a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (1951 film)">Alice in Wonderland</a></i>, the Cheshire Cat is depicted as an intelligent yet mischievous character that sometimes helps Alice and sometimes gets her into trouble. He frequently sings the first verse of the <a href="/wiki/Jabberwocky" title="Jabberwocky">Jabberwocky</a> poem, "Twas brillig" and tells Alice that "Most everyone's mad here". The 1951 Disney interpretation of the Cheshire Cat is able to separate his body parts and rearrange them as he pleases. The animated character was voiced by <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Holloway" title="Sterling Holloway">Sterling Holloway</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (1951 film)">Alice in Wonderland</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Cummings" title="Jim Cummings">Jim Cummings</a> (2004–present).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Disney.27s_1999_TV_film.2C_Alice">Disney's 1999 TV film, <i>Alice</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Disney's 1999 TV film, Alice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(1999_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (1999 film)">1999 television adaptation</a> of Carroll's books, the Cheshire Cat is portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Whoopi_Goldberg" title="Whoopi Goldberg">Whoopi Goldberg</a>. She fully acts as an ally and friend to Alice, the latter defending the cat when she is threatened with execution.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tim_Burton_for_Disney.27s_2010_live-action_film.2C_Alice_in_Wonderland">Tim Burton for Disney's 2010 live-action film, <i>Alice in Wonderland</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tim Burton for Disney's 2010 live-action film, Alice in Wonderland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Cheshire Cat appears in Walt Disney's 2010 <i><a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(2010_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (2010 film)">Alice in Wonderland</a>,</i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Burton" title="Tim Burton">Tim Burton</a>. British actor <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Fry" title="Stephen Fry">Stephen Fry</a> voices the character. In the movie, Cheshire (as he is referred to by other Wonderland characters) first appears to Alice as she is walking alone in the forest. He helps her by binding the wound she suffered earlier by the <a href="/wiki/Bandersnatch" title="Bandersnatch">Bandersnatch</a> and taking her to <a href="/wiki/The_Hatter" title="The Hatter">The Hatter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/March_Hare" title="March Hare">March Hare</a>. He is later blamed by the Hatter for deserting during the Red Queen's attack where the White Queen's throne was usurped. The Cheshire Cat defends himself by saying that he did not leave, but merely turned invisible. However, as the plot moves along, he redeems himself by changing his form to resemble the Hatter during his execution and then evaporates as the axe is about to make contact, thus fooling everyone as the Hatter's hat floats up to the Queen's seat and Cheshire appears and winks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-script_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-script-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The character was voiced by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Fry" title="Stephen Fry">Stephen Fry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily_cast_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-daily_cast-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> In the video game adaptation of the movie, Cheshire is a playable character who can not only turn himself invisible, but other objects around him as well.</p>

<p>In addition to the Cheshire Cat's appearances in films central to its <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a> origins, the Cheshire Cat has been featured in other cinematic works:</p>
<ul>
<li>The late filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Chris_Marker" title="Chris Marker">Chris Marker</a> gave his monumental documentary on the New Left movement of 1967–1977, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_fond_de_l%27air_est_rouge" title="Le fond de l'air est rouge">Le fond de l'air est rouge</a></i> (1977), the English title <i>Grin Without a Cat</i>. Like the original, it signifies that revolution was in the air but failed to take root. In the film, it is also stated: <i>a spearhead without a spear, a grin without a cat.</i></li>
<li>The Disney version of the character can also be spotted during the final scene of the film <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Framed_Roger_Rabbit" title="Who Framed Roger Rabbit">Who Framed Roger Rabbit</a></i> (1988). The Cheshire Cat is heard singing the poem "<a href="/wiki/Jabberwocky" title="Jabberwocky">Jabberwocky</a>" before he materialises in front of Alice.</li>
<li>The Cheshire Cat made non-speaking cameos in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Mouse" title="House of Mouse">House of Mouse</a></i>.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anime_and_manga">Anime and manga</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Anime and manga">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_science">In science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheshire_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: In science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The scientific community has embraced the Cheshire Cat as a <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> to explain several scientific phenomena:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Cheshire Cat effect, as described by Sally Duensing and Bob Miller, is a <a href="/wiki/Binocular_rivalry" title="Binocular rivalry">binocular rivalry</a> which causes stationary objects seen in one eye to disappear from view when an object in motion crosses in front of the other eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Each eye sees two different views of the world, sends those images to the <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">visual cortex</a> where they are combined, and creates a three-dimensional image. The Cheshire Cat effect occurs when one eye is fixated on a stationary object, while the other notices something moving. Since one eye is seeing a moving object, the brain will focus on it, causing parts of the stationary object to fade away from vision entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>In another scientific context, catalytic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNAs" title="RNAs">RNAs</a> have been deemed Cheshire cats. This metaphor is used to describe the fading of the <a href="/wiki/Ribonucleotide" title="Ribonucleotide">ribonucleotide</a> construct, which leaves behind only a <i>smile</i> of only the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> components of the <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Similarly, the Cheshire Cat has been used out of its traditional context to help define another scientific phenomenon: the "Cheshire Cat" escape strategy. When <a href="/wiki/Coccolithophore" title="Coccolithophore">Coccolithophore</a>—a successful ocean <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>—is able to <a href="/wiki/Ploidy#Haploid_and_monoploid" title="Ploidy">resist the haploid phase</a> of its life cycle it escapes meiosis and its dominant diploid genes are passed on in a virus-free environment, freeing the host from the danger of infection during reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The algae escape death (beheading) by means of disappearance (vanishing his head):</li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p>"…[T]aken from Lewis Carroll, we liken this theory to the strategy used by the Cheshire Cat in Alice's Adventures in Wonderland of making its body invisible to make the sentence “off with his head” pronounced by the Queen of Hearts impossible to execute…CC dynamics, which rely to some extent on separation of the sexual processes of meiosis and fusion in time and/or space, release the host from short-term pathogen pressure, thus widening the scope for the host to evolve in other directions."</p>
</blockquote>
<ul>
<li>Other gestures to the Cheshire Cat's tropes of disappearance and mystique have been seen in scientific literature coming from the field of Physics. "The Cheshire Cat" is a phenomenon in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> in which a particle and its property behave as if they are separated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> or when a particle separates from one of its physical properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> To prove that this is not just a theory, researchers used an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interferometer" title="Interferometer">interferometer</a> where neutron beams passed through silicon crystal. The crystal physically separated the neutrons and allowed them to go to two paths. Researchers reported "the system behaves as if the neutrons go through one beam path, while their magnetic moment travels along the other."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Similar to the term "quantum Cheshire Cat", in quantum mechanics, scientists have coined the term <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger%27s_cat" title="Schrödinger's cat">Schrödinger's cat</a>. In this quantum phenomenon, a cat is both alive and dead inside a box. This is known as a superposition, and again points to the separation between the Cheshire Cat and his grin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
