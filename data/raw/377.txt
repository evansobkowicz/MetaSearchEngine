<p><b>Alice</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_character" title="Fictional character">fictional character</a>, the child <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s children's novel <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a></i> (1865) and its sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass</a></i> (1871). The character has her origins in stories told by Carroll to the young Liddell sisters, Edith, <a href="/wiki/Alice_Liddell" title="Alice Liddell">Alice</a> and Lorina, while rowing on <a href="/wiki/The_Isis" title="The Isis">the Isis</a> with his friend <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Duckworth" title="Robinson Duckworth">Robinson Duckworth</a>, and on subsequent rowing trips. Carroll wrote down the stories as <i>Alice's Adventures Under Ground</i>, which were later expanded into <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i>. While Alice takes her name from Alice Liddell's <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, it is controversial among scholars as to whether or not Alice can be identified as Alice Liddell. Often assumed by scholars to be seven years old in the first novel, Alice is seven and a half in the sequel. In a retrospective article, Carroll characterized her as "loving and gentle," "courteous to all," "trustful," and "wildly curious".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annotated_Alice_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Annotated_Alice-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_cartoon" title="Political cartoon">Political cartoonist</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Tenniel" title="John Tenniel">John Tenniel</a> illustrated <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> and <i>Through the Looking-Glass</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AAUG_p06.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1189" data-file-width="1068" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/AAUG_p06.png/220px-AAUG_p06.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/AAUG_p06.png/330px-AAUG_p06.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/AAUG_p06.png/440px-AAUG_p06.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AAUG_p06.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of Carroll's drawings of Alice from <i>Alice's Adventures Under Ground</i> (1864)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Alice debuted in <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s first draft of <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a></i>, <i>Alice's Adventures Under Ground</i> (1864).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199810_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199810-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Carroll, <i>Under Ground</i> originated from stories told to the Liddell sisters during an afternoon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-_4-0"><a href="#cite_note--4"><span>[</span>nb 1<span>]</span></a></sup> on 4 July 1862<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199810_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199810-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Rowing" title="Rowing">rowing</a> on <a href="/wiki/The_Isis" title="The Isis">the Isis</a> with his friend <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Duckworth" title="Robinson Duckworth">Robinson Duckworth</a>, and on subsequent rowing trips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAUG_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAUG-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> At the request of ten-year-old <a href="/wiki/Alice_Liddell" title="Alice Liddell">Alice Liddell</a>, Carroll wrote down the stories as <i>Alice's Adventures Under Ground</i>, which he completed in February 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAUG_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-AAUG-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Under Ground</i> contains thirty-seven illustrations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAUG_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-AAUG-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and as his drawings of Alice bears little physical resemblance to Alice Liddell, whose <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> she shares, biographer Anne Clark has suggested that Alice's younger sister, Edith, might have been Carroll's model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClark1979131_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClark1979131-6"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Carroll portrays his protagonist as wearing a tunic, in contrast to the "fitted children's dresses with soft trim" that the Liddell sisters might have worn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199874_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199874-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Jeffery Stern, Carroll's illustrations drew influence from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Raphaelite" title="Pre-Raphaelite">Pre-Raphaelite</a> painters <a href="/wiki/Dante_Gabriel_Rossetti" title="Dante Gabriel Rossetti">Dante Gabriel Rossetti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Hughes_(artist)" title="Arthur Hughes (artist)">Arthur Hughes</a>, whose painting <i>Girl with the Liliacs</i> (1863) he possessed and visually alluded to in one drawing in <i>Under Ground</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Carroll gave the hand-written <i>Alice's Adventures Under Ground</i> to Alice Liddell in November 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010168_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010168-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Tenniel" title="John Tenniel">John Tenniel</a> (1820-1914) illustrated <i>Alice Adventures in Wonderland</i> (1865) for a fee of <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>138, which was roughly a fourth of what Carroll earned each year and which he paid for himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010269_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010269-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Tenniel, who was forty-four years old at the time, was an already successful, well-known lead illustrator for the satirical magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone1998251_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone1998251-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> when Carroll employed him as an illustrator in April 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010169_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010169-12"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In contrast, "Carroll was a total unknown".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010169_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010169-12"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Tenniel took a drastic departure from Carroll's original illustrations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJanesGladstone199874_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJanesGladstone199874-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> although Carroll carefully oversaw his work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010169-70_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010169-70-14"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Among Carroll's suggestions was that Alice should have long, light-colored hair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010169-70_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010169-70-14"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Tenniel's depiction of Alice has its origins in a physically similar character which appeared in at least eight cartoons in <i>Punch</i>, during a four-year period that began in 1860.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In an 1860 cartoon, this character wore "the full skirt, pale stockings, flat shoes, and a hairband over her loose hair."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The character served as "one of Tenniel's prototypical representations of a nice middle-class girl," according to scholar Will Brooker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004112_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004112-16"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Alice's clothes are typical of what a girl belonging to <a href="/wiki/Social_structure_of_the_United_Kingdom#Middle_class" title="Social structure of the United Kingdom">the middle class</a> in the mid-Victorian era might have worn at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nursery_%22Alice%22" title='The Nursery "Alice"'>The Nursery "Alice"</a></i> (1890), (Carroll's adaptation of <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> for very young readers) Alice is blonde and her dress is yellow, with blue stockings; <i>The Nursery "Alice"</i> marked the first time that Tenniel's illustrations had been colored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Tenniel's fee for illustrating the sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass</a></i> (1871) rose to £290, which Carroll again paid for out of his own pocket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010269_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010269-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Tenniel changed Alice's clothing slightly in the sequel, where she wears horizontal-striped stockings instead of plain ones and has a more ornate <a href="/wiki/Pinafore" title="Pinafore">pinafore</a> with a bow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The headband that she wears in Tenniel's illustrations is called an "<a href="/wiki/Alice_band" title="Alice band">Alice band</a>", after the character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Her clothing as a queen and in the railway carriage is a " 'polonaise' dress" with a <a href="/wiki/Bustle" title="Bustle">bustle</a>, which would have been fashionable at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone199875-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The clothing worn by the characters in "My First Sermon" (1863) by pre-Raphaelite painter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Millais" title="John Millais">John Millais</a> and "The Travelling Companions" (1862) by Victorian painter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Leopold_Egg" title="Augustus Leopold Egg">Augustus Leopold Egg</a> have some elements in common with Alice's clothing in the railway carriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004113_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004113-19"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Carroll felt that some of Tenniel's illustrations of Alice lacked proper proportions, with her "head decidedly too large and feet decidedly too small", as a result of Tenniel's refusal to use a model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClark1979135_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClark1979135-20"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Michael Hancher, the evidence is lacking for the hypothesis that either Mary Hilton Badcock or Kate Lemon served as the visual model for Tenniel's Alice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler2014149.E2.80.9350_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler2014149.E2.80.9350-21"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characterization">Characterization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characterization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scholars debate whether or not that the character of Alice can be identified as Alice Liddell. Some biographies, such as <a href="/wiki/Morton_N._Cohen" title="Morton N. Cohen">Morton N. Cohen</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll:_A_Biography" title="Lewis Carroll: A Biography">Lewis Carroll: A Biography</a></i> (1995), identify the character as Liddell, writing that Liddell was Carroll's muse whom he had unrequited love for.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeach1999161-3_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeach1999161-3-22"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liddell_and_Carroll_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liddell_and_Carroll-24"><span>[</span>nb 2<span>]</span></a></sup> Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Karoline_Leach" title="Karoline Leach">Karoline Leach</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Shadow_of_the_Dreamchild" title="In the Shadow of the Dreamchild">In the Shadow of the Dreamchild</a></i> (1999), suggest that Carroll considered his protagonist and Liddell to be separate entities and that he was never in love with Liddell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeach1999163-174_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeach1999163-174-25"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010171-5_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010171-5-26"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Carroll, his character was not based on any real child, but was entirely fictional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010175_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2010175-27"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>Alice' Adventures in Wonderland</i>, which takes place on 4 May (the birthday of Liddell)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone19987_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone19987-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> the character has been suggested to be seven years old by several scholars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone19987_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone19987-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClark1979118_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClark1979118-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> although Carroll does not give the year which the story takes place;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone19987_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone19987-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Alice gives her age as seven and a half in the sequel, which takes place on 4 November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone19987_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone19987-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> In the text of the two <i>Alice</i> books, Carroll often did not remark on the physical appearance of his protagonist, offering only "very few details".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004106_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004106-30"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Details of her fictional life can be discovered from the text of the <i>Alice</i> books, such as that her governess teaches her lessons starting at nine in the morning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Triple_Alice_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Triple_Alice-31"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Richard Kelly writes that Alice's "educated speech, dress, and surroundings, all testify to her upper-class character".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKelly201111_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKelly201111-32"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When writing on her personality in "Alice on the Stage" (April 1887), Carroll described her as "loving and gentle," "courteous to <i>all</i>," "trustful," and "wildly curious, and with the eager enjoyment of Life that comes only in the happy hours of childhood, when all is new and fair, and when Sin and Sorrow are but names — empty words signifying nothing!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annotated_Alice_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Annotated_Alice-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Commentators characterize her as "innocent",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-English_Journal_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-English_Journal-33"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> "imaginative,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Triple_Alice_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Triple_Alice-31"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> introspective,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Triple_Alice_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Triple_Alice-31"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> and "witty and intelligent".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-English_Journal_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-English_Journal-33"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Others see less positive traits in Alice, writing that she frequently shows "casual cruelty" in her conversations with the animals in Wonderland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> takes violent action against the character <a href="/wiki/Bill_the_Lizard" title="Bill the Lizard">Bill the Lizard</a> by kicking him into the air,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen1995137_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen1995137-35"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> and reflects her social upbringing in her lack of sensitivity and her "rude" replies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen1995137_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen1995137-35"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearances">Appearances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appearances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking_Glass" title="Through the Looking Glass">Through the Looking Glass</a></div>
<p>Set in summer, <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> opens with Alice on the riverbank with her elder sister. She falls down a rabbit hole into Wonderland after following the <a href="/wiki/White_Rabbit" title="White Rabbit">White Rabbit</a>. Meeting many characters and changing her size several times, she eventually finds herself at the trial of the <a href="/wiki/Knave_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Knave of Hearts (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Knave of Hearts</a> as a witness. Ordered to leave by the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="King of Hearts (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">King</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Queen of Hearts (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Queen of Hearts</a>, Alice argues with them, eventually claiming that they are just a pack of cards. As they swarm her, Alice awakens on the riverbank and realizes that it was just a dream.</p>
<p>Set during autumn, <i>Through the Looking Glass</i> begins with Alice indoors as she plays with the kittens of her pet cat, Dinah. Through a mirror, she travels to Looking-Glass Land, an alternate world where chess pieces are alive. Encountering various characters, she eventually becomes a queen, and attends a party celebrating her coronation, although it quickly grows chaotic. Grabbing and shaking the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Queen_(Through_the_Looking_Glass)" title="Red Queen (Through the Looking Glass)">Red Queen</a>, Alice awakens back indoors, holding one of the kittens. The novel concludes with Alice's speculation on whether or not it was her dream or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_King_(Through_the_Looking_Glass)" title="Red King (Through the Looking Glass)">Red King</a>'s.</p>

<p>The expiration of the copyright of <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> in 1907 resulted in eight new printings, among them one illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rackham" title="Arthur Rackham">Arthur Rackham</a> (1867-1939).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rackham_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rackham-36"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Modelling Alice after young Doris Dormett, Rackham used an <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">art-nouveau</a> style, which sparked controversy among reviewers as to whether they were a better or worse interpretation of Carroll's work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rackham_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rackham-36"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Other notable illustrators include <a href="/wiki/Peter_Newell" title="Peter Newell">Peter Newell</a> (1901), who used <a href="/wiki/Monochrome" title="Monochrome">monochrome</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mabel_Lucie_Atwell" title="Mabel Lucie Atwell">Mabel Lucie Atwell</a> (1910); <a href="/wiki/Harry_Furniss" title="Harry Furniss">Harry Furniss</a> (1926); and <a href="/wiki/Willy_Pogany" title="Willy Pogany">Willy Pogany</a> (1929), who featured an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_deco" title="Art deco">art deco</a> style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004107_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004107-37"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Notable illustrators from the 1930s onwards include Edgar Thurstan (1931), and his visual allusions to the <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash_of_1929" title="Wall Street Crash of 1929">Wall Street Crash of 1929</a>; D.R. Sexton (1933) and J. Morton Sale (1933), both of whom featured an older Alice; <a href="/wiki/Mervyn_Peake" title="Mervyn Peake">Mervyn Peake</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Steadman" title="Ralph Steadman">Ralph Steadman</a> (1967), for which he received the Francis Williams Memorial award in 1972; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvador_Dali" title="Salvador Dali">Salvador Dali</a> (1969), who used <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealism</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker200478-9_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker200478-9-38"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blake_(artist)" title="Peter Blake (artist)">Peter Blake</a> with his watercolors (1970).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004108-110_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004108-110-39"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1972, there were ninety illustrators of <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> and twenty-one of <i>Through the Looking-Glass</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone1998126-130_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone1998126-130-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Among the notable illustrators of Alice in the 1980s, 1990s, and early 2000s are <a href="/wiki/Barry_Moser" title="Barry Moser">Barry Moser</a> (1982); <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greg_Hildebrandt" title="Greg Hildebrandt">Greg Hildebrandt</a> (1990); David Frankland (1996); <a href="/wiki/Lisbeth_Zwerger" title="Lisbeth Zwerger">Lisbeth Zwerger</a> (1999), who used watercolors in her adaptation; <a href="/wiki/Helen_Oxenbury" title="Helen Oxenbury">Helen Oxenbury</a> (1999), who won two awards, the <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Maschler_Award" title="Kurt Maschler Award">Kurt Maschler Award</a> in 1999 and the <a href="/wiki/Kate_Greenaway_Medal" title="Kate Greenaway Medal">Kate Greenaway Medal</a> in 2000, for her work; and DeLoss McGraw (2001), who used an "abstract style".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004111_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004111-41"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><br style="clear:both;"/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portrayals_of_Alice_in_Wonderland" title="Portrayals of Alice in Wonderland">Portrayals of Alice in Wonderland</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage">Stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first public stage adaptation of <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i>, <i>Alice in Wonderland</i>, debuted at the Royal Polytechnic Institute in London, England, on 17 April 1876, and continued until 19 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELovett1990107_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELovett1990107-42"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(musical)" title="Alice in Wonderland (musical)">Alice in Wonderland, a Dream Play for Children, in two acts</a></i>, an adaptation of <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> and <i>Through the Looking Glass</i>, appeared on 23 December 1886 at the <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales_Theatre" title="Prince of Wales Theatre">Prince of Wales Theatre</a> in London, and continued until 18 March 1887, to good reviews;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone1998242-3_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesGladstone1998242-3-43"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELovett1990107_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELovett1990107-42"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> it starred Phoebe Carlo as Alice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELovett1990107_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELovett1990107-42"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> The musical was later <a href="/wiki/Revival_(theatre)" title="Revival (theatre)">revived</a> and performed at the <a href="/wiki/Globe_Theatre" title="Globe Theatre">Globe Theatre</a> from 26 December 1888 to 9 February 1889, with Carroll's friend, <a href="/wiki/Isa_Bowman" title="Isa Bowman">Isa Bowman</a>, as Alice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELovett199097.2C_107_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELovett199097.2C_107-44"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The musical was frequently revived during the "<a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> season," being produced eighteen times from 1898 to 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELovett199099_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELovett199099-45"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> has since been adaptated for various forms of the stage, including "ballets, operas, experimental theatre, Broadway musicals, puppet plays, mime acts, and rock musicals."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELovett1990106_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELovett1990106-46"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Directed and produced by <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Hepworth" title="Cecil Hepworth">Cecil Hepworth</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(1903_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (1903 film)">first as Alice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1st_film_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-1st_film-49"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Two more silent film adaptations of <i>Alice's Adventures in teenager as opposed to a child (<a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(2010_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (2010 film)">2010</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph100215_52-0"><a adaptations include <a href="/wiki/Janet_Waldo" title="Janet Waldo">Janet Waldo</a> (<a href="/wiki/The_New_Alice_in_Wonderland_(film)" title="The New Alice in Wonderland (film)">1966</a>), <a href="/wiki/Tina_Majorino" title="Tina Majorino">Tina Majorino</a> (<a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(1999_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (1999 film)">1999</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alice_films_50-3"><a href="#cite_note-Alice_films-50"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Alice has appeared in television series, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Once_Upon_a_Time_in_Wonderland" title="Once Upon a Time in Wonderland">Once Upon a Time in Wonderland</a></i> (2013–14), as portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Lowe" title="Sophie Lowe">Sophie Lowe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alice has also appeared in video games. In <i><a href="/wiki/American_McGee%27s_Alice" title="American McGee's Alice">American McGee's Alice</a></i> (2000), Alice appears as the protagonist and <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">player character</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Psychological_trauma" title="Psychological trauma">psychologically traumatized</a> young woman who returns to a corrupted Wonderland after being sent to an insane asylum,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> while in its sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Alice:_Madness_Returns" title="Alice: Madness Returns">Alice: Madness Returns</a></i> (2011), Alice, still traumatized by the loss of her family, seeks the truth behind their deaths in Wonderland and London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Walt Disney version of Alice is present in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hearts_(video_game)" title="Kingdom Hearts (video game)">first installment</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hearts" title="Kingdom Hearts">Kingdom Hearts</a></i> series, as a <a href="/wiki/Non-player_character" title="Non-player character">non-player character</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> In the 2003 video game, she is the protagonist of <i><a href="/wiki/Alice_in_the_Country_of_Hearts" title="Alice in the Country of Hearts">Alice in the Country of Hearts</a></i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reimagining" title="Reimagining">reimagining</a> of <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Shin_Megami_Tensei" title="Shin Megami Tensei">Shin Megami Tensei</a></i> series of games, Alice is one of the most iconic demons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> She appears as a young blonde girl with incredible dark magic powers, with strong allusions to the Alice in <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i>. She is most famous for the line, which is said directly to the player: "Will you die for me, please?".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The character of Alice has appeared in other literature, besides Carroll's novels. The premise of the <a href="/wiki/Young-adult_fiction" title="Young-adult fiction">young-adult fiction</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Looking_Glass_Wars" title="The Looking Glass Wars">The Looking Glass Wars</a></i> series is that its warrior-princess protagonist and rightful heir to the throne of Wonderland, Alyss Heart, fled to Victorian England from Wonderland, where she met Lewis Carroll and told him her story, which he turn into a nonsensical fairy tale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Alan_Moore" title="Alan Moore">Alan Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melinda_Gebbie" title="Melinda Gebbie">Melinda Gebbie</a>'s pornographic graphic novel <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_Girls" title="Lost Girls">Lost Girls</a></i> (1991), she appears as Alice Fairchild, a central character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004156-8_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004156-8-60"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">Manga</a> (Japanese comics) adaptions or reimaginings of the <i>Alice</i> books include <i><a href="/wiki/Miyuki-chan_in_Wonderland" title="Miyuki-chan in Wonderland">Miyuki-chan in Wonderland</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i>Key Princess Story: Eternal Alice Rondo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_impact">Cultural impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:37%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"In all her different and associated forms—underground and through the looking glass, textual and visual, drawn and photographed, as Carroll's brunette or Tenniel's blonde or Disney's prim miss, as the real Alice Liddell [...] in novel, poem, satire, play, film, cartoon, newspaper, magazine, album cover or song—Alice is the ultimate cultural icon, available for any and every form of manipulation, and as ubiquitous today as in the era of her first appearance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Men_in_Wonderland_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Men_in_Wonderland-63"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;">—Catherine Robson, <i>Men in Wonderland</i></div>
</div>
<p>Alice has been described as a <a href="/wiki/Cultural_icon" title="Cultural icon">cultural icon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler2014xxi_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler2014xxi-64"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004xiv_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2004xiv-65"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Men_in_Wonderland_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Men_in_Wonderland-63"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> and <i>Through the Looking-Glass</i> were critically and commercially successful in Carroll's lifetime;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen1995133-34_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen1995133-34-66"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> more than 150,000 copies of <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> and 100,000 copies of <i>Through the Looking-Glass</i> had been printed by 1898.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen1995134_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen1995134-67"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> Since then, the <i>Alice</i> books have continued to remained in print and "have been translated into more than seventy languages".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen1995134-5_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen1995134-5-68"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> As Sigler writes, "Carroll's independent, unconventionally inquisitive, and often bad-tempered protagonist has become iconic, as have John Tenniel's images of her, and a vast and profitable 'Alice industry' has continued to produce countless spinoffs and sequels, comic books and manga, cartoons and animé, stage and film productions, board and video games as well as a vast array of collectibles".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler2014xxi_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler2014xxi-64"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Alice's continuing appeal has been discussed by critics. Biographer <a href="/wiki/Morton_N._Cohen" title="Morton N. Cohen">Morton N. Cohen</a> suggested that an underlying subject of the <i>Alice</i> books is maturity; he writes that children can identify with Alice, and as she becomes more self-confident through her encounters with "a hostile, unpredictable environment," she gains "advancement, recognition, [and] acceptance" and the books ultimately reassure the child that he or she can mature into an adult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECohen1995135-40_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohen1995135-40-69"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> According to literary critic Valerie Krips, Alice is familiar and unchanging for adult readers, as well as a reminder of the bygone Victorian era and a lost childhood, during which they discovered the <i>Alice</i> books, which their parents enjoyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKrips20048_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKrips20048-70"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some critics have highlighted Alice's character as unusual or a departure from the typical mid-nineteenth-century child protagonists. According to Krips, "Alice is a seditious character when compared to the children who precede her in books written for children. She cries, displays temper, is forthright, finds things and people tiresome—and says so—yet she always remembers her manners and speaks tenderly of her cat, Dinah."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKrips20047_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKrips20047-71"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> Alison Lurie wrote that Alice, excluding her manners, defies mid-Victorian conventions of the ideal young girl: "she is not gentle, timid, and docile, but brave, active, and impatient; she is highly critical of her surroundings and the adults she meets."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parodies_and_inspired_works">Parodies and inspired works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Parodies and inspired works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Clara-in-blunderland-cover-1902.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2171" data-file-width="1479" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d5/Clara-in-blunderland-cover-1902.png/210px-Clara-in-blunderland-cover-1902.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d5/Clara-in-blunderland-cover-1902.png/315px-Clara-in-blunderland-cover-1902.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d5/Clara-in-blunderland-cover-1902.png/420px-Clara-in-blunderland-cover-1902.png 2x" width="210"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Clara-in-blunderland-cover-1902.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The cover of <i>Clara in Blunderland</i> (1902), a parody of <i>Alice in Wonderland</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the half century after the publication of <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i>, "almost two hundred literary imitations, revisions, and parodies" appeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler1997xii-xxi_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler1997xii-xxi-73"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> They featured one or more protagonists with characteristics similar to Alice's ("typically polite, articulate, and assertive"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler1997xvii_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler1997xvii-74"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> regardless of gender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler1997xvii_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler1997xvii-74"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> Notable revisions which explore the Victorian ideas on gender and childhood and feature protagonists "who demonstrate power over their fantasy adventures" include <i>Mopsa the Fairy</i> (1869) by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Ingelow" title="Jean Ingelow">Jean Ingelow</a>, <i>Speaking Likeness</i> (1874) by <a href="/wiki/Christina_Rossetti" title="Christina Rossetti">Christina Rossetti</a>, <i>Wanted—A King; or How Merle Set the Nursery Rhymes to Right</i> (1890) by <a href="/wiki/Maggie_Brown" title="Maggie Brown">Maggie Brown</a>, and <i>A New Alice in an Old Wonderland</i> (1895) by Anna M. Richards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler1997vii.2C_xviii-xix_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler1997vii.2C_xviii-xix-75"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> Imitations inspired by <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> and part of Carroll's collection of books similar to his <i>Alice</i> ones include <i>From Nowhere to the North Pole</i> (1874) by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hood" title="Tom Hood">Tom Hood</a> and <i>Mabel in Rhymeland; or, Little Mabel's Journey to Norwich, and Her Wonderful Adventures with the Man in the Moon and other Heroes and Heroines of Nursery Rhyme</i> (1885) by Edward Holland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooker200477_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker200477-76"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler1997389_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler1997389-77"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> Among the recreations that use <a href="/wiki/Sentimentality" title="Sentimentality">sentimentality</a> are <i><a href="/wiki/Davy_and_the_Goblin" title="Davy and the Goblin">Davy and the Goblin</a></i> (1885) by <a href="/wiki/Charles_E._Carryl" title="Charles E. Carryl">Charles E. Carryl</a> and <i>New Adventures of "Alice"</i> (1917) by John Rae,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler1997vii_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler1997vii-78"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> while the parodies, which were for adult readers, include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Westminster_Alice" title="The Westminster Alice">The Westminster Alice</a></i> (1900–02) by <a href="/wiki/Saki" title="Saki">Saki</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Clara_in_Blunderland" title="Clara in Blunderland">Clara in Blunderland</a></i> (1902) by Caroline Lewis, and <i>Alice in the Delighted States</i> (1928) by Edward Hope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler1997vii_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler1997vii-78"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After the early 1930s, when the <i>Alice</i> books were considered to be serious, literary works for adults by <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernists</a>, fewer parodies and inspired works appeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler1997xiii.2C_xvi_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler1997xiii.2C_xvi-79"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> According to scholar Carolyn Sigler, subsequent works inspired by the <i>Alice</i> books tended to be for adult readers, which "refer[...] minimally or obliquely to the <i>Alice</i> books"; examples include <i>Dreamhouse</i> (1995) by Alison Habens, <i>Alice in Thunderland</i> (1993) by <a href="/wiki/Maeve_Kelly" title="Maeve Kelly">Maeve Kelly</a>, and <i>Alice in Bed</i> (1993) by <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sontag" title="Susan Sontag">Susan Sontag</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESigler1997xvi-xvii_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESigler1997xvi-xvii-80"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
