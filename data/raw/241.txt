<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, an <b>avatar</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ə/ 'u' in 'syrup'">ə</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ɑr/ 'ar' in 'bard'">ɑr</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English">,</a></span> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ə/ 'u' in 'syrup'">ə</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted" title="/ɑr/ 'ar' in 'bard'">ɑr</span></a></span><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <small>Hindustani: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Hindi_and_Urdu" title="Help:IPA for Hindi and Urdu">[əʋˈt̪aːr]</a></span> from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%85%E0%A4%B5%E0%A4%A4%E0%A4%BE%E0%A4%B0#Sanskrit" title="wikt:अवतार">अवतार</a> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">avatāra</span></i> "descent") is a deliberate descent of a <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> to Earth, or a descent of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Being" title="Supreme Being">Supreme Being</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a> for <a href="/wiki/Vaishnavism" title="Vaishnavism">Vaishnavites</a>), and is mostly translated into English as "<a href="/wiki/Incarnation" title="Incarnation">incarnation</a>", but more accurately as "appearance" or "manifestation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matchett_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matchett-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The phenomenon of an avatar is observed in <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ayyavazhi" title="Ayyavazhi">Ayyavazhi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a>. Avatar is regarded as one of the core principles of Hinduism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The term is most often associated with <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a>, though it has also come to be associated with other deities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gale.27s_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gale.27s-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Varying lists of avatars of Vishnu appear in Hindu scriptures, including the ten <a href="/wiki/Dashavatara" title="Dashavatara">Dashavatara</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Garuda_Purana" title="Garuda Purana">Garuda Purana</a></i> and the twenty-two avatars in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bhagavata_Purana" title="Bhagavata Purana">Bhagavata Purana</a></i>, though the latter adds that the incarnations of Vishnu are innumerable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryant_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bryant-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The avatars of Vishnu are a primary component of <a href="/wiki/Vaishnavism" title="Vaishnavism">Vaishnavism</a>. An early reference to avatar, and to avatar <i>doctrine</i>, is in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bhagavad_Gita" title="Bhagavad Gita">Bhagavad Gita</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheth_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheth-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ganesha" title="Ganesha">Ganesha</a> are also described as descending in the form of avatars. The various manifestations of <a href="/wiki/Devi" title="Devi">Devi</a>, the Divine Mother principal in Hinduism, are also described as avatars or incarnations by some scholars and followers of <a href="/wiki/Shaktism" title="Shaktism">Shaktism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheth_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sheth-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hawley_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hawley-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The avatars of Vishnu carry a greater theological prominence than those of other deities, which some scholars perceive to be imitative of the Vishnu avatar lists.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a>, an avatar is a deliberate descent of a soul to earth in any form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Page_792.2C_Line_7.2C_Bhagat_Kabir_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Page_792.2C_Line_7.2C_Bhagat_Kabir-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Guru_Granth_Sahib" title="Guru Granth Sahib">Guru Granth Sahib</a> believes in the existence of the Dashavatara. In <i><a href="/wiki/Dasam_Granth" title="Dasam Granth">Dasam Granth</a></i>, Guru Gobind Singh wrote three composition on historical avatars which include Vishnu Avatar, Brahma Avatar, and Rudra Avatar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dasam_Granth_Sahib_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dasam_Granth_Sahib-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_meaning">Etymology and meaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and meaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sanskrit noun <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">avatāra</span></i> is derived from the verbal root <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration"><i>tṝ</i></span> "to cross over", joined with the prefix <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">ava</span></i> "off, away, down". The word does not occur in the Vedas, but is recorded in <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration"><a href="/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini" title="Pāṇini">Pāṇini</a></span> (3.3.120). <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Avatāra</span></i> was initially used to describe different deities, then around the 6th century AD it began to be used primarily to describe the manifestations of <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> While earlier texts mention deities taking on different forms, the <i><a href="/wiki/Bhagavad_Gita" title="Bhagavad Gita">Bhagavad Gita</a></i> (4.5-9) is the first text to discuss the doctrine associated with the term even though the word <i>avatāra</i> itself is not mentioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to some scholars like <a href="/wiki/Mercy_Oduyoye" title="Mercy Oduyoye">Mercy Oduyoye</a>, H. M. Vroom, and Noel Sheth, the common translation "<a href="/wiki/Incarnation" title="Incarnation">incarnation</a>" is somewhat misleading as the concept of an avatar corresponds more closely to the view of <a href="/wiki/Docetism" title="Docetism">Docetism</a> in <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">Christian theology</a>, as different from the idea of God 'in the flesh' in mainstream <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Avatars_of_Vishnu">Avatars of Vishnu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Avatars of Vishnu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Matsya_painting.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="977" data-file-width="700" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Matsya_painting.jpg/220px-Matsya_painting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Matsya_painting.jpg/330px-Matsya_painting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Matsya_painting.jpg/440px-Matsya_painting.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Matsya_painting.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Matsya" title="Matsya">Matsya</a>, fish avatar of Vishnu</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The concept of avatar within Hinduism is most often associated with <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a>, the preserver or sustainer aspect of God within the Hindu Trinity or <a href="/wiki/Trimurti" title="Trimurti">Trimurti</a> or the one and only supreme God for followers of <a href="/wiki/Vaishnavism" title="Vaishnavism">Vaishnavism</a>.</p>
<p>Vishnu's avatars typically descend for a very specific purpose. An oft-quoted passage from the <a href="/wiki/Bhagavad_Gita" title="Bhagavad Gita">Bhagavad Gita</a> describes the typical role of an avatar of Vishnu—as bringing <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">dharma</a>, or righteousness, back to the social and cosmic order:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matchett_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matchett-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gale.27s_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gale.27s-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Whenever righteousness wanes and unrighteousness increases I send myself forth.<br/>
<p>For the protection of the good and for the destruction of evil,<br/>
and for the establishment of righteousness,<br/>
I come into being age after age. (Gita:4.7–8)</p>
</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
</blockquote>
<p>The descents of Vishnu are also integral to His teaching and tradition, whereas the accounts of other deities are not so strictly dependent on their avatar stories. Although it is usual to speak of Vishnu as the source of the avatars, within the <a href="/wiki/Vaishnavism" title="Vaishnavism">Vaishnavism</a> branch of Hinduism <a href="/wiki/Narayana" title="Narayana">Narayana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vasudeva" title="Vasudeva">Vasudeva</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a> are also seen as names denoting divine aspects which descend as avatars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matchett_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Matchett-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bhagavata_Purana" title="Bhagavata Purana">Bhagavata Purana</a> describes Vishnu's avatars as innumerable, though there are ten incarnations (<i><a href="/wiki/Dashavatara" title="Dashavatara">Dashavatara</a></i>), that are widely seen as his major appearances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matchett_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Matchett-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryant_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bryant-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Krishna and <a href="/wiki/Rama" title="Rama">Rama</a> are the two mostly widely known and worshiped avatars of Vishnu, with their stories told in the two major Sanskrit epics, the <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-King-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Different lists of Vishnu's avatars appear in different texts, including: the <i>dasavatara</i> from the <a href="/wiki/Garuda_Purana" title="Garuda Purana">Garuda Purana</a>; lists of twenty-two, twenty-three, and sixteen avatars in the <a href="/wiki/Bhagavata_Purana" title="Bhagavata Purana">Bhagavata Purana</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mishra_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mishra-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> thirty-nine avatars in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pa%C3%B1caratra" title="Pañcaratra">Pañcaratra</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> the <i>dasavatara</i> again in <a href="/wiki/Agni_Purana" title="Agni Purana">Agni Purana</a>; the first eight of the <i>dasavatara</i> in <a href="/wiki/Padma_Purana" title="Padma Purana">Padma Purana</a>. The commonly accepted number of ten was fixed well before the 10th century CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mishra_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mishra-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, various Vaishnava saints and founders are considered to be partial avatars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The various avatars categorized in many different ways. For example: <i>Purusavatara</i> is the first avatara; <i>Gunavataras</i> are represented by the <a href="/wiki/Trimurti" title="Trimurti">Trimurti</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">Brahma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a>) who each preside over one of the <a href="/wiki/Gu%E1%B9%87a" title="Guṇa">Guṇas</a> (rajas, sattva, and tamas); <i>Lilavataras</i> are the well-known ones, and include <i>Avesavataras</i> (beings into whom part of God Himself has entered) and <i>saktyamsavesa</i> (into whom only parts of His power enter); <i>Kalpa-</i>, <i>Manvantara-</i>, and <i>Yuga-avataras</i> descend during different cosmic ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Vaishnavism" title="Vaishnavism">Vaishnavism</a> schools consider Krishna to be the source of all avatars (<a href="/wiki/Krishnaism" title="Krishnaism">Krishnaism</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dashavatara">Dashavatara</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dashavatara">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dashavatara" title="Dashavatara">Dashavatara</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Varaha_avtar,_killing_a_demon_to_protect_Bhu,_c1740.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="394" height="296" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Varaha_avtar%2C_killing_a_demon_to_protect_Bhu%2C_c1740.jpg/220px-Varaha_avtar%2C_killing_a_demon_to_protect_Bhu%2C_c1740.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Varaha_avtar%2C_killing_a_demon_to_protect_Bhu%2C_c1740.jpg/330px-Varaha_avtar%2C_killing_a_demon_to_protect_Bhu%2C_c1740.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cd/Varaha_avtar%2C_killing_a_demon_to_protect_Bhu%2C_c1740.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Varaha_avtar,_killing_a_demon_to_protect_Bhu,_c1740.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Varaha" title="Varaha">Varaha</a>, the boar avatar of Vishnu (18th-century painting, probably of <a href="/wiki/Pahari_people" title="Pahari people">Pahari</a> provenance)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ten best known avatars of Vishnu are collectively known as the <i>Dasavatara</i> (a <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit_compound" title="Sanskrit compound">Sanskrit compound</a> meaning "ten avatars"). This list is included in the <a href="/wiki/Garuda_Purana" title="Garuda Purana">Garuda Purana</a> (1.86.10"11).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The first four are said to have appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Satya_Yuga" title="Satya Yuga">Satya Yuga</a> (the first of the four <a href="/wiki/Yuga" title="Yuga">Yugas</a> or ages in the time cycle described within Hinduism). The next three avatars appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Treta_Yuga" title="Treta Yuga">Treta Yuga</a>, the eighth descent in the <a href="/wiki/Dvapara_Yuga" title="Dvapara Yuga">Dvapara Yuga</a> and the ninth in the <a href="/wiki/Kali_Yuga" title="Kali Yuga">Kali Yuga</a>. The tenth, Kalki, is predicted to appear at the end of the Kali Yuga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matsya" title="Matsya">Matsya</a>, the fish-avatar who saved <a href="/wiki/Sraddhadeva_Manu" title="Sraddhadeva Manu">Manu</a> – the progenitor of mankind from the great deluge and rescued the Vedic scriptures by killing a demon. Story can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Matsya_Purana" title="Matsya Purana">Matsya Purana</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kurma" title="Kurma">Kurma</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a>-avatar, who helped in the <a href="/wiki/Samudra_manthan" title="Samudra manthan">Samudra manthan</a> – the churning of the ocean. Story can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Kurma_Purana" title="Kurma Purana">Kurma Purana</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Varaha" title="Varaha">Varaha</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boar" title="Boar">boar</a>-avatar, who rescued the earth from the ocean, by killing her kidnapper-demon <a href="/wiki/Hiranyaksha" title="Hiranyaksha">Hiranyaksha</a>. Story can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Varaha_Purana" title="Varaha Purana">Varaha Purana</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Narasimha" title="Narasimha">Narasimha</a>, the half man-half <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a> avatar, who killed the tyrant demon-king <a href="/wiki/Hiranyakashipu" title="Hiranyakashipu">Hiranyakashipu</a>, to rescue the demon's son <a href="/wiki/Prahlada" title="Prahlada">Prahlada</a>, who was a Vishnu-devotee</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vamana" title="Vamana">Vamana</a>, the dwarf-avatar, who subdued the king <a href="/wiki/Mahabali" title="Mahabali">Maha Bali</a>. Story can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Vamana_Purana" title="Vamana Purana">Vamana Purana</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Parashurama" title="Parashurama">Parashurama</a>, sage with the axe who killed the thousand-armed king <a href="/wiki/Kartavirya_Arjuna" title="Kartavirya Arjuna">Kartavirya Arjuna</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rama" title="Rama">Rama</a>, the king of <a href="/wiki/Ayodhya" title="Ayodhya">Ayodhya</a> and the hero of the Hindu epic <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a>, the king of <a href="/wiki/Dwarka" title="Dwarka">Dwarka</a>, a central character in the <a href="/wiki/Bhagavata_Purana" title="Bhagavata Purana">Bhagavata Purana</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a> and reciter of <a href="/wiki/Bhagavad_Gita" title="Bhagavad Gita">Bhagavad Gita</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha_in_Hinduism" title="Gautama Buddha in Hinduism">Buddha</a>, the sage</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kalki" title="Kalki">Kalki</a> ("Eternity", or "time", or "The Destroyer of foulness"), who is expected to appear at the end of Kali Yuga. Story can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Kalki_Purana" title="Kalki Purana">Kalki Purana</a>.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Note: Some versions include <a href="/wiki/Balarama" title="Balarama">Balarama</a> (the elder brother of Krishna) as the eighth avatar, with Krishna listed as the ninth instead of Buddha, while others replace Buddha with Balarama as the ninth avatar.Jayadeva in his Git Govinda instead adds both Balarama and Buddha,but omits Krishna as he is taken as the equivalent of Vishnu,the origin of all avatars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Bhagavata_Purana">In the Bhagavata Purana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In the Bhagavata Purana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mohini_in_Belur_temple.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="992" data-file-width="430" height="508" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Mohini_in_Belur_temple.jpg/220px-Mohini_in_Belur_temple.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Mohini_in_Belur_temple.jpg/330px-Mohini_in_Belur_temple.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4f/Mohini_in_Belur_temple.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mohini_in_Belur_temple.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Mohini" title="Mohini">Mohini</a>, the only female avatar of Vishnu (statue in a <a href="/wiki/Belur" title="Belur">Belur</a> temple, Karnataka.)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As many as forty specific avatars of Vishnu are mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Bhagavata_Purana" title="Bhagavata Purana">Bhagavata Purana</a>, though the book adds that the number is innumerable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rukmani_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rukmani-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Twenty-two avatars of Vishnu are listed numerically in the first book:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Four_Kumaras" title="Four Kumaras">Four Kumaras</a> (Catursana) [BP 1.3.6] – the four Sons of god <a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">Brahma</a> and exemplified the path of devotion.</li>
<li>Varaha [BP 1.3.7]</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Narada" title="Narada">Narada</a> [BP 1.3.8] the divine-sage who travels the worlds as a devotee of Vishnu</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nara-Narayana" title="Nara-Narayana">Nara-Narayana</a> [BP 1.3.9] – the twin-sages</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kapila" title="Kapila">Kapila</a> [BP 1.3.10] – a renowned sage spoken of in the <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a>, son of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kardama_Muni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kardama Muni (page does not exist)">Kardama Muni</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Devahuti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Devahuti (page does not exist)">Devahuti</a> and sometimes identified with the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Samkhya" title="Samkhya">Samkhya</a> school of philosophy</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dattatreya" title="Dattatreya">Dattatreya</a> [BP 1.3.11] – the combined avatar of the Hindu <a href="/wiki/Trimurti" title="Trimurti">trinity</a> Brahma, Vishnu and Shiva. He was born to the sage <a href="/wiki/Atri" title="Atri">Atri</a> became a great seer himself.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yajna_(avatar)" title="Yajna (avatar)">Yajna</a> [BP 1.3.12] – the lord of fire-sacrifice, who took was the <a href="/wiki/Indra" title="Indra">Indra</a> – the lord of <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">heaven</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rishabha_(Hinduism)" title="Rishabha (Hinduism)">Rishabha</a> [BP 1.3.13] – the father of <a href="/wiki/Bharata_(emperor)" title="Bharata (emperor)">King Bharata</a> and Bahubali</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prithu" title="Prithu">Prithu</a> [BP 1.3.14] – the sovereign-king who milked the earth as a cow to get the world's grain and vegetation and also invented agriculture</li>
<li>Matsya [BP 1.3.15]</li>
<li>Kurma [BP 1.3.16]</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dhanvantari" title="Dhanvantari">Dhanvantari</a> [BP 1.3.17] – the father of <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">Ayurveda</a> medicine and a physician to the <a href="/wiki/Deva_(Hinduism)" title="Deva (Hinduism)">Devas</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mohini" title="Mohini">Mohini</a> [BP 1.3.17] – the enchantress</li>
<li>Narasimha [BP 1.3.18]</li>
<li>Vamana [BP 1.3.19]</li>
<li>Parashurama [BP 1.3.20]</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vyasa" title="Vyasa">Vyasa</a> [BP] 1.3.21] – the compiler of the scriptures – <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a> and writer of the scriptures <a href="/wiki/Puranas" title="Puranas">Puranas</a> and the epic <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></li>
<li>Rama [BP 1.3.22]</li>
<li>Krishna [BP 1.3.23]</li>
<li>Balarama [BP 1.3.23]</li>
<li>Buddha [BP 1.3.24]</li>
<li>Kalki [BP 1.3.25]</li>
</ol>
<p>Besides these, several others are mentioned like Sri Hayagriva, Hari, Hamsa, Prsnigarbha, Vibhu, Satyasena, Vaikuntha, Sarvabhauma, Visvaksena, Dharmasetu, Sudhama, Yogesvara, Brhadbhanu. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The Golden incarnation [BP 11.5.32]  the avatara in Kali-yuga for propagating hari-namasankirtan, which is sometimes interpreted as the saint <a href="/wiki/Chaitanya_Mahaprabhu" title="Chaitanya Mahaprabhu">Chaitanya Mahaprabhu</a> is also mentioned, though there is no explicit mention of a name in the verse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>There are many senses and shades of meaning of the term avatar within Hinduism.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Purusha_avatars">Purusha avatars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Purusha avatars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Purusha avatars are sometimes described as the original avatars of Vishnu within the Universe:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vasudeva" title="Vasudeva">Vasudeva</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shesha" title="Shesha">Sankarshan</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pradyumna" title="Pradyumna">Pradyumna</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aniruddha" title="Aniruddha">Aniruddha</a></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Guna_avatars">Guna avatars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Guna avatars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Trimurti" title="Trimurti">Trimurti</a></div>
<p>The personalities of the <a href="/wiki/Trimurti" title="Trimurti">Trimurti</a> (Hindu trinity) are also sometimes referred to as <i>Guna avatars</i>, because of their roles of controlling the three modes (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guna" title="Guna">gunas</a>) of nature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> even though they have not descended upon an earthly planet in the general sense of the term 'avatar'.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">Brahma</a> – As controller of the mode of goodness (<a href="/wiki/Sattva" title="Sattva">sattva</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a> – Controller of the mode of passion and desire (<a href="/wiki/Rajas" title="Rajas">rajas</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a> – Controller of the mode of ignorance (<a href="/wiki/Tamas_(philosophy)" title="Tamas (philosophy)">tamas</a>)</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Manvantara_avatars">Manvantara avatars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Manvantara avatars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Manvantara avatars are beings responsible for creating progeny throughout the Universe. There are said to be unlimited numbers of these avatars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (January 2010)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> "During the hundred years of Brahmā's life, there are <i>504,000 manvantara-avatāras</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Shaktyavesa_and_Avesa_avatars">Shaktyavesa and Avesa avatars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Shaktyavesa and Avesa avatars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Avataric incarnations are classified as two kinds</p>
<ul>
<li>direct (<i>sakshat</i>)</li>
<li>indirect (<i>avesa</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>When Vishnu himself descends, he is called <i>sakshat</i> or <i>shaktyavesa-avatara</i>, a direct incarnation of God. But when he does not incarnate directly, but indirectly empowers some living entity to represent him, that living entity is called an indirect or <i>avesa</i> avatar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (January 2010)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There are said to be a great number of <i>avesa</i> avatars. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Narada" title="Narada">Narada Muni</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha_as_an_Avatar_of_Vishnu" title="Buddha as an Avatar of Vishnu">Sugata Buddha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Parashurama" title="Parashurama">Parashurama</a>. Parashurama is the only one of the traditional ten avatars that is not a direct descent of Vishnu.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Sri_Vaishnavism" title="Sri Vaishnavism">Sri Vaishnavism</a> sect of Hinduism, there are two types of primary or direct avatars, Purna avatars and Amsarupavatars:</p>
<ol>
<li>Purna avatars are those in which Vishnu takes form directly and all the qualities and powers of God are expressed, (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Narasimha" title="Narasimha">Narasimha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rama" title="Rama">Rama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a>).,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (January 2010)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Amsarupavatars are those in which Vishnu takes form directly but He is manifest in the person only partially. (e.g. avatars from <a href="/wiki/Matsya" title="Matsya">Matsya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurma" title="Kurma">Kurma</a> etc.).</li>
</ol>
<p>The <i>avesa</i> or indirect avatars are generally not worshiped as the Supreme being. Only the direct, primary avatars are worshiped in this way. In practice, the direct avatars that are worshiped today are the Purna avatars of Narasimha, Rama and Krishna. Among most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaishnava" title="Vaishnava">Vaishnava</a> traditions, <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a> is considered to be the highest Purna avatar. However, followers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaitanya" title="Chaitanya">Chaitanya</a> (including <a href="/wiki/International_Society_for_Krishna_Consciousness" title="International Society for Krishna Consciousness">ISKCON</a>), <a href="/wiki/Nimbarka" title="Nimbarka">Nimbarka</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vallabha_Acharya" title="Vallabha Acharya">Vallabha Acharya</a> differ philosophically from other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaishnavas" title="Vaishnavas">Vaishnavas</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramanujacharya" title="Ramanujacharya">Ramanujacharya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madhvacharya" title="Madhvacharya">Madhvacharya</a>, and consider <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a> to be the ultimate Godhead, not simply an avatar. That said, all Hindus believe that there is no difference between worship of <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a> and His avatars as it all leads to Him. According to <a href="/wiki/Madhvacharya" title="Madhvacharya">Madhvacharya</a> (chief proponent of <a href="/wiki/Dvaita" title="Dvaita">Dvaita</a> or school of differential <a href="/wiki/Monism" title="Monism">monism</a>), all avatars of <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Vishnu</a> are alike in potency and every other quality. There is no gradation among them, and perceiving or claiming any differences among avatars is a cause of eternal damnation. See Madhva's commentary on <a href="/wiki/Katha_Upanishad" title="Katha Upanishad">Katha Upanishad</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Avatars_of_Ganesha">Avatars of Ganesha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Avatars of Ganesha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Linga_Purana" title="Linga Purana">Linga Purana</a> declares that Ganesha incarnates to destroy demons and to help the gods and pious people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimes_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grimes-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> The two <a href="/wiki/Upapurana" title="Upapurana">upapuranas</a> – <a href="/wiki/Ganesha_Purana" title="Ganesha Purana">Ganesha Purana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mudgala_Purana" title="Mudgala Purana">Mudgala Purana</a> – detail the avatars of Ganesha. Both these upapuranas are core scriptures of the <a href="/wiki/Ganapatya" title="Ganapatya">Ganapatya</a> sect – exclusively dedicated to Ganesha worship.</p>
<p>Four avatars of Ganesha are listed in the Ganesha Purana:Mohotkata, Mayūreśvara, Gajanana and Dhumraketu. Each avatar corresponds to a different yuga, has a different mount and different skin complexion, but all the avatars have a common purpose – to slay demons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Mudgala Purana describes eight avatars of Ganesha:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granoff_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Granoff-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Vakratunda (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Vakratuṇḍa</span>) ("twisting trunk"), his mount is a lion.</li>
<li>Ekadanta ("single tusk"), his mount is a mouse.</li>
<li>Mahodara ("big belly"), his mount is a mouse.</li>
<li>Gajavaktra (or Gajānana) ("elephant face"), his mount is a mouse.</li>
<li>Lambodara ("pendulous belly"), his mount is a mouse.</li>
<li>Vikata (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Vikaṭa</span>) ("unusual form", "misshapen"), his mount is a peacock.</li>
<li>Vighnaraja (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Vighnarāja</span>) ("king of obstacles"), his mount is the celestial serpent <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śeṣa</span>.</li>
<li>Dhumravarna (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Dhūmravarṇa</span>) ("grey color") corresponds to Śiva, his mount is a horse.</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Avatars_of_Shiva">Avatars of Shiva</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Avatars of Shiva">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sarabha_Narasinmha_Kangra.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="430" data-file-width="579" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sarabha_Narasinmha_Kangra.jpg/220px-Sarabha_Narasinmha_Kangra.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sarabha_Narasinmha_Kangra.jpg/330px-Sarabha_Narasinmha_Kangra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sarabha_Narasinmha_Kangra.jpg/440px-Sarabha_Narasinmha_Kangra.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sarabha_Narasinmha_Kangra.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sharabha (right) with Narasimha (18th-century painting, <a href="/wiki/Pahari_people" title="Pahari people">Pahari</a>/<a href="/wiki/Kangra_painting" title="Kangra painting">Kangra</a> School)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Puranas" title="Puranas">Puranic scriptures</a> contain occasional references to avatars of Shiva, the idea is not universally accepted in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saivism" title="Saivism">Saivism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheth_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sheth-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> As an avatar requires residence in a womb, Shiva as ayonija (not of a womb) cannot manifest himself as an avatar. The <a href="/wiki/Linga_Purana" title="Linga Purana">Linga Purana</a> speaks of twenty-eight forms of Shiva which are sometimes seen as avatars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Shiva_Purana" title="Shiva Purana">Shiva Purana</a> there is a distinctly Saivite version of a traditional avatar myth: Shiva brings forth <a href="/wiki/Virabhadra" title="Virabhadra">Virabhadra</a>, one of his terrifying forms, in order to calm <a href="/wiki/Narasimha" title="Narasimha">Narasimha</a>, an avatar of Vishnu. When that fails, Shiva manifests as the human-lion-bird <a href="/wiki/Sharabha" title="Sharabha">Sharabha</a>. The story concludes with Narasimha becoming a devotee of Shiva after being bound by Sharabha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> However, Vaishnava followers including <a href="/wiki/Dvaita" title="Dvaita">Dvaita</a> scholars, such as Vijayindra Tirtha (1539–95) refute this Shaivite view of Narasimha based on their reading of <a href="/wiki/Puranas" title="Puranas">Sattvika Puranas</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Aruti" title="Śruti">Śruti</a> texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The monkey-god <a href="/wiki/Hanuman" title="Hanuman">Hanuman</a> who helped Rama – the Vishnu avatar is considered by some to be the eleventh avatar of <a href="/wiki/Rudra" title="Rudra">Rudra</a> (Shiva).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Some regional deities like <a href="/wiki/Khandoba" title="Khandoba">Khandoba</a> are also believed by some to be avatars of Shiva.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Other stated avatars of Shiva, according to some sources, are 8th century non-dualist <a href="/wiki/Vedanta" title="Vedanta">Vedanta</a> philosopher (<a href="/wiki/Advaita_Vedanta" title="Advaita Vedanta">Advaita Vedanta</a>) <a href="/wiki/Adi_Shankara" title="Adi Shankara">Adi Shankara</a>. He was named "Shankara" after Lord Shiva and is considered by some to have been an incarnation of the god<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Virabhadra" title="Virabhadra">Virabhadra</a> who was born when Shiva grabbed a lock of his matted hair and dashed it to the ground. Virabhadra then destroyed <a href="/wiki/Daksha" title="Daksha">Daksha</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Yajna" title="Yajna">yajna</a> (fire sacrifice) and severed his head as per Shiva's instructions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mani_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mani-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Dasam_Granth" title="Dasam Granth">Dasam Granth</a>, Guru Gobind Singh have mentioned two avatars of Rudra: <a href="/wiki/Dattatreya" title="Dattatreya">Dattatreya</a> Avatar and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasnath" title="Parasnath">Parasnath</a> Avatar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Avatars_of_Devi">Avatars of Devi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Avatars of Devi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hanuman_before_Rama.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="744" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hanuman_before_Rama.jpg/300px-Hanuman_before_Rama.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hanuman_before_Rama.jpg/450px-Hanuman_before_Rama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hanuman_before_Rama.jpg/600px-Hanuman_before_Rama.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

A 17th-century painting depicting Hanuman worshiping Rama and his wife Sita. Lakshmana is also seen in this painting from <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a> collection. Rama is considered the avatar of Vishnu, Sita of Vishnu's consort Lakshmi, Lakshmana of <a href="/wiki/Shesha" title="Shesha">Shesha</a> – the serpent on whom Vishnu sleeps, and Hanuman is believed to be an avatar of Shiva.</div>

<p>Avatars are also observed in <a href="/wiki/Shaktism" title="Shaktism">Shaktism</a>, the sect dedicated to the worship of the Goddess (<a href="/wiki/Devi" title="Devi">Devi</a>), but they do not have universal acceptance in the sect. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devi_Bhagavata_Purana" title="Devi Bhagavata Purana">Devi Bhagavata Purana</a> describes the descent of Devi avatars to punish the wicked and defend the righteous—much as the <a href="/wiki/Bhagavata_Purana" title="Bhagavata Purana">Bhagavata Purana</a> does with the avatars of Vishnu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Triumph_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Triumph-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Like Vishnu, his consort <a href="/wiki/Lakshmi" title="Lakshmi">Lakshmi</a> incarnates as <a href="/wiki/Sita" title="Sita">Sita</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radha" title="Radha">Radha</a> – the consorts of Rama and Krishna avatars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Nilakantha, an 18th-century commentator on the Devi Bhagavata Purana – which includes the Devi Gita – says that various avatars of the Goddess includes <a href="/wiki/Shakambhari" title="Shakambhari">Shakambhari</a> and even the masculine Krishna and Rama – generally thought to be Vishnu's avatars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lakshmi" title="Lakshmi">Lakshmi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saraswati" title="Saraswati">Saraswati</a> are also goddesses worshipped as Devi avatars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Avatars_of_Lakshmi">Avatars of Lakshmi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avatar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Avatars of Lakshmi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sridevi" title="Sridevi">Sridevi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bhudevi" title="Bhudevi">Bhudevi</a> are two different forms of Goddess <a href="/wiki/Lakshmi" title="Lakshmi">Lakshmi</a>. Dharini, the consort of <a href="/wiki/Parashurama" title="Parashurama">Parashurama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sita" title="Sita">Sita</a>, the consort of <a href="/wiki/Rama" title="Rama">Rama</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yashodhara" title="Yashodhara">Yashodhara</a>, the consort of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha" title="Buddha">Buddha</a> along with the consorts of the previous incarnations of Vishnu are all considered full incarnations of Lakshmi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Radha" title="Radha">Radha</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gopi" title="Gopi">gopis</a> , <a href="/wiki/Rukmini" title="Rukmini">Rukmini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satyabhama" title="Satyabhama">Satyabhama</a> and the rest of <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a>'s wives with the exception of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalindi" title="Kalindi">Yamuna</a> are all considered partial incarnations of <a href="/wiki/Lakshmi" title="Lakshmi">Lakshmi</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
