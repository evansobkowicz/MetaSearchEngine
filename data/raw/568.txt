<p><i><b>Elvis Presley</b></i> (released in the UK as <i><b>Elvis Presley Rock n' Roll</b></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chartstats_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-chartstats-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup>) is the debut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio album</a> by <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>. It was released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RCA_Victor" title="RCA Victor">RCA Victor</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Monaural" title="Monaural">mono</a>, catalogue number LPM 1254, in March 1956. The recording sessions took place on January 10 and January 11 at <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA</a> <a href="/wiki/Recording_studio" title="Recording studio">recording studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, and on January 30 and January 31 at RCA studios in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>. Additional material originated from sessions at <a href="/wiki/Sun_Studio" title="Sun Studio">Sun Studio</a> in <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis</a>, Tennessee, on July 5, August 19 and September 10 of 1954, and on July 11, 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album spent ten weeks at number 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard Top Pop Albums</a> chart in 1956, the first rock and roll album ever to make it to the top of the charts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and the first million-selling album of that genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Victor136_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Victor136-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2003, it was ranked number 56 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Elvis Presley</i> was also one of three Presley albums accoladed in the reference book <a href="/wiki/1001_Albums_You_Must_Hear_Before_You_Die" title="1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die">1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die</a>, the others being <i><a href="/wiki/Elvis_Is_Back!" title="Elvis Is Back!">Elvis Is Back!</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/From_Elvis_in_Memphis" title="From Elvis in Memphis">From Elvis in Memphis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It was certified Gold on November 1, 1966 and Platinum on August 8, 2011 by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Presley_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the second half of 1955, <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">singles</a> on <a href="/wiki/Sun_Records" title="Sun Records">Sun Records</a> by Presley began making the national <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country and western</a> <a href="/wiki/Hot_Country_Songs" title="Hot Country Songs">singles</a> chart, "<a href="/wiki/Baby_Let%27s_Play_House" title="Baby Let's Play House">Baby Let's Play House</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/I_Forgot_to_Remember_to_Forget" title="I Forgot to Remember to Forget">I Forgot to Remember to Forget</a>" going to number 5 and number 1 respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Colonel_Tom_Parker" title="Colonel Tom Parker">Colonel Tom Parker</a>, the new manager of Presley, had extensive dealings with RCA through his previous client, singer <a href="/wiki/Eddy_Arnold" title="Eddy Arnold">Eddy Arnold</a>, especially with the head of the Country and Western and <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">Rhythm and blues</a> division, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steve_Sholes" title="Steve Sholes">Steve Sholes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> At the urging of Parker, on November 21, 1955, Sholes bought Presley's contract from <a href="/wiki/Sam_Phillips" title="Sam Phillips">Sam Phillips</a>, the head of Sun Records and Studio, for the unprecedented sum of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US$" title="US$">$35,000</a>. Presley and <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> were still untested properties for the major <a href="/wiki/Record_label" title="Record label">labels</a> in the music business, but this album, along with the number 1 single "<a href="/wiki/Heartbreak_Hotel" title="Heartbreak Hotel">Heartbreak Hotel</a>", proved the selling power of both: it was RCA's first pop album to earn more than $1,000,000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Victor136_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Victor136-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and in 1966 it had sold over one million units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Victor136_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Victor136-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Presley made appearances in four consecutive weeks on the <a href="/wiki/The_Dorsey_Brothers" title="The Dorsey Brothers">Dorsey Brothers</a> television program <i><a href="/wiki/Stage_Show_(TV_series)" title="Stage Show (TV series)">Stage Show</a></i> in early 1956, on January 28, February 4, February 11, and February 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> RCA wanted an album in the stores fast to capitalize both on the nationwide TV exposure and the success of his first hit single on the pop charts with "<a href="/wiki/Heartbreak_Hotel" title="Heartbreak Hotel">Heartbreak Hotel</a>", swiftly climbing to the top after its release on January 27. At the same time, there had only been two series of Presley recording sessions for RCA by the end of the Dorsey stint, after which Presley and his band were back on the road. Those two sessions yielded an additional eleven tracks, almost enough to fill an entire LP, although some tracks had singles potential. In the 1950s, general practice dictated tracks having greater commercial potential to be released as singles, with tracks of lesser appeal placed on albums; as such, RCA neither took all eleven tracks and simply made an album, nor placed the already released and briskly-selling "Heartbreak Hotel" on it. The rights to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_songs_recorded_by_Elvis_Presley_on_the_Sun_label" title="List of songs recorded by Elvis Presley on the Sun label">Sun Studio tapes</a> had transferred to RCA with the sale of his contract, so five previously unreleased Sun songs, "<a href="/wiki/I_Love_You_Because_(song)" title="I Love You Because (song)">I Love You Because</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Just_Because_(Nelstone%27s_Hawaiians_song)" title="Just Because (Nelstone's Hawaiians song)">Just Because</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Tryin%27_to_Get_to_You" title="Tryin' to Get to You">Tryin' to Get to You</a>", "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Never_Let_You_Go" title="I'll Never Let You Go">I'll Never Let You Go (Little Darlin')</a>", and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Moon_(song)" title="Blue Moon (song)">Blue Moon</a>" were added to seven of the RCA sessions tracks to bring the running time of the album up to an acceptable length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Victor136_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Victor136-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Phillips produced the sessions at Sun, and no producer was officially listed for the RCA sessions, leading to the belief that Presley himself produced them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As the Sun tracks were mostly country-styled, Elvis and RCA leavened the selections with covers of recent rhythm and blues songs. Two of these, "<a href="/wiki/Money_Honey_(Clyde_McPhatter_and_The_Drifters_song)" title="Money Honey (Clyde McPhatter and The Drifters song)">Money Honey</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Stone" title="Jesse Stone">Jesse Stone</a>, known to Elvis from a version by <a href="/wiki/Clyde_McPhatter" title="Clyde McPhatter">Clyde McPhatter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a>' 1955 hit "<a href="/wiki/I_Got_a_Woman" title="I Got a Woman">I Got a Woman</a>", had been in Presley's live act for a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> A third was the frenetic announcement to the world of the existence of <a href="/wiki/Little_Richard" title="Little Richard">Little Richard</a> in 1955, "<a href="/wiki/Tutti_Frutti_(song)" title="Tutti Frutti (song)">Tutti Frutti</a>". A <a href="/wiki/Rockabilly" title="Rockabilly">rockabilly</a> number that was believed to be a potential hit and could hold its own with the R&amp;B material, "<a href="/wiki/Blue_Suede_Shoes" title="Blue Suede Shoes">Blue Suede Shoes</a>", was not initially released as a single from a promise by Sholes to Sam Phillips to protect the career of another Sun artist, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Perkins" title="Carl Perkins">Carl Perkins</a>, the author of the song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Instead, it was diverted into being the opening track on the album.</p>
<p>On August 31, 1956, RCA took the unusual step of releasing the entire album as singles, which undoubtedly kept the new single released simultaneously, "<a href="/wiki/Shake,_Rattle_and_Roll" title="Shake, Rattle and Roll">Shake, Rattle and Roll</a>" backed with "<a href="/wiki/Lawdy_Miss_Clawdy" title="Lawdy Miss Clawdy">Lawdy Miss Clawdy</a>," from reaching the charts. However, "Blue Suede Shoes", released in single form as a part of this experiment by RCA, kept the promise to Phillips and Perkins by waiting over eight months since the song's <a href="/wiki/Blue_Suede_Shoes" title="Blue Suede Shoes">release on Sun</a>, and made it to number 20 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">singles chart</a>. RCA first issued the original 12 track album in reprocessed (fake) stereo on <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a> in 1984. This issue was quickly withdrawn and the album was reissued in original monophonic sound. On May 18, 1999, it was reissued with an altered running order, adding on six bonus tracks from three non-album singles, including the chart-toppers "<a href="/wiki/Heartbreak_Hotel" title="Heartbreak Hotel">Heartbreak Hotel</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_You,_I_Need_You,_I_Love_You" title="I Want You, I Need You, I Love You">I Want You, I Need You, I Love You</a>." On January 11, 2005, <a href="/wiki/Sony_BMG" title="Sony BMG">Sony BMG</a> reissued the album again, remastered using <a href="/wiki/Direct_Stream_Digital" title="Direct Stream Digital">DSD</a> technology with the six bonus tracks appended in standard fashion. A two-disc set was released on the Follow That Dream collectors label on August 15, 2006, with bonus tracks and numerous <a href="/wiki/Take" title="Take">alternate takes</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Album_cover">Album cover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Presley_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Album cover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cover is ranked number 40 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone's</a></i> list of the 100 greatest album covers, published in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The photograph was taken at the Fort Homer Hesterly Armory in <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa, Florida</a>, on July 31, 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Initially it was thought that <a href="/wiki/Popsie_Randolph" title="Popsie Randolph">Popsie Randolph</a> took the iconic image featured on the front cover, due to the fact that the album only credited one photographer. However, in August, 2002, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Tunzi" title="Joseph A. Tunzi">Joseph A. Tunzi</a> documented that the actual photographer was William V. "Red" Robertson of Robertson &amp; Fresch. The Popsie credit attributed to the album only applied to a series of photos featured on the back cover, taken in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> in early December, 1955, shortly after Presley had signed with RCA Victor. Tunzi was quoted in the Tampa Tribune as saying, "Forget about Popsie. Popsie did not take that photo." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biwa_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-biwa-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The graphic and photo were also used on an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> and a double-EP comprising songs from this album, also released in March 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The design was echoed by <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a> for the front of their 1979 album <i><a href="/wiki/London_Calling" title="London Calling">London Calling</a></i>; that cover is number 39 on the list. Other acts of cover homage include <a href="/wiki/Tom_Waits" title="Tom Waits">Tom Waits'</a> 1985 <i><a href="/wiki/Rain_Dogs" title="Rain Dogs">Rain Dogs</a></i>, <i>Distorted Dimensions</i> by <a href="/wiki/Mad_Sin" title="Mad Sin">Mad Sin</a> in 1990, <i><a href="/wiki/F-Punk" title="F-Punk">F-Punk</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Big_Audio_Dynamite" title="Big Audio Dynamite">Big Audio Dynamite</a> in 1995, and <i><a href="/wiki/Reintarnation" title="Reintarnation">Reintarnation</a></i> in 2006 by <a href="/wiki/K.d._lang" title="K.d. lang">k.d. lang</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chumbawamba" title="Chumbawamba">Chumbawamba</a>'s controversial single "<a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair_(song)" title="Tony Blair (song)">Tony Blair</a>" takes homage too.</p>
